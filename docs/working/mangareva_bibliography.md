# Mangarevan Society at Contact: Bibliography of Primary and Near-Primary Sources (Pre-Buck 1938)

---

## Pre-Missionary European Accounts

**Wilson, James. 1799. *A Missionary Voyage to the Southern Pacific Ocean, Performed in the Years 1796, 1797, 1798, in the Ship Duff*. London: S. Gosnell for T. Chapman.**
First European contact with the Gambier Islands, May 1797. LMS missionaries bound for Tahiti. Brief navigational visit; named the islands after Admiral Gambier. Minimal ethnographic content. Check for any observations on inhabitants encountered during the boat landing.

**Beechey, Frederick William. 1831. *Narrative of a Voyage to the Pacific and Beering's Strait, to Co-operate with the Polar Expeditions: Performed in His Majesty's Ship Blossom, in the Years 1825, 26, 27, 28*. 2 vols. London: Henry Colburn and Richard Bentley.**
Only substantive pre-missionary account. January 1826 visit. First European to land in the Gambier group. Identifies Maputeoa as monarch; describes religious structures (thatched temple with carved idols, coral platform, offering stands); notes population dispersed across four islands; observes rafts but no canoes. The key baseline for what Mangarevan society looked like before missionization. Digitized on Internet Archive and Google Books.

## Missionary Primary Sources: Letters and Periodicals

**Caret, François. c.1836–1837. Letters in *Annales de la Propagation de la Foi*, Vol. 9.**
Among the very earliest written descriptions of Mangarevan society. Caret arrived with Laval in August 1834, left for Europe 1837, returned December 1838. Letters describe encounters with the akariki, religious practices, marriage customs, and the conversion process. Written while traditional structures were still partially intact. The *Annales* are digitized on Gallica (BnF).

**Laval, Honoré. 1840. Letter in *Annales de la Propagation de la Foi*, Vol. 70 (May 1840), pp. 563–565.**
Letter to the Archbishop of Chalcedon about the island of Moé. One of multiple letters Laval published in the *Annales* during his tenure. Other volumes of the *Annales* likely contain additional Laval correspondence; a systematic search of the digitized run on Gallica is warranted.

**Maigret, Louis-Désiré. c.1835–1840. Diary (unpublished manuscript, ~900 pages in 4 notebooks). Archives of the Cathedral of Our Lady of Peace, Honolulu, Hawaii.**
Picpus Father who spent c.1835–1840 in the Gambier Islands before becoming Bishop of Hawaii. Diary records idol destruction, encounters with Maputeoa, mass baptisms, a list of Mangarevan gods. Largely untranslated and unpublished. Analyzed by Jean Charlot (see below). A major underexploited source covering the critical early conversion period with daily firsthand observation. Pacific Manuscripts Bureau may hold microfilm.

**Soulié, Gilbert. c.1836–1863. Notebooks (published by Jean-Paul Delbos).**
Lay brother and builder who arrived 1836, died on Mangareva 1863. Notebooks transcribed and published in: Delbos, Jean-Paul. *La Mission du bout du monde: La fantastique aventure des bâtisseurs de cathédrales dans l'archipel des Gambier*. Papeete: Éditions de Tahiti / Éditions Univers Polynésiens, 2011. Described as "a goldmine" for daily life detail during the missionary period. Captures ordinary social interactions and working relationships.

## Missionary Primary Sources: Major Works

**Laval, Honoré. 1938. *Mangareva: L'histoire ancienne d'un peuple polynésien*. Braine-le-Comte: Maison des Pères des Sacrés-Coeurs; Paris: P. Geuthner. xxviii + 374 pp.**
The foundational ethnographic source. Manuscript composed c.1868 at Rikitea in collaboration with Father Tiripone Mama Taira Putairi (first indigenous Mangarevan priest). Edited by Père Maurice Desmedt; annotated by Alfred Métraux; preface by Peter Buck. 38 chapters: chs. 1–26 are genealogical chronicle of ancient kings; chs. 27–38 are ethnographic (daily life, social organization, property, religion, death customs, subsistence). Describes akariki, togoʻiti, pakacra, ʻurumanu, tapu, tribute, marae. Print run of only 600 copies. Re-edition: *Mangareva, ère païenne*. Papeete: Éditions Haere Pō, 2013. Critically: this is informant reconstruction, not direct observation, composed 34 years after arrival and 32 years after the system was dismantled.

**Laval, Honoré. 1968. *Mémoires pour servir à l'histoire de Mangareva, ère chrétienne, 1834–1871*. Edited by C.W. Newbury and Patrick O'Reilly. Paris: Société des Océanistes, Publications No. 15. cxlii + 672 pp.**
Laval's memoirs of the mission period (the Christian era companion to the above). Contains a 142-page scholarly introduction by Newbury and O'Reilly. Describes the political transformation: King Maputeoa's role, chiefly councils under mission influence, the establishment of a theocratic regime. This volume offers direct observation of the transition period and is essential for understanding the speed and completeness of political-religious transformation.

## Missionary Linguistic Works

**Janeau, V.F. (ed.). 1908. *Essai de grammaire de la langue des îles Gambier ou Mangareva*. Braine-le-Comte: Imprimerie Zech et fils. 354 pp.**
Compiled by the Catholic missionaries of the archipelago. 197 pp. grammar, 14 pp. dialogues, 124 pp. dictionary (double columns). The dictionary entries embed ethnographic information about rank, political offices, religious practices, and social categories. S. Percy Smith (1918) noted it is "full of references to the traditional history of the Group, the manners and customs of the natives."

**Tregear, Edward. 1899. *A Dictionary of Mangareva (or Gambier Islands)*. Wellington: J. Mackay, Government Printer. 121 pp.**
Compiled from missionary-supplied manuscript vocabularies, almost certainly from Roussel and other Picpus missionaries. ~6,600 Mangarevan entries; lacks English check vocabulary. Tregear never visited Mangareva. Digitized on Internet Archive.

**Roussel, Hippolyte. 1908 [compiled earlier]. *Vocabulaire de la langue de l'Île-de-Pâques ou Rapanui*.**
Not a Mangarevan source per se, but Roussel also contributed to the Mangarevan linguistic materials used by Tregear. Relevant for tracking Roussel's linguistic-ethnographic framework across both islands.

## Non-Missionary 19th Century Accounts

**Moerenhout, Jacques-Antoine. 1837. *Voyages aux îles du Grand Océan*. 2 vols. Paris: Arthus Bertrand.**
Belgian-born trader based in Tahiti. Includes a section on "Gambier et îles voisines" covering geography, ethnography, language, religion, social customs. Greatest depth on Tahiti and Society Islands; Gambier material appears limited and possibly secondhand. Check Vol. 2 for the Gambier section.

**Dumont d'Urville, Jules. 1842–1854. *Voyage au Pôle Sud et dans l'Océanie sur les corvettes l'Astrolabe et la Zélée*. 24+ vols. Paris: Gide.**
Visited Gambier Islands August 1838. Hydrographer Vincendon-Dumoulin produced first detailed chart. By 1838 the conversion was already complete (Maputeoa baptized 1836). Primarily hydrographic, but check for ethnographic observations. The relevant volume would be in the "Histoire du voyage" series.

**Cuzent, Gilbert. 1872. *Voyage aux Îles Gambier (Archipel de Mangarèva)*. Paris: V. Masson et fils. ~176 pp., with map and 5 drawings.**
French naval pharmacist and botanist who visited during his service in Tahiti (arrived 1854). Describes landscapes, fauna, flora, "culture and customs of the local inhabitants," including the insular kingdoms. Published nearly four decades after missionization, so describes a transformed society, but may preserve observations about surviving customs. Digitized on Google Books.

## Early 20th Century Secondary and Compilation Sources (Pre-Buck)

**Caillot, A.C.E. 1910. *Histoire de la Polynésie orientale*. Paris: Ernest Leroux.**
Included Mangarevan material based on native manuscripts. Buck explicitly warned it was "misleading through faulty translation." Despite unreliability, it was the source available to Williamson and others before Laval's 1938 publication. Check for the specific Mangarevan chapters/sections.

**Smith, S. Percy. 1918. "Notes on the Mangareva, or Gambier Group of Islands, Eastern Polynesia." *Journal of the Polynesian Society* 27(107): 115–131.**
Translated and published selections from the 1908 missionary grammar's dictionary portion. Provided the first English-language access to Mangarevan ethnographic material. Includes notes on early European visitors. Prompted by receipt of a copy of the 1908 grammar from Père Hervé Audran. Available in the JPS digital archive (jps.auckland.ac.nz).

**Williamson, Robert W. 1924. *The Social and Political Systems of Central Polynesia*. 3 vols. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.**
Comparative treatment. For Mangareva, Williamson relied on Caret's letters in the *Annales*, Caillot's flawed translations, and Beechey's narrative. He had no access to Laval's manuscript. Coverage necessarily limited and derivative.

**Eskridge, Robert Lee. 1931. *Manga Reva: The Forgotten Islands*. Indianapolis: Bobbs-Merrill. 286 pp.**
American painter who visited in the late 1920s. Chapters on "Cannibal Kings" and "The Native Way of Living" discuss traditional political hierarchy and pre-missionary social organization. Popular rather than scholarly, but preserves late oral traditions and provides firsthand observations of surviving practices.

**Janeau, V.F. 1935. "Archipel des Gambiers." Manuscript, Archives of the Congregation of the Sacred Hearts, Rome/Braine-le-Comte.**
Van Bakel demonstrated this is merely a re-translation of Laval's work from Mangarevan back into French. Not an independent source despite sometimes being cited as one.

**Williamson, Robert W. 1937. *Religion and Social Organization in Central Polynesia*. Edited by Ralph Piddington. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.**
Posthumous companion to the 1924 volumes. Includes Mangareva. Same limited source base as the 1924 work.

## Unpublished and Archival Sources

**Routledge, Katherine. 1921. Unpublished Mangarevan fieldwork materials. Royal Geographical Society, London.**
Visited Mangareva in 1921. Collected vocabularies, folk tales, songs, chants, myths, and translations. Also obtained a copy of the native manuscript tradition. Nothing was ever published. Buck noted: "The Routledge Expedition visited Mangareva and procured a copy of the native manuscript, but nothing eventuated in the way of publications." The RGS confirmed in recent years that Routledge manuscripts are held in their collections.

**Roussel, Hippolyte. Correspondence. Catholic Archdiocese of Papeete administrative archives. Microfilmed as PMB 1080, Pacific Manuscripts Bureau, Australian National University.**
Letters from Roussel's various postings (Tuamotus, Mangareva, Easter Island, return to Mangareva). No known separate publication about Mangarevan society, but correspondence may contain relevant observations about Mangarevan social organization and authority, particularly from his late period (1871–1898).

**Picpus Congregation archives, Rome (and formerly Braine-le-Comte, Belgium).**
General repository for correspondence, reports, and manuscripts of the Sacred Hearts missionaries. May contain additional letters from Caret, Laval, Roussel, Soulié, and other missionaries stationed in the Gambier Islands not published in the *Annales*.

## Key Analytical and Secondary Sources (for evaluating the above)

**Charlot, Jean. 1969. "Le 'Journal' du Picpucien Louis Maigret." *Journal de la Société des Océanistes* 25 (December): 320–335.**
Analysis of Maigret's unpublished diary. In French. Essential for understanding what the diary contains without accessing the original. Also available: English translation at vault.jeancharlot.org.

**Van Bakel, Martin A. 1989. "The Political Economy of a Stateless Society: Mangareva." In *Bijdragen tot de Taal-, Land- en Volkenkunde* 145(2/3): 275–298 (or in Henri Claessen and Pieter van de Velde, eds., *Ethnographic Studies in Honour of Pieter de Josselin de Jong*).**
Critical analysis demonstrating that Laval, Buck, and Janeau all derive from the same single information chain. Also estimated population at ~2,500 in the early 19th century. Essential for evaluating the independence (or lack thereof) of the source base.

**Buck, Te Rangi Hiroa (Peter H.). 1938. *Ethnology of Mangareva*. Bernice P. Bishop Museum Bulletin 157. Honolulu.**
The standard ethnographic reference and the terminal date for this bibliography. Buck drew on Laval's manuscript (which he also helped get published), his own limited fieldwork in 1934, and missionary sources. He acknowledged the poverty of surviving traditional culture. This is the work everyone cites; the point of the present bibliography is to identify what came before it and what it actually rests on.

**Goldman, Irving. 1970. *Ancient Polynesian Society*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press.**
Dedicated chapter on Mangareva. Classifies it among the "Stratified" societies of Eastern Polynesia. Primary sources: Buck and Laval.

**Sahlins, Marshall D. 1958. *Social Stratification in Polynesia*. Monographs of the American Ethnological Society, 29. Seattle: University of Washington Press.**
Places Mangareva in an intermediate tier of stratification. Primary source: Buck (1938). The comparative framework that shaped all subsequent treatments.

**Kirch, Patrick V., and Eric Conte (eds.). 2004. *Archaeological Investigations in the Mangareva Islands (Gambier Archipelago), French Polynesia*. Archaeological Research Facility Contributions No. 62. Berkeley: University of California.**
First major independent archaeological evidence. Characterizes Mangareva as having features of "open societies" with fluid statuses, qualifying the classic portrait of rigid stratification.
