# The Chiefdom Concept: Revised Outline for *Current Anthropology*

---

## Part 1: Strategic Considerations for CA

### Why CA

- The argument is a four-field intervention, not archaeological housekeeping
  - Epistemological critique (Lewontin, Perreault) speaks to philosophy of science
  - Sociology of knowledge thread (Latour, Fleck) speaks to STS and intellectual history
  - Decolonial thread speaks to cultural anthropology
  - Collective action alternative connects to political science and institutional economics through Ostrom
- CA is the only venue where all of those audiences intersect

### The Commentary Format

- CA's commentary format is the paper's biggest strategic asset
- The argument is provocative enough that editors will want commentaries
- The commentaries are where the disciplinary impact happens
- Write the paper to force commentators to engage with the epistemological argument rather than retreating to "chiefdoms are useful heuristics"
- Anticipated objections the paper must preemptively address:
  - **"The concept has evolved beyond the straw man"** (Earle-type response)
    - Counter: the concept fails on empirical sufficiency grounds regardless of refinement; every variant is defined by social relationships invisible in the record
  - **"We can see chiefdoms archaeologically"** (Kirch/McCoy-type response)
    - Counter: the equifinality argument; every claimed archaeological signature is equally consistent with collective action alternatives
  - **"You're just replacing one model with another"** (methodological conservative)
    - Counter: not proposing a replacement model but an epistemological standard; any model must generate expectations measurable in the archaeological record
  - **"The concept is heuristically useful even if imprecise"** (pragmatist)
    - Counter: a heuristic that fits any evidence discriminates nothing; its "utility" is that it gives archaeologists something to say, not that it tells us something about the past
  - **"This is just Pauketat's argument again"**
    - Counter: Pauketat critiqued the chiefdom on theoretical and historical-processual grounds; this paper adds the epistemological critique (empirical sufficiency, equifinality), the ethnohistoric circularity demonstration, the sociology of knowledge analysis, and the decolonial dimension
  - **"Future methods (isotopes, Bayesian modeling, aDNA) will resolve the equifinality problem"**
    - Counter: the gap is ontological, not technical; the concept's defining features are social relationships (hereditary rank, redistribution, centralized decision-making), not material properties; improved resolution does not change what the record fundamentally contains; no amount of finer-grained data on strontium isotopes or paleogenomics tells you whether surplus mobilization was directed by a chief or organized collectively

### Tone

- CA readers include the people who built careers on the concept
- The paper needs to be respectful of the intellectual history while being uncompromising on the epistemological point
- Framing: "the concept was productive for its time but the evidence base cannot support it," not "everyone who used this concept was wrong"
- Pauketat's *Chiefdoms and Other Archaeological Delusions* was effective but the title alone guaranteed defensive responses
- A stronger argument can be made with a more measured tone
- Acknowledge real contributions (Earle's power sources framework, Blanton et al.'s dual-processual model) while showing they don't require the chiefdom category

### The Decolonial Thread: Integration Strategy

- Will resonate with CA's current editorial direction (indigenous epistemologies, collaborative archaeology, colonial legacies)
- **Key structural decision: integrate the colonial dimension into the ethnohistoric projection section rather than giving it a standalone section**
  - Rationale: the contact-period accounts are distorted both temporally (single time slice projected backward) and culturally (European political categories imposed on non-European realities); these are two dimensions of the same problem
  - A separate decolonial section invites readers to treat it as optional or as a political add-on
  - Integrated, the colonial dimension strengthens the epistemological argument rather than sitting alongside it
- The decolonial critique should strengthen the epistemological argument, not replace it
  - If the paper reads as primarily a political critique, defenders can dismiss it
  - If it reads as an epistemological critique with decolonial implications, it's much harder to deflect

### Length and Format

- CA major articles typically 10,000-12,000 words
- The argument needs that space
- Estimated 60-80 citations in the paper itself; the full bibliography is a research resource, not a works-cited list
- Deciding early which sources do argumentative work in the text versus which serve as background is essential

### Suggested Commentators

- **Timothy Earle**: the most sophisticated defender of chiefdom-as-analytical-tool; his response would sharpen the debate
- **Timothy Pauketat**: historical processualism; has already made part of this argument from different grounds

## Part 2: Paper Outline

### Working Title

"Empirical Sufficiency and the Chiefdom Concept: Why Archaeology Cannot Diagnose Political Organization from the Archaeological Record"

*Alternative titles:*
- "The Chiefdom as Prehistoric Ethnography: Epistemological Failure, Colonial Inheritance, and the Path Forward"
- "What the Archaeological Record Cannot Tell Us: Empirical Sufficiency, Equifinality, and the End of the Chiefdom"

---

### Revised Structure: Four Movements

The original outline proposed five movements. This revision compresses to four by folding the theoretical critique into the introduction and integrating the decolonial thread into the ethnohistoric section.

**Rationale for the restructuring:**

1. **The theoretical failure section (old section II) is compressed into the introduction.** The theoretical critique (Yoffee, Pauketat, Feinman and Neitzel) is well-trodden ground. If it receives a full section, reviewers will feel they've read this paper before. Framing it explicitly as "the critique that didn't kill the concept" sets up the epistemological argument as the one that actually closes the door. The intellectual history needs to be there, but it should be efficient: one to two pages establishing what has already been said and why it was insufficient.

2. **The decolonial critique (old section IX in the argument outline) is integrated into the ethnohistoric projection section.** The contact-period accounts are distorted both temporally and culturally. These are two dimensions of the same problem, and the argument is stronger when they are presented together. A standalone decolonial section risks being treated as a political appendix. Integrated, it becomes part of the evidentiary case.

3. **The constructive alternative is organized around a worked example (Rapa Nui) rather than a list of research programs.** A list of programs (cultural transmission, phylogenetics, ABM, Bayesian inference) reads like a catalog. Building the section around Lipo and Hunt's Rapa Nui work shows concretely what empirically sufficient archaeology looks like in a case where chiefdom models were previously dominant. Other programs are presented as meeting the same standard, but the reader sees one example in depth.

4. **Kuhn is used sparingly in the introduction; the sociological analysis in its own section relies on Latour and Fleck.** The original outline invoked Kuhn in both the theoretical section (ad hoc proliferation as paradigm defense) and the sociology-of-knowledge section (paradigm persistence). These are somewhat different explanatory frameworks. Kuhn describes intellectual crisis and replacement; Latour describes network stabilization regardless of intellectual merit. This revision uses Kuhn briefly in the introduction to name the pattern of ad hoc modification, then does the real sociological work with Latour and Fleck in the dedicated section.

**The four movements:**

1. The chiefdom fails as epistemology (the evidence cannot adjudicate)
2. The chiefdom fails as methodology (the evidence base is circular and colonial)
3. Why it persists despite these failures (institutional utility, not intellectual merit)
4. What an empirically grounded alternative looks like (worked example plus standard)

---

### I. Introduction (approx. 1,500-2,000 words)

**The concept and its ubiquity:**
- The chiefdom is one of the most widely used and least examined concepts in anthropological archaeology
- It appears in virtually every introductory textbook, structures CRM reports, frames grant proposals, organizes regional syntheses
- Despite four decades of sustained critique, it persists as the default framework for interpreting non-state complex societies

**The theoretical critique: what has already been said**
- Brief intellectual history: Service (1962), Fried (1967), Carneiro (1970), Earle (1987, 1991, 1997)
- The concept was productive: it opened comparative analysis of non-state complex societies
- But the theoretical critique is now well established:
  - Feinman and Neitzel (1984): ethnographic data shows continuous variation, not discrete types
  - Yoffee (1993): the model is a theory of classification, not a theory of social change
  - Pauketat (2007): the category prevents understanding societies on their own terms
  - Earle (2011) himself acknowledges "typological exercises are arbitrary divisions"
- The response has been ad hoc proliferation: simple chiefdoms, complex chiefdoms, paramount chiefdoms, group-oriented chiefdoms, theocratic chiefdoms
  - Each refinement is an attempt to save the category by accommodating anomalies (note the Kuhnian pattern without belaboring it)
- The collective action alternative (Blanton and Fargher 2008, 2016; Carballo et al. 2014) demonstrates that the same evidence supports fundamentally different models
- Heterarchy (Crumley 1987, 1995), historical processualism (Pauketat 2001), and network approaches (Holland-Lulewicz 2021; Brughmans and Peeples 2023) offer frameworks that do not require fitting societies into predetermined evolutionary categories

**Why the theoretical critique hasn't been enough:**
- Despite all this, the concept persists
- The reason: the theoretical critique challenged the model's adequacy, but defenders can always respond with further refinement
- What has not been adequately addressed is whether the archaeological record can support the concept at all, regardless of which version is on offer
- This paper makes three independent arguments that the concept is unsalvageable:
  - **Epistemologically**: the archaeological record lacks the quality and resolution to distinguish chiefdom organization from alternatives
  - **Methodologically**: the evidence base is circular, derived from post-contact ethnohistoric accounts projected backward, filtered through colonial categories
  - **Sociologically**: the concept persists through institutional utility, not intellectual merit
- The paper then articulates the epistemological standard any replacement must meet and demonstrates what research calibrated to that standard looks like

---

### II. The Epistemological Failure (approx. 3,000-3,500 words)

*The archaeological record cannot adjudicate*

This is the paper's most original and most important argument. It should receive the most space.

**The core problem:**
- The chiefdom describes social relationships: hereditary rank, redistribution, centralized decision-making, ideological legitimation
- None of these are directly observable in the archaeological record
- What archaeologists observe: settlement patterns, architectural differentiation, mortuary variability, evidence of surplus mobilization
- The inferential leap from material patterns to chiefdom organization depends entirely on ethnographic analogy (Wobst 1978; Wylie 1985, 2002)

**The empirical sufficiency problem (Lewontin):**
- Lewontin's career-long commitment: claims in science should not go beyond what the evidence can demonstrate
- The chiefdom concept claims more than the evidence can demonstrate
- The question is not whether the model is internally coherent but whether available data can distinguish it from alternatives
- Gould and Lewontin (1979): accepting interpretations merely consistent with evidence, without testing against alternatives, is "just-so storytelling"

**The data quality problem (Perreault):**
- Archaeological data operates at sampling intervals of hundreds to thousands of years
- The social processes chiefdom models describe (redistribution cycles, succession disputes, tribute extraction) operate at scales of years to decades
- The record cannot resolve these processes
- Archaeologists are doing prehistoric ethnography with data that can only support macroscale pattern analysis

**The equifinality problem (Dunnell, Schiffer, Rogers):**
- Multiple distinct organizational forms produce indistinguishable material signatures
- A platform mound: chiefly labor mobilization OR cooperative construction by autonomous groups
- A settlement hierarchy: paramount-subordinate political relationships OR periodic aggregation of independent communities
- Differential mortuary treatment: hereditary rank OR achieved status in a society resisting permanent hierarchy
- Rogers (2000): as the number of plausible alternative processes increases, the probability of correctly identifying the cause from the outcome alone approaches zero

**The classification problem (Dunnell 1971, 1980):**
- The chiefdom is an intensionally defined ideational unit imposed on the record, not an extensionally defined empirical unit derived from it
- Archaeological phenomena do not arrive pre-sorted into "chiefdoms"
- Researchers sort them based on analogical expectations from ethnography
- The category conflates classification with explanation

**Preemptive address: can future methods solve this?**
- Improved resolution (isotopic analysis, aDNA, high-precision radiocarbon) does not change the fundamental problem
- The concept's defining features are social relationships, not material properties
- Better data on strontium ratios or paleogenomics tells you about mobility and kinship, not about whether surplus mobilization was directed by a chief or organized collectively
- The gap is ontological, not technical: the record contains material, not social relationships
- This is not an argument against methodological innovation; it is an argument that no amount of technical improvement can make the record answer a question it is structurally unable to answer

### III. The Methodological Failure (approx. 3,000-3,500 words)

*The evidence base is circular and colonial*

**Structural note:** This section integrates what were previously two separate threads (the ethnohistoric projection and the decolonial critique) into a single argument. The contact-period accounts are distorted both temporally and culturally. Presenting these as two dimensions of the same problem is more analytically coherent and harder to dismiss.

**The ethnohistoric projection:**
- Much of what archaeologists "know" about prehistoric political organization comes from post-contact historical accounts, not from the archaeological record
- The direct historical approach (Strong 1935; Lyman and O'Brien 2001) assumes contact-period societies are adequate models for their predecessors
- But contact-period observations are doubly problematic:
  - **Temporal distortion**: observations were made during massive demographic and political disruption; they were collapsed into a "timeless ethnographic present" (Stahl 1993; Fabian 1983; Bailey 2007)
  - **Cultural distortion**: shaped by European political categories; observers saw "chiefs" because they looked for recognizable authority structures; what they recorded tells us as much about European political imagination as about the societies described (Smith 1999/2021; Wobst 2005)
- The two distortions are not independent. Colonial observers brought European temporal and political assumptions simultaneously. They recorded a single disrupted moment as timeless tradition, and they filtered that moment through their own political vocabulary. Both distortions enter the archaeological interpretive chain at the same point.

**The five-step circularity:**
1. European observers record contact-period political arrangements, shaped by European categories, colonial power dynamics, and transformations already underway
2. Accounts are treated as descriptions of "traditional" or "prehistoric" society, collapsing centuries of change into a timeless ethnographic present
3. Archaeologists generate expectations about what the record should contain
4. Expectations are general enough that the record always satisfies them
5. The "confirmation" is circular: the model drives the interpretation, which is then presented as independent support

**Cross-regional demonstration:**
- Polynesia: Cook-era and missionary accounts frame interpretation of temples, field systems, settlement patterns (Kirch 1984, 2010; McCoy 2006; but see Dye 2010 for the counter)
  - Sahlins and Kirch (1992) illustrate the asymmetry: ethnohistory provides the social framework, archaeology provides material confirmation; the archaeology never challenges the ethnohistoric model because it was never designed to do so
- U.S. Southeast: de Soto-era accounts frame interpretation of Mississippian mound centers (Hudson 1997; but see Ethridge 2010 on contact-period disruption; Pauketat 2004 on Cahokia's historical specificity)
- Caribbean: Columbus-era accounts frame interpretation of Taino ceremonial architecture (Wilson 1990; but see Keegan 2007 on colonial-period transformation)
- In each case: the contact-period account provides the narrative, and the archaeological evidence provides the illustrations

**The colonial dimension (integrated):**
- The projection is not only temporal but cultural
- European categories imposed on non-European political realities
- The chiefdom concept reproduces colonial epistemologies regardless of researchers' intentions (Smith 1999/2021; Atalay 2006, 2012)
- The violence of classification: when a Polynesian society is labeled a "chiefdom," that label comes from a Western evolutionary typology imposed on a non-Western political reality
- When indigenous communities participate as research partners, the analytical categories shift (Nicholas 2010; Supernant et al. 2020; Hau'ofa 1994 on Oceania as "sea of islands" versus bounded chiefdom environments)
- Graeber and Wengrow (2021): indigenous peoples were conscious political agents who chose and experimented with social arrangements, not passive subjects of evolutionary forces
- This is not a peripheral concern; it goes to the foundations of what categories we use to describe whose past

**Breaking the circle:**
- Collective action research succeeds because it derives expectations from measurable properties of the record rather than from ethnohistoric narratives
- Transition to the constructive alternative (section V)

### IV. Why the Chiefdom Persists (approx. 2,000-2,500 words)

*Institutional utility, not intellectual merit*

**Structural note:** This section uses Latour and Fleck as its primary analytical frameworks. Kuhn is referenced only to the extent that earlier sections have named the ad hoc modification pattern; the deeper sociological analysis happens here.

- If the concept fails epistemologically and methodologically, why does anyone still use it?
- The answer is sociological, not intellectual
- Scientific concepts persist through networks of citation, institutional practice, and rhetorical convention, not through empirical adequacy (Latour 1987; Latour and Woolgar 1979)

**Institutional embedding:**
- Textbooks: the band-tribe-chiefdom-state sequence provides a clean four-act narrative
- CRM reports: the vocabulary is expected by clients and regulatory agencies
- Grant proposals: the framework is recognizable and uncontroversial for reviewers
- Introductory courses: easy to teach, fits a 50-minute lecture
- All of these uses require the concept; none require it to be empirically adequate

**Thought style (Fleck 1935):**
- A thought collective shares assumptions, vocabulary, and methods that constrain what can be asked and answered
- The chiefdom provides a vocabulary ("paramount chief," "redistribution," "prestige goods economy"), expected material correlates (settlement hierarchies, differential mortuary treatment), and a narrative arc
- Once internalized through graduate training, it becomes the lens through which evidence is perceived

**Unexamined premise (Schiffer 1988):**
- Premises are unstated, untested assumptions structuring research
- The chiefdom is assumed before fieldwork begins
- It determines what gets recorded and how results are reported
- Nobody excavates and concludes "the evidence is insufficient to determine political organization"
- The framework demands a diagnosis, and the epistemological looseness of the record ensures the diagnosis can always be made

**Disciplinary identity (Lyman 2007):**
- Archaeology's attachment to anthropological models reflects a desire for legitimacy within anthropology
- The chiefdom gives archaeologists something to say about social organization that sounds authoritative
- Abandoning it means acknowledging that the archaeological record cannot answer the questions anthropology departments reward

### V. The Constructive Alternative (approx. 2,000-2,500 words)

*Models calibrated to the data we actually have*

**Structural note:** This section is reorganized around a worked example rather than a list of research programs. The Rapa Nui case demonstrates concretely what empirically sufficient archaeology looks like in a context where chiefdom models were previously dominant. Other programs are then presented as meeting the same standard, but the reader sees one case in depth.

**The standard: empirical sufficiency**
- Not a counsel of despair; a call to match research questions to evidence quality
- Before building a model, ask: "what would I measure, and can the record provide that measurement?"
- This is Lewontin translated into archaeological practice
- Not championing a single replacement but articulating the criterion any replacement must meet

**Worked example: Rapa Nui**
- The conventional narrative: powerful chiefs commanded massive labor forces to carve and transport moai as competitive displays; overexploitation led to ecological and social collapse (Diamond 2005; drawing on earlier chiefdom interpretations)
- The chiefdom narrative was driven by ethnohistoric accounts (Roggeveen 1722, later European visitors) projected backward onto the archaeological sequence
- Lipo and Hunt's program replaced this with empirically grounded analysis:
  - **Moai morphology**: 962 moai analyzed; morphological features consistent with vertical transport, not horizontal sledge-and-roller transport predicted by the chiefly command model (Lipo et al. 2025)
  - **Transport mechanics**: experimental replication demonstrated vertical "walking" requiring only 18 people with ropes, not hundreds under centralized direction (Lipo, Hunt, and Rapu Haoa 2013)
  - **Spatial distribution**: ahu locations explained by proximity to freshwater sources, not political or competitive factors (DiNapoli et al. 2019)
  - **Production organization**: 3D modeling of Rano Raraku quarry reveals 30 distinct quarrying foci with redundant production features, indicating decentralized, clan-based production (Lipo et al. 2025)
  - **Demographic resilience**: Bayesian analysis shows population resilience rather than pre-contact collapse (DiNapoli et al. 2021)
  - **Commons management**: Ostrom's design principles applied to Rapa Nui resource management (DiNapoli, Lipo, and Hunt 2021)
- What makes this empirically sufficient: it builds a cable of independent evidence lines (morphological, spatial, experimental, computational, demographic) from measurable properties of the record, without requiring inference about invisible social relationships
- The chiefdom interpretation required projecting a contact-period narrative onto the record; the collective action interpretation lets the record generate its own account

**Other programs meeting this standard:**
- Cultural transmission theory (Boyd and Richerson 1985; Cavalli-Sforza and Feldman 1981): provides theoretical warrant for studying measurable artifact variation
- Cultural evolutionary archaeology (Shennan 2002; Mesoudi 2011; Lycett 2015): treats the record as a population of cultural variants subject to transmission, selection, and drift
- Phylogenetic methods (O'Brien and Lyman 2003; Tehrani and Collard 2002; Lipo et al. 2006): reconstructs cultural lineages from measurable trait variation without requiring inference about social organization
- Agent-based modeling (Kohler and van der Leeuw 2007; Crabtree et al. 2017): generates testable expectations from explicit theoretical models; one of the few methods that can break equifinality by generating divergent predictions
- Bayesian generative inference (Kandler and Powell 2018): formalizes the relationship between process and pattern
- Network and institutional approaches (Brughmans and Peeples 2023; Holland-Lulewicz et al. 2020): studies political organization as relational, not categorical; draws on Ostrom's institutional analysis framework

**What these share:**
- They work with measurable properties of the archaeological record
- They generate testable, potentially falsifiable expectations
- They do not require inference about unmeasurable social relationships
- They are not consolation prizes; they are the mature toolkit of a discipline calibrated to its evidence

---

### VI. Conclusion (approx. 500-750 words)

- The chiefdom has been rendered obsolete by converging developments:
  - A theoretical alternative that explains the same evidence without centralized authority
  - An epistemological critique revealing the evidence was never sufficient
  - A demonstration that the diagnostic narrative was ethnohistoric, circular, and colonial
  - A sociological analysis of persistence
- The trajectory: away from what societies *were* (typological classification of invisible social relationships) and toward what societies *did* (empirically grounded analysis of measurable material patterns)
- The chiefdom concept asked a question the archaeological record cannot answer
- It is time to ask better questions

## Part 3: Revision Rationale Summary

This section documents the key structural changes from the original outline and the reasoning behind each.

### 1. Compressed the theoretical failure into the introduction

**Original:** A full section (II) devoted to the theoretical critique (Yoffee, Pauketat, Feinman and Neitzel, Earle).

**Revised:** The theoretical critique is presented efficiently in the introduction as established ground, framed as "the critique that didn't kill the concept."

**Rationale:** This material is well known to the CA audience. Giving it a full section risks making the paper feel derivative. Compressing it into the introduction serves a rhetorical function: it establishes that the theoretical argument alone was insufficient and sets up the epistemological argument as the decisive intervention. The introduction now does double duty, providing intellectual history and motivating the paper's distinctive contribution.

### 2. Integrated the decolonial critique into the ethnohistoric projection section

**Original:** Separate sections for the ethnohistoric projection (VII in the argument outline) and the decolonial critique (IX in the argument outline), which were mapped to sections IV and a portion of IV in the paper outline.

**Revised:** A single section (III) treats temporal distortion and cultural distortion as two dimensions of the same problem.

**Rationale:** The contact-period accounts are problematic because they represent a single time slice *and* because that time slice was recorded through European political categories. These are not independent problems. Presenting them together makes the argument more analytically coherent. It also prevents the decolonial thread from being treated as an optional political addition. Integrated into the evidentiary case, it becomes part of the epistemological argument rather than sitting alongside it. This addresses the strategic concern in the original strategy memo: "the decolonial critique should strengthen the epistemological argument, not replace it."

### 3. Reorganized the constructive alternative around a worked example

**Original:** A list of research programs (cultural transmission, phylogenetics, ABM, Bayesian inference, network approaches) with brief descriptions of each.

**Revised:** The section leads with the Rapa Nui case as a detailed worked example of empirically sufficient archaeology, then presents other programs as meeting the same standard.

**Rationale:** A list reads like a catalog and gives the reader no concrete sense of what the alternative looks like in practice. The Rapa Nui case is ideal because: (a) it directly replaces a chiefdom interpretation, (b) the bibliography contains extensive material on it, (c) it demonstrates multiple independent evidence lines (morphological, spatial, experimental, computational, demographic), and (d) it shows the ethnohistoric circularity in action (the collapse narrative was driven by Roggeveen-era accounts) and the alternative method breaking that circle. After the worked example, the other programs are presented efficiently as further instances of the same epistemological standard.

### 4. Added a preemptive response to the "future methods" objection

**Original:** Not addressed.

**Revised:** Section II includes a subsection addressing whether methodological advances (isotopic analysis, aDNA, high-precision radiocarbon, Bayesian modeling) could eventually resolve the equifinality problem.

**Rationale:** A CA commentator will almost certainly raise this. The answer is that the gap is ontological, not technical. The concept's defining features are social relationships, not material properties. Improved resolution on material data (mobility patterns, genetic kinship, dietary reconstruction) does not resolve whether surplus mobilization was directed by a chief or organized collectively. The paper should address this directly rather than leaving it for commentators to exploit.

### 5. Clarified the division of labor between Kuhn and Latour/Fleck

**Original:** Kuhn invoked in both the theoretical section (ad hoc proliferation as paradigm defense) and the sociology-of-knowledge section (paradigm persistence).

**Revised:** Kuhn is referenced briefly in the introduction to name the pattern of ad hoc modification. The dedicated sociology-of-knowledge section (IV) relies on Latour and Fleck.

**Rationale:** Kuhn and Latour offer different explanatory frameworks. Kuhn describes paradigms that persist until intellectual crisis forces replacement. Latour describes concepts stabilized through networks of citation, institutional practice, and professional identity, regardless of intellectual merit. Fleck describes thought styles internalized through training. The Latour/Fleck account is more appropriate for the chiefdom case because the concept has survived intellectual crisis for decades. The persistence is not about insufficient anomalies; it is about institutional embedding. Using Kuhn for the pattern and Latour/Fleck for the explanation avoids conflating these frameworks.
