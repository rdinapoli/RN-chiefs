# The Chiefdom Concept: Argument Outline

---

## Introduction

- The chiefdom has been one of the most productive and contested concepts in anthropological archaeology for over six decades
- The field has shifted from typological classification toward understanding diverse processes by which complex societies emerge
- Collective action theory, heterarchy, and historical processualism now offer robust alternatives to the band-tribe-chiefdom-state sequence
- The most fundamental challenge is epistemological:
  - The concept describes social relationships the archaeological record cannot observe
  - It relies on contact-period ethnohistoric accounts projected backward onto the prehistoric past
  - It produces material expectations so general that any archaeological evidence can be made to fit the model

---

## I. Foundational Neoevolutionary Frameworks

- These works established the chiefdom as a formal analytical category
- They developed causal theories for transitions from egalitarian organization to centralized political hierarchy
- The neoevolutionary school drew on White's cultural evolutionism and Steward's multilinear evolution
- The resulting typological frameworks dominated political anthropology for three decades

---

## II. Critiques of the Chiefdom Concept

- Beginning in the late 1980s, scholars from diverse theoretical traditions mounted sustained challenges
- Critiques targeted:
  - The typology's inability to capture variability
  - Its conflation of classification with explanation
  - Its Eurocentric and teleological assumptions
  - Its erasure of non-hierarchical pathways to complexity

---

## III. Collective Action Alternatives

- A major theoretical shift reframing evidence traditionally interpreted as top-down chiefly command as products of collective action, cooperation, and bottom-up organization
- Draws on rational choice theory, Ostrom's institutional analysis, and cross-cultural comparison
- Fundamentally challenges the assumption that monumentality, surplus mobilization, and supra-local integration require hierarchical leadership

## VI. Epistemological Foundations: The Problem of Empirical Sufficiency

- A critical dimension of the chiefdom debate is whether the archaeological record can even adjudicate between competing models of political organization
- The chiefdom describes social relationships (redistribution, hereditary rank, chiefly authority) that are either:
  - Invisible in the archaeological record, or
  - Produce material signatures indistinguishable from those generated by collective action
- This section draws on Lewontin's empirical sufficiency and Perreault's analysis of archaeological data quality
- The chiefdom framework fails not just theoretically but empirically, as a form of prehistoric ethnography applied to a record incapable of supporting it

---

## VII. The Ethnohistoric Projection Problem

- The epistemological problems are compounded by a specific methodological circularity
- Much of what archaeologists "know" about prehistoric political organization derives not from the archaeological record but from post-contact historical accounts
- The mechanism:
  - European observers recorded political arrangements at the moment of contact
  - Those accounts were treated as descriptions of "traditional" or "prehistoric" society
  - Archaeological evidence was interpreted to fit that narrative
  - The archaeological data becomes window dressing for a story already written from ethnohistoric sources
- The result: any archaeological evidence can be, and routinely is, made to fit the chiefdom model, because the model was never derived from the archaeological record in the first place
- This is acute in Polynesia (Cook-era and missionary accounts) but pervades:
  - The U.S. Southeast (de Soto and early colonial accounts)
  - The Caribbean (Columbus-era accounts)
  - Mesoamerica
  - Everywhere chiefdom models are applied
- The five-step circular logic:
  1. European observers record political arrangements at contact -- arrangements shaped by European categories, colonial power dynamics, and transformations already underway
  2. These accounts are treated as descriptions of "traditional" or "prehistoric" society, collapsing centuries of change into a timeless ethnographic present
  3. Archaeologists use these accounts to generate expectations about what the archaeological record should contain (settlement hierarchies, monumental architecture, differential mortuary treatment, surplus mobilization)
  4. Because these expectations are framed at a level of generality the record can always satisfy, archaeological evidence invariably "confirms" the ethnohistoric narrative
  5. The confirmation is circular: the model was derived from ethnohistory, the evidence is interpreted through the model, and the result is presented as independent archaeological support
- Collective action breaks this circle by deriving expectations from measurable properties of the archaeological record (moai morphology, ahu placement relative to freshwater, settlement spacing, energetic costs) rather than from ethnohistoric narratives about chiefly power

## VIII. The Sociology of Knowledge: Why the Chiefdom Persists

- The chiefdom has survived decades of critique
- Understanding why requires the sociology of scientific knowledge: how concepts are stabilized through institutional practice, pedagogical convention, and rhetorical networks rather than through empirical adequacy
- The chiefdom persists not because it is intellectually compelling but because it is institutionally useful:
  - Provides a ready-made narrative arc for textbooks (band to tribe to chiefdom to state)
  - Gives CRM reports a vocabulary clients and agencies understand
  - Gives grant proposals a recognizable framework reviewers won't question
  - Structures introductory courses in a way that's easy to teach
  - Gives archaeologists something to say about social organization that sounds authoritative even when the data can't support it
- The concept's persistence is a sociological phenomenon, not an intellectual one
- Latour: scientific "facts" are stabilized through networks of citation, institutional practice, and rhetorical convention, not empirical adequacy
  - To abandon the chiefdom requires dismantling an entire infrastructure of professional practice
- Fleck: thought collectives share assumptions, vocabulary, and methods that constrain what questions can be asked and what answers are recognizable
  - The chiefdom functions as a thought style: once internalized through graduate training, it becomes the lens through which evidence is perceived
- Schiffer: "premises" are unstated, untested assumptions that structure research
  - The chiefdom is the paradigmatic archaeological premise: assumed before fieldwork begins, determines what gets recorded, shapes how results are reported
  - Nobody excavates and concludes "the evidence is insufficient to determine political organization" -- the framework demands a diagnosis

---

## IX. The Decolonial Critique: Whose Categories, Whose Past?

- The chiefdom is not only theoretically weak and epistemologically unsupported; it is a product of colonial knowledge production
- The label was derived from European observers' descriptions of non-Western societies
  - Filtered through European political categories
  - Imposed on archaeological records worldwide
- The projection problem is not just temporal (contact period onto prehistory) but also cultural (European categories onto non-European realities)
  - Captains, missionaries, and colonial administrators saw "chiefs" because they were looking for recognizable authority structures
  - What they recorded tells us as much about European political imagination as about the societies being described
- Graeber and Wengrow's two core decolonial moves:
  1. Recovering indigenous intellectual contributions to Enlightenment political thought (especially the Wendat critique of European hierarchy through Kandiaronk)
  2. Arguing that indigenous peoples were conscious political agents who deliberately chose and experimented with social arrangements, rather than passive subjects of evolutionary forces
- But the decolonial archaeology literature goes further:
  - Raises the question: whose categories are we using to describe whose past?
  - When a Polynesian society is labeled a "chiefdom," that label comes from a Western evolutionary typology imposed on a non-Western political reality
  - When indigenous communities participate as partners rather than subjects, the chiefdom label rarely survives
- Works best in the paper as a dimension of the ethnohistoric projection argument rather than a standalone section:
  - The contact-period accounts are problematic not only because they represent a single time slice but because they represent a *colonial* time slice filtered through European political categories and recorded by observers with specific agendas
  - Double distortion: temporal and cultural

## X. Models Calibrated to Data: The Constructive Alternative

- The argument leads to a specific positive conclusion:
  - Any theoretical model deployed in archaeology must generate expectations that can be evaluated with the data the archaeological record actually provides
  - The chiefdom fails this test because its defining features produce no unique material signatures
- The solution is not a specific replacement model but an epistemological discipline:
  - Before building a model, ask: "what would I measure, and can the record provide that measurement?"
  - This is Lewontin's empirical sufficiency translated into archaeological practice
- This framing means the paper doesn't need to champion one particular alternative
  - It articulates the criterion any alternative must meet
  - That's more powerful because it's not tied to the fortunes of any single research program
- Research programs that meet this standard:
  - Cultural transmission theory (Boyd and Richerson, Cavalli-Sforza and Feldman): provides theoretical warrant for studying measurable artifact variation as a legitimate research program
  - Cultural evolutionary archaeology (Shennan, Mesoudi, Lycett): treats the record as a population of cultural variants subject to transmission, selection, and drift
  - Phylogenetic methods (O'Brien and Lyman, Tehrani and Collard, Lipo et al.): reconstructs cultural lineages from measurable trait variation without requiring inference about social organization
  - Agent-based modeling (Kohler, Crabtree): generates testable expectations from explicit theoretical models, forces specification of assumptions
  - Bayesian generative inference (Kandler and Powell): formalizes the relationship between process and pattern, provides tools for discriminating between competing explanations
- These are not consolation prizes for failing to reconstruct social organization
- They are the mature toolkit of a discipline calibrated to its evidence

## Conclusion

- The chiefdom's arc: born in the 1950s as a typological stage, productively elaborated through the 1970s-1980s, now rendered obsolete

**The theoretical problem:**
- Conflates classification with explanation
- Obscures more variation than it captures
- Projects Western evolutionary assumptions onto diverse human experiences
- These are serious but not the most damaging problems

**The epistemological problem (the most damaging):**
- The concept describes social relationships not directly observable in the archaeological record
- What we observe: settlement patterns, architectural differentiation, mortuary variability, surplus mobilization
- The inferential leap depends entirely on ethnographic analogy
- This is Wobst's "tyranny of the ethnographic record" and Lewontin's failure of empirical sufficiency

**The equifinality problem:**
- Archaeological data operates at scales orders of magnitude coarser than chiefdom models describe (Perreault)
- At these scales, top-down chiefly command is indistinguishable from bottom-up collective action
- A platform mound is consistent with chiefly labor mobilization AND with cooperative construction by autonomous kin groups
- A settlement hierarchy is consistent with paramount-subordinate relationships AND with a central aggregation site
- Differential mortuary treatment is consistent with hereditary rank AND with achieved status in a society resisting permanent hierarchy
- The record cannot adjudicate; no amount of additional excavation at the same resolution will change this

**The circularity problem:**
- Much of what archaeologists "know" comes from post-contact historical accounts, not from the archaeological record
- European observers' accounts were shaped by European categories, colonial power dynamics, and transformations already underway
- These were treated as descriptions of "traditional" society, collapsing centuries of change into a timeless ethnographic present
- Archaeologists generated expectations the record can always satisfy
- The evidence invariably "confirmed" the narrative; the data never got to speak for itself
- This operates across all major regional traditions

**The persistence problem:**
- The concept persists because it is institutionally useful, not intellectually compelling
- It is embedded in textbooks, CRM templates, grant frameworks, professional identities
- It operates as a thought style (Fleck): once internalized through training, it becomes the lens through which evidence is perceived
- It functions as an unexamined premise (Schiffer): assumed before fieldwork, determines what gets recorded, shapes how results are reported

**The colonial problem:**
- The projection is not only temporal but cultural: European categories onto non-European realities
- Observers saw "chiefs" because they were looking for recognizable authority structures
- What they recorded tells us as much about European political imagination as about the societies described
- When indigenous communities participate as partners, the chiefdom label rarely survives

**The constructive alternative:**
- Not a counsel of despair; a call to match research questions to the quality of evidence
- Any model must meet the standard of empirical sufficiency: do not claim more than the evidence can demonstrate
- Collective action succeeds because it grounds arguments in what the record can demonstrate (e.g., Lipo and Hunt on Rapa Nui: moai morphology, transport mechanics, spatial distributions, experimental tests)
- Cultural evolutionary approaches, phylogenetics, agent-based modeling, Bayesian inference all work with measurable properties of the record
- The path forward: abandon the chiefdom as explanatory framework; replace it not with a single model but with the epistemological discipline of asking "what would I measure, and can the record provide that measurement?"
- Dunnell's insistence: if we cannot define chiefdoms in terms of measurable archaeological properties (and we cannot), the category has no place in archaeological analysis

**The closing:**
- The chiefdom is not dead as a word people use
- But as an analytical tool it has been rendered obsolete by converging developments:
  - A theoretical alternative (collective action) that explains the same evidence without requiring centralized authority
  - An epistemological critique (empirical sufficiency, equifinality, formation processes) revealing the evidence was never sufficient
  - A demonstration that the diagnostic narrative was never archaeological but ethnohistoric and circular
  - A sociological analysis of why the concept persists despite these failures
  - A decolonial critique exposing the colonial origins of the categories being imposed
- The trajectory: away from what societies *were* (typological classification of invisible social relationships) and toward what societies *did* (empirically grounded analysis of measurable material patterns)
- The chiefdom concept asked a question the archaeological record cannot answer
- It is time to ask better questions
