# The Chiefdom Model for Rapa Nui Is Empirically Insufficient

## Detailed Paper Outline

---

### Paper Overview

**Core thesis:** The classification of Rapa Nui as a chiefdom or complex chiefdom has no independent archaeological support. The attribution derives from (1) a Polynesian chiefdom model imported from other islands where it is itself built on circular ethnohistoric reasoning, (2) contact-era European accounts that do not describe chiefdom organization and in several cases describe its absence, and (3) post-slave-raid ethnographic work conducted on a demographically shattered society that cannot serve as a window onto pre-contact political life. When the archaeological record is analyzed on its own terms, it supports decentralized cooperative production and community-level resource management, not centralized chiefly control.

**Target venue:** Journal of Archaeological Method and Theory or Journal of Anthropological Archaeology

**Anticipated length:** 8,000-12,000 words

**Authors:** Lipo and Hunt, possibly others

**Relationship to broader project:** This paper establishes the Rapa Nui case in detail, providing the empirical foundation for a subsequent Current Anthropology paper arguing that the chiefdom concept fails on epistemological grounds across archaeology. The CA paper will cite this as a worked demonstration of the general argument.

## I. Introduction

### Argument
- Rapa Nui is routinely classified as a chiefdom or complex chiefdom in textbooks, comparative syntheses, and regional studies
- This classification is treated as established fact, not as a hypothesis requiring evidential support
- The paper asks a simple question: what is the evidentiary basis for this classification?
- The answer: there is none that is independent of (a) an imported Polynesian analogy, (b) ambiguous and often contrary contact-era European accounts, and (c) post-catastrophe ethnographic reconstructions
- The archaeological record, when analyzed without these imported frameworks, supports cooperative, decentralized organization
- This is not primarily an argument for collective action (that case has been made in prior publications) but an argument that the chiefdom attribution was never warranted by the evidence

## II. The Chiefdom Label on Rapa Nui: Who Says It and On What Basis

### Argument
- The chiefdom classification for Rapa Nui is widespread but its proponents are not all making the same argument
- Some apply the Polynesian evolutionary model as a regional analogy (Kirch)
- Some assume monumental architecture requires centralized authority (Van Tilburg, Martinsson-Wallin)
- Some embed the chiefdom in a collapse narrative that requires a powerful polity to collapse (Diamond)
- Some use the term as conventional vocabulary without making an independent argument (various)
- None derive the classification from independent analysis of Rapa Nui's archaeological record
- Sorting active arguments from inherited labels reveals how thin the evidential basis is

- The key point: no one has made an independent, archaeologically grounded argument for chiefdom organization on Rapa Nui

## III. What the European Observers Actually Recorded

### Argument
- The contact-era European accounts of Rapa Nui do not describe chiefdom political organization
- In several cases, observers are actively frustrated by the absence of centralized authority: they seek someone to "treat with" and cannot find a leader who commands others
- The ariki or important men presented to Europeans appear to be senior figures (possibly with hereditary standing or achieved status) who lack coercive authority over others
- The behavioral descriptions (as distinct from the labels Europeans applied) are consistent with decentralized, non-hierarchical organization
- These accounts have been selectively read to extract "chiefs" while ignoring the descriptions of their apparent lack of authority
- The contact-era record for Rapa Nui is also simply thin: brief visits, limited observation, language barriers, and European categorical assumptions all constrain what can be drawn from it

### What this section needs
- Chronological treatment of each major contact-era visit, focused specifically on what was observed and recorded about political authority, leadership, and social organization
- For each account: (a) what the observer describes behaviorally, (b) what labels or inferences the observer applies, (c) the gap between description and inference
- Attention to what is not described: redistribution, tribute, territorial hierarchy, hereditary succession, labor mobilization under command
- An honest assessment of the limitations of these sources (brevity, language barriers, European categories) without overclaiming what they demonstrate

### Contact-era accounts (chronological)

### Key points to establish
- Across all contact-era visits, the descriptions of political authority are thin and ambiguous at best, and contrary to chiefdom expectations at worst
- The Europeans are looking for hierarchical authority and not finding it clearly
- The ariki figure presented to Europeans does not behave as a chief in the Service/Earle sense
- The accounts cannot bear the interpretive weight that the chiefdom attribution places on them

## IV. The Post-Slave-Raid Ethnographic Record

### Argument
- The slave raids of 1862-1863 and subsequent epidemics killed or removed the majority of the Rapanui population, including most holders of specialized knowledge
- The ethnographic and oral historical work that has been most influential on archaeological interpretations (Thompson 1891, Routledge 1914-1915, Metraux 1934-1935) was conducted on a post-catastrophe society
- These researchers recorded oral traditions, genealogies, and accounts of political offices (ariki mau, tangata manu, mata toa) from survivors and descendants who were reconstructing their own past under radically altered conditions
- This material has been treated as a reliable window onto pre-contact political organization, but it cannot be: the society that produced it had been through a demographic and cultural catastrophe of extraordinary severity
- The oral traditions describe political offices and structures, but we cannot know how much of this reflects pre-contact organization versus post-catastrophe reconstruction, consolidation, or reinterpretation
- Using this material as the basis for classifying pre-contact Rapa Nui as a chiefdom commits the same error as the broader Polynesian ethnohistoric projection, compounded by the catastrophe problem

### What this section needs
- A brief account of the slave raids and their demographic and cultural impact (sufficient to establish the scale of disruption, not a full history)
- Identification of the key post-slave-raid ethnographic sources and what they claim about political organization
- A specific analysis of how these sources have been used in archaeological interpretation of pre-contact Rapa Nui
- An honest acknowledgment that these sources may contain genuine pre-contact information, while arguing that they cannot be uncritically treated as descriptions of pre-contact political life
- Connection to the broader ethnohistoric projection problem: the post-slave-raid accounts function similarly to contact-era accounts elsewhere, providing a narrative that the archaeological record is then made to illustrate

### Key sources

**Primary ethnographic sources**
- Thompson 1891 (USS Mohican visit; first extended ethnographic work)
- Routledge 1919 (most extensive early ethnography; worked with elderly informants who were children during the slave raids)
- Metraux 1940 (Ethnology of Easter Island; the standard ethnographic reference for decades)
- Englert 1948/1970 (missionary-ethnographer; long residence)

**The political offices described in these sources**
- Ariki mau (paramount chief?)
- Tangata manu (birdman; often described as an alternative or successor to chiefly authority)
- Mata toa (warrior leaders?)
- How have these been mapped onto archaeological phases and features?

**Secondary uses in archaeological interpretation**
- Which archaeological studies cite Thompson, Routledge, or Metraux as the basis for interpreting pre-contact political organization?
- How does the tangata manu system get periodized and mapped onto archaeological evidence?

### Key points to establish
- The demographic catastrophe makes these sources unreliable as descriptions of pre-contact political life
- The oral traditions were recorded from a small surviving population under colonial conditions
- The political structures described may represent post-contact transformations, consolidations, or reinterpretations
- Archaeological interpretations built on these sources inherit all of these problems
- This is not an argument that the oral traditions are worthless, but that they cannot serve as the independent evidential basis for classifying pre-contact political organization

### Open questions
- How much of the archaeological periodization of Rapa Nui (e.g., the shift from "ahu moai phase" to "birdman phase") is driven by the post-slave-raid ethnographic accounts?
- Is there archaeological evidence for the tangata manu system that is independent of the oral traditions?
- How do we handle the political sensitivity of critiquing oral traditions? This needs to be done carefully, distinguishing between respecting oral tradition as cultural knowledge and using it as archaeological evidence for pre-contact political structure.

## V. The Polynesian Analogy

### Argument
- Given that the Rapa Nui-specific evidence (contact-era accounts, post-slave-raid ethnography) does not independently support chiefdom classification, the attribution rests primarily on a regional analogy: Polynesian societies are chiefdoms, Rapa Nui is Polynesian, therefore Rapa Nui was a chiefdom
- This analogy depends on the Polynesian chiefdom model being well-established for other islands, particularly Hawaii, Tonga, and Samoa
- But that model has its own circularity problems: it is built substantially on ethnohistoric accounts (Cook-era and missionary observations) projected backward onto the archaeological record
- The analogy thus transfers a problematic interpretation from one context to another without independent validation in either
- The assumption that Polynesia is a coherent unit with respect to political organization is itself an untested premise; the diversity of Polynesian societies is well-documented
- Monumental architecture is the primary material basis for the analogy (ahu/moai on Rapa Nui mapped onto heiau/marae elsewhere), but monumental architecture is equifinal: it does not uniquely indicate chiefdom organization

### What this section needs
- A concise account of how the Polynesian chiefdom model was constructed (Sahlins 1958, Sahlins 1963, Kirch 1984, Kirch and Green 2001)
- Identification of the specific analogical reasoning that transfers the model to Rapa Nui
- A demonstration that the analogy is doing the interpretive work rather than local evidence
- A brief treatment of the circularity problems in the source cases (Hawaii, Tonga) without fully prosecuting those cases (that would be another paper)
- The equifinality of monumental architecture: ahu/moai do not uniquely index chiefdom organization

### Key sources
- Sahlins 1958 (Polynesian stratification gradient; Rapa Nui positioned on this gradient)
- Sahlins 1963 (big-man vs. chief distinction)
- Kirch 1984 (evolutionary model for Polynesian chiefdoms)
- Kirch 2010 (chiefs becoming kings; Rapa Nui as part of this trajectory?)
- Kirch and Green 2001 (ancestral Polynesian society reconstruction)
- Wobst 1978 (tyranny of the ethnographic record)
- Wylie 1985, 2002 (formal vs. relational analogy)

### Key points to establish
- The chiefdom label for Rapa Nui arrives via analogy, not via local evidence
- The analogy is a formal analogy in Wylie's terms (based on surface similarity: Polynesian + monumental architecture = chiefdom) rather than a relational analogy (grounded in demonstrated causal connections)
- The source cases for the analogy have their own evidential problems
- Polynesian societies are diverse enough that assuming political uniformity is unwarranted

### Open questions
- How explicitly does Kirch extend his model to Rapa Nui? Does he make an argument, or simply include it?
- Does the Kirch and Green (2001) reconstruction of Ancestral Polynesian Society include political organization, and if so, how does Rapa Nui fit?
- Are there other Polynesian cases where the chiefdom label has been questioned? (If so, Rapa Nui is not an outlier but part of a pattern.)

## VI. The Archaeological Evidence on Its Own Terms

### Argument
- When the archaeological evidence from Rapa Nui is analyzed independently of ethnohistoric narratives and the Polynesian analogy, it does not support chiefdom organization
- For each claimed archaeological correlate of chiefdom organization, the evidence is either equifinal (consistent with both chiefdom and collective action models) or actively favors decentralized cooperative organization
- The empirical sufficiency standard requires that we not claim more than the evidence can demonstrate; the evidence cannot demonstrate chiefdom organization on Rapa Nui

### What this section needs
- Treatment of each major category of archaeological evidence, structured as: (a) what the chiefdom model predicts or assumes, (b) what the evidence actually shows, (c) whether the evidence can distinguish between chiefdom and collective action interpretations
- This section draws heavily on published Lipo and Hunt work but reframes it around the epistemological question rather than presenting it as an alternative narrative

### Archaeological evidence categories

**Moai production and transport**
- Chiefdom claim: moai construction required centralized command over large labor forces
- Evidence: moai morphology is consistent with vertical transport by small cooperative teams (Lipo et al. 2013, Lipo et al. 2025a); quarry organization shows 30+ redundant production foci indicating decentralized, clan-based production rather than centralized control (Lipo et al. 2025b); no evidence of the infrastructure (roads, rollers, sledges, provisioning stations) required by mass-labor transport models
- Assessment: the evidence actively favors decentralized cooperative production

**Ahu placement and distribution**
- Chiefdom claim: ahu represent chiefly territorial centers or markers of chiefly authority
- Evidence: ahu locations are explained by proximity to freshwater sources (DiNapoli et al. 2019), consistent with community resource needs rather than political territories
- Assessment: the evidence favors community-level functional placement over political-territorial models

**Settlement patterns**
- Chiefdom claim: settlement patterns reflect territorial hierarchy under chiefly control

- Assessment: [to be determined based on available evidence]

**Subsistence and surplus mobilization**
- Chiefdom claim: agricultural surplus was mobilized under chiefly direction for monument construction and redistribution
- Evidence: [what do the agricultural and subsistence data show? lithic mulch gardens, marine resources, etc.]
- Assessment: [to be determined]

**Mortuary evidence**
- Chiefdom claim: differential mortuary treatment reflects hereditary social ranking
- Evidence: [what is the state of mortuary evidence on Rapa Nui? is there clear evidence of hierarchical differentiation?]
- Assessment: [to be determined]

**Population and sustainability**
- Chiefdom claim (via collapse narrative): a powerful chiefdom overexploited the island's resources, leading to collapse
- Evidence: population data show resilience rather than collapse before European contact (DiNapoli et al. 2021); the "ecocide" narrative is not supported by archaeological, palynological, or demographic evidence (Hunt and Lipo 2011)
- Assessment: the collapse narrative that requires a chiefdom to collapse is not supported

- The evidence that does exist is more consistent with decentralized, cooperative organization
- The chiefdom model for Rapa Nui has been sustained not by the archaeological evidence but despite it

- Are there any material signatures on Rapa Nui that are uniquely predicted by the chiefdom model and not by collective action? (The answer is almost certainly no, but this needs to be demonstrated rather than assumed.)

## VII. Empirical Sufficiency as the Standard

### Argument
- The Rapa Nui case demonstrates a general epistemological principle: attributions of political organization in archaeology must be grounded in evidence the archaeological record can actually provide
- The chiefdom model for Rapa Nui fails the empirical sufficiency test: its defining features (hereditary rank, centralized authority, redistribution, ideological legitimation) produce no unique material signatures distinguishable from collective action alternatives
- The standard is not "which model is more likely" but "can the evidence discriminate between models at all"
- On Rapa Nui, the evidence cannot sustain the chiefdom diagnosis, and in several domains it actively contradicts it
- This is not a counsel of despair; it is a call to ask questions the evidence can answer

### What this section needs
- Explicit articulation of the empirical sufficiency standard (Lewontin) applied to this case
- The equifinality problem stated clearly for Rapa Nui: for each claimed correlate, show that chiefdom and collective action models predict indistinguishable material outcomes, except where the evidence actively favors one over the other
- Connection to Perreault's data quality argument: the temporal resolution of Rapa Nui's archaeological record cannot resolve the social processes (redistribution cycles, succession, tribute extraction) that chiefdom models describe
- Brief articulation of what empirically sufficient research on Rapa Nui looks like, pointing to the approach exemplified by existing published work (morphological analysis, spatial modeling, experimental testing, Bayesian inference)
- This section bridges the Rapa Nui case to the broader epistemological argument the CA paper will make

### Key sources
- Lewontin 1974 (empirical sufficiency)
- Gould and Lewontin 1979 (just-so stories)
- Perreault 2019 (archaeological data quality)
- Rogers 2000 (equifinality formalized)
- Dunnell 1971, 1980 (classification vs. grouping; ideational vs. empirical units)
- Wylie 2002 (cable vs. chain inference)
- Currie 2018 (methodological omnivory in historical sciences)

### Key point to establish
- Rapa Nui is not an anomalous case where the chiefdom model happens to fit poorly
- It is a clear case where the epistemological problems with chiefdom attribution are fully visible because the ethnohistoric record is thin enough that the archaeological evidence gets a chance to speak for itself
- In cases where the ethnohistoric record is thicker (Hawaii, the Southeast), the circularity is harder to see but no less real

## VIII. Implications and Conclusion

### Argument
- The Rapa Nui case has implications beyond the island
- If the chiefdom cannot be sustained where the evidence is this clear (thin ethnohistoric record, contrary contact-era accounts, strong independent archaeological evidence for collective action), it raises the question of how well-supported the attribution is in cases where the ethnohistoric record is thicker and the circularity harder to detect
- The paper does not claim to have resolved the question of Rapa Nui political organization; it claims to have shown that the chiefdom classification is unwarranted by the available evidence
- The path forward is not a replacement typological label but the discipline of asking, before any model is applied, whether the evidence can discriminate between competing interpretations

### What this section needs
- A concise restatement of the paper's findings (not a summary of each section, but the core result)
- The connection to broader chiefdom debates (briefly; the CA paper will develop this fully)
- An explicit statement of what the paper does and does not claim
- No overreach: this paper establishes the case for Rapa Nui and signals broader implications without trying to prosecute the general argument

### Open questions
- How much to signal the CA paper? A sentence or two connecting to the broader epistemological critique is sufficient. The Rapa Nui paper should stand on its own.
