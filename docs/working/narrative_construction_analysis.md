# Narrative Construction Analysis: Political Authority on Rapa Nui, 1722-1957

[UNVERIFIED: working notes for manuscript drafting. All claims below should be verified against claims files and primary sources before use in manuscript prose.]

## Purpose

This document traces how the narrative about political authority on Rapa Nui was constructed across 235 years of European and ethnographic accounts. It reads the full chronological sequence from Roggeveen (1722) through Métraux (1957) to identify when specific chiefdom-narrative elements enter the record, why they appear where they do, and how they build on each other. This feeds directly into Sections III and IV of the manuscript and connects to the paper's central argument about the five-step circularity.

# Part I: Source-by-Source Register

## 1. Roggeveen 1722

**Observer context**: Jacob Roggeveen, Dutch navigator commanding three ships. First European contact. Arrived April 5, landed April 10. On-island contact limited to one day. No language access; all interpretation through observed behavior. No intermediary.

**Population**: Estimated 2,000-3,000 (no census; ship observers estimated "many hundreds" on the beach).

**What is described about political authority**: A single individual wearing distinctive dress (white feathers, white garment, white shell on breast) directed the provisioning of food after the Dutch landing party shot and killed 10-12 islanders. "One who seemed to be in authority over the other headmen, for, giving a general direction that everything they had should be fetched and laid before us, including fruit, root crops, and poultry, the order was promptly obeyed with reverence and bowing" (Corney, p. 27). Bouman independently describes an individual using physical threat (picking up a stone) to enforce compliance; the threatened person prostrated himself, was forgiven, then obeyed (von Saher 1994, p. 99).

**European labels applied**: "King or Head Chief" (Roggeveen, used once, p. 35); "headmen" and "chiefs" (Bouman, plural).

**Gap between observation and label**: Roggeveen uses "King or Head Chief" based on a single episode of food-provisioning direction during a crisis. The label carries connotations of permanent, institutionalized authority; the observation shows situational crisis management. Bouman's plural "chiefs" implies distributed authority, not paramountcy.

**What is NOT described**: No hereditary succession or genealogy. No tributary system. No redistribution. No formal diplomatic protocol on initial contact (first visitor was a lone, naked individual). No differentiated dwelling. No centralized housing or visible wealth differentiation. No weapons of any kind (Bouman explicitly: "absolutely no weapons at all"). No religious authority connected to political power. No territorial control. No named individuals.

**Critical timing**: The feathered individual appears ONLY after the shooting, not during two days of peaceful prior contact. This pattern is consistent with crisis/emergency leadership, not institutionalized authority (claims file SP-2).

## 2. González 1770

**Observer context**: Spanish naval expedition under Felipe González de Haedo. Two ships. Landed November 15-20, 1770 (six days). Formal annexation ceremony. Primary ethnographic observer: Sub-lieutenant Don Antonio de Aguera y Infanzón. No language access; communication through gesture. No intermediary. 48 years after Roggeveen.

**Population**: Not estimated by observers; probably 2,000-3,000.

**What is described about political authority**: Age-graded social differentiation: "the older and more important men retain some preference" in sexual and social matters, and "to whom the women render obedience, and not to the younger men" (Aguera, p. 61). Dwelling hierarchy observed: caves (most people), reed huts ("more polished or powerful persons"), stone dwellings near statues (those Aguera "believes to be their ministers," explicit inference). One vocabulary entry: *tequeteque*, glossed as "High Chief or Lord," elicited through "signs, demonstrations, and figures shown by drawings" (p. 73). An annexation treaty was signed by "some of the natives present" (p. 68).

**European labels applied**: "SIGNATURES OF CHIEFS OF EASTER ISLAND, TO THE SPANISH TREATY, 1770" (plate caption, p. 75). This label does not appear in Aguera's text. The original says only "some of the natives present" signed. Neither Aguera nor Herve identifies who the signatories were, how they were selected, or what authority they held. "Chiefs" is a Corney/Hakluyt Society editorial insertion driven by Spanish colonial legal requirements for treaty cession.

**Critical translation error**: Jakubowska (2014, p. 117) demonstrates that the original Spanish *hombres mayores* ("elderly men") was translated by Corney as "principal men" or "those in authority." This systematic inflation of age-based description into political authority language is one of the most consequential translation errors for the chiefdom argument. Additional mistranslations: *canasto* (basket) rendered as "crown" (importing royal symbolism); *sin reparo alguno* ("without hesitation") rendered as "without making any return" (changing meaning from behavior to economic non-reciprocity).

**Gap between observation and label**: Every observation of social differentiation in the González accounts tracks with age, not hereditary rank. The dwelling hierarchy's top tier rests on Aguera's explicit speculation ("believes to be their ministers"). The vocabulary entry *tequeteque* tells us what the Spanish were looking for, not what Rapanui political structure was. The treaty signatories are a legal fiction required by colonial procedure.

**What is NOT described**: No individual directing other islanders. No tribute or redistribution. No centralized exchange. No war leaders. No feasting or food distribution. No named individuals. No council or deliberative body. No sacred restrictions enforced by authority. No hereditary succession. No differentiated chiefly residence (the inference about "ministers" near statues is speculative).

## 3. Cook/Forsters 1774

**Observer context**: James Cook's second voyage. HMS Resolution, with Johann Reinhold Forster and Georg Forster as naturalists. Arrived March 13-14, 1774. On-island contact approximately two days. Cook and the Forsters had previously visited Tahiti, Tonga, and the Society Islands, where they had directly observed elaborate chiefly deference. Some language access through a Tahitian crew member.

**Population**: Cook estimated "six or seven hundred" (well below other estimates; possibly reflects visible population only).

**What is described about political authority**: Cook observed a chief distribute sugar cane during a walk. The Forsters describe social differentiation. But the key passage is the explicit negative comparison: "We did not observe that he was held in a very high esteem or that he was paid a humiliating homage, as it is demanded by despots from Asiatic islands" (Jakubowska 2014, p. 82). Cook himself: "I confess myself quite ignorant" about the island's governance.

**European labels applied**: "Chief" (used by all three observers). The Forsters apply the most interpretive framework, drawing comparisons to other Polynesian societies and reading more political structure into their observations than Cook does.

**Gap between observation and label**: Cook and the Forsters had a comparative baseline: they knew what chiefly deference looked like from direct observation in Tahiti and Tonga. Their explicit statement that they did NOT find it on Rapa Nui is the single most damaging passage for the chiefdom model from any contact-era source, because it comes from observers who were looking for exactly that behavior and recognized its absence.

**What is NOT described**: No homage or humiliating deference. No redistribution networks. No centralized exchange. No war canoes or fleets (canoes were "a very wretched thing, patched together of several pieces," Forster, von Saher 1999, RNJ 13(2), p. 43). No visible coercive authority. Cook noted ecological constraint: "As everything must be raised by dint of labour, it cannot be supposed the inhabitants plant much more than is sufficient for themselves." Multiple independent cooking fires. No household utensils beyond gourds.

## 4. La Pérouse 1786

**Observer context**: Jean-François de Galaup, comte de La Pérouse. French naval expedition. The most extensive contact-era visit: 10-11 hours ashore, April 9-10, 1786. Well-staffed: naturalists (Dagelet, Lamanon), artists (Duché), botanist, clergy, approximately 70 persons landed. Well-read in prior accounts. Best-provisioned of the four major visits.

**Population**: La Pérouse estimated approximately 2,000.

**What is described about political authority**: La Pérouse describes "a chief in each district" using custodial language ("chief" of a garden or area), not command language. He observes that selling produce required "almost a general consent" (p. 324), describing consensus-based decision-making. Villages were located near burial platforms, suggesting kinship/lineage-based territorial organization, not administrative districts.

**European labels applied**: "Chief" in the custodial sense described above.

**Gap between observation and label**: Despite having the largest landing party, the longest single visit, the most scientific observers, and full knowledge of prior accounts, La Pérouse does not identify any paramount authority figure. The "chiefs" he describes are horticultural custodians, not political rulers. His "general consent" observation actively contradicts chiefly command.

**What is NOT described**: No paramount chief or king. No tributary or redistributive system. No centralized authority over exchange. No political hierarchy. No diplomatic reception. The absence is notable given the scale and expertise of the expedition and the explicit expectation (from prior accounts) that such authority existed.

## 5. Post-La Pérouse Aggregate, 1793-1862

**Observer context**: Dozens of ship visits over nearly 70 years, compiled by Richards (2008). Key observers include: Captain Foster's crew (1822, secondhand report); Beechey's HMS Blossom (1825, Peard and Wolfe); Du Petit-Thouars (1838). National origins: British, French, American, Russian. Visit durations: hours to days. No observer had extended language access.

**Population**: Declining from La Pérouse's ~2,000 but still in the low thousands until the 1862 raids.

**What is described about political authority**:

HMS Blossom 1825 provides the strongest evidence. Peard and Wolfe conducted what amounts to a deliberate hypothesis test: (1) they observed individuals with distinctive adornment (feathered crowns, body paint); (2) they formed the hypothesis "It was first thought that these might be people having authority"; (3) they looked for behavioral correlates; (4) they found none: "we could discern no particular respect paid to them" (Richards, p. 52). During a violent confrontation, "Two apparent chiefs... attempted in conformity with our wish to keep back the multitude, but their authority was very limited" (pp. 50-51). Peard's conclusion: "nor, indeed, as I have said before, were there any who appeared to possess any superiority at least in the way of command over the rest" (p. 52).

Du Petit-Thouars (1838), a trained French naval officer accustomed to identifying hierarchy, spent hours with islanders and recorded detailed observations of monuments, trade, and the collective demand for wood (*miro*). Complete silence on authority figures. "All the natives repeated frequently and vigorously the word *miro*" (p. 71): horizontal resource negotiation, not chiefly mediation.

**European labels applied**: "Chief" and "chiefs" used by several visitors, always based on visual markers (tattooing, feathered ornaments, body paint, physical stature), never on observed command behavior.

**Gap between observation and label**: The Peard/Wolfe episode is the clearest case in the entire record: the label was applied, tested against behavior, and found to fail. This is not silence; it is a negative result from deliberate observation.

**What is NOT described**: No observer between 1793 and 1862 provides clear evidence of centralized political authority. Trade was consistently individual, never centrally organized. No intermediary or chiefly delegate mediated exchange across dozens of ship visits. No ceremonial deference. No territorial boundaries. No feasting. The "bipartite warfare" claim (Foster 1822) is a secondhand newspaper report with no corroboration from any other visitor.

## ========== [1862-1863 SLAVE RAIDS] ==========

Between December 1862 and mid-1863, Peruvian slavers removed 1,000-1,500 people from an island population of approximately 3,000. The raids specifically targeted visible leaders: "including chiefs and priests." Of approximately 1,000 taken to Peru, only 15 returned, carrying smallpox. The subsequent epidemic, combined with continuing warfare and Dutrou-Bornier's later removal of survivors to Tahiti, reduced the population from ~3,000 to 111 by 1877, a reduction of approximately 96%. The ariki, priests, and rongorongo experts were disproportionately killed or removed. Every ethnographic source from this point forward draws from a post-catastrophe population that had lost its primary knowledge holders.

## 6. Eyraud 1864

**Observer context**: Brother Eugène Eyraud, French lay missionary. First sustained European residence. Arrived January 1864, approximately one year after the slave raids. Stayed nine months. Limited language skills. No intermediary. Focused on conversion.

**Population**: ~1,200 (Eyraud's estimate); approximately 60% of pre-raid population.

**What is described about political authority**: "I don't even know the basis for such authority. It seems that authority depended only on the influence that some natives were able to exert over their neighbors and that the people gradually grew accustomed to such displays of superiority" (p. 14). Torometi, the "chief" who claimed Eyraud as personal property, was not a native of the island. Torometi ruled by coercion and theft, not institutional authority, and was collectively punished by community action (hut-burning) without appeal to any higher authority.

**European labels applied**: "Chief" (applied to Torometi, who exercised personal dominance).

**Gap between observation and label**: Eyraud applies "chief" to someone exercising brute personal coercion, not institutional or hereditary authority. He explicitly states he cannot characterize the basis of chiefly authority on the island.

**What is NOT described**: No organized political reception on arrival (sharp contrast with chiefly societies). No tribute or redistribution. No religious ceremonies observed ("Religion seems to occupy a very minor place in their lives"). No formal justice system. No differentiated wealth. No mention of ahu or moai as active religious sites.

**Key evidence for collective action**: Boat-building: "I had noted this custom of making everyone contribute to a project that was considered important. Nobody would think of not participating" (p. 27). Social obligation, not chiefly command.

## 7. Roussel 1869

**Observer context**: Father Hippolyte Roussel, Sacred Hearts missionary. Arrived 1866, stayed until 1873 (seven years). The longest-residing European observer of the post-raid period before Chilean annexation. Notes written 1869 (published posthumously 1926 in Annales). Learned the language; extended sustained contact with surviving population. Arrived from Mangareva, where the Sacred Hearts mission had established a rigid hierarchical order.

**Population**: ~650 by Roussel's census.

**What is described about political authority**:

The most detailed early post-raid political account, and the source of several chiefdom-narrative elements that appear here for the first time in the record:

- **Four social classes**: ariki (kings), ivi atua (priests), matatoa (warriors), kio (farmers/servants) (p. 41).
- **King list**: 22 generations from Hotu Matua to Gregorio (endnote 7).
- **"Absolute power"**: Kings "exercised absolute power" with first-fruits privileges and tapu on head and hands. BUT in the same account: authority had "passed completely into the hands of the matatoa" and was "absent or totally unrecognized" (p. 41).
- **Succession rule**: King abdicated when his son married.
- **First-fruits ceremony**: Roussel directly witnessed a procession bringing first yams to the child Gregorio, "the one and only true chief" and last surviving royal line member (pp. 39-40). [EYEWITNESS]
- **Tangata manu**: Brief description as mechanism for transferring chiefly authority to matatoa (p. 47).
- **Pre-raid religious collapse**: "The idea of a religious cult disappeared with the last priests and priestesses, who died a long time ago" (before the slave raids).

**European labels applied**: "King" (ariki), "absolute power," "classes." Roussel applies the most elaborated European political vocabulary of any source to date.

**Gap between observation and label**: Roussel simultaneously describes kings with "absolute power" AND a system where royal authority is "absent or totally unrecognized." Both cannot describe the same functioning system. The "absolute power" is reported tradition; the matatoa dominance and absent authority appear to describe what Roussel actually observed. The one ceremony he directly witnessed involved a child who was the last of his line in a society reduced to 650 people.

**What is NOT described**: No centralized redistribution (property is family-based with rahui fishing rights). No administrative system. No justice system run by kings. No reconciliation of the internal contradiction between "absolute power" and "absent" authority.

**First appearances in the record**: Four-class hierarchy, king list, "absolute power" claim, succession rule, named social classes. NONE of these elements appear in any contact-era source.

## 8. Loti 1872

**Observer context**: Pierre Loti (Julien Viaud), French naval officer and literary writer aboard the frigate *La Flore*. Brief visit (approximately 5 days). Literary observer, not ethnographer. Ten years after slave raids.

**Population**: ~300-400 (Loti's impression); concentrated entirely at Hanga Roa. Interior "basically deserted."

**What is described about political authority**: An "old chief" identified only by visual markers (tattoos, feather headdress), not by observed command behavior. The only individual exercising any actual authority is a European resident (the Dane). One woman encountered in entire interior crossing.

**European labels applied**: "Chief" (visual identification only).

**Gap between observation and label**: "Chief" applied to appearance, not function. No command, no deference, no institutional behavior observed.

**What is NOT described**: No command authority. No tribute, redistribution, or centralized provisioning. No territorial divisions. No warfare. No religious ceremonies or priests. No hierarchical dwelling differentiation. No sacred status for ahu/moai: islanders "start to dance on the funerary stones and on the fallen idols."

## 9. Pinart 1877

**Observer context**: Alphonse Pinart, French trained ethnographer. Six-day visit. Population at demographic nadir. 14 years after slave raids.

**Population**: 111 (26 women only).

**What is described about political authority**: Only colonial authority. "Queen" (Koreto) and "regent" were Dutrou-Bornier's widow and daughter: entirely colonial-era imposition. A "major domo" with baton maintained order in Koreto's courtyard. All governance structures were colonial artifacts.

**European labels applied**: "Queen" and "regent" (colonial impositions, not traditional).

**The most significant finding**: "The present inhabitants of the Easter Island, as we had seen, had retained no memory at all of those who had come before them" (pp. 122-123). Complete loss of traditional knowledge by 1877. Rongorongo tablets used as fishing line spools.

**What is NOT described**: No functioning traditional political authority. No social classes. No traditional chiefs. No memory of monument builders.

**Implication**: A trained ethnographer at the demographic nadir finds NO recoverable political tradition. Every subsequent ethnographic source (Geiseler, Thomson, Routledge, Métraux) claims to recover political traditions from a population that had partially rebounded from this point.

## 10. Geiseler 1883

**Observer context**: Captain Wilhelm Geiseler, German naval officer. SMS Hyäne. Four-day visit (September 19-23, 1882; published 1883). Alexander P. Salmon served as translator and cultural broker. Salmon was half-Tahitian, related to the Tahitian royal family, fluent in Rapanui, with strong opinions about Polynesian origins (he favored a Tuamotu origin).

**Population**: 150 (67 men, 39 women, 44 children; approximately 20 Tahitians among them). Partially recovered from nadir of 111.

**What is described about political authority**:

Direct observation: "A king does not exist, since the last one died in the Peruvian slavery" (p. 5). "Since that time every man is his own master, who recognizes no authority over himself and seeks his own justice" (pp. 22-23). "Old chief" title without substance: "These so-titled people appear to be nothing less than 'chiefs'; almost childish, they show for example nothing of the refined deportment of the old Samoan chiefs; they are not at all respected on account of their age" (pp. 22-23). Chiefs cannot punish theft (pp. 28-29). "Complete dissolution of all political community" (p. 23).

Reported tradition (through Salmon): Former regime described as "almost despotic" with kings and chiefs as "advisors" (p. 42). Succession to "the brother" (p. 42). "Every family father has the right to declare war."

**European labels applied**: "King" (for the now-dead institution); "despotic" (for the former regime); "chiefs" (for individuals without actual authority).

**Gap between observation and label**: The observed reality is total political collapse. The "despotic" former regime is Salmon's reported tradition, not observation. The comparison to Samoan chiefs actively undermines the label: Rapa Nui "chiefs" are nothing like actual Polynesian chiefs.

**What is NOT described**: No tribute, redistribution, or taxation. No special ceremonies for succession. No tangata manu as political succession mechanism. No functioning priesthood. Rongorongo knowledge lost: "no one could demonstrate reading it."

**Succession rule**: Passes to "the brother," contradicting both Thomson (primogeniture) and Roussel (abdication when son marries).

**The Salmon filter**: ALL ethnographic content mediated through Salmon. This is the first of two accounts (Geiseler and Thomson) that share the same intermediary. Where these accounts converge, the convergence may reflect Salmon's consistent framing rather than independent traditions.

## 11. Thomson 1891

**Observer context**: Paymaster William J. Thomson, U.S. Navy. USS Mohican. Twelve-day visit (December 1886; published 1891). Alexander P. Salmon again served as translator and cultural broker: the same intermediary as Geiseler four years earlier. Tradition-collection confined to evenings, some with alcohol provided to elderly informant Ure Vaeiko.

**Population**: 155 (68 men, 43 women, 44 children).

**What is described about political authority**:

Reported tradition (through Salmon): "The ancient government of Easter Island was an arbitrary monarchy. The supreme authority was vested in a king and was hereditary in his family. The person of the king was held sacred" (p. 472). King list of 57 names from Hotu-Matua to Maurata (pp. 533-534). The king "reigned over the entire island" and could remove chiefs.

Immediate qualifications: "It does not appear that any great homage was paid the king, and no tax was exacted of the people" (p. 473). "There was no confederation, each clan being independent of all the rest, except as the powerful are naturally dominant over the weak" (p. 472). "The people avenged their own injuries" (p. 473).

Direct observation: "Since that time there has been no recognized authority among the natives; every man is his own master" (p. 473). Ure Vaeiko could NOT read rongorongo tablets despite claiming ability (direct observation confirming knowledge loss).

Tangata manu: "No especial authority was vested in him" (pp. 482-483).

**European labels applied**: "Arbitrary monarchy" (the most consequential European political category applied to Rapa Nui in any source). "King." "Sacred."

**Gap between observation and label**: Thomson describes a "monarchy" without taxation, without homage, without confederation, where clans are independent and people avenge their own injuries. This is a monarchy in name only. Thomson himself acknowledges the paradox: "In view of the fact that the islanders all acknowledged the authority of one king, their wars were surprisingly numerous, barbarous, and unrelenting" (p. 476).

**What is NOT described**: No functioning authority (direct observation). No redistribution (explicitly: "no tax was exacted"). No effective control over clans. No observed ceremonies of authority.

**King list**: 57 names, irreconcilable with Roussel's 22 generations: only approximately 12 names overlap, few in same order. The discrepancy between lists recorded 17 years apart from the same small post-catastrophe population demonstrates that the king lists cannot sustain the inferential weight chiefdom models place on them.

**Succession rule**: Eldest son nominated (primogeniture) (p. 527). Contradicts Geiseler's "brother" (from the same Salmon four years earlier) and Roussel's abdication-at-marriage.

**The Salmon filter**: Same intermediary as Geiseler. The convergence on a "despotic"/"arbitrary" former regime AND the divergence on succession rules (brother vs. eldest son) both implicate Salmon. The narrative framework is consistent (hierarchical monarchy); the institutional details shift.

## 12. Routledge 1919

**Observer context**: Katherine Routledge, British researcher. The Mana Expedition. Seventeen months on the island (March 1914 to August 1915). The first extended scientific residence. Learned Rapanui directly. Worked with multiple named informants from multiple clans (Miru, Tupahotu, Marama, Ureohei). Primary informant: Juan Tepano. The first account independent of Alexander P. Salmon.

**Population**: ~250.

**What is described about political authority**:

"With the exception of the Miru, of which more will be said, there were no chiefs nor any form of government; any man who was expert in war became a leader" (p. 224). Only the Miru clan (one of ten) had a chief (ariki-mau). That chief was explicitly NOT a political leader: "Ngaara held official position for the whole island, but he was neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest; he can best be described as the custodian of certain customs and traditions" (p. 242).

Functions of the ariki-mau: supernatural food-increase magic (especially chickens), oversight of rongorongo knowledge (annual assembly at Anakena), rain prayers (delegated to ariki-paka), house inauguration ceremonies, receiving the annual pua offering (visitors "retired backwards," p. 243). All ceremonial, ritualistic, or supernatural, not political.

Ariki-mau lacked coercive power: could not compel his own servant to return a stolen tablet (p. 249). Captured by the Ngaure clan and held captive with his son and grandson for five years at Akahanga (p. 246).

Tangata manu: rotating competitive institution. The Ao (ascendant group) rotated among clans. Food gifts were "the sole practical advantage of victory; those who did not contribute were apt to have their houses burnt" (p. 264). Winner spent five months in strict taboo, then "returned to ordinary life" after one year. The ariki-mau "never appeared at Orongo" (p. 260): the two institutions were separate.

Marriage: "No group restrictions on marriage, which took place indiscriminately between members of the same or of different clans" (p. 221). Inconsistent with a ranked society maintaining hierarchy through marriage rules.

**European labels applied**: "Chief" and "headman" (applied to ariki-mau only, and immediately qualified with the denial of political power).

**Gap between observation and label**: Routledge applies the label but immediately empties it of political content. The ariki-mau had supernatural prestige, not political authority.

**What is NOT described**: No routine tribute, redistribution, or taxation. No king list provided (Routledge collected lists but found fundamental confusion and did not consider them reliable enough to reproduce). No kio (servant/slave) class. No priests (p. 239). No formal worship or sacrifice (p. 236). No succession rules elaborated beyond "resign in favour of his son" when "old and feeble" (p. 241).

**Independence from Salmon**: Routledge explicitly questioned Thomson's data by noting Salmon "failed to understand" surviving customs (p. 209). The first researcher independent of Salmon found a markedly less hierarchical political picture than either Geiseler or Thomson.

## 13. Métraux 1937, 1940, 1957

**Observer context**: Alfred Métraux, Swiss-Argentine ethnographer. Trained Polynesianist. Franco-Belgian expedition (1934). Several months on the island. Worked with Juan Tepano (shared with Routledge) and Victoria Rapahango (independent). Published three works: "The Kings of Easter Island" (JPS, 1937), *Ethnology of Easter Island* (Bishop Museum, 1940), *Easter Island: A Stone-Age Civilization of the Pacific* (1957 popular synthesis). Also had independent access to Roussel's published 1926 Annales text, which Routledge did not.

**Population**: ~450-500.

**What is described about political authority**:

1937 (JPS article): "Nothing is known about the activities and duties of the king to suggest any great political power. He was not a ruler in the common sense of the word" (p. 58). Dismisses Roussel's "absolute power" claim as "an entirely gratuitous assumption" (p. 58). Endorses Routledge's characterization. "His political power was little" (p. 61). Describes more chiefly prerogatives than Routledge (first-fruits, harvest tapus, tūra/haka-pāpa servants, tuna distribution to tangata-honui, tattooing inspection, twin-naming, fertility chant) while reaching the same conclusion about no political authority. Food flows through the ariki-mau to tangata-honui (elite old men), not to the general population: elite-to-elite circulation, not redistribution.

1940 (comprehensive monograph): "The ariki-mau was respected because of his mana, his influence over nature, and the magic connected with his person, but he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (p. 136). King list comparison (Table 2, pp. 88-93): four independently recorded lists (Roussel 23, Jaussen 32, Thomson 57, Métraux 30) share only 7 names in common, with shared names predominantly tribal/geographic eponyms (Marama, Raa, Hotu-iti, Tuu), not historical individuals. Roussel's four-class hierarchy "only superficially exact" (p. 135). Matatoa were "not only the leaders in war but were also the actual rulers of the tribes" (pp. 138-139). Kio were war captives, not a hereditary class. Clan territories diffuse: some clans had no distinct territory. Marriage restrictions minimal. "The true significance of stone statues is not known. There is no direct evidence that they were ever worshipped" (p. 306). "The downfall of the religion took place long before the arrival of the missionaries" (Roussel, quoted p. 309).

1957 (popular synthesis): "The political authority of the ariki-mau is not clear. On this point we are reduced to conjecture" (p. 93). Matatoa compared to South American caudillo: "Like the South American caudillo, he was both a civil and military leader" (p. 96). "The political position of the matato'a on Easter Island was much stronger than that of the warriors in the rest of Polynesia, where they were subordinate to the ariki" (p. 97). Kainga named as island-wide warrior leader (p. 97). Statue production as competitive feast sponsorship: "A half-serious, half-humorous rivalry turned the undertaking into an immense pleasure party" (pp. 164-165). Feast system (koro, hareauti) organized by lineage heads "anxious to consolidate or enhance their prestige" with lateral gift circulation (pp. 171-175). King's authority temporary: "after which the old king sank into oblivion" (p. 93).

**European labels applied**: "King" (retained by convention but immediately emptied of political content in every occurrence). Métraux explicitly labels the institution as religious/ceremonial, not political.

**Gap between observation and label**: Métraux describes MORE prerogatives than any previous source yet reaches the MOST explicitly skeptical conclusion. The gap between the accumulating descriptions of chiefly functions and the consistent denial of political authority demonstrates that the argument against the chiefdom model is not based on absence of evidence but on the character of the evidence: supernatural/ceremonial functions do not constitute political authority.

**What is NOT described**: No governance, administration, labor mobilization, or judicial authority attributed to the ariki-mau in any of the three publications. No centralized redistribution to the general population. No stable dynastic succession (four irreconcilable king lists).

# Part II: Seven Analytical Threads

## Thread 1: The Paradox of Increasing Elaboration

The sources closest to functioning pre-contact society describe the LEAST political structure. The sources farthest from it describe the MOST.

| Source | Year | Years post-raid | Pop. | Political structure described |
|--------|------|-----------------|------|------------------------------|
| Roggeveen | 1722 | pre-raid | ~3,000 | One individual directs food provisioning during crisis |
| González | 1770 | pre-raid | ~3,000 | Age-graded differentiation; no political authority observed |
| Cook | 1774 | pre-raid | ~3,000 | One chief; "no humiliating homage"; Cook "quite ignorant" |
| La Pérouse | 1786 | pre-raid | ~2,000 | "Chiefs in each district" (custodial); consensus for sales |
| Post-LP | 1793-1862 | pre-raid | ~3,000 | No centralized authority across dozens of visits |
| Eyraud | 1864 | 1 year | ~1,200 | Authority based only on "influence"; no institutional basis |
| Roussel | 1869 | 6 years | ~650 | First: class system, king list, "absolute power," succession rule |
| Loti | 1872 | 9 years | ~300 | Informal "old chief" only |
| Pinart | 1877 | 14 years | 111 | "No memory at all" of predecessors |
| Geiseler | 1883 | 20 years | 150 | "No king"; former regime "almost despotic" (via Salmon) |
| Thomson | 1891 | 24 years | 155 | "Arbitrary monarchy" with 57-king list (via same Salmon) |
| Routledge | 1919 | 52 years | ~250 | "No chiefs nor any form of government" (except Miru ariki-mau) |
| Métraux | 1937-57 | 72+ years | ~500 | Most prerogatives described; "certainly not a ruler" |

The pattern is striking. 140 years of contact-era observation (1722-1862) by dozens of visitors from multiple nations produce no evidence for hereditary political hierarchy, no king lists, no class systems, no succession rules, no descriptions of monarchical authority. Then, between 1869 and 1891, from a population that had collapsed from approximately 3,000 to approximately 150, the full apparatus of monarchical government enters the record: classes, king lists, "absolute power," "arbitrary monarchy," hereditary succession.

The paradox cannot be explained by access. Roussel had better access than most contact-era observers, but the contact-era observers cumulatively represent 140 years of encounters, some by trained scientists and experienced Polynesian observers (Cook, Forsters, La Pérouse). If a functioning chiefdom existed, the cumulative record should contain more evidence of it, not less.

Possible explanations for the paradox:

1. **Elaboration in the telling**: Survivors reconstructed a more elaborate political past than had actually existed, perhaps under the influence of their interlocutors (missionaries, Salmon) or their own needs for cultural coherence after catastrophe.

2. **Salmon's interpretive framework**: The most elaborate accounts (Geiseler, Thomson) both filter through Salmon, whose Tahitian background and royal connections may have predisposed him to interpret Rapanui traditions through a hierarchical lens.

3. **Observer sophistication**: Later, better-trained observers (Routledge, Métraux) describe more prerogatives but interpret them differently, recognizing the distinction between supernatural prestige and political power that earlier, briefer observers (Geiseler, Thomson) did not make.

4. **Selective survival**: The specific claims about monarchical authority may preserve real memory fragments, but their elaboration into a coherent political system (class hierarchy, succession rules, king lists) reflects post-hoc reconstruction rather than institutional memory.

## Thread 2: When Chiefdom-Narrative Elements First Appear

| Element | First appearance | Source | Contact-era precedent? |
|---------|-----------------|--------|----------------------|
| "King" language | 1722 | Roggeveen ("King or Head Chief") | Yes, but used once, with hesitation |
| "Chiefs" (plural) | 1722 | Bouman | Yes, but visual identification only |
| "CHIEFS" (editorial) | 1770/1903 | Corney/Hakluyt plate caption | NO: editorial imposition on the González record |
| Hereditary succession | 1869 | Roussel | NO |
| Class hierarchy | 1869 | Roussel (four classes) | NO |
| "Absolute power" | 1869 | Roussel | NO |
| King lists | 1869 | Roussel (22 generations) | NO |
| Succession rules | 1869 | Roussel (abdication at son's marriage) | NO |
| "Despotic" regime | 1883 | Geiseler (via Salmon) | NO |
| "Arbitrary monarchy" | 1891 | Thomson (via Salmon) | NO |
| Extended king list (57) | 1891 | Thomson (via Salmon) | NO |
| Territorial districts | 1891 | Thomson (six districts for six sons) | NO |
| Redistributive claims | Never | --- | NO (Métraux 1937 documents elite-to-elite circulation, not redistribution) |

The pattern is unambiguous. Contact-era sources use "king" and "chief" as European labels imposed on observed individuals, always with uncertainty and always based on visual markers rather than observed authority. The structural elements of the chiefdom narrative (hereditary succession, class hierarchy, absolute power, king lists, territorial districts, "arbitrary monarchy") all enter the record between 1869 and 1891, from a population that had collapsed by 96%. No contact-era source describes any of these elements.

Redistributive claims, the economic mechanism chiefdom models require, never appear in any source. The closest approach is Métraux's documentation of food flowing through the ariki-mau to elite old men (tangata-honui), which is elite-to-elite circulation, not the downward redistribution from paramount to commoners that chiefdom models posit.

## Thread 3: The Salmon Effect

Alexander P. Salmon served as translator and cultural broker for both Geiseler (1882) and Thomson (1886). Salmon was half-Tahitian, related to the Tahitian royal family through his mother, and spoke Rapanui fluently. He had been living on the island since the early 1870s, having arrived with his father (who worked for Dutrou-Bornier). He favored a Tuamotu origin for the Rapanui (Geiseler, pp. 43-44) and had strong opinions about Polynesian cultural connections.

**Convergences (consistent narrative through Salmon)**:

Both Geiseler and Thomson describe a former political system that was:
- Hierarchical, with kings and subordinate chiefs
- "Despotic" (Geiseler) or "arbitrary" (Thomson)
- Sacred (the king's person was held sacred)
- Hereditary within one royal line
- Now completely destroyed by the slave raids

This convergence has been treated as mutual confirmation. But both accounts were filtered through the same individual. The convergence may reflect Salmon's consistent presentation of Rapanui political traditions through the lens of the Tahitian hierarchical model he knew from his own background. A Tahitian-connected resident related to a royal family had obvious reasons to interpret (or present) Rapanui traditions in terms of legitimate monarchy.

**Divergences (inconsistency between Salmon's two accounts)**:

Despite the consistent narrative framework, specific institutional details shift between the two accounts:
- Succession: Geiseler records "to the brother" (1882); Thomson records eldest son/primogeniture (1886). The same intermediary provided incompatible succession rules four years apart.
- Tangata manu: Geiseler describes it primarily as food-procurement with associated combat; Thomson describes it as a ritual with spiritual approval but "no especial authority."
- King list: Geiseler does not provide one; Thomson provides 57 names.

The succession rule divergence is particularly damaging. This is the most fundamental institutional procedure of a hereditary polity, the mechanism by which authority transfers between generations. Salmon described it differently to two interviewers four years apart. Either Salmon's understanding of the tradition changed, or the tradition itself was unstable, or Salmon was tailoring his account to his interlocutors. In any case, the instability of this foundational claim from the same source undermines confidence in the reported political system.

**Routledge's independence**: Routledge (1914-1915) explicitly questioned Thomson's data and noted that Salmon "failed to understand" surviving customs (p. 209). Working without Salmon, with multiple informants in their own language over 17 months, she found "no chiefs nor any form of government" outside the Miru. The first researcher independent of Salmon found a markedly less hierarchical picture.

## Thread 4: The Succession Rule Test

If a hereditary polity existed on Rapa Nui, its succession rule, the mechanism by which authority transferred between generations, should be consistently described by sources drawing from the same post-catastrophe population within a roughly 60-year window.

| Source | Date | Succession rule | Intermediary |
|--------|------|----------------|--------------|
| Roussel | 1869 | King abdicates when son marries | Direct contact |
| Geiseler | 1883 | Passes to the brother | Salmon |
| Thomson | 1891 | Eldest son nominated (primogeniture) | Salmon |
| Routledge | 1919 | "Resign in favour of his son" when old and feeble | Tepano |
| Métraux | 1937 | "Resigned his power in favour of his first-born son when he married" | Tepano + Roussel text |

Five sources, three distinct rules:
1. **Abdication at son's marriage** (Roussel, partially Métraux)
2. **Fraternal succession** (Geiseler)
3. **Primogeniture** (Thomson)
4. **Retirement when aged** (Routledge)

Métraux synthesizes Roussel and Routledge but calls Geiseler "certainly mistaken" about brother succession (1937, p. 52). The Routledge and Thomson accounts partially converge on "son" as heir but differ on the trigger (old age vs. nomination). Roussel and Métraux converge on marriage as the trigger but this alignment reflects Métraux's direct use of Roussel's published text.

The fundamental problem: Geiseler and Thomson used the same intermediary (Salmon) and got different rules. If Salmon could not provide a consistent succession rule, the tradition was either unknown to him, unknown to his informants, or did not exist in stable institutional form. This single test, the ability to consistently describe the most basic political procedure, fails across all sources. A functioning hereditary polity produces a known succession rule. Rapa Nui's record produces at least three incompatible ones.

## Thread 5: The Routledge/Métraux Paradox

The two most rigorous researchers in the sequence, Routledge (17-month residence, multiple informants, direct language access, independent of Salmon) and Métraux (trained Polynesianist, multiple informants, access to all prior published sources), describe the most chiefly prerogatives in the record while most firmly denying that these prerogatives constitute political authority.

Routledge provides: annual pua offering with visitors retiring backwards, rongorongo assembly oversight, rain prayers, house inaugurations, food-increase magic, chicken distributions, strict tapu on the ariki-mau's person. She concludes: "he was neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest; he can best be described as the custodian of certain customs and traditions" (p. 242).

Métraux adds: first-fruits ceremony (via Roussel), harvest tapus, tūra/haka-pāpa servant classes, tuna distribution to tangata-honui, tattooing inspection, twin-naming, fertility chant, mana legends. He concludes: "he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (1940, p. 136) and "His political power was little" (1937, p. 61).

The chiefdom model conflates supernatural/ceremonial functions with political/administrative power. Routledge and Métraux do not. Both are explicit that the ariki-mau's functions were supernatural (food increase, mana transmission, tapu enforcement through spiritual sanction) and ceremonial (rongorongo oversight, house inauguration, pua offering), not political (governance, justice, labor mobilization, tribute collection, military command). Their distinction matters because the chiefdom concept as formalized by Sahlins and applied by Kirch treats the conflation of sacred and political authority as a defining feature of Polynesian chiefdoms. On Rapa Nui, the two ethnographers with the best access both insist the sacred authority existed without the political authority. This is not the chiefdom model with some features attenuated; it is a different kind of institution.

The paradox deepens with Métraux's trajectory across three publications. Between 1937 and 1957, his assessment moved toward greater skepticism, even as his accumulated evidence grew. The 1957 formulation ("On this point we are reduced to conjecture") is more uncertain than the 1940 formulation ("certainly not a ruler or civilian chief"), which itself is more emphatic than what any previous source provides. More evidence, more carefully analyzed, produced more doubt, not more certainty.

---

## Thread 6: Property Without Redistribution

A consistent thread runs through all sources from Cook (1774) to Métraux (1940) describing individual and familial property without centralized redistribution.

**Contact era**:
- Cook (1774): Plantation owner demands return of stolen property; "As everything must be raised by dint of labour, it cannot be supposed the inhabitants plant much more than is sufficient for themselves."
- La Pérouse (1786): Selling produce requires "almost a general consent"; household-level food preparation.
- Post-La Pérouse (1793-1862): All trade conducted by individual swimmers or canoe owners carrying personal produce. No intermediary or chiefly mediation across dozens of ship visits.

**Post-raid**:
- Roussel (1869): Land ownership in family-based plantations. Rahui stones mark exclusive fishing rights. Property disputes arise from displacement, not chiefly allocation.
- Thomson (1891): "No tax was exacted of the people" (p. 473). Community enforcement of property rights through collective action ("the entire community" aiding the weak against the strong, p. 465).
- Routledge (1919): No routine tribute, redistribution, or taxation described.
- Métraux (1940): Lineage land (henua poreko ranga). Sea rights through rahui attached to estates. "Formerly, every family owned the land that either hereditary, compulsory, or voluntary occupation put into its hands" (Roussel, quoted pp. 142-143).

**The food-flow complication**: Métraux (1937) documents food flowing through the ariki-mau: vaka-vaero tuna to king to tangata-honui (p. 50), first tuna after tapu-lifting to king to tangata-honui (p. 50), first-fruits with harvest tapu (pp. 54-55). This is genuine redistribution in the narrow sense. But the flow is: producers → king → tangata-honui (elite old men), not king → general population. Elite-to-elite circulation through the sacred office is different in kind from the broad downward redistribution (paramount collects surplus, reallocates to commoners) that chiefdom models posit as the economic foundation of political authority.

**The feast complication**: Métraux (1957, pp. 164-165, 171-175) describes koro and hareauti feasts organized by lineage heads seeking prestige through generosity, with lateral obligatory gift circulation. This feast system provides the economic mechanism for monument production: "The heads of lineages who wished to raise a new bust on their ahu undoubtedly organized feasts... The guests harnessed themselves to the statue and strained their muscles... A half-serious, half-humorous rivalry turned the undertaking into an immense pleasure party." This is competitive prestige exchange among autonomous lineage heads, not top-down redistribution from a paramount.

No source in 235 years describes the centralized redistributive economy that chiefdom models require as the economic foundation of political authority.

## Thread 7: The Religious Vacuum

The pre-raid collapse of organized religion, documented by the two observers with the earliest sustained post-raid access, has implications for the chiefdom model that have not been fully explored.

**Eyraud (1864)**: "Religion seems to occupy a very minor place in their lives... I never witnessed any act that was really indicative of a religious cult" (p. 20). Statuettes in homes treated with indifference.

**Roussel (1869)**: "The idea of a religious cult disappeared with the last priests and priestesses, who died a long time ago." This describes a pre-raid condition: religion had already collapsed before the slave raids took the remaining ariki and priests.

**Métraux (1940)**: "Easter Island religion was more deeply affected by the several catastrophes which befell the people than any other phase of their culture. Roussel says that the downfall of the religion took place long before the arrival of the missionaries" (p. 309). Whether priests even existed as a functioning class is disputed among sources: Geiseler and Routledge deny it; Thomson reduces them to "wizards"; only Roussel affirms their status.

**Implication for the chiefdom model**: If the ariki-mau's authority was supernatural/ceremonial (as Routledge and Métraux conclude), then that authority depended on a functioning religious system. If organized religion had already collapsed before the slave raids (as both Eyraud and Roussel independently indicate), then the ariki institution may have been functionally empty before the catastrophe. The supernatural authority that Routledge and Métraux describe as the substance of the ariki-mau's role may not have been operative by the time Roussel describes it. What Roussel calls "former" authority may have been former in a deeper sense than he recognized: not just transferred to the matatoa, but hollowed out by the prior collapse of the religious system that sustained it.

This creates a temporal problem for the chiefdom model. If we accept Métraux's characterization of the ariki-mau as a sacred figure whose authority derived from supernatural functions, and if we accept the Eyraud/Roussel evidence that those supernatural functions had ceased before the slave raids, then the "chiefdom" whose collapse the slave raids are said to have caused may have already been non-functional. The "absolute power" Roussel attributes to former kings would then refer to a period before the religious collapse, a period for which no source provides direct evidence and for which oral traditions were being transmitted through the very population that had experienced both the religious collapse and the demographic catastrophe.

# Part III: Connection to the Five-Step Circularity

The narrative construction analysis maps onto the paper's central argument as follows:

## Step 1: European accounts generate the political vocabulary

The vocabulary precedes the evidence. Roggeveen uses "King or Head Chief" (1722) based on one crisis-response episode. Corney's Hakluyt Society edition labels González's treaty signatories as "CHIEFS" (1770/1903), a designation that appears nowhere in the original Spanish text. The Forsters create an elaborate political portrait that Cook, their own captain, contradicts with "I confess myself quite ignorant." Thomson applies "arbitrary monarchy" (1891) to a reported tradition that his own account immediately undermines with "no tax," "no homage," and independent clans.

The labels accumulate: "King," "Chiefs," "monarch," "arbitrary monarchy," "despotic." Each label carries European assumptions about institutionalized hierarchy. These labels were not forced on resistant evidence; they were the default categories European observers used to describe any human society, and subsequent researchers adopted them as factual descriptions rather than recognizing them as interpretive impositions.

## Step 2: Polynesian analogies fill gaps no source describes

No source in 235 years describes: centralized redistribution to the general population, stable hereditary succession (the most fundamental institutional procedure cannot be consistently specified), labor mobilization through chiefly command, administrative districts organized under a paramount, or judicial authority exercised by the ariki-mau. These are the structural features that distinguish a chiefdom from other forms of social organization.

The Polynesian analogy, drawn from Hawai'i, Tonga, and Samoa via Sahlins and Kirch, fills every one of these gaps. Where no source describes redistribution, the analogy supplies it. Where no source describes stable succession, the analogy assumes it. Where no source describes centralized labor command, the analogy provides the mechanism for monument production. The analogy is doing the work that evidence should do, but the evidence does not exist.

## Step 3: Post-raid sources provide institutional detail from a shattered population

Between 1869 and 1891, from a population that had collapsed from ~3,000 to ~150, the full apparatus of monarchical government enters the record for the first time. The paradox of increasing elaboration (Thread 1) and the timeline of first appearances (Thread 2) demonstrate that the chiefdom narrative's structural elements (class hierarchy, king lists, "absolute power," succession rules, territorial districts) were all recorded from a post-catastrophe population, not from direct observation of a functioning society. The institutional details are internally contradictory (Thread 4: succession rules), partially attributable to a single intermediary (Thread 3: Salmon), and rejected as descriptions of political authority by the two most rigorous researchers who examined them (Thread 5: Routledge/Métraux paradox).

## Step 4: Archaeological evidence is read through this framework

This step falls outside the chronological scope of this document (it requires analysis of the archaeological literature from the 1960s onward), but the ethnographic foundation for it is clear. When Kirch, McCoy, and others interpret monumental architecture (ahu, moai) as evidence for chiefly command, they are reading the archaeological record through a framework constructed from:
- European labels imposed on contact-era observations that did not support them
- Polynesian analogies that filled gaps the evidence left open
- Post-raid traditions from a shattered population that contradict each other on fundamental points

The archaeological evidence (monument scale, quarry production patterns, territorial distribution of ahu) is real. The question is whether it demonstrates chiefly command or is equally consistent with collective action, competitive signaling among autonomous lineages, and the prestige feast system that Métraux (1957) describes as the mechanism for monument production.

## Step 5: The framework prevents alternatives from being considered

The 140 years of contact-era accounts describing decentralized exchange, consensus-based decision-making, household-level resource control, and the absence of chiefly command were available to every researcher who applied the chiefdom model to Rapa Nui. The Peard/Wolfe hypothesis test (1825), Cook's explicit comparison to other Polynesian societies (1774), and La Pérouse's "general consent" observation (1786) were not hidden or inaccessible. They were simply not incorporated because the chiefdom framework had already been established from the post-raid sources and Polynesian analogy. The framework directed attention to evidence that confirmed it (Thomson's "arbitrary monarchy," Roussel's "absolute power") and away from evidence that contradicted it (Thomson's "no tax," Roussel's "authority absent," Cook's "no humiliating homage," Peard's "no superiority in the way of command").

Métraux's feast-sponsorship model of monument production (1957, pp. 164-165) provides an explicit alternative mechanism: competitive lineage heads organized festive labor mobilization, not a paramount chief commanding corvée. This model, from the most comprehensive ethnographer of Rapa Nui, has been largely overlooked by the chiefdom literature because it does not fit the framework.

# Part IV: Honest Complications

The following passages genuinely complicate the argument and must be addressed rather than minimized.

## 1. Roussel's Eyewitness First-Fruits Ceremony

Roussel directly witnessed a procession bringing first yams to the child Gregorio, "the one and only true chief" (pp. 39-40). This is the single strongest piece of direct evidence for chiefly prerogatives in the entire post-raid record, because it is observation, not reported tradition.

**Honest assessment**: This ceremony occurred in 1866-1869, 3-6 years after the slave raids, in a population of ~650. Gregorio was a child and the last surviving member of the royal line. The ceremony may represent genuine institutional continuity, residual performance of a remembered practice, or revival by missionaries who found the institution useful for social control (Roussel was a missionary who stood to benefit from recognized hierarchy). We cannot distinguish among these possibilities. What we can say is that the ceremony demonstrates that the first-fruits concept was known and performable, not that it was a functioning institution of a political system. A ceremony performed for a child who is the last of his line in a shattered population is qualitatively different from a ceremony performed for a functioning paramount in a stable society.

## 2. The Annual Pua Offering

Routledge describes the annual pua offering to the ariki-mau, with visitors "retired backwards" (p. 243). This demonstrates genuine ritual deference to the office.

**Honest assessment**: Ritual deference to a sacred figure is consistent with the Routledge/Métraux characterization of the ariki-mau as a supernaturally powerful but politically impotent office. The question is not whether the ariki-mau received deference but whether that deference constituted political authority. Routledge herself, who recorded this detail, concluded that it did not.

## 3. Food Flows Through the Ariki-Mau

Métraux (1937) documents three instances of food flowing through the ariki-mau: vaka-vaero tuna distribution to tangata-honui (p. 50), first tuna after tapu-lifting (p. 50), first-fruits ceremony with harvest tapu (pp. 54-55).

**Honest assessment**: These are genuine redistributive behaviors and the strongest evidence for an economic dimension to the ariki-mau's role. However, the flow pattern is: producers → king → tangata-honui (elite old men), not king → general population. This is elite-to-elite circulation through the sacred office, not the broad downward redistribution that chiefdom models require. The harvest tapu operates through supernatural sanction, not political taxation. The claim against the chiefdom model is not that no food flowed through the ariki-mau, but that the pattern of flow does not match what chiefdom models predict.

## 4. Métraux's "Vast Resources" Passage

Métraux (1957, pp. 159-160): "The chiefs, who had vast resources at their disposal, employed them in maintaining and paying groups of craftsmen who carved statues to ornament the mausoleums belonging to the various lineages."

**Honest assessment**: This passage, taken in isolation, sounds like chiefly command over resources and craft production. But the "chiefs" are plural lineage heads, not a paramount, and the monuments belong to "the various lineages," not to the paramount. In context, Métraux places this within his feast-sponsorship model where lineage heads organized competitive feasts to mobilize labor. "Vast resources" may describe accumulated feast contributions, not centralized surplus extraction. The passage describes multiple autonomous leaders competing, not a centralized chief commanding. But the language "maintaining and paying groups of craftsmen" does imply sustained employment relationships that go beyond a single feast event, and this deserves careful treatment.

## 5. Eyraud's Observation of Directed Boat-Building

Eyraud describes boat-building as collective action with someone directing the work: the passage that also says "nobody would think of not participating" (p. 27).

**Honest assessment**: Someone directing cooperative labor is consistent with both chiefly command and collective action with task leadership. The distinction is whether the director held permanent institutionalized authority or temporary task-specific leadership. Eyraud's account provides no evidence for the former. The passage is actually better evidence for collective action than for chiefly command: the social obligation to participate ("nobody would think of not participating") operates through community norms, not through a chief's authority to compel.

## 6. The Ngaure Capture Problem (Inverted)

The Ngaure capture of the ariki-mau and his family is used throughout this analysis as evidence against chiefly political power (a real paramount cannot be enslaved by a subordinate group). But it also demonstrates that the ariki-mau was a recognized office important enough to capture, and that the Miru subsequently organized a military alliance to rescue him. If the office were truly powerless, why bother to capture or rescue its holder?

**Honest assessment**: The capture and rescue demonstrate that the ariki-mau had VALUE, specifically supernatural value (mana). The capture makes sense if the Ngaure wanted to control the sacred figure's mana for their own benefit, and the rescue makes sense if the Miru needed to recover their supernatural asset. This is consistent with Routledge and Métraux's characterization: supernaturally powerful, politically impotent. A king with armies cannot be captured; a sacred figure whose mana is valued but whose political authority does not extend beyond his own clan can be. The capture incident is evidence that the ariki-mau's value was real but was supernatural, not political.
