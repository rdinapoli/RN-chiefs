# The Diffusion of the Chiefdom Label into Rapa Nui Archaeology

## Status: HYPOTHESIS — to be tested during Era 5 processing

## Core Claim

The chiefdom label entered Rapa Nui archaeology through citation diffusion from Kirch 1984, not through independent archaeological or ethnohistoric evidence from Rapa Nui. The archaeology did not change between the early 1980s and the mid-1990s; the same ahu, settlement patterns, and moai were being described before and after 1984. What changed was the interpretive vocabulary, imported from Kirch's comparative Polynesian framework.

## The Chronological Sequence

### Before Kirch 1984: No chiefdom language

- Sahlins 1958: classified Rapa Nui as relatively open, low-hierarchy (Group IIa) within his Polynesian stratification ranking. Goldman 1970: classified as "Open" society, never evolved central authority.
- Mulloy school (Mulloy, Ayres, McCoy, Stevenson): worked within kinship and corporate descent-group frameworks. No chiefdom terminology; Service 1962 not cited.
- Stevenson 1984 dissertation: segmentary political groups, lineage territories, inter-kin competition, drawing on Hayden and Cannon 1982. Service's chiefdom typology absent from bibliography. Four autonomous groups, no rank differences, system "reverts to non-ranked structure" post-1680. Goldman's "Open" classification engaged. 11+ explicit hedging statements.
- Ayres 1973: ahu as kin-group structures; Goldman's "Open" framework adopted; no redistribution discussion.
- McCoy 1976: chiefdom "retarded" by warrior class; not confirmed.

**Already documented in pipeline**: Stevenson 1984 (95 claims), Ayres 1973 (65 claims), McCoy 1976 (claims), Mulloy & Figueroa 1966 (37 claims), Mulloy 1979 (24 claims).

### The Pivot: Kirch 1984

Kirch reframed all of Polynesia within an evolutionary chiefdom framework derived primarily from Hawaiian, Tongan, and Samoan cases. Rapa Nui incorporated by analogy, not on the basis of Rapa Nui-specific evidence. Kirch never conducted archaeology on Rapa Nui. Key: Kirch himself wrote that ahu "do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority" (p. 270) and quarry work was by "independently working crews" (pp. 270-271), yet maintained the chiefdom label via APS inheritance.

**Already documented**: Kirch 1984 (121 claims).

### 1986: First adoption

Stevenson 1986 publishes in Kirch-edited *Island Societies* volume using chiefdom-adjacent language (ariki mau as paramount chief, tribute). The text does NOT use "chiefdom"; Service and Kirch not cited. But theoretical infrastructure stripped, anti-evolutionary commitments dropped, "reverted to non-ranked" reframed as "weakened." Editorial context does the reframing.

**Already documented**: Stevenson 1986 (90 claims), circularity_demonstration.md Step 3.

### Late 1980s-early 1990s: Spread

- Van Tilburg 1986 PhD: earliest work to explicitly frame moai as chiefdom evidence. Takes Kirch 1984 as scaffolding; Polynesian cultural matrix "assumed" (p. 299).
- Van Tilburg & Lee 1987: brings "chiefly mana" framing into journal literature.
- Ramirez Aliaga 1989: explicitly treats chiefdom structure, social roles, cosmological justification.

**Already documented**: Van Tilburg 1986 (95 claims), Van Tilburg & Lee 1987 (70 claims).

### Mid-1990s: Consolidation as default

- Van Tilburg 1994: most comprehensive single-volume treatment; chiefdom treated as established.
- Martinsson-Wallin 1994: ahu interpreted in terms of chiefdom-level power, lineage territories.
- Skjølsvold 1994: cites Stevenson 1986 extensively at Anakena.
- Fischer 1997: most extensive textual discussion of sociopolitical context; ariki mau 22+ mentions.

**NOT YET PROCESSED**: These are Batch 7 Priority B sources. Need to verify citation chains.

### Late 1990s-2001: Label as given

- Van Tilburg et al. 1998: models "average Rapa Nui chiefdom" with labor estimates.
- Stevenson, Wozniak & Haoa 1999: "social competition amongst the elite components of chiefdoms" in *Antiquity*.
- Stevenson et al. 2001: publishes map of "Rapa Nui chiefdom districts circa AD 1500."
- Stevenson 2002: explicitly adopts chiefdom language: "evolved into a well-developed chiefdom."

## What to Test During Processing

For each Era 5 source, check:

1. **What new Rapa Nui evidence is presented?** Is there archaeological or ethnohistoric evidence not available before 1984 that specifically supports the chiefdom interpretation?
2. **What is cited as justification for using chiefdom language?** Other Rapa Nui papers that use it? Kirch 1984? Polynesian analogy? Or Rapa Nui-specific data?
3. **Does the source acknowledge the pre-1984 non-chiefdom interpretations?** Does it engage with Goldman's "Open" classification, Stevenson 1984's segmentary model, or the Mulloy school's kin-group framework?
4. **Is there a moment of explicit adoption?** Does the author note that they are applying a chiefdom model, or is the label used without comment as if it were always the standard description?

## The Circular Chain

External model arrives (Kirch 1984) → local researchers adopt it (Stevenson 1986, Van Tilburg 1986) → subsequent researchers cite the local adoptions as evidence (Van Tilburg 1994, Martinsson-Wallin 1994) → the model appears independently confirmed by Rapa Nui data → but it was never derived from Rapa Nui data in the first place.

This is the five-step circularity operating within a single regional case, traceable through a specific citation chain. It should be documented explicitly in the catalog section of the paper, showing the before/after contrast.

## Potential Complications

- Some new evidence MAY have emerged in the 1990s (e.g., Beardsley 1990's spatial analysis, Cristino et al. 1999's Tongariki sequence). Need to assess whether any of this genuinely supports the chiefdom model independently of the pre-existing label.
- The birdman cult literature (Orongo, tangata manu) describes a political institution that some interpret as replacing hereditary chieftainship. This complicates a simple "chiefdom" narrative and may have been acknowledged by some Era 5 authors.
- Robinson & Stevenson 2017 later admit paramountcy "not discovered archaeologically or reported in ethnohistoric oral history" (p. 90), which is a partial retraction from within the Stevenson tradition.
