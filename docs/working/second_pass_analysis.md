# Second-Pass Analysis: Contact-Era Accounts

[UNVERIFIED: working notes for manuscript drafting. All claims below should be verified against claims files and primary sources before use in manuscript prose.]

## Purpose

These notes capture the analytical framework developed during the second-pass review of all pre-slave-raid European accounts (Roggeveen 1722, González 1770, Cook 1774, La Pérouse 1786, and post-La Pérouse visitors 1793-1862 via Richards 2008). The first pass focused on extracting individual claims; the second pass identified cross-cutting patterns, "between the lines" inferences, and analytical connections that strengthen the paper's epistemological argument.

## Six Cross-Cutting Patterns for Section 3 (Contact-Era Accounts)

### 1. 140 Years of Consistent Decentralized Exchange (1722-1862)

Every recorded trade encounter follows the same pattern: individual islanders carry their own produce to ships and exchange directly with sailors. No intermediary, no collection point, no chiefly delegate. This consistency across 140 years and dozens of independent observers from multiple nations suggests stable structural decentralization, not a transitional condition.

**Manuscript use**: This belongs in Section 3 as a primary argument. The stability of the pattern across time is the analytical point; it cannot be explained by a chiefdom in any phase (consolidation, stability, or collapse).

### 2. Authority Identified by Appearance, Never Confirmed by Behavior

La Pérouse, Cook, Peard, and Wolfe all followed the same sequence: identified candidates by visual markers, tested against behavioral evidence, found no authority. This is not four independent failures to find something; it is four replications of the same negative result.

**Manuscript use**: Frame as a replicated negative finding. The consistency of the negative result across different observers, different decades, and different national traditions of observation strengthens the conclusion.

### 3. The Ecological Constraint

Cook: "As everything must be raised by dint of labour, it cannot be supposed the inhabitants plant much more than is sufficient for themselves." Chiefdoms require extractable surplus. Multiple independent observations (multiple cooking fires, individual food preparation, no storehouses, universal poverty in household goods, coconut shells as most valuable trade item) converge on the conclusion that Rapa Nui's ecology did not produce the surplus chiefdoms require.

**Manuscript use**: This supports the argument but is secondary to the epistemological case. Note: this argument can be overdrawn. Monument construction proves that large-scale cooperative labor was possible; the question is whether this required chiefly command or can be explained by collective action.

### 4. Temporal Pattern in Authority Attributions

Early observers attribute more political structure; later, more systematic observers find less. Most parsimoniously explained as increasing observer sophistication, not actual political change. The Forsters' elaborate characterization vs. Cook's honest ignorance is the clearest case.

**Manuscript use**: Use cautiously. Do not overstate; the temporal decline in authority attributions could also reflect actual political change. But the observer-sophistication explanation is supported by the fact that the most detailed, most careful observers are consistently the ones who find the least authority.

### 5. No Ceremony of Authority Across 140 Years

No coronation, inauguration, council, judicial proceeding, or authority-conferring ceremony ever described. This is a significant absence because chiefdoms are characteristically marked by elaborate public ritual conferring and displaying political authority.

**Manuscript use**: List among the systematic absences. Note: the absence of OBSERVED ceremonies is not the same as the absence of ceremonies; brief visits may simply have missed them. But 140 years of cumulative observation with no sighting is suggestive.

### 6. Age-Grading as Alternative Explanation

Both Roggeveen and González describe differentiation tracking with age, not hereditary rank. Jakubowska's correction (*hombres mayores* = "elderly men," not "principal men") removes one of the key textual supports for hereditary rank in the González accounts.

**Manuscript use**: Age-grading and gerontocracy are well-documented in Polynesian societies at various levels of political complexity. The point is not that age-grading precludes chiefdom, but that the observed differentiation is more parsimoniously explained by age than by hereditary political rank.

## Five "Between the Lines" Inferences for Manuscript Prose

### 1. Property is Individually Defended, Not Institutionally Protected

Cook's plantation owner (1846, p. 450) plus La Pérouse's "general consent" (p. 324) = household-level resource control with collective consensus for disposition. No institutional property protection. Opposite of chiefly redistribution.

### 2. Violence Response Reveals Authority Limits

Cook shooting (1774) and Blossom confrontation (1825): both responses are persuasion and de-escalation by individuals, not organized retaliation or chiefly justice. Effective but non-institutional. Consistency across 52 years suggests structural norm.

### 3. Forsters vs. Cook: Selective Reading as Circularity Instance

The chiefdom literature relies on the Forsters' elaborate political portrait while ignoring Cook's "I confess myself quite ignorant." This preferential citation of the more interpretive source because it provides the political structure the model requires is itself an instance of the five-step circularity. This is a strong point for the manuscript spine.

### 4. Property Norms Resolve as Hybrid System

Communal sharing of portable items and living spaces (Roggeveen, González) + individual control of land and food production (Cook, La Pérouse) = hybrid system inconsistent with chiefly redistribution, consistent with collective action models.

### 5. Isolation Removes the Structural Conditions for Polynesian Chiefdoms

Bouman's conclusion of complete isolation (von Saher 1990b, p. 52) means the inter-polity competition that Kirch identifies as the engine of Polynesian political complexity was absent. The structural conditions that produced chiefdoms in Hawaii and Tonga did not apply.

## Connection to Paper's Spine

The five-step circularity argument benefits from the second-pass review in three specific ways:

1. **Step 1 (European accounts generate the framework)**: The Forsters vs. Cook comparison demonstrates that the more interpretive, ideologically shaped account was preferentially adopted by later researchers. The circularity begins not just with European observation but with selective citation of the most politically elaborated European account.

2. **Step 3 (archaeological evidence "confirms" the framework)**: The cross-cutting absence of redistribution, tribute, surplus extraction, and directed labor across 140 years of observation means the framework was never independently confirmed by contact-era evidence. The "confirmation" came from archaeological evidence read through a lens that the contact-era evidence does not support.

3. **Step 5 (the framework prevents alternatives)**: The consistent pattern of decentralized exchange, consensus-based decision-making, and household-level resource control described across all contact-era accounts is entirely consistent with collective action models. The chiefdom framework prevented researchers from seeing what the observers themselves described: a decentralized cooperative society.
