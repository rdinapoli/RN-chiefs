## Summary Assessment

Geiseler's report, based on a four-day visit in September 1882, provides several categories of information relevant to the chiefdom question:

**Direct observations (1882):**
- No king exists; no effective chiefly authority; every man is his own master
- "Old chief" title used but carries no actual power or respect
- Complete dissolution of political community; family is the sole organizational unit
- Land ownership is individual/familial, hereditary, and fiercely maintained
- Tabu system still strong, especially regarding ancestors and burial sites
- Idols still regarded as possessing power; tabu on them enforced

**Reported traditions (via Salmon and native informants):**
- Earlier regime described as "almost despotic" (faft befpotifd)) kingship
- Kings served as priests; succession went to "the brother" (not the son)
- Chiefs (Häuptlinge) served as advisors (Rathgeber)
- Marriage was arranged by chiefs and restricted by rank
- Punishment differed by rank (chiefs' wives killed for adultery; commoners received beatings)
- Special occupational classes existed (idol-makers in high esteem)
- The script (rongorongo) was known only to kings and chiefs
- Multiple competing origin traditions circulated (from Rapa/Oparo, from Galapagos, from the west)

**Key interpretive cautions:**
1. Salmon served as the primary translator and cultural broker for all information; his views shaped what was communicated
2. The visit lasted only four days (19-23 September 1882), severely limiting the depth of ethnographic inquiry
3. Information about "earlier times" comes from a population that had declined from ~3,000 to 150 in just 20 years, during which the political system had entirely collapsed
4. The description of former "despotic" kingship may reflect either actual practice, idealized memory, or Salmon's interpretation through a Tahitian lens (he was related to the Tahitian royal family)
5. The term "ariki" appears only once, in reference to a deceased person buried in a special tomb; Geiseler does not record it as a political title in current use
6. The report that kings served as priests is consistent with Polynesian models but unverifiable from this source alone
