# The Chiefdom Attribution: Evidence Compilation

Tracks who attributed chiefdom status to Rapa Nui, when, on what basis, and what evidence they cited. This is the empirical foundation for Section II of the manuscript ("The Chiefdom Attribution: Origins and Development").

## Key Questions

1. Who first applied the chiefdom label to Rapa Nui, and on what evidence?
2. How did the attribution develop from ethnographic description to archaeological claim?
3. What specific evidence has been cited in support of the chiefdom interpretation at each stage?
4. What assumptions about the Polynesian analogy, ethnohistoric reliability, and archaeological signatures does each attribution depend on?
5. Where does the five-step circularity enter the chain of inference?

## Chronological Register of the Attribution

### Pre-chiefdom period: Ethnographic descriptions (1722-1957)

Note: None of the ethnographic sources through Métraux 1957 use the term "chiefdom." They describe political authority in various terms, often contradicting each other. The chiefdom label is a post-1958 imposition. See `ethnographic_sources_evidence.md` for the full register.

Key facts from the ethnographic period:
- Contact-era observers (1722-1862): No observer describes institutional chieftainship. La Pérouse (1786) explicitly found "no need of a chief." HMS Blossom (1825) tested and rejected the chief hypothesis.
- Post-slave-raid ethnographers (1864-1957): Routledge (1919) found "no chiefs nor any form of government." Métraux (1937, 1940) concluded ariki-mau was "certainly not a ruler or civilian chief." Thompson (1891) described "arbitrary monarchy" but simultaneously qualified it out of existence.
- The strongest chiefdom-supporting claims come from Roussel (1869) and Thompson (1891), both filtered through Alexander Salmon as translator/informant.

### Stage 2: First archaeological application to Rapa Nui

McCoy 1976 is the pivotal source. His monograph (*Easter Island Settlement Patterns in the Late Prehistoric and Protohistoric Periods*) is routinely cited as among the first archaeological applications of the chiefdom framework to Rapa Nui. However, McCoy's actual conclusions are the opposite of what has been attributed to him:

**What McCoy actually concluded about the chiefdom question:**
- "If there existed any tendencies toward the development of a chiefdom, the political flux caused by the ascendancy of the warrior class to positions of authority clearly retarded it." (p. 145)
- The warrior class (*matatoa*) displaced hereditary authority and drove social fragmentation, not consolidation.
- "The ascendancy of a warrior class to powers of authority probably functioned to promote more warfare. The net effect was decreasing social integration and increasing dispersion—shifting—of people." (p. 154)

**What McCoy described that was later reread as chiefdom evidence:**
- A "dual status system" in domestic architecture: commoners' huts vs. chiefly/priestly *hare paenga* with dressed curbstone foundations (p. 149). The classification depends on ethnohistoric descriptions, not independent archaeological analysis.
- Settlement organized around ahu-centered "communal centers" described as "the fundamental unit of economics, since it functioned as a base of supply and redistribution" (p. 152). No archaeological evidence for redistribution is presented; the function is inferred from ethnography.
- Tribal territories mapped from Routledge (1919) and Métraux (1940) and superimposed on archaeological density data (p. 139). The boundaries are ethnographic, not archaeologically derived.

**Critical methodological admission:**
- "Ethnographic data is the basis for much of the temporal-functional interpretation and societal reconstruction in this study. It is justified on the basis of demonstrable genetic affinity between archaeological remains and the known historic Easter Island culture." (p. 2)
- He explicitly describes projecting ethnohistoric data back in time and checking archaeological data against this baseline (p. 70). This is the methodology that creates the circularity.

**Sahlins exception:**
- McCoy noted that Sahlins (1958) himself treated Easter Island as an "exception" to his Polynesian chiefdom model (p. 152), not a confirmation of it.

**The attribution gap:** The distance between what McCoy wrote and how his work was subsequently used is itself evidence of the circularity problem. McCoy's careful settlement data was later reinterpreted through Kirch's (1984) framework as confirming the chiefdom model that McCoy himself said did not develop.

### Stage 5: Archaeological challenges emerge

Multiple independent lines of evidence now challenge the chiefdom attribution:

**Settlement and chronology:**
- Mulrooney 2013: Settlement continuity, not collapse. The "Chiefdom Integration" phase (Stevenson) was built on uncritically assessed radiocarbon data. The prediction that inland agricultural areas would be abandoned after "breakdown of the chiefly economy" fails empirically.
- DiNapoli et al. 2020 (own work): Steady ahu construction tempo through 1722 contact; no pre-contact cessation. Removes the chronological scaffolding for the "Ahu Moai" to "Huri Moai" phase transition.

**Monument production and placement:**
- DiNapoli et al. 2019 (own work): Ahu placement explained by freshwater access, not chiefly territorial control. Rock mulch gardens do not explain ahu placement. No inter-polity competitive spacing needed.
- Lipo et al. 2013 (own work): Walking moai transport feasible with 18 people, no timber, no chiefly command; "small-scale social groups rather than the product of laborers unified under a powerful centralized chiefdom" (p. 2866).
- Lipo et al. 2025 (own work): Quarry production decentralized; 30 redundant workshops, 3 carving methods, small teams (4-6 carvers), no restricted access.

**Population and agriculture:**
- Davis et al. 2024: Rock gardens ~1/5 of prior estimates; carrying capacity ~3,000 matches contact-era estimates; no Malthusian overshoot.
- Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024: Ancient DNA rejects 1600s population collapse; steady growth from founding bottleneck; chronically small population (ROH pattern).

**From within the Stevenson research program:**
- Robinson & Stevenson 2017: Paramountcy "has not been discovered archaeologically or reported in the ethnohistoric oral history" (p. 90). Peak Orongo activity c. AD 1795, placing birdman formalization in the contact period. The authors acknowledge they "presently have no empirical data to connect" ecological pressures to the cult's emergence (p. 99).

**From the early archaeological record:**
- McCoy 1976: "If there existed any tendencies toward the development of a chiefdom, the political flux caused by the ascendancy of the warrior class to positions of authority clearly retarded it" (p. 145). The first comprehensive settlement study found evidence against, not for, the chiefdom.
- Pollard et al. 2010: Birdman system replaced hereditary chiefly authority with competitive warrior-leadership. Orongo chronology may be entirely post-contact.

## The Five-Step Circularity Applied

1. **European observers use European categories**: Contact-era accounts interpret Rapa Nui social organization through monarchical/aristocratic frameworks
2. **Post-raid ethnographers elaborate on disrupted traditions**: Thompson and Roussel describe elaborate political hierarchies that Routledge and Métraux could not confirm
3. **Polynesian analogy imports the chiefdom model**: Sahlins and Kirch build a Polynesian chiefdom framework from Hawaii, Tonga, Samoa and apply it to Rapa Nui
4. **Archaeological evidence is read through the chiefdom lens**: Ahu as chiefly territorial markers, moai as displays of chiefly power, settlement patterns as chiefdom territories
5. **The archaeological reading "confirms" the ethnohistoric-analogical framework**: The circularity closes
