# The Chiefdom Attribution: Evidence Compilation

Tracks who attributed chiefdom status to Rapa Nui, when, on what basis, and what evidence they cited. This is the empirical foundation for Section II of the manuscript ("The Chiefdom Attribution: Origins and Development").

## Key Questions

1. Who first applied the chiefdom label to Rapa Nui, and on what evidence?
2. How did the attribution develop from ethnographic description to archaeological claim?
3. What specific evidence has been cited in support of the chiefdom interpretation at each stage?
4. What assumptions about the Polynesian analogy, ethnohistoric reliability, and archaeological signatures does each attribution depend on?
5. Where does the five-step circularity enter the chain of inference?

## Chronological Register of the Attribution

### Pre-chiefdom period: Ethnographic descriptions (1722-1957)

Note: None of the ethnographic sources through Métraux 1957 use the term "chiefdom." They describe political authority in various terms, often contradicting each other. The chiefdom label is a post-1958 imposition. See `ethnographic_sources_evidence.md` for the full register.

Key facts from the ethnographic period:
- Contact-era observers (1722-1862): No observer describes institutional chieftainship. La Pérouse (1786) explicitly found "no need of a chief." HMS Blossom (1825) tested and rejected the chief hypothesis.
- Post-slave-raid ethnographers (1864-1957): Routledge (1919) found "no chiefs nor any form of government." Métraux (1937, 1940) concluded ariki-mau was "certainly not a ruler or civilian chief." Thompson (1891) described "arbitrary monarchy" but simultaneously qualified it out of existence.
- The strongest chiefdom-supporting claims come from Roussel (1869) and Thompson (1891), both filtered through Alexander Salmon as translator/informant.

### Stage 1: The chiefdom concept is created and applied to Polynesia

**The genealogy of the concept:**

The chiefdom typology did not emerge from Polynesian data; it was imposed on Polynesia from a neoevolutionary framework developed in the Columbia school (Steward's students):

- **Oberg 1955**: Coined the term "chiefdom" as a typological category for South American lowland societies.
- **Steward 1955**: Multilinear cultural evolution framework; the intellectual context for Service and Sahlins.
- **White 1959**: Unilinear cultural evolution; the broader neoevolutionary backdrop.
- **Sahlins & Service (eds.) 1960** (*Evolution and Culture*): **Processed 2026-03-16** (31 claims). Theoretical manifesto defining general vs. specific evolution. Provides the intellectual scaffolding for treating "chiefdom" as a general evolutionary stage applicable beyond Polynesia. "Chiefdom" appears exactly once in the volume (Sahlins Ch. II, p. 36), in an embryonic sketch of evolutionary stages: "chiefless bands ... egalitarian tribes ... chiefdoms with internal status differentiation and developed chieftainship ... nation state." Not elaborated. Easter Island appears 6 times, all citing Sahlins 1955: moai as "esoteric efflorescence" (adaptive specialization within ramage organization), not as evidence for chiefdom-level organization. Service's own chapter (V) contains no sociopolitical typology. Redistribution not discussed in connection with political organization. Position in the genealogy: this book bridges Sahlins's empirical Polynesian work (1954/1955/1957/1958) and Service's formal typology (1962) by providing the theoretical justification for universal evolutionary stages.
- **Service 1962** (*Primitive Social Organization*): **Processed 2026-03-16** (42 claims). Formalizes band/tribe/chiefdom/state as universal evolutionary stages. Chiefdom defined as "redistributional societies with a permanent central agency of coordination" (p. 144). Draws extensively on Sahlins 1958 for Polynesian data; Sahlins read the manuscript. Easter Island is NEVER MENTIONED in the book. Polynesia is the paradigmatic chiefdom region, with Hawaii, Tahiti, Tonga, Maori, and Samoa cited specifically but not Easter Island. The feedback loop is explicit: Sahlins uses Polynesia to characterize stratified kin societies -> Service formalizes these into the chiefdom stage -> Kirch applies the stage back to Polynesia. After this book, "chiefdom" becomes a universal evolutionary category, not just a Polynesian descriptor; Easter Island must be a chiefdom because it is too complex for "tribe" and the only other option is "state." Critical finding: Easter Island fails EVERY defining criterion of Service's chiefdom. No centralized redistribution (Goldman 1970, pp. 114-115; Sahlins 1954, p. 101). No hereditary offices outside the Miru (Goldman 1970, p. 120). No centrally directed labor (Goldman 1970, pp. 106-107; Kirch 1984, pp. 270-271). No continuous genealogical ranking (Goldman 1970, p. 116). No craft subsidization from a redistributive center (Sahlins 1955, p. 1050). Service himself notes the Maori "reverted" from chiefdom to tribal levels (pp. 147-148), admitting that being Polynesian does not automatically mean being a developed chiefdom, but this logic was never applied to Easter Island.
- **Fried 1967**: Alternative typology (egalitarian/ranked/stratified/state) that competes with Service's scheme. Relevant because it shows the typology was contested from the start.

**The Polynesian application:**

- **Sahlins 1954/1958** (*Social Stratification in Polynesia*): The 1954 Columbia PhD dissertation (published 1958 by University of Washington Press) established the Polynesian stratification gradient. Sahlins placed Rapa Nui in **Group IIa** (moderate-high stratification), alongside Mangareva, Mangaia, and Uvea, below Group I (Hawaii, Tonga, Samoa, Tahiti) but above Group IIb (Marquesas, Tikopia, Futuna). The Easter Island section (1954, pp. 99-106) relies **entirely on post-slave-raid sources**: Metraux 1940 (~30+ citations), Metraux 1937b, Thomson 1889, Routledge 1919, and Williamson 1924. No contact-era accounts (Roggeveen, González, Cook, La Pérouse) are cited. Sahlins himself admitted "there is little direct evidence that the ariki mau and the birdmen were focal points of accumulation and redistribution" (p. 101) and filled this gap by Polynesian analogy: "We may surmise from comparative data" that they served this function. He reported Metraux's own conclusion that government was "decentralized and localized" (p. 104), that Thomson found "no great homage" for the ariki mau (p. 106), and that confiscation of surplus "does not seem to have been enforced" (p. 101). He classified Easter Island as a ramage society but acknowledged it was ecologically anomalous for this category (pp. 361-362). The matatoa claim was challenged as resting on "tenuous evidence" (p. 284). Full claims extraction: `docs/references/claims/sahlins_1954_claims.md` (133 claims).
- **Sahlins 1963** ("Poor Man, Rich Man, Big-Man, Chief"): **Processed 2026-03-16** (40 claims). Crystallizes the big-man (Melanesia) vs. chief (Polynesia) distinction. Easter Island mentioned once as geographic boundary (p. 285), never analyzed or cited. The "Polynesian chief" is built from Hawaii (Malo, Fornander), Tahiti (Handy), and Tonga (Gifford), then attributed to "Polynesia" as a whole. By placing all Polynesia on the "chief" side by geographic definition, the question shifts from "was Easter Island a chiefdom?" to "what kind of chiefdom?" Service 1962 listed as "in press." Goldman cited for status rivalry but the implication that some Polynesian societies do not fit the "chief" type is not drawn. Critically, Easter Island's matatoa-dominated system (achieved leadership, personal followings, factional warfare, competitive status rivalry) fits Sahlins's own "big-man" description better than his "chief" type.
- **Goldman 1970** (*Ancient Polynesian Society*): Status rivalry framework. **Processed 2026-03-16** (full 632-page book, 141 claims). Goldman independently classified Rapa Nui as an **"Open" society** that "had never evolved a central authority" (p. 94). The Open classification places Easter Island alongside Mangaia, Marquesas, Samoa, and Niue, defined by warrior displacement of hereditary chiefs: "power is dominant and status is subordinate" (p. 22). On Easter Island, warriors gained both political AND religious power (p. 96), unique in Polynesia. Goldman's key findings for the chiefdom critique: (1) Redistribution explicitly lay "not with the ariki mau, but with heads of commoner groups" (pp. 114-115); (2) no central authority ever undertook local monument construction (pp. 106-107); (3) the ariki mau was "probably never a powerful secular ruler" (p. 115); (4) political tradition was "separatist" (p. 119), committed "only to the principle of religious unity, political ties remaining tenuous" (pp. 118-119); (5) Easter Island lacked the rangatira rank, which in Traditional societies was held by segment heads (p. 116). Goldman's framework classifies by status-power relationship, not productivity-stratification (Sahlins) or inherited template (Kirch). The classification was available to Kirch: Table 2 (p. 37) of Kirch 1984 includes it but the Easter Island chapter never engages with it. See `docs/references/claims/goldman_1970_claims.md` and `docs/references/summaries/goldman_1970_summary.md`.

**The feedback loop:** Sahlins uses Polynesia to illustrate evolutionary stages → Service formalizes those stages partly based on Sahlins's Polynesian data → Kirch applies Service's formalized stages back to Polynesia archaeologically. This loop is itself part of the circularity: the Polynesian data that "confirm" the chiefdom model are the same data from which the model was partly constructed. For Rapa Nui specifically, the circularity is compounded: Sahlins admitted the redistribution evidence was lacking and filled the gap by analogy with better-documented islands (1954, p. 101). When Kirch later treats Rapa Nui as a chiefdom, he inherits Sahlins's analogical inference as if it were an empirical finding.

**For this paper (Section II):** Mention the Oberg-Steward-Service genealogy in a paragraph to establish that the chiefdom concept was not derived from Polynesian data but imposed on it. Focus the detailed analysis on the Sahlins 1958 → Service 1962 → Goldman 1970 → Kirch 1984 chain, since that is the sequence that specifically positions Rapa Nui. The deeper intellectual history (Oberg, Steward, White, Fried) is more appropriate for the CA paper.

**Processing order:** Sahlins 1954/1958 → Service 1962 → Sahlins 1963 → Goldman 1970 → Kirch 1984. Foundations before Kirch so we can distinguish what Kirch inherits vs. transforms vs. adds.

**Sahlins 1954 processed** (2026-03-16): Full 464-page dissertation read chapter by chapter. 133 claims extracted. See `docs/references/claims/sahlins_1954_claims.md` and `docs/references/summaries/sahlins_1954_summary.md`.

**Sahlins 1955 processed** (2026-03-16): "Esoteric Efflorescence in Easter Island," *AA* 57:1045-1052. Only Sahlins article entirely on Easter Island. Environmental channeling hypothesis: moai as redirection of communal labor blocked from subsistence by environmental constraints. Key new evidence: stone carver passage (Metraux 1940:137) describes lineage-level commissioning by "people who wanted a monument for their ahu," paid directly in food under craft-guild leadership, not centralized chiefly command through redistribution. Sahlins infers the lineage head gave the order (p. 1050), but the evidence supports decentralized production. Redistribution asserted flatly as "predominant method" (p. 1049), stripping the dissertation's "little direct evidence" qualification. The environmental channeling argument is more compatible with collective action than chiefly command: Sahlins himself notes the feedback loop between chiefly power and communal labor is strongest when labor is subsistence-increasing, and moai are explicitly non-strategic. See `docs/references/claims/sahlins_1955_claims.md` and `docs/references/summaries/sahlins_1955_summary.md`.

### Stage 2: First archaeological application to Rapa Nui

McCoy 1976 is the pivotal source. His monograph (*Easter Island Settlement Patterns in the Late Prehistoric and Protohistoric Periods*) is routinely cited as among the first archaeological applications of the chiefdom framework to Rapa Nui. However, McCoy's actual conclusions are the opposite of what has been attributed to him:

**What McCoy actually concluded about the chiefdom question:**
- "If there existed any tendencies toward the development of a chiefdom, the political flux caused by the ascendancy of the warrior class to positions of authority clearly retarded it." (p. 145)
- The warrior class (*matatoa*) displaced hereditary authority and drove social fragmentation, not consolidation.
- "The ascendancy of a warrior class to powers of authority probably functioned to promote more warfare. The net effect was decreasing social integration and increasing dispersion—shifting—of people." (p. 154)

**What McCoy described that was later reread as chiefdom evidence:**
- A "dual status system" in domestic architecture: commoners' huts vs. chiefly/priestly *hare paenga* with dressed curbstone foundations (p. 149). The classification depends on ethnohistoric descriptions, not independent archaeological analysis.
- Settlement organized around ahu-centered "communal centers" described as "the fundamental unit of economics, since it functioned as a base of supply and redistribution" (p. 152). No archaeological evidence for redistribution is presented; the function is inferred from ethnography.
- Tribal territories mapped from Routledge (1919) and Métraux (1940) and superimposed on archaeological density data (p. 139). The boundaries are ethnographic, not archaeologically derived.

**Critical methodological admission:**
- "Ethnographic data is the basis for much of the temporal-functional interpretation and societal reconstruction in this study. It is justified on the basis of demonstrable genetic affinity between archaeological remains and the known historic Easter Island culture." (p. 2)
- He explicitly describes projecting ethnohistoric data back in time and checking archaeological data against this baseline (p. 70). This is the methodology that creates the circularity.

**Sahlins exception:**
- McCoy noted that Sahlins (1958) himself treated Easter Island as an "exception" to his Polynesian chiefdom model (p. 152), not a confirmation of it.

**The attribution gap:** The distance between what McCoy wrote and how his work was subsequently used is itself evidence of the circularity problem. McCoy's careful settlement data was later reinterpreted through Kirch's (1984) framework as confirming the chiefdom model that McCoy himself said did not develop.

**Ayres 1973** ("The Cultural Context of Easter Island Religious Structures"), Tulane University PhD dissertation:

Ayres 1973 is the first systematic study of Easter Island ahu in cultural context and represents the pre-chiefdom-model archaeological baseline. Key findings for the attribution chain:

1. **"Chiefdom" does not appear in the dissertation.** Service 1962 is not cited. Ayres works entirely within Goldman's (1970) "Traditional" / "Open" / "Stratified" framework.

2. **Ahu as kin-group structures, not chiefly command centers.** "Land ownership is important because the associated social unit would be the one whose membership was involved in constructing and maintaining ahu on their land" (p. 329). Construction attributed to nonunilinear descent groups (mata, lineages/ivi) controlling land.

3. **Ariki mau as spiritual, not political.** "At the time of European contact the king was not a political ruler but more of a spiritual one who presided over the entire island but did not engage in intertribal feuding" (p. 25).

4. **No redistribution mechanism.** No discussion of redistribution, tribute, or centralized economic coordination as mechanisms for ahu construction or social organization.

5. **Craft guild and kin-group labor.** "A craft guild was responsible for carving" the standardized statues (p. 338). Large ahu construction attributed to growing kin groups and (following Goldman) slave labor from military conquest (p. 337).

6. **Goldman's "Open" classification adopted.** Explicitly uses Goldman's framework in which Easter Island "had never evolved a central authority" (Goldman 1970:94). Goldman cited throughout for social organization.

7. **Social class reality questioned.** Four reported classes but "the classification is a superficial one and the reality of clear-cut classes is in doubt" (p. 25).

8. **Trajectory toward decentralization.** Phase sequence shows shift from sacred/religious authority toward military/warrior control. "The prerogatives of rank were no longer as closely associated with status lineages...but increasingly with military power" (p. 337).

**Significance for the attribution chain:** Ayres (Tulane, under Mulloy) and Stevenson (Penn State, also trained by Mulloy) independently produced ahu-focused dissertations that avoid chiefdom terminology and work within descent-group frameworks. Both use Goldman rather than Service/Sahlins as their comparative framework. This demonstrates that the chiefdom label was not an inevitable reading of the archaeological evidence; it was imposed by Kirch 1984. The fact that Mulloy's own students interpreted the same ahu data without chiefdom concepts makes the subsequent reframing under that label all the more conspicuous.

**Mulloy & Figueroa 1966** ("The Archaeological Heritage of Rapa Nui"), UNESCO report, 111 pp:

The most comprehensive archaeological heritage assessment before Kirch 1984, written by the two archaeologists with the most field experience on the island (combined ~4 years of fieldwork). Key findings for the pre-chiefdom baseline:

1. **Settlement as independent kin groups.** "The people were scattered widely along the coasts and adjacent interior areas of the island in relatively small independent or partially independent kin groups of some sort" (pp. 12-13). No centralized authority, administrative centers, or chiefly compounds described.

2. **Ahu as kin-group capacity.** "The size and ostentation of features of many ahu reflected the numbers and capacity of the kin groups that built them" (p. 16). Ahu variation attributed to "individual preferences of kin groups and a sense of competition among them" (pp. 15-16). This is the kin-group capacity model: ahu size reflects group resources, not chiefly power.

3. **No chiefdom terminology in 111 pages.** "Chiefdom," "chief" (as political term), "hierarchy," "rank," "stratified," "redistribution," and "tribute" are entirely absent. Service, Sahlins, and Goldman not cited.

4. **Orongo as competitive, nonhereditary, annual.** The birdman ceremony involved "representatives of the various lineage groups" competing annually; the winner's sponsor occupied "a position of special ceremonial importance" for one year (pp. 52-53). No permanent political office.

5. **Source later cited by Kirch.** Kirch 1984:270-271 cites a later Mulloy & Figueroa publication (1978:134) for "independently working crews, probably sent periodically by their kin groups," demonstrating that Mulloy & Figueroa's kin-group framework was available to Kirch but recontextualized within the chiefdom model.

**Mulloy 1979** ("A Preliminary Culture-Historical Research Model for Easter Island"), 3-page excerpt:

Mulloy's final publication, representing his mature assessment after 23 years of fieldwork (1955-1978). Key findings:

1. **No chiefdom terminology.** Does not use "chiefdom," "chief," "hierarchy," or "redistribution." Service, Sahlins, and Goldman not cited.

2. **Assumes "class organized society" without evidence.** "A class organized society with enough coercive power to bring large work crews together" is stated without theoretical justification and not developed into a chiefdom argument. This assumption stands in tension with Mulloy & Figueroa 1966's own kin-group capacity model.

3. **Post-raid source unreliability explicitly recognized.** "Only a demoralized and to a considerable extent deculturated remnant population has been available as culture-historical informants" (p. 3). "A proportionately much greater reliance has had to be placed on archaeological evidence" than on other Pacific islands.

4. **Archaeological evidence "far from adequate."** Multiple knowledge gaps enumerated: settlement period evidence "entirely lacking," domestic architecture lacking time perspective, environmental modification data "sketchy."

5. **Model framed as tentative and expendable.** "Studies aimed at testing this framework will surely modify the model and may ultimately demonstrate that it should be discarded" (p. 4). Explicitly rejects imposing "a foreign historical sequence" on local developments.

6. **Ahu destruction hypothesis.** Proposes ahu may have been "destroyed by elements of the societies of their owners rather than by enemy groups from other localities" (p. 4), suggesting internal rather than inter-polity processes.

**The Mulloy school pattern:** Mulloy himself (1966, 1979), plus his students Ayres (1973), McCoy (1976), and Stevenson (1984), all interpreted Easter Island's archaeological record without chiefdom concepts. This is four independent researchers working from the same excavation tradition, using kin-group/descent-group frameworks and Goldman's "Open" classification rather than Service's chiefdom typology. The chiefdom attribution came from outside this tradition (Kirch 1984, working primarily from Hawaii) and was imposed on evidence that its primary investigators did not read that way.

**Stevenson 1984** (*Corporate Descent Group Structure in Easter Island Prehistory*), Pennsylvania State University PhD dissertation:

Stevenson 1984 is the first work to systematically apply a political-organizational model to Rapa Nui archaeological settlement data. However, the model applied is NOT the chiefdom typology. Key findings:

1. **Service 1962 is not cited.** The band/tribe/chiefdom/state framework is entirely absent. Stevenson's theoretical framework draws on British social anthropology (Fortes 1953, Firth 1936/1957), corporate group theory (Hayden & Cannon 1982, Brown 1974), and the conical clan concept (Kirchhoff 1955). The term "chiefdom" is not used as an analytical category.

2. **Goldman 1970 IS engaged** (pp. 16, 178). Stevenson cites Goldman for the dual status system: hereditary chiefs had sacred but not secular authority; warrior chiefs (matatoa) controlled positions of force. This contrasts with Kirch 1984, who includes Goldman's "Open" classification in Table 2 but never engages with it.

3. **Four politically autonomous groups** are identified by Phase 9 (1501-1600 AD), persisting until Phase 11 (p. 163). These are NOT hierarchically organized relative to each other.

4. **No institutionalized rank differences**: "each of these groups does not exhibit internal or between group differences in ascribed social rank" (p. 180). Burial data from semipyramidal interment structures show "none of the data indicates the possibility of social ranking" (p. 161).

5. **System reverted to non-ranked structure** after ~1680 AD: "the ranked corporate descent group structure is not present... reverted to a non-ranked corporate structure" (p. 168).

6. **Explicit rejection of unilinear evolution**: "it is not assumed a priori that the prehistoric social system of Easter Island is headed full tilt on a course of increased social complexity, but that it may adopt alternate forms of social structure as conditions dictate" (p. 50).

7. **Multiple hedging statements**: Conclusions are "tentative in nature and require verification through future research" (p. 20); the model is "preliminary" and "needs to be evaluated by additional research" (p. 181).

**Stevenson, Shaw & Cristino 1984** ("Obsidian Procurement and Consumption on Easter Island," *Archaeology in Oceania* 19:120-124):

Published the same year as the dissertation, this obsidian quarry study confirms that Stevenson's 1984 interpretive framework had no place for chiefdom terminology. "Chiefdom," "chief," "hierarchy," "stratification," and "redistribution" are entirely absent. Service, Sahlins, and Kirch are not cited. The paper describes the Orito obsidian quarry as having 33+ open pits with quarrying activity around the full circumference of the hill (p. 120), with no evidence of restricted access or centralized control. The obsidian consumption curve (AD 1350-1864) shows no dramatic disruption at the traditional AD 1680 "collapse" date. Two alternative hypotheses for post-1700 increased obsidian use are offered: population nucleation, or political instability reducing territorial control and increasing access (p. 124). Neither invokes chiefly authority or centralized management.

**The progressive hardening problem:** The 1984 dissertation and the 1984 obsidian paper both describe autonomous, non-hierarchical descent groups and explicitly avoid the chiefdom framework. By 1986 (Stevenson's chapter in Kirch's *Island Societies*), the same data is reframed within a chiefdom context. By later publications (Stevenson 1997, 2002), the "Chiefdom Integration" periodization (AD 1425-1680) becomes established terminology. This trajectory parallels the Sahlins 1954-to-1958 hardening: tentative, qualified findings are progressively stripped of caveats and absorbed into a framework the original author did not use.

**Stevenson 1986** ("The Socio-Political Structure of the Southern Coastal Area of Easter Island: AD 1300-1864," in Kirch ed. *Island Societies*, pp. 69-77):

Stevenson 1986 documents the critical transition between the independent corporate group analysis (1984) and its absorption into the chiefdom framework. The chapter is the 1984 dissertation condensed to 9 pages and placed in Kirch's programmatic volume on evolutionary approaches to island societies. Key findings for the chiefdom attribution chain:

1. **"Chiefdom" still absent from Stevenson's text.** Service 1962 and Kirch 1984 are not cited. The analytical framework remains corporate descent groups; the term "chiefdom" does not appear on any of the 9 pages. Stevenson did not adopt the chiefdom label.

2. **But the theoretical infrastructure that made this an alternative is stripped away.** The 1984 dissertation was grounded in British social anthropology (Fortes, Firth) and corporate group archaeology (Hayden & Cannon), constituting a principled intellectual alternative to the Sahlins-Service-Kirch framework. In 1986, every one of these theoretical citations is absent. The method survives; the theory that positioned it as an alternative does not.

3. **Strongest anti-chiefdom commitments dropped.** The 1984 statement that "it is not assumed a priori that the prehistoric social system of Easter Island is headed full tilt on a course of increased social complexity" (p. 50) is absent. The three-model framework (organizational alternatives, not evolutionary stages) is absent. The "tentative in nature and require verification" caveat is absent.

4. **Reversion reframed as weakening.** The 1984 finding that the system "reverted to a non-ranked corporate structure" (p. 168) becomes "corporate solidarity of these communities had been weakened" (p. 77). Structural flexibility (the system can adopt simpler forms) becomes degradation (the system declined).

5. **Editorial context does the reframing.** The volume title (*Island Societies: Archaeological Approaches to Evolution and Transformation*), Kirch's editorship, and Cambridge University Press positioning transform the reader's interpretation of the data. Four autonomous groups become chiefdom-level polities; Type 1 ahu become chiefly centers; the late-period "weakening" becomes chiefdom collapse. None of these readings are in Stevenson's text; the volume's framing invites them.

6. **Four autonomous groups and no higher-level integration retained.** "Whether these groups were integrated at a higher level has not been detected archaeologically within the study area" (p. 77). This hedge survives and directly contradicts the chiefdom interpretation's assumption of supra-local authority.

7. **Ibo analogue retained over Polynesian models.** Stevenson explicitly states that Polynesian ethnographic data are "limited and often anecdotal" (p. 70) and selects an African corporate group case study instead. This is a deliberate rejection of Polynesian chiefdom models as the interpretive frame, retained from 1984.

**The progressive hardening trajectory:** The 1984-to-1986 transition is the first step in a three-stage hardening: tentative corporate group analysis (1984) -> editorial reframing within chiefdom volume (1986) -> "Chiefdom Integration" periodization (Stevenson 1997, 2002). By the late 1990s, the "Chiefdom Integration" phase (AD 1425-1680) becomes established terminology for the period that the 1984 dissertation described using non-chiefdom, non-evolutionary corporate group models. This parallels the Sahlins 1954-to-1958 hardening but operates through editorial context and selective attrition rather than the author's own theoretical shift.

### Stage 3: Kirch's comprehensive evolutionary framework

**Kirch 1984 processed** (2026-03-16): *The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms*, Cambridge University Press, 312 pp. Full claims extraction: `docs/references/claims/kirch_1984_claims.md` (121 claims).

This is where the chiefdom attribution becomes archaeological. Kirch transforms Sahlins's synchronic ethnographic classification into a diachronic narrative of cultural evolution, applying it to Easter Island through a three-phase scheme (Colonization → Ahu Moai → Decadent/Huri Ahu).

**What Kirch adds to the chain:**

1. **Ancestral Polynesian Society as inherited template** (pp. 62-65, 281): Kirch reconstructs APS from archaeological and linguistic evidence (Proto-Polynesian *\*ariki, \*mana, \*tapu, \*toa, \*kainanga*). His core argument: chiefdom institutions were carried to every Polynesian island as "cultural baggage" (p. 40). The chiefdom is the default; it need not be independently demonstrated for each island. This shifts the burden of proof from demonstrating chiefdom development to assuming it.

2. **The ecocide narrative** (pp. 274-278, 280): Easter Island as ecological tragedy. Three-phase cultural sequence with overpopulation → environmental degradation → warfare → statue toppling → cultural degeneration. This becomes the dominant narrative through Bahn & Flenley (1992) and Diamond (2005).

3. **Archaeological chronology** (pp. 266-269): 30+ radiocarbon dates providing chronological framework for the three-phase scheme (Fig. 89, p. 267).

4. **Population estimate of ~7,000** (p. 271): Unsourced, higher than Métraux's 3,000-4,000 that Sahlins used. Drives the overshoot narrative.

**What Kirch inherits unchanged from Sahlins:**

1. Post-slave-raid source dependency: No contact-era primary accounts cited. All Easter Island ethnographic claims from Métraux 1940, Englert 1970, Routledge 1919/1920.
2. Redistribution assumed: Sahlins's admission of "little direct evidence" (1954, p. 101) not acknowledged. Redistribution assumed via APS inheritance.
3. Ramage classification retained as "developed form of the Ancestral Polynesian segmentary ramage system" (p. 272).

**What Kirch changes from Sahlins:**

1. **Moai production**: Sahlins (1954, p. 100) attributed monument transport to "members of the chiefly status level." Kirch explicitly describes decentralized, lineage-level production: "independently working crews, probably sent periodically by their kin groups, rather than the activities of a single work force under centralized direction" (Mulloy and Figueroa 1978:134, quoted pp. 270-271). This is a significant departure.
2. **Matatoa**: Sahlins (1954, p. 284) challenged the matatoa claim as "tenuous." Kirch accepts the matatoa as central to Easter Island's political trajectory, sourcing the warrior concept to APS heritage (*\*toa*) (p. 282).
3. **Redistribution theory reversed** (p. 281): Kirch argues chiefs and redistribution were present from the beginning (APS), not that they developed because of functional needs. This undercuts the Sahlinsian functionalist rationale.

**Key internal tensions in Kirch's Easter Island chapter:**

1. **Ahu as descent group structures** (p. 270): "Virtually all ethnographic and archaeological authorities agree that the *ahu* were conceived, constructed, and used by local descent groups, and do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority." This directly contradicts the implication of chiefly command over monument production.

2. **Goldman's "Open" classification** (Table 2, p. 37): Goldman classified Easter Island as "Open" (military/political effectiveness over religious seniority), not "Stratified" (Hawaii, Tonga). This classification is consistent with the matatoa-dominated, non-hierarchical system described by Routledge and Métraux, but Kirch never engages with it in the Easter Island chapter.

3. **Ariki-mau qualified** (p. 272): "may have exercised more ritual than secular power." Consistent with Routledge's and Métraux's stronger findings, but not developed.

4. **Redistribution declines with stratification** (p. 167): Kirch notes this general Polynesian pattern. Combined with Sahlins's admission that Easter Island lacked redistribution evidence, this doubly undermines the functional basis for calling Easter Island a chiefdom.

**How the chiefdom label is maintained despite this evidence:**

Through the APS inheritance argument (p. 281). The chiefdom is not demonstrated for Easter Island; it is assumed as the inherited default condition. The archaeological evidence that Kirch presents for Easter Island specifically (ahu distribution, moai production, settlement patterns) actually describes decentralized descent-group-level organization. The gap between the evidence and the label is bridged by the assumption that what colonizers brought, they kept.

**Assessment against current evidence:**

The ecocide narrative has been substantially undermined: DiNapoli et al. 2020 (steady ahu tempo through contact), Davis et al. 2024 (agricultural capacity 1/5 of estimates, max pop ~3,900), Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 (aDNA rejects population collapse), Mulrooney 2013 (settlement continuity). The population estimate of 7,000 is challenged. The three-phase chronological scheme lacks empirical support. ### Stage 5: Archaeological challenges emerge

Multiple independent lines of evidence now challenge the chiefdom attribution:

**Settlement and chronology:**
- Mulrooney 2013: Settlement continuity, not collapse. The "Chiefdom Integration" phase (Stevenson) was built on uncritically assessed radiocarbon data. The prediction that inland agricultural areas would be abandoned after "breakdown of the chiefly economy" fails empirically.
- DiNapoli et al. 2020 (own work): Steady ahu construction tempo through 1722 contact; no pre-contact cessation. Removes the chronological scaffolding for the "Ahu Moai" to "Huri Moai" phase transition.

**Monument production and placement:**
- DiNapoli et al. 2019 (own work): Ahu placement explained by freshwater access, not chiefly territorial control. Rock mulch gardens do not explain ahu placement. No inter-polity competitive spacing needed.
- Lipo et al. 2013 (own work): Walking moai transport feasible with 18 people, no timber, no chiefly command; "small-scale social groups rather than the product of laborers unified under a powerful centralized chiefdom" (p. 2866).
- Lipo et al. 2025 (own work): Quarry production decentralized; 30 redundant workshops, 3 carving methods, small teams (4-6 carvers), no restricted access.

**Population and agriculture:**
- Davis et al. 2024: Rock gardens ~1/5 of prior estimates; carrying capacity ~3,000 matches contact-era estimates; no Malthusian overshoot.
- Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024: Ancient DNA rejects 1600s population collapse; steady growth from founding bottleneck; chronically small population (ROH pattern).

**From within the Stevenson research program:**
- Robinson & Stevenson 2017: Paramountcy "has not been discovered archaeologically or reported in the ethnohistoric oral history" (p. 90). Peak Orongo activity c. AD 1795, placing birdman formalization in the contact period. The authors acknowledge they "presently have no empirical data to connect" ecological pressures to the cult's emergence (p. 99).

**From the early archaeological record:**
- McCoy 1976: "If there existed any tendencies toward the development of a chiefdom, the political flux caused by the ascendancy of the warrior class to positions of authority clearly retarded it" (p. 145). The first comprehensive settlement study found evidence against, not for, the chiefdom.
- Pollard et al. 2010: Birdman system replaced hereditary chiefly authority with competitive warrior-leadership. Orongo chronology may be entirely post-contact.

## The Five-Step Circularity Applied

1. **European observers use European categories**: Contact-era accounts interpret Rapa Nui social organization through monarchical/aristocratic frameworks
2. **Post-raid ethnographers elaborate on disrupted traditions**: Thompson and Roussel describe elaborate political hierarchies that Routledge and Métraux could not confirm
3. **Polynesian analogy imports the chiefdom model**: Sahlins and Kirch build a Polynesian chiefdom framework from Hawaii, Tonga, Samoa and apply it to Rapa Nui
4. **Archaeological evidence is read through the chiefdom lens**: Ahu as chiefly territorial markers, moai as displays of chiefly power, settlement patterns as chiefdom territories
5. **The archaeological reading "confirms" the ethnohistoric-analogical framework**: The circularity closes

**With page-numbered citations from Kirch 1984:**

Step 1: **European categories**: Kirch does not cite any contact-era primary accounts (Roggeveen, González, Cook, La Pérouse) for Easter Island. The European categorical imposition is invisible because it is mediated through post-raid ethnographic compilations.

Step 2: **Post-raid elaboration**: Kirch relies on Métraux 1940 (~15 citations in Ch. 11) and Englert 1970 (~8 citations) for political organization claims. These post-slave-raid sources become the ethnographic baseline. Routledge 1919 is cited only for birdman (p. 277); her finding of "no chiefs" is absent.

Step 3: **Polynesian analogy imports chiefdom**: Kirch's APS inheritance argument (p. 281): "Colonizing Polynesians in every case carried with them concepts of pyramidal social structure." The chiefdom is assumed from the ancestral template, not demonstrated for Easter Island. Sahlins's redistribution gap (1954, p. 101) is not acknowledged.

Step 4: **Archaeological evidence read through chiefdom lens**: Ahu distribution interpreted as "segmentary pattern, with each *ahu* representing the religious focus of an ancestor-based kin group" (p. 271). Settlement patterns interpreted through ramage territorial model (p. 272). Yet the evidence Kirch presents (pp. 270-271) describes decentralized descent-group production, not centralized command.

Step 5: **Archaeological reading "confirms" framework**: Kirch's Easter Island chapter treats the chiefdom interpretation as established ("Easter Island political organization seems to have been dominated by a developed form of the Ancestral Polynesian segmentary ramage system," p. 272), citing the same ahu and moai evidence whose production he himself describes as decentralized and non-centralized (pp. 270-271). The circularity closes.
