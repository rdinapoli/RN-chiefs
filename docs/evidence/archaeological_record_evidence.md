# The Archaeological Record: Evidence Compilation

## Key Questions

1. What is the direct archaeological evidence for hereditary rank on Rapa Nui (differential mortuary treatment, elite residential compounds, prestige goods, evidence of tribute)?
2. What does the spatial distribution of ahu tell us about political territories vs. resource-centered organization (DiNapoli et al. 2019)?
3. What does the ahu construction chronology show about the tempo and character of monument production (DiNapoli et al. 2020)?
4. What does the quarry production evidence show about centralized vs. decentralized organization (Lipo et al. 2025)?
5. What does the moai transport evidence show (Hunt and Lipo 2011, Lipo et al. 2013)?
6. What do settlement patterns show about population distribution, internal differentiation, and change over time?
7. How do collective action frameworks generate different expectations from chiefly-command frameworks, and which better fits the record?

## Evidence by Category

### Moai Production and Transport

**Chiefdom prediction**: Centralized command over large labor forces required
**Evidence**:

Lipo et al. 2013 (own work; 62 road moai analyzed + walking experiment):
- Road moai differ systematically from ahu moai: wider bases, forward-leaning center of mass, no eye sockets (pp. 2861-2863). These differences are consistent with engineering for vertical transport and inconsistent with horizontal transport.
- Walking experiment: 18 people with three ropes moved a 4.35-ton scaled replica ~100 m in 40 minutes (p. 2865). This is "activities by small-scale social groups rather than the product of laborers unified under a powerful centralized chiefdom" (p. 2866).
- Concave road cross-sections rule out transverse rollers (p. 2860), severing the link between moai transport and deforestation.
- Fall patterns of road moai are statistically non-random: facedown on downhill slopes, on backs going uphill (p. 2861), consistent with vertical walking and inconsistent with horizontal transport.
- Scaling argument: larger moai proportionally easier to walk due to "bowling pin" shape (p. 2865-2866); severs the assumption that larger statues required proportionally more labor.
- No timber required for walking transport; eliminates the moai-transport-deforestation-collapse linkage that supports the ecocide/chiefdom narrative.

McCoy 1976 (south corner survey):
- Statues "represent deified ancestors" (p. 91), per ethnohistoric interpretation
- No direct archaeological evidence presented for how statues were transported or what social organization this required

**Assessment**: The chiefdom prediction (moai transport required large coordinated labor under chiefly command) is undermined by the walking hypothesis. The archaeological evidence from road moai (shapes, fall positions, road cross-sections) is consistent with small-team vertical transport and inconsistent with the large-scale horizontal transport that implied chiefly command. The walking method requires no timber, severing the deforestation link. However, the experiment used a scaled replica (4.35 tons vs. up to 80+ tons for the largest moai), and the inferential step from "small teams could do this" to "no chiefly command was needed" is plausible but not directly demonstrated.

### Ahu Placement and Distribution

**Chiefdom prediction**: Ahu represent chiefly territorial centers
**Evidence**:

DiNapoli et al. 2019 (own work; 93 image-ahu in eastern Rapa Nui):
- Ahu placement is best explained by proximity to freshwater sources (coastal groundwater seeps) and the coastline (BIC weight = 0.675 for the additive model; p. 15)
- Rock mulch garden locations do NOT explain ahu placement (p. 16), directly contradicting the claim that ahu mark elite agricultural territories (Stevenson et al.)
- Marine resource correlation is spurious; once freshwater and coastline are accounted for, marine access adds no explanatory power (p. 16)
- No second-order clustering or dispersion at distances under ~1500 m after accounting for freshwater/coastline intensity (Fig. 3B, p. 12). No competitive spacing dynamics between polities needed.
- Ahu are spatially offset from domestic activity areas (100-200 m seaward), positioned at freshwater access points rather than residential centers

McCoy 1976 (south corner survey, 43 ahu):
- Ahu were "always located seaward of houses and usually separated by an open space of 100 meters or more" (p. 109)
- Ahu distribution showed continuity in location through time based on masonry style differences (p. 95)
- McCoy noted that water was "the major limiting factor in the exploitation of uninhabited lands" (p. 142), anticipating the freshwater finding

Ayres 1973 (Tulane PhD; Tahai excavations under Mulloy; 402 pp):
- Ahu are kin-group religious structures: "Land ownership is important because the associated social unit would be the one whose membership was involved in constructing and maintaining ahu on their land" (p. 329)
- Ahu associated with specific lineages (ivi) within larger mata; villages located "directly behind the ahu" (p. 25)
- Four ahu types classified (following Métraux 1937): image ahu, semi-pyramidal, ahu poepoe, rectangular ahu avanga (pp. 43-46)
- Thomson counted 113 ahu, 555 statues (238 on ahu); Routledge counted ~100 image ahu, 231 statues (pp. 41-42)
- "A craft guild was responsible for carving" the standardized statues (p. 338)
- Regional quarry areas in early phases: different stone types (red scoria, Rano Raraku tuff, basalt) for different regions (p. 336)
- Large ahu construction attributed to "growing population" and kin-group labor, plus Goldman's slave labor hypothesis (p. 337); no redistribution mechanism
- Ahu considered "the equivalent of the marae found in other parts of Polynesia" (p. 43)
- Pre-chiefdom-model interpretation: ahu data coherently explained through descent group and ancestor veneration frameworks without chiefdom concepts

Mulloy & Figueroa 1966 (UNESCO heritage report, 111pp; ~350 ahu surveyed):
- "The size and ostentation of features of many ahu reflected the numbers and capacity of the kin groups that built them" (p. 16)
- Ahu variation attributed to "individual preferences of kin groups and a sense of competition among them" (pp. 15-16)
- Settlement described as "relatively small independent or partially independent kin groups" (pp. 12-13)
- Typical settlement: "a segment of coast and its adjacent inland zone" (p. 15) with ahu near coast, hare paenga behind plaza, domestic sites extending inland
- ~350 ahu total, ~150 with 1-15 fallen statues; platforms over 150 m long, statues up to ~10 m
- Statues "said to have represented ancestors and to have possessed supernatural power" (p. 15)
- No chiefdom, hierarchy, or redistribution terminology in the entire 111-page report

**Assessment**: The chiefdom prediction (ahu as territorial markers of chiefly domains) is not supported by the spatial evidence. Ahu placement is parsimoniously explained by community-level resource access (freshwater) rather than by elite territorial control. Ayres's earlier interpretation of the same structures as kin-group ancestor veneration sites is consistent with this finding, as is Mulloy & Figueroa's attribution of ahu size to kin-group "numbers and capacity." This does not disprove hierarchy, but it removes the spatial evidence that has been cited in its support.

### Ahu Construction Chronology

**Chiefdom prediction**: Temporal patterns reflecting political consolidation; cessation of construction marks chiefdom collapse
**Evidence**:

DiNapoli et al. 2020 (own work; Bayesian modeling of 11 ahu with 14C dates):
- Ahu construction began in early 14th to mid-15th centuries, later than previous estimates
- Steady construction tempo through European contact in 1722; no pre-contact cessation
- The tempo plot is "important falsifying evidence" against the collapse narrative
- Removes the chronological scaffolding for the "Ahu Moai" to "Huri Moai" phase transition (Kirch, Martinsson-Wallin)
- Ahu construction framed as "group-level activity" for "long term sustainability," not chiefly command
- Corroborated by contact-era observations: Roggeveen 1722 and González 1770 describe active ahu ritual use
- Limitation: 11/~150 ahu is a small sample

Mulrooney 2013 (island-wide radiocarbon reassessment):
- Stevenson's "Chiefdom Integration" phase (AD 1425-1680) built on uncritically assessed dates
- Southern Hemisphere calibration plateau (c. AD 1450-1650) creates artificial temporal gaps

**Assessment**: The chiefdom prediction (ahu construction as marker of political consolidation, with cessation reflecting collapse) is not supported. Construction continued at a steady rate through European contact. No temporal evidence for the kind of political disruption the chiefdom-to-warfare transition would produce.

### Quarry Production Organization

**Chiefdom prediction**: Centralized control over moai production at Rano Raraku
**Evidence**:

Lipo et al. 2025 (own work; spatial analysis of Rano Raraku quarry):
- 30 spatially discrete, functionally redundant quarrying foci identified
- Three distinct carving methods employed across the quarry, indicating technological autonomy not centralized standardization
- Physical constraints limited active carving teams to 4-6 people per moai
- No evidence of restricted quarry access; workshops bounded by natural features, not imposed barriers
- Standardized moai forms coexisted with decentralized production methods
- Pattern matches decentralized organization seen in settlement (Morrison 2012), transport (Lipo & Hunt 2005), lithic distribution (Lipo et al. 2010)

**Assessment**: The chiefdom prediction (centralized chiefly control over the most important shared resource) is not supported. The quarry evidence shows decentralized, small-team production with multiple independent workshops. This is the strongest direct evidence against chiefly command of monument production, since it applies to the single most critical shared resource on the island.

### Settlement Patterns

**Chiefdom prediction**: Territorial hierarchy under chiefly control
**Evidence**:

McCoy 1976 (south corner survey, 1,738 sites in 1,973 hectares):
- Villages were "partially dispersed settlements" with a "central core of communal houses, communal structures, and an *ahu*, with more scattered family residences further inland" (p. 109)
- Site density decreased with distance from shoreline; sharp boundary between high and low density zones at 1,000-1,500 m from coast (p. 135)
- Density correlated with "accessibility to drinking water, marine resources, and the community center" (p. 135), not with chiefly territorial organization
- McCoy superimposed Routledge/Métraux tribal boundaries on density data and found some correlations (p. 139), but these boundaries are ethnographic constructs, not archaeologically derived
- Settlement spacing and village size were "set by requirements of social structure—kin groupings and marriage patterns" and "ecological factors such as availability of land and drinking water" (p. 122), not by chiefly command
- "There is no recognizable plan to the layout of the three components. The amorphous character of settlements and degree of clustering conforms to the general Polynesian pattern summarized by Green" (p. 152)
- McCoy explicitly noted that Sahlins (1958) treated Easter Island's dispersed settlement as an exception to, not confirmation of, his chiefdom model (p. 152)

**Assessment**: McCoy's settlement data shows dispersed, ahu-centered communities organized around marine resources and freshwater access. The pattern is compatible with, and perhaps better explained by, resource-centered collective organization than by chiefly territorial hierarchy. McCoy himself did not interpret the pattern as evidence for a chiefdom.

Stevenson 1984 (southern coastal survey, 65 ceremonial + 184 residential sites):
- Four residential site types identified plus elite dwellings (hare paenga clusters up to 10 houses)
- Settlement concentrated within 1 km of coast throughout all phases
- Population growth from sparse occupation (pre-1300 AD) to peak at Phase 9-10 (~1500-1700 AD), then decline
- "four politically autonomous groups" identified by distribution of multiple lineage centers (p. 163)
- No competitive spacing between groups demonstrated; boundaries "not possible to draw" (p. 162)
- Corporate group strength "relatively weak" for majority of period; "Descent groups most likely exercised a significant amount of autonomy" (p. 176)
- All site types contemporaneous, used continuously until depopulation
- Non-uniform distribution of garden enclosures within 1 km of coast suggests localized agricultural adaptation

### Subsistence and Surplus Mobilization

**Chiefdom prediction**: Agricultural surplus mobilized under chiefly direction; extensive agricultural hinterlands under elite territorial control
**Evidence**:

Davis et al. 2024 (satellite remote sensing of rock gardens):
- Rock garden extent approximately one-fifth of prior estimates (Ladefoged et al., Stevenson et al.)
- Maximum population supportable by rock gardening alone: ~3,900, not ~17,000
- Carrying capacity of ~3,000 matches contact-era population estimates, eliminating supposed Malthusian overshoot
- Rock gardens concentrated in coastal zones rather than spread across the interior
- Weakens models of chiefly territorial control over extensive agricultural hinterlands

McCoy 1976:
- "Production of individual households was supervised by household heads rather than directly through chiefs" (p. 148, citing Sahlins 1958)
- Gardens and chicken houses were household-level production features, not centrally managed
- Household was "the smallest unit of production and consumption" (p. 148)

**Assessment**: The chiefdom prediction (elite mobilization of agricultural surplus) lacks supporting evidence. Rock gardens were smaller and more coastal than previously estimated, household production was decentralized, and the demographic base was insufficient for the Malthusian overshoot that the ecocide/chiefdom narrative requires.

### Population and Sustainability

**Chiefdom prediction (collapse variant)**: Powerful chiefdom overexploited resources; inland agricultural abandonment after breakdown of chiefly economy
**Evidence**:

Mulrooney 2013 (island-wide radiocarbon reassessment, 428 dates):
- Central finding: settlement continuity, not collapse. "Inland agricultural activity continues right through the period when the collapse scenario predicts it should decline or cease" (claims file)
- The prediction that inland agricultural areas would be abandoned after "breakdown of the chiefly economy" is empirically tested and fails
- Stevenson's "Chiefdom Integration" phase (AD 1425-1680) was built on uncritically assessed chronometric data
- Apparent temporal gaps previously interpreted as cultural disruption may be calibration curve artifacts (Southern Hemisphere calibration plateau c. AD 1450-1650)
- Reliability classification of existing dates reveals that many dates used to support the collapse chronology fail quality assessment
- Data from far upland region (>3.5 km inland) remain too thin to distinguish abandonment from insufficient sampling

McCoy 1976:
- Estimated maximum population of 7,000+ based on carrying capacity and 18th-19th century estimates (p. 141)
- Postulated environmental deterioration from ~AD 1500-1600 driving warfare and settlement disruption (p. 145)
- However, his developmental model was "a highly conjectural model of settlement pattern changes stemming from population expansion" (p. 148)

Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 (ancient DNA, *Nature*):
- Genomic data reject a 1600s population collapse through multiple independent analytical approaches (HapNe-LD, coalescent simulations, SROH distributions)
- Population grew steadily from founding bottleneck through European contact
- ROH pattern (many short segments, no long ones) indicates a chronically small population, not a recently crashed one; genetic signature of a population always in the low thousands
- Contact-era estimates of 1,500-3,000 are consistent with normal growth from small founding population; no collapse needed to explain them
- Late colonization (~1200-1250 CE) confirmed by independent genetic evidence

Davis et al. 2024:
- Carrying capacity of ~3,000 matches contact-era estimates; no overshoot required

**Assessment**: The chiefdom-collapse prediction (powerful chiefdom overexploited resources, leading to collapse) is not supported. Multiple independent lines of evidence (radiocarbon settlement data, ancient DNA, remote-sensed agricultural extent) converge on the same conclusion: the population was chronically small, grew steadily, did not crash before European contact, and the demographic base was insufficient for the Malthusian dynamics the ecocide narrative requires.
