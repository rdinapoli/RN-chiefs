# Post-Slave-Raid Ethnographic Sources: Evidence Compilation

## Key Questions

1. When were Thompson (1891), Routledge (1919), Metraux (1940), and Englert recorded relative to the 1862-1863 slave raids and subsequent epidemics?
2. What is known about the demographic recovery process and the surviving population's composition and knowledge?
3. What specifically do these sources say about hereditary rank, the ariki mau, mata group organization, and chiefly authority? How consistent are they with each other?
4. Which chiefdom-supporting claims in the archaeological literature depend primarily on these sources?
5. What do these sources describe that is consistent with post-collapse political reorganization rather than pre-contact tradition?

## The Demographic Catastrophe (1862-1877)

The trajectory of population collapse is now documented by four sequential witnesses:

| Year | Observer | Population Estimate | Context |
|------|----------|-------------------|---------|
| pre-1862 | (multiple estimates) | ~3,000-5,000 | Pre-raid; Roussel estimates ~5,000 maximum based on settlement distribution |
| 1864 | Eyraud | ~1,200 | 1 year post-raids; six Rapanui repatriated from Peru had just returned |
| 1869 | Roussel | ~650 | Census figure; 3-6 years post-raids; tuberculosis, continued decline |
| 1872 | Loti | ~100 | 10 years post-raids; concentrated at Hanga Roa; rest of island deserted |
| 1877 | Pinart | 111 (26 women) | 14 years post-raids; Dutrou-Bornier had removed most survivors to Tahiti |
| 1882 | Geiseler | 150 (67 men, 39 women, 44 children) | 20 years post-raids; includes ~20 Tahitians; Salmon as translator |
| 1886 | Thomson | 155 (68 men, 43 women, 44 children) | 24 years post-raids; Salmon again as translator |
| 1914-15 | Routledge | ~250 | 52 years post-raids; first researcher independent of Salmon |
| 1900 | Father Butaye | 231 (213 natives) | Recovery beginning under Chilean annexation |

This means that between 1862 and 1877, the population declined from ~3,000 to 111, a reduction of approximately 96%. The decline was not a single event but a cascading catastrophe: slave raids (1862-1863), repatriation-borne smallpox (1863-1864), continued epidemic disease (tuberculosis), warfare among survivors (documented by Eyraud and Roussel), and Dutrou-Bornier's removal of population to Tahiti (1870s).

**Critical implication**: By the time Thompson (1891) and Routledge (1919) recorded their ethnographic accounts, the population had partially recovered from a bottleneck of ~111 people. The "traditions" they recorded had survived through this bottleneck, filtered through survivors who were predominantly non-elite (the ariki and priests were specifically targeted by the slavers), traumatized, converted to Christianity, and living under colonial administration.

## Evidence from Immediate Post-Raid Sources (1864-1877)

### Eyraud 1864

**Recorded**: 1 year after slave raids
**Population context**: ~1,200; "a time of considerable anarchy" (Lee, Introduction)
**Duration on island**: 9 months (longest of four observers)

**Political organization observed**: Eyraud describes authority as purely personal: "It is hard for me to characterize the authority of chiefs on the island. I don't even know the basis for such authority. It seems that authority depended only on the influence that some natives were able to exert over their neighbors and that the people gradually grew accustomed to such displays of superiority" (p. 14). His "chief" Torometi ruled by coercion and theft, not institutional authority, and was collectively punished by community action (hut-burning).

**Evidence for collective action**: The boat-building episode documents voluntary collective contribution without chiefly command: "I had noted this custom of making everyone contribute to a project that was considered important. Nobody would think of not participating" (p. 27).

**Religion**: "Religion seems to occupy a very minor place in their lives... I never witnessed any act that was really indicative of a religious cult" (p. 20). Statuettes in homes treated with indifference.

**Evidential type**: Almost entirely direct observation (a). Eyraud was too new and linguistically limited to collect traditions.

### Roussel 1869

**Recorded**: 3-6 years after slave raids (notes written 1869)
**Population context**: ~650
**Duration on island**: 1866-1873 (7 years)

**Political organization described**: Most ethnographically detailed early post-raid account. Key claims:

1. **Four social classes**: ariki (kings), ivi atua (priests), matatoa (warriors), kio (farmers/servants). Whether these were rigid castes or contextual roles cannot be determined.

2. **King list**: 22 generations from Hotu Matua to Gregorio. This list DISAGREES with other recorded lists (Thomson, Jaussen, Métraux) in both names and order. Only 12 of 23 names overlap with Thomson; only 9 with Jaussen. "Few are given in the same order" (endnote 7). This inconsistency across informants recorded within a 25-year window (1869-1891) undermines the lists' reliability as historical records.

3. **Royal authority "absent or totally unrecognized"**: Roussel's own account contains an internal contradiction. He describes kings who "exercised absolute power" AND admits their authority "had passed completely into the hands of the matatoa" (p. 41). Both cannot describe the same functioning system. The "absolute power" is reported tradition; the matatoa dominance appears to describe what Roussel actually observed.

4. **First-fruits ceremony**: Roussel directly witnessed a procession bringing first yams to the child Gregorio, "the one and only true chief" and last surviving royal line member (pp. 39-40). This is the strongest direct evidence for chiefly prerogatives. However, Gregorio was a child in a devastated society of 650 people; the ceremony may have been performative or residual.

5. **Tangata manu**: Roussel describes the birdman competition as a mechanism for transferring chiefly authority to the matatoa (p. 47). This brief description has been cited as evidence for the tangata manu institution but provides minimal detail.

**Property**: Land ownership existed but was "in an almost inextricable tangle" due to displacement from wars and slave raids (p. 50). Plantations held communally within families. Rahui stones marked exclusive fishing rights. This describes a property system, not a redistributive chiefdom economy.

**Evidential type**: Mix of direct observation (a) and reported tradition (b), often not clearly distinguished. The king list, royal powers, and origin story are all reported tradition from survivors.

### Loti 1872

**Recorded**: 10 years after slave raids
**Population context**: ~100 people, concentrated at Hanga Roa
**Duration on island**: 5 days

**Political organization observed**: Only an informal "old chief" mentioned; no political structure described. Dutrou-Bornier's commercial operation dominates the political landscape. Most of the island is deserted.

**Evidential type**: Direct observation (a) of extreme demographic collapse. Loti is a literary observer, not an ethnographer. His value is in documenting conditions, not recording traditions.

### Pinart 1877

**Recorded**: 14 years after slave raids
**Population context**: 111 people (26 women); demographic nadir
**Duration on island**: 6 days

**Political organization observed**: Only colonial authority (Dutrou-Bornier's widow as "regent"). A trained ethnographer who records NO traditional political structure, NO social classes, NO chiefs. Islanders had "retained no memory at all" of earlier inhabitants (pp. 122-123). Rongorongo tablets used as fishing line spools.

**Evidential type**: Direct observation (a) by a trained scientist. The complete silence on traditional political structure from a professional anthropologist is itself significant evidence of how thoroughly traditional knowledge had been lost by 1877.

### Geiseler 1883 [PROCESSED]

**Recorded**: 20 years after slave raids (visit September 19-23, 1882; published 1883)
**Population context**: 150 people (67 men, 39 women, 44 children; ~20 Tahitians among them); recovered from nadir of ~111 in 1877
**Duration on island**: 4 days

**Political organization observed**: Total political collapse. "A king does not exist, since the last one died in the Peruvian slavery" (p. 5). "Since that time every man is his own master, who recognizes no authority over himself and seeks his own justice" (p. 22-23). "Old chief" title used but carries no actual power or respect; Geiseler notes these so-titled people "appear to be nothing less than 'chiefs'" and are compared unfavorably to Samoan chiefs (p. 22-23). "Complete dissolution of all political community" (p. 23). After the collapse, "the entire organization rests solely on the family" (p. 42). Chiefs "have no power" and cannot even punish theft (pp. 28-29).

Former regime described as "almost despotic" (faft befpotifdjes Regiment) with succession going to "the brother" (not the son), contradicting both Thomson (primogeniture) and Roussel (abdication when son marries) (p. 42). Kings formerly served as priests; "they have no priests and are said never to have had them" (p. 33).

**CRITICAL FINDING: Salmon as shared intermediary.** Alexander P. Salmon, a half-blood Tahitian related to the Tahitian royal family, served as translator and cultural broker for Geiseler in 1882 AND for Thomson in 1886. All traditions recorded in both accounts were filtered through the same individual. The convergence between Geiseler's and Thomson's accounts of "despotic" kingship may reflect Salmon's interpretive framework rather than independent traditions. Salmon spoke Rapanui fluently (p. 24), had strong opinions about Polynesian origins (he favored a Tuamotu origin, p. 43-44), and was related to a royal family, all of which may have predisposed him to interpret Rapanui politics through a hierarchical lens.

**Evidential type**: Mix of (a) direct observation (political collapse, population, land ownership, tabu system) and (b) reported tradition (former regime, succession, idol-makers, origin stories), all filtered through Salmon. Based on the German original (Fraktur OCR); English translation (Ayres & Ayres 1995) not yet in repo.

### Thompson 1891 [PROCESSED]

**Recorded**: 24 years after slave raids (visit December 1886; published 1891)
**Population context**: 155 people (68 men, 43 women, 17 boys, 27 girls); recovered from nadir of ~111 in 1877
**Duration on island**: 12 days

**Political organization described**: Thomson's "Government" section (p. 472) describes an "arbitrary monarchy" with a sacred hereditary king and subordinate hereditary district chiefs. However, Thomson immediately qualifies this: "no tax was exacted of the people," "it does not appear that any great homage was paid the king," each clan was "independent of all the rest," and "the people avenged their own injuries" (pp. 472-473). The description is a European political category ("monarchy") applied to reported traditions about a system that lacked the structural features of centralized authority.

Key claims for political authority:

1. **King list of 57 names** from Hotu-Matua to Maurata (pp. 533-534). IRRECONCILABLE with Roussel's 22-generation list: only ~12 names overlap out of 23; few in same order. This disagreement between lists recorded 17 years apart from the same small population demonstrates that the king lists cannot be treated as historical records of a stable dynasty.

2. **Tangata manu**: "No especial authority was vested in him" (p. 483). The winner received food contributions and spiritual approval, NOT political power. This CONTRADICTS Roussel's description of the birdman exercising "the chief's authority." The king's only role was to start the race.

3. **No tax, no homage, independent clans**: Thomson's own qualifications negate the structural requirements of a chiefdom: no tribute system, no regular homage to the paramount, and clans operating independently in perpetual warfare.

4. **Image-makers exempt from other work**: One passage (p. 533) states fishermen were "taxed" to support image-makers. This contradicts Thomson's own statement about no taxes and, if accurate, describes a specific inter-group arrangement for craft production, not a general redistributive economy.

5. **Post-raid absence of authority**: "Since that time there has been no recognized authority among the natives; every man is his own master" (p. 473). This is what Thomson directly observed; whether it represents collapse of functioning authority or removal of a nominal figurehead is the interpretive question.

**Evidential type**: Almost entirely (b) reported tradition, filtered through Alexander P. Salmon as translator. Thomson's 12-day visit was predominantly devoted to archaeological survey; tradition-collection was confined to evenings. The session with Ure Vaeiko (the claimed tablet reader) involved coercion and alcohol. Thomson directly observed that Ure Vaeiko could not actually read the tablets (p. 516), demonstrating the complete destruction of the elite knowledge system by 1886.

### Routledge 1919 [PROCESSED]

**Recorded**: 52 years after slave raids (visit March 1914 to August 1915; published 1919)
**Population context**: ~250 people; recovered from nadir of ~111 in 1877
**Duration on island**: 17 months (Mana Expedition)

**Political organization described**: Routledge's account is paradoxical: it simultaneously provides the most explicit denial of centralized authority and the most detailed description of the one exception. "With the exception of the Miru, of which more will be said, there were no chiefs nor any form of government; any man who was expert in war became a leader" (p. 224). Only the Miru clan (one of ten) had a chief (ariki-mau). This ariki-mau was explicitly NOT a political leader: "Ngaara held official position for the whole island, but he was neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest; he can best be described as the custodian of certain customs and traditions" (p. 242).

Key claims for political authority:

1. **Ariki-mau's actual functions**: Supernatural food-increase magic (especially chickens), oversight of rongorongo knowledge (annual assembly at Anakena where he sat on tablet seats, addressed scholars, gave each a chicken), rain prayers (delegated to ariki-paka), house inauguration ceremonies, receiving the annual pua offering (visitors "retired backwards," p. 243). All ceremonial, ritualistic, or supernatural, not political.

2. **Ariki-mau's lack of coercive power**: Could not compel his own servant to return a stolen tablet, resorting to attempted bribery and failing (p. 249). Captured by the Ngaure clan and held captive with his son and grandson for five years at Akahanga; required alliance with Tupahotu to be rescued (p. 246). Died at his daughter's home in Marama territory, not at Anakena (p. 246).

3. **Tangata manu as rotating competitive institution**: The Ao (ascendant group) rotated among clans. "Only those who belonged to the clan in the ascendancy for the time being could enter on the quest" (p. 258). The tangata manu's benefits: food gifts were "the sole practical advantage of victory; those who did not contribute were apt to have their houses burnt" (p. 264). Winner spent five months in strict taboo "sleeping all day," then "returned to ordinary life" after one year (pp. 264-265).

4. **Ariki-mau absent from the bird cult**: "it is noteworthy that he himself never appeared at Orongo" (p. 260). The hereditary office and the rotating competitive institution were separate, potentially competing spheres of authority.

5. **Matatoa/matakio as rotating statuses, not permanent classes**: "The men of the ascendant clan are also often spoken of as the Mata-toa, or warriors, the other clans being the Mata-kio, or servants" (p. 259, footnote). This rotated with the Ao, contradicting Roussel's permanent four-class hierarchy.

6. **No king list provided.** Routledge collected "all the lists" but found fundamental confusion: "The sons of Hotu-matua are not known exactly" (pp. 281-282). She does not reproduce a numbered king list.

7. **No routine tribute, redistribution, or taxation** described anywhere. The closest approach is the rongorongo assembly (p. 245), where "the inhabitants of the neighbouring districts brought offerings of food to Ngaara, that he should be able to supply the multitude," and the ariki gave each rongorongo man a chicken (p. 246). This food mobilization was specific to the annual rongorongo gathering, not routine economic redistribution. The annual pua offering (p. 243) involved ritual deference, not material tribute.

8. **No kio (servant/slave) class mentioned.** No succession rules elaborated beyond "resign in favour of his son" when "old and feeble" (p. 241). No priests (p. 239). No formal worship or sacrifice (p. 236).

**CRITICAL FINDING: First account independent of Salmon.** Routledge worked directly with Rapanui informants in their own language. She explicitly questioned Thomson's data by noting Salmon "failed to understand" the surviving customs (p. 209). Divergences from Thomson and Geiseler on political organization may reflect Salmon's framework in the earlier accounts.

**CRITICAL FINDING: Marriage unrestricted.** "No group restrictions on marriage, which took place indiscriminately between members of the same or of different clans" (p. 221). An ordinary Ureohei woman (Viriamo) married into the Miru clan at Anakena with no described restrictions (p. 227-228). Inconsistent with a hierarchical society maintaining rank through marriage rules.

**Evidential type**: Mix of (a) direct observation (archaeological survey, informant behavior, language analysis), (b) reported tradition from named informants of multiple clans (Miru, Tupahotu, Marama, Ureohei), and (c) Routledge's own inference. The multi-clan informant base and cross-examination methodology make this the most carefully collected post-raid ethnographic data, but all political organization claims remain reported tradition from a post-catastrophe population.

### Métraux 1937 [PROCESSED]

**Recorded**: 72 years after slave raids (fieldwork 1934; published 1937)
**Population context**: ~450-500 people; recovered from nadir of ~111 in 1877
**Duration on island**: Several months (Franco-Belgian expedition, 1934)

**Political organization described**: Métraux's article "The Kings of Easter Island" is focused specifically on the ariki-mau institution. His central conclusion: "Nothing is known about the activities and duties of the king to suggest any great political power. He was not a ruler in the common sense of the word" (p. 58). His extended formulation: "The *ariki-mau* was respected because of his pedigree which related him with the gods and because of the sacredness of his person, but he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief. He transmitted the *mana* of the god, and it was necessary for the welfare of the island to keep him, but beyond this usefulness he was not allowed to enjoy any real power other than this more or less strictly religious and aristocratic prestige" (p. 60). His final summary: "His political power was little" (p. 61).

Key claims for political authority:

1. **Ariki-mau as sacred figure, not political leader**: Métraux endorses Routledge's characterization of the ariki-mau as "neither a leader in war nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest" (p. 58). He describes extensive supernatural functions (fertility chant, food-increase magic, harvest tapu, mana legends) while concluding these constitute "strictly religious and aristocratic prestige," not political power.

2. **More prerogatives described, same conclusion**: Métraux records more chiefly functions than Routledge: first-fruits ceremony (Roussel eyewitness), harvest tapu, tuna distribution to tangata-honui, tūra/haka-pāpa servant classes (themselves ariki/noblemen), tattooing inspection, twin-naming, full fertility chant retranslated from Thompson. Despite this richer picture, he reaches the same conclusion about no political authority. This demonstrates the argument is based on the CHARACTER of the evidence (supernatural/ceremonial), not its absence.

3. **Food flowing through the king: elite-to-elite circulation**: Three new pieces of evidence for food flowing through the ariki-mau: (a) vaka-vaero tuna presented to king, who distributed to tangata-honui (important old men) (p. 50); (b) first tuna after tapu-lifting tasted by king, rest given to tangata-honui (p. 50); (c) first-fruits ceremony with harvest tapu controlled by king (Roussel eyewitness, pp. 54-55). The pattern is fishermen/farmers → king → tangata-honui (elite old men), not king → general population. This is elite-to-elite circulation through the sacred office, not the downward redistribution chiefdom models require.

4. **Roussel's "absolute power" dismissed as "entirely gratuitous assumption"**: Métraux accepts Roussel's observation that royal authority was "nil and entirely disregarded" in historical times but explicitly rejects Roussel's projection of ancient absolute power as unsupported (p. 58). This is a trained Polynesian ethnographer making a methodological judgment about the key source for chiefly authority.

5. **Critical engagement with Thomson**: Métraux quotes Thomson's "arbitrary monarchy" passage at length, then reads Thomson's own qualifications (no tax, no great homage, independent clans) as supporting the limited-power interpretation (p. 59). He notes there is "no way to check" Thomson's assertion of real influence.

6. **Geiseler dismissed**: "not a very reliable source" who is "certainly mistaken" about brother succession (pp. 52, 58-59).

7. **Honga lineage traced to Tangaroa and Rongo**: Victoria Rapahango told Métraux that kings "always belonged to the Honga, a lineage or sub-group of the Miru" (p. 44), whose first ancestors were Tangaroa and Rongo, probably the Polynesian gods. This is standard divine-chief genealogy legitimating supernatural authority, not political power.

8. **Tangata manu not a secular king**: Métraux rejects Williamson's hypothesis that the tangata-manu was a "secular king" parallel to the ariki-mau, characterizing the tangata-manu as "a man incarnating provisionally the god Makemake and entitled to rights of an aggressive nature" (p. 60).

**CRITICAL FINDING: Second independent confirmation of no political power.** Métraux is the second trained ethnographer (after Routledge) to conclude independently that the ariki-mau had no political authority. The convergence between Routledge (1914-1915, direct fieldwork) and Métraux (1934, partly overlapping informants but independent analysis of Roussel's texts) strengthens both accounts. However, the shared reliance on Tepano means the convergence is partially, though not entirely, attributable to a single informant's consistent narrative.

**CRITICAL FINDING: Roussel's "entirely gratuitous assumption."** Métraux's explicit dismissal of Roussel's claim of ancient absolute power as an "entirely gratuitous assumption" (p. 58) directly addresses the inferential move by which later scholars projected political power backward into Rapa Nui's past. If the ethnographer who "best understood the true nature of kingship" (Métraux's assessment of Roussel, p. 43) offered an unsupported assumption about ancient power, the entire foundation for the chiefdom attribution is weakened.

**Evidential type**: Mix of (b) reported tradition from named informants (Tepano, Victoria Rapahango) and (c) Métraux's synthesis and critical analysis. The first-fruits ceremony is uniquely valuable as Roussel's direct observation (a). The legends (Rokoroko-he-tau, Tu-ki-haka-he-vari) are traditional narratives collected by Métraux. The fertility chant is a retranslation of Thompson's garbled original, reconstructed with Tepano's help.

## Later Sources

### Métraux 1940 [PROCESSED]

**Recorded**: 72 years after slave raids (fieldwork 1934; published 1940)
**Population context**: ~450-500 people; recovered from nadir of ~111 in 1877
**Duration on island**: Several months (Franco-Belgian expedition, 1934)

**Political organization described**: The comprehensive monograph expands the 1937 JPS article while confirming every conclusion. Métraux's core assessment: "The ariki-mau was respected because of his mana, his influence over nature, and the magic connected with his person, but he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief. As a direct descendant of the first king who settled on the island and thus of the gods, the ariki-mau had a status superior to that of other chiefs or heads of tribes, but he probably had little authority over them" (p. 136). His definitive summary: "The king was the man with the most aristocratic pedigree and the most exalted social position on the island. His person was overflowing with mana and his sacredness caused him to be feared and respected. His function in society was to insure through his very being the abundance of crops and the fertility of the ground and to exercise his influence on animal life" (p. 136). No mention of governance, administration, tribute, labor mobilization, or judicial authority.

Key claims for political authority:

1. **King list comparison (Table 2)**: Side-by-side comparison of Roussel (23 names), Jaussen (32 names), Thomson (57 names), and Métraux (30 names). Only **7 names common to all four lists**. Names that recur are primarily tribal/geographic eponyms (Marama, Raa, Hotu-iti, Tuu), not historical individuals. Priests/experts (tuhunga series) and mythological figures (Tiki-te-hatu, Te Riri-katea) appear interpolated. Only three individuals have independent historical corroboration: Hotu-matua, Nga-ara, Maurata (pp. 88-93).

2. **Roussel's four-class system "only superficially exact"**: "There were four grades or classes in the Easter Island society: ariki (noblemen), ivi-atua (priests), matatoa (warriors), and kio (servants or farmers). This schematic outline made by Roussel is only superficially exact. It does not indicate the actual position of the aristocracy and of the commoners and does not include the specialized groups like those of the experts (maori)" (p. 135).

3. **Matatoa as "actual rulers"**: "Legends and missionary accounts fully agree that the matatoa were not only the leaders in war but were also the actual rulers of the tribes." However, "there is no specific record that a matatoa ever ruled over the whole island" (pp. 138-139). Matatoa were "nearly, if not exactly, professional warriors" who "probably did no work" (p. 138).

4. **Kio as war captives**: "The kio were defeated people who were obliged to serve their conquerors or to pay tribute to them with the produce of their lands... All became slaves and had to plant a certain portion of land for their respective masters" (pp. 139-140). This describes a consequence of inter-clan warfare, not a hereditary caste.

5. **Clan territories diffuse**: Some clans (Hamea, Raa) had no distinct territory but lived within Miru lands. Others (Tupa-hotu, Ureohei, Koro-orongo) "had no districts of their own but were more or less scattered in the eastern part of the island" (pp. 121-125). This territorial picture is considerably more diffuse than a chiefdom model predicts.

6. **Marriage restrictions minimal**: "There were no marriage restrictions among tribes, save those of the tumu" (p. 123). One persistent tradition: improper for an outsider to marry a Miru woman. Minimal marriage restrictions are inconsistent with a ranked hierarchical society.

7. **Stone image significance unknown**: "The true significance of stone statues is not known. There is no direct evidence that they were ever worshipped" (p. 306). Métraux identifies "tribal pride and competitive instinct" as likely motivations (p. 307), consistent with competitive signaling among autonomous groups.

8. **Bird cult details**: Tangata manu was "elected for a year, without any lack of regard for the king who reigned by right of primogeniture" (p. 337). Privileges limited to marauding, food extraction, and house-burning threats. The tangata manu designated human sacrifices, "which caused a war among the tribes every year" (p. 337). Métraux rejects the hypothesis of a secular kingship parallel to the ariki-mau.

9. **Religion collapsed before missionaries**: "The downfall of the religion took place long before the arrival of the missionaries" (Roussel, quoted p. 309). Whether priests even existed as a class is disputed: Geiseler and Routledge deny it; Thomson reduces them to "wizards"; only Roussel affirms their status.

10. **Capture of ariki-mau confirmed**: Same incident as 1937 article: "The Ngaure tribe defeated the Miru whom they took as slaves (mata-kio). The king, his son Kamakoi, and his grandson Maurata were held in captivity" (p. 136).

**CRITICAL FINDING: King list comparison (Table 2).** The side-by-side comparison of four independently recorded king lists provides definitive evidence that these lists cannot sustain the inferential weight chiefdom models place on them. Only 7 of dozens of names appear in all four lists, and these are predominantly tribal eponyms, not historical individuals. The "dynastic succession" invoked by chiefdom models is an artifact of conflated tribal names, interpolated priests, and mythological figures.

**CRITICAL FINDING: Social class critique.** Métraux's assessment that Roussel's four-class hierarchy is "only superficially exact" undermines the use of this hierarchy as evidence for a ranked chiefdom. The kio were war captives, the matatoa were the actual rulers (not administrators in a chiefly hierarchy), and the ivi-atua's very existence is disputed among sources.

**CRITICAL FINDING: Stone images not worshiped.** The ethnographer who produced the most comprehensive study of Easter Island material culture concludes there is "no direct evidence" of statue worship and attributes monument construction to "tribal pride and competitive instinct," consistent with collective action/competitive signaling models.

**Evidential type**: Mix of (b) reported tradition from named informants (Tepano, Victoria Rapahango) and (c) Métraux's comprehensive synthesis across all available sources. The monograph represents the fullest treatment of Easter Island social organization available from the ethnographic era.

### Métraux 1957 [PROCESSED]

**Recorded**: 72 years after slave raids (same 1934 fieldwork as the 1940 monograph; published 1957)
**Population context**: Same data as 1940; popular synthesis
**Type**: Popular-audience book based on the 1940 monograph

**Political organization described**: The popular synthesis produces Métraux's clearest formulations, which are if anything more skeptical of chiefly authority than the technical monograph.

Key claims for political authority:

1. **"Political authority not clear"**: "The political authority of the ariki-mau is not clear. On this point we are reduced to conjecture. Although he was the civil leader of the Miru, he was not regarded by the other tribes as more than an extremely sacred personage" (p. 93). More explicit than the 1940 formulation.

2. **Political power with the matatoa**: "Political power seems to have fallen into the hands of the mata-to'a, who formed an aristocracy of happy warriors" (p. 93). "The political position of the matato'a on Easter Island was much stronger than that of the warriors in the rest of Polynesia, where they were subordinate to the ariki" (p. 97). The caudillo analogy is new: "Like the South American caudillo, he was both a civil and military leader" (p. 96).

3. **Statue production as competitive feast sponsorship**: "The heads of lineages who wished to raise a new bust on their ahu undoubtedly organized feasts... The guests harnessed themselves to the statue... A half-serious, half-humorous rivalry turned the undertaking into an immense pleasure party" (pp. 164-165). Also: "The chiefs, who had vast resources at their disposal, employed them in maintaining and paying groups of craftsmen who carved statues to ornament the mausoleums belonging to the various lineages" (pp. 159-160). The "chiefs" are plural lineage heads, not a paramount, and the monuments belong to "the various lineages."

4. **Statues as profane objects without ritual**: "The sanctity of the statues was, therefore, temporary and depended on rites... In the ordinary way, the image was simply an ornament, a lifeless block of stone... They became noa—profane" (pp. 159-160).

5. **Total cultural break in 1862**: "The year 1862 was decisive in the history of Easter Island. It saw the end of its civilization" (p. 47). "The religious and social system had been destroyed" (p. 48).

**CRITICAL FINDING: No evolution toward chiefdom model.** Between 1940 and 1957, Métraux's assessment moved toward greater skepticism about chiefly political authority. The 1957 formulations are more explicitly uncertain about the ariki-mau's political role and more emphatic about warrior dominance.

**CRITICAL FINDING: Feast-sponsorship model of monument production.** Métraux's 1957 reconstruction of statue transport as competitive feast sponsorship by lineage heads is directly relevant to the collective action alternative. This is the most detailed model of monument-production organization from any post-raid ethnographer, and it describes decentralized competition among autonomous lineages, not centralized command.

**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's mature synthesis for popular audience. Same underlying data as 1940.

## Political Offices Described

### Ariki / Ariki Mau

**Roussel 1869**: Kings (ariki) formed the highest class. Hotu Matua was first king; succession by abdication when son married. Kings "regarded as gods" who "exercised absolute power" with first-fruits privileges and tapu on head and hands. BUT: their authority had "passed completely into the hands of the matatoa." The child Gregorio was "the one and only true chief" and last of the royal line. [REPORTED TRADITION from survivors, except the observation of Gregorio]

**Eyraud 1864**: No ariki mentioned. Authority described as personal influence without institutional basis.

**Loti 1872**: No ariki mentioned. Only an informal "old chief."

**Pinart 1877**: No ariki mentioned. Only colonial "queen" (Dutrou-Bornier's daughter).

**Geiseler 1883**: No king exists (p. 5). Political authority completely dissolved. Former regime described as "almost despotic" with succession to "the brother" (not the son), contradicting both Thomson (eldest son) and Roussel (abdication when son marries) (p. 42). Kings formerly served as priests (p. 33). The term "ariki" appears only once, referring to a dead person in a special tomb (p. 10), not as a current political title. "Old chief" title carries no power or respect (pp. 22-23). ALL information filtered through Salmon. [REPORTED TRADITION from informants 20 years post-raids, through Salmon as translator]

**Thompson 1891**: Describes "arbitrary monarchy" with sacred hereditary king. King list of 57 names from Hotu-Matua to Maurata (pp. 533-534). King "reigned over the entire island" and could remove chiefs (p. 472). Kaitae identified as "nearest descendant of the last king," approximately 80 years old, holding no political authority (p. 461). BUT: "no tax was exacted of the people" and "it does not appear that any great homage was paid the king" (p. 473). Clans "independent of all the rest" (p. 472). King list irreconcilable with Roussel's 22-generation list. [REPORTED TRADITION from informants 24 years post-raids, through SAME Salmon as translator]

**Routledge 1919**: The most detailed and methodologically rigorous account. Only the Miru clan had a chief (ariki-mau): "The Miru were unique in other ways; they were the only group which had a headman or chief" (p. 241). That chief was explicitly NOT a political leader: "he was neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest; he can best be described as the custodian of certain customs and traditions" (p. 242). His functions were supernatural food-increase magic, rongorongo oversight, rain prayers (delegated), house inaugurations, and receiving the annual pua offering. He lacked coercive power: unable to recover stolen property from his own servant (p. 249), captured and enslaved for five years (p. 246). Hereditary within the Miru, with the ariki-mau required to marry within his own clan (p. 241). "It was customary when he was old and feeble that he should resign in favour of his son" (p. 241). [REPORTED TRADITION from multiple named informants 52 years post-raids, working directly in native language without Salmon]

**Métraux 1937**: The most analytical assessment of ariki-mau authority. Métraux describes more chiefly functions than any predecessor (first-fruits, harvest tapus, tūra/haka-pāpa servant classes, tuna distribution to tangata-honui, tattooing inspection, twin-naming, fertility chant) while concluding: "he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (p. 60) and "His political power was little" (p. 61). Critically, he dismisses Roussel's claim of ancient absolute power as "an entirely gratuitous assumption" (p. 58) while accepting Roussel's observation that authority was "nil and entirely disregarded" in historical times. He endorses Routledge's characterization ("neither a leader in war nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest"), engages directly with Thomson's "arbitrary monarchy" passage and reads Thomson's own qualifications as supporting limited power, and dismisses Geiseler as "not a very reliable source" (p. 58). New information from Victoria Rapahango: kings "always belonged to the Honga, a lineage or sub-group of the Miru" traced to Tangaroa and Rongo (p. 44). Servants (tūra and haka-pāpa) were themselves "noblemen (*ariki*)" (p. 49), describing an elite retinue, not a commoner labor force. [REPORTED TRADITION from informants 72 years post-raids, including Tepano (shared with Routledge) and Victoria Rapahango; independent analysis of Roussel's published texts]

**Métraux 1940**: The comprehensive monograph confirms the 1937 article in every particular while adding detail. "The ariki-mau was respected because of his mana, his influence over nature, and the magic connected with his person, but he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (p. 136). Métraux's definitive summary: "The king was the man with the most aristocratic pedigree and the most exalted social position on the island. His person was overflowing with mana... His function in society was to insure through his very being the abundance of crops and the fertility of the ground and to exercise his influence on animal life. Certain religious activities were derived from his sacredness and he held supervisory control over various practices connected with religion" (p. 136). No political, administrative, or judicial functions mentioned. He "did not interfere in the intertribal wars" and "may occasionally have acted as mediator in quarrels." The Ngaure capture incident confirmed (p. 136). King list comparison (Table 2, pp. 88-93) shows only 7 names common to all four independently recorded lists, with shared names predominantly tribal/geographic eponyms. [REPORTED TRADITION from informants 72 years post-raids]

**Assessment**: The ariki institution is described by Roussel, Geiseler (via Salmon), Thomson (via the same Salmon), Routledge (independently, without Salmon), Métraux 1937, and now Métraux 1940 (the comprehensive monograph confirming the 1937 conclusions with fuller evidence). The accounts diverge on the character of the office but converge on a critical point. Thomson and Geiseler (both through Salmon) describe "monarchy" and "despotic" authority. Roussel describes kings with "absolute power" but admits that power had "passed completely into the hands of the matatoa." Routledge, the most thorough researcher working independently, finds: (a) only one of ten clans had a chief; (b) that chief was explicitly not a leader, judge, or priest; (c) that chief could be captured and enslaved. Métraux, the second independent researcher, describes more prerogatives than anyone else yet reaches the same conclusion as Routledge about the absence of political power. The convergence between Geiseler and Thomson, both intermediated through Salmon, raises the question of whether the "despotic" picture reflects Salmon's framework rather than independent traditions. The convergence between Routledge and Métraux on limited power is partially attributable to their shared reliance on Tepano but is strengthened by Métraux's independent analysis of Roussel's texts and additional informants. Métraux's explicit dismissal of Roussel's "absolute power" claim as "entirely gratuitous" directly addresses the interpretive move by which that claim has been projected backward as evidence for a pre-contact chiefdom.

The succession rule disagreement is now a five-source problem: Roussel says abdication when son marries; Thomson says eldest son; Geiseler says brother; Routledge says resign when "old and feeble" in favor of son; Métraux (via Tepano and Roussel) says resign when son marries, confirming Roussel and partially aligning with Routledge and Thomson on the "son" element. Geiseler remains the outlier with brother succession, which Métraux calls "certainly mistaken" (p. 52).

### Tangata Manu

**Roussel 1869**: Brief mention of birdman competition as mechanism for matatoa authority (p. 47). "Luck alone, the discovery of a few eggs of a seabird, was all that was needed to transfer the chief's authority to the happy nest-robbers."

**Other immediate post-raid sources**: Not mentioned by Eyraud, Loti, or Pinart.

**Thompson 1891**: Most detailed early description of the bird-egg ceremony (pp. 482-483). Explicitly states "No especial authority was vested in him." Winner received food contributions and spiritual approval from Meke-Meke, not political power. The king's role was to pronounce the word "go" to start the race; he was "about the only able-bodied man on the island who did not participate" (p. 483).

**Routledge 1919**: The most detailed account of the tangata manu. The Ao (ascendant group) system rotated clan dominance. "Only those who belonged to the clan in the ascendancy for the time being could enter on the quest" (p. 258). Entry required supernatural nomination by an ivi-atua through dreams (p. 260). Food gifts were "the sole practical advantage of victory; those who did not contribute were apt to have their houses burnt" (p. 264). Winner spent five months in strict taboo "sleeping all day," then "returned to ordinary life" after one year (pp. 264-265). The tangata manu feared entering enemy territory (p. 263). 86 bird-man names recorded (pp. 265-266), suggesting many generations of the institution. The ariki-mau "never appeared at Orongo" (p. 260), indicating the two institutions were separate. Even the Miru were victimized when another clan held Ao status (p. 259).

**Métraux 1937**: Rejects Williamson's hypothesis of a dual kingship with the tangata-manu as "secular king." Based on Thompson, Routledge, and his own evidence, Métraux considers the tangata-manu "a man incarnating provisionally the god Makemake and entitled to rights of an aggressive nature" (p. 60). Quotes Roussel (15, p. 427): matatoa "activity consisted in marauding day and night and in trying to fool as many people as they could during the year they were in power, and in taking part in continuous feasts, the most childish and licentious" (p. 61). The tangata-manu winner "seems to have been endowed with religious power which manifested itself in the right to plunder and to bully others" (p. 60).

**Métraux 1940**: The comprehensive monograph adds detail to the 1937 article's assessment. The tangata manu was "elected for a year, without any lack of regard for the king who reigned by right of primogeniture" (p. 337). Privileges included "daily and nightly marauding and the fooling of the people" and "spending the year in continuous childish and wanton feasts" (p. 337). Non-contribution of food "could be punished by the burning of one's house" (p. 337). NEW: the tangata manu "designated two or three persons who must be sacrificed for the prosperity of his reign. It was the choice of the victims which caused a war among the tribes every year" (p. 337). The competition was organized along the dual division (Hotu-iti vs. Ko Tuu). Métraux rejects Williamson's hypothesis of a secular kingship; the bird-man was "an incarnation of the god" Makemake. [REPORTED TRADITION from informants 72 years post-raids]

**Assessment**: Routledge's account, the most detailed, aligns with Thomson rather than Roussel on the tangata manu's political significance. Thomson: "no especial authority." Routledge: food gifts were "the sole practical advantage." Métraux rejects the hypothesis of a secular kingship parallel to the ariki-mau, viewing the tangata-manu as a temporary incarnation of Makemake with aggressive privileges, not political authority. Roussel's brief description of "the chief's authority" being transferred is not supported by the three more detailed accounts (Thomson, Routledge, Métraux). Routledge adds the house-burning coercion (absent from Thomson), indicating some enforcement mechanism, but her overall characterization ("sole practical advantage") is closer to Thomson's "no especial authority" than to Roussel's "chief's authority."

The Ao system as described by Routledge is a rotating competitive institution, not a permanent hierarchy. The matatoa/matakio binary was not a permanent class system but a description of which clans currently held vs. did not hold Ao status (p. 259, footnote). This reframing undermines Roussel's four-class hierarchy (ariki, ivi atua, matatoa, kio) and suggests that what Roussel observed as permanent classes may have been a rotating dominance system.

### Matatoa

**Roussel 1869**: Warriors who had seized effective political power from the ariki. "Decided everything and fought among themselves without prior consultations with His Majesty" (p. 41). At harvest festivities, matatoa "fight like vultures for everything that took their fancy" (p. 48). Matatoa as tangata manu winners ruled for one year, "limited to marauding day and night" (p. 47).

**Eyraud 1864**: Torometi behaves as a matatoa would (domination through personal coercion, theft, intimidation) but is not labeled as such by Eyraud.

**Assessment**: The matatoa system described by Roussel is the aspect of post-raid political organization most consistent with what Eyraud independently observed. This suggests that matatoa-style personal dominance was the functioning political reality, while ariki authority was a memory or narrative.

### Kio

**Roussel 1869**: "Farmers" or "domestic servants" (p. 41). Kio eat leftovers from festivities (p. 48). Defeated warriors kept as slaves, forced to plant for masters (pp. 43-44).

**Assessment**: Described only by Roussel. Whether kio was a hereditary class or a status imposed by warfare cannot be determined.

### Mata Groups

Not described by any of the four immediate post-raid sources (Eyraud, Roussel, Loti, Pinart).

**Thompson 1891**: Thomson refers to "clans" throughout his account, describes the island divided into six districts for Hotu-Matua's six sons (pp. 527-528), and records traditions of inter-clan warfare. He does not use the term "mata" but describes territorial clans with hereditary chiefs who led them in battle (p. 476). Each clan is described as "independent of all the rest" (p. 472). The clan system as Thomson describes it is a territorial-kinship organization, not a hierarchical administrative system under the king.

**Routledge 1919**: The most detailed description. Ten named clans (mata) grouped into two divisions: Kotuu/Mata-nui (western: Marama, Haumoana, Ngatimo, Miru, Hamea, Raa) and Hotu Iti/Mata-iti (eastern: Tupahotu, Koro-orongo, Hitiuira/Ureohei, Ngaure). Territorial boundaries "blend and overlap; members of one division settled not infrequently among those of another" (p. 221). The dual division was spoken of "more especially in legend or speaking of the remote past" (p. 221), suggesting a conceptual framework rather than an active political division. "No group restrictions on marriage" between clans (p. 221). Viriamo (Ureohei) married into three different clans including the Miru (pp. 227-228). Children belonged to father's clan but "the clan of the mother was not ignored" (p. 226). [REPORTED TRADITION from informants of multiple clans, confirmed by direct observation that each person still knew their clan]

**Assessment**: The mata/clan system appears in later sources (Thomson, Routledge, Métraux) as a territorial-kinship organization. Routledge's account is the most detailed and reveals: flexible territorial boundaries, frequent inter-clan residence, unrestricted inter-clan marriage, and only one clan (Miru) with a hereditary chief. This describes a segmentary system with clan-level organization, not a centralized chiefdom. The absence of marriage restrictions between clans or between the "chiefly" Miru and other clans is particularly inconsistent with a ranked hierarchical society.

## Cross-Cutting Patterns

### 1. Declining Knowledge Trajectory

Each successive observer encounters LESS traditional knowledge than the one before:
- Eyraud (1864): Language barrier limits what he can learn; still observes large population with festivals, cave-hiding, collective practices
- Roussel (1869): Records king lists, social classes, customs from survivors; but population declining
- Loti (1872): Finds ~100 people, no traditional structure visible, dancing on overturned moai
- Pinart (1877): Islanders have "retained no memory at all"; rongorongo used as fishing line spools

- Geiseler (1882): Population 150 (~20 Tahitians); political collapse total; "old chief" title without substance; traditions of "despotic" kingship reported through Salmon
- Thompson (1886/1891): Population 155; records elaborate traditions through SAME Salmon; king list of 57 names irreconcilable with Roussel's 22

The paradox: later sources record MORE elaborate traditions from FEWER surviving informants, further from the events. Thomson's 57-king list is far more detailed than Roussel's 22-generation list, yet was recorded 17 years later from a population that had declined by 75% (from ~650 to ~111 to ~155). This suggests elaboration rather than preservation. The discovery that Salmon served as intermediary for both Geiseler and Thomson deepens this concern: the convergence between these accounts may reflect Salmon's framework rather than independent traditions.

Routledge (1919) partially resolves this paradox. Working independently of Salmon with multiple informants over 17 months, she found "no chiefs nor any form of government" outside the Miru (p. 224), no king list she considered reliable enough to reproduce, and an ariki-mau who was "neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest" (p. 242). The first researcher independent of Salmon found a markedly less hierarchical political picture than either Geiseler or Thomson. This suggests that the "arbitrary monarchy" and "almost despotic" regime described by those two sources may indeed reflect Salmon's interpretive framework. However, Routledge was also 28-32 years further from the events than Thomson and Geiseler, and her informants were children rather than adults during the period described.

Métraux (1934/1937/1940) adds a further step in this trajectory but with a distinctive contribution. Working 72 years post-raids with a trained Polynesian ethnographer's comparative framework, Métraux describes MORE chiefly prerogatives than Routledge (first-fruits, harvest tapus, servant classes, tuna distribution, fertility chant) yet reaches the SAME conclusion about no political power. He had access to Roussel's recently published 1926 Annales text, which Routledge did not. His conclusion, based on both fieldwork and textual analysis, is that Roussel's claim of ancient absolute power is "an entirely gratuitous assumption" (1937, p. 58). The convergence between Routledge and Métraux on limited power is partly attributable to their shared reliance on Tepano, but Métraux's independent access to Roussel's texts and additional informants (Victoria Rapahango) provide partial independence.

The 1940 monograph further strengthens this picture. Despite the fuller treatment adding detail on clan structure, marriage rules, social classes, warfare, bird cult, and stone image significance, Métraux reaches exactly the same political assessment in nearly identical wording ("certainly not a ruler or civilian chief," p. 136). His king list comparison (Table 2, pp. 88-93) reveals that the "dynastic succession" underpinning chiefdom models rests on lists where only 7 names appear in all four independently recorded versions, and those shared names are predominantly tribal/geographic eponyms rather than historical individuals. His assessment of Roussel's four-class hierarchy as "only superficially exact" (p. 135), with kio as war captives and matatoa as "the actual rulers of the tribes" (pp. 138-139), further undermines the structural features chiefdom models require.

### 2. Internal Contradiction in Roussel and Thomson

Roussel and Thomson both describe hierarchical political systems while simultaneously reporting facts that contradict them.

Roussel simultaneously describes: (a) kings with "absolute power," first-fruits privileges, and tapu; and (b) a system where royal authority is "absent or totally unrecognized" and matatoa "decided everything." He does not reconcile these descriptions. Later scholars have treated (a) as describing the "traditional" system and (b) as a recent development, but Roussel provides no basis for this temporal assignment.

Thomson similarly describes: (a) an "arbitrary monarchy" with the king's supreme authority, hereditary chiefs, and the right to remove them; and (b) a system where "no tax was exacted," "no great homage was paid the king," clans were "independent of all the rest," and "the people avenged their own injuries." Both accounts present a chiefdom-like narrative contradicted by their own details.

### 3. Collective Action Evidence

Eyraud's boat-building episode ("national cooperation... Nobody would think of not participating") provides direct evidence for community-wide collective action without chiefly command. This is consistent with the archaeological evidence for collective moai production (DiNapoli et al. 2019, 2020; Lipo et al. 2013).

### 4. Property Without Systematic Redistribution

Roussel describes land ownership, family-based plantations, rahui fishing rights, and first-fruits obligations, but NO centralized redistribution system. Property disputes arise from displacement, not from chiefly allocation. This is a property system, not the redistributive economy that chiefdom models predict.

Thomson explicitly states "no tax was exacted of the people" (p. 473), directly negating a key chiefdom criterion. His one reference to a "tax" (fishermen supporting image-makers, p. 533) contradicts this and describes a specific craft-production arrangement, not general tribute. Community enforcement of property rights through collective action ("the entire community" aiding the weak against the strong, p. 465) is evidence for decentralized social control, not chiefly administration.

Métraux (1937) complicates this picture by documenting three instances of food flowing through the ariki-mau: (a) vaka-vaero tuna presented to king, who distributed to tangata-honui (important old men) (p. 50); (b) first tuna after tapu-lifting tasted by king, rest given to tangata-honui (p. 50); (c) first-fruits ceremony with harvest tapu controlled by king (Roussel eyewitness, pp. 54-55). These are genuine redistributive behaviors, but the pattern is telling: food flows fishermen/farmers → king → tangata-honui (elite old men), not king → general population. This is elite-to-elite circulation through the sacred office, not the downward redistribution that chiefdom models predict. The harvest tapu operates through supernatural sanction ("Alas for the man who dared to violate the *tapu*"), not political taxation. The rongorongo assembly food mobilization (documented from both Routledge and Métraux) is specific to a ceremonial context, not routine economic redistribution. Together with Routledge's pua offering and annual chicken gift to rongorongo men, these describe a set of ceremonial and supernatural food-flows that pass through the ariki-mau, but none constitutes the systematic redistributive economy that chiefdom models require.

### 5. Religious Vacuum

Both Eyraud and Roussel describe the near-complete disappearance of religious practice. Eyraud: "religion seems to occupy a very minor place" and statuettes treated with indifference. Roussel: "the idea of a religious cult disappeared with the last priests and priestesses, who died a long time ago." If priests (ivi atua) had constituted a distinct class with institutional power, their disappearance should have produced a political crisis, not the indifference both observers describe. This suggests the ivi atua were not a powerful political class in the period immediately preceding the raids.

### 6. The Roussel Access Problem

The simplest version of the argument from these four sources would be: "three of four observers describe no hierarchy, therefore Roussel is the outlier and his chiefdom-supporting claims are suspect." This framing is misleading because it ignores a real tension: Roussel is the outlier precisely because he had access the others lacked.

Roussel lived on the island for seven years. Eyraud stayed nine months but was linguistically limited and focused on conversion. Loti and Pinart were each there less than a week. Roussel had time to learn the language, build relationships with informants, and observe the society in sustained depth. If anyone was positioned to learn about political structure, it was Roussel. Pinart finding "no memory" of earlier inhabitants after a six-day visit tells us what was available to a brief visitor, not necessarily what survivors actually knew. The manuscript must acknowledge this directly rather than minimize it.

However, Roussel's privileged access cuts both ways:

**(a) Longer stay enabled framework imposition.** Roussel arrived from Mangareva, where the Sacred Hearts mission had established a rigid hierarchical order. He may have been primed to hear hierarchy in what informants told him, or informants may have offered the kind of political narrative they thought a European authority figure expected. A five-day visitor has less opportunity to impose interpretive frameworks; a seven-year resident has more.

**(b) The internal contradiction stands regardless of Roussel's credibility.** Even granting Roussel full credibility as an observer, his account says royal authority was "absent or totally unrecognized" and had "passed completely into the hands of the matatoa." His own evidence argues against the functioning chiefdom he simultaneously describes. The question is not whether to believe Roussel, but which part of Roussel to believe.

**(c) The observation/tradition distinction persists.** What Roussel directly observed (the first-fruits ceremony for a child, the matatoa dominance, the property tangle) is one kind of evidence. What survivors told him about how things used to be (the king list, "absolute power," the class system) is another. His long stay gave him better access to both, but the reported traditions remain reported traditions, transmitted through a demographic catastrophe that killed or removed the ariki and priests who would have been their primary custodians.

**(d) Eyraud's shorter stay may be more diagnostically useful on one specific question.** Eyraud arrived when approximately 1,200 people were still alive, just one year post-raids, before the further collapse that reduced the population to 650 by Roussel's census. If institutional hierarchy had survived the raids in any functional form, Eyraud should have encountered it despite his limited language skills. A formal chiefdom with hereditary authority, tributary obligations, and territorial jurisdiction is not the kind of institution that becomes invisible to a nine-month resident. Eyraud's inability to characterize chiefly authority despite living under Torometi's personal domination is evidence, even if his access to oral traditions was limited.

The honest framing is therefore: Roussel's long stay gave him access to survivors' accounts of what their society had been, and those accounts include claims about hierarchical political organization. The question is not whether Roussel accurately recorded what he was told, but whether what he was told can bear the inferential weight that chiefdom models place on it, given that (i) his own direct observations of actually functioning authority contradict the traditions he recorded, (ii) the traditions were transmitted through a demographic catastrophe, (iii) the king list he recorded disagrees with other independently recorded lists (Métraux 1940:88-93, Table 2: only 7 of dozens of names appear in all four lists; 12 of 23 Roussel names are in Thomson's list; shared names are primarily tribal/geographic eponyms like Marama, Raa, Hotu-iti, and Tuu, not historical individuals), and (iv) the one ceremony he directly witnessed involved a child who was the last of his line in a society reduced to 650 people.
