## The Demographic Catastrophe (1862-1877)

The trajectory of population collapse is now documented by four sequential witnesses:

| Year | Observer | Population Estimate | Context |
|------|----------|-------------------|---------|
| pre-1862 | (multiple estimates) | ~3,000-5,000 | Pre-raid; Roussel estimates ~5,000 maximum based on settlement distribution |
| 1864 | Eyraud | ~1,200 | 1 year post-raids; six Rapanui repatriated from Peru had just returned |
| 1869 | Roussel | ~650 | Census figure; 3-6 years post-raids; tuberculosis, continued decline |
| 1872 | Loti | ~100 | 10 years post-raids; concentrated at Hanga Roa; rest of island deserted |
| 1877 | Pinart | 111 (26 women) | 14 years post-raids; Dutrou-Bornier had removed most survivors to Tahiti |
| 1900 | Father Butaye | 231 (213 natives) | Recovery beginning under Chilean annexation |

This means that between 1862 and 1877, the population declined from ~3,000 to 111, a reduction of approximately 96%. The decline was not a single event but a cascading catastrophe: slave raids (1862-1863), repatriation-borne smallpox (1863-1864), continued epidemic disease (tuberculosis), warfare among survivors (documented by Eyraud and Roussel), and Dutrou-Bornier's removal of population to Tahiti (1870s).

**Critical implication**: By the time Thompson (1891) and Routledge (1919) recorded their ethnographic accounts, the population had partially recovered from a bottleneck of ~111 people. The "traditions" they recorded had survived through this bottleneck, filtered through survivors who were predominantly non-elite (the ariki and priests were specifically targeted by the slavers), traumatized, converted to Christianity, and living under colonial administration.

## Evidence from Immediate Post-Raid Sources (1864-1877)

### Eyraud 1864

**Recorded**: 1 year after slave raids
**Population context**: ~1,200; "a time of considerable anarchy" (Lee, Introduction)
**Duration on island**: 9 months (longest of four observers)

**Political organization observed**: Eyraud describes authority as purely personal: "It is hard for me to characterize the authority of chiefs on the island. I don't even know the basis for such authority. It seems that authority depended only on the influence that some natives were able to exert over their neighbors and that the people gradually grew accustomed to such displays of superiority" (p. 14). His "chief" Torometi ruled by coercion and theft, not institutional authority, and was collectively punished by community action (hut-burning).

**Evidence for collective action**: The boat-building episode documents voluntary collective contribution without chiefly command: "I had noted this custom of making everyone contribute to a project that was considered important. Nobody would think of not participating" (p. 27).

**Religion**: "Religion seems to occupy a very minor place in their lives... I never witnessed any act that was really indicative of a religious cult" (p. 20). Statuettes in homes treated with indifference.

**Evidential type**: Almost entirely direct observation (a). Eyraud was too new and linguistically limited to collect traditions.

### Roussel 1869

**Recorded**: 3-6 years after slave raids (notes written 1869)
**Population context**: ~650
**Duration on island**: 1866-1873 (7 years)

**Political organization described**: Most ethnographically detailed early post-raid account. Key claims:

1. **Four social classes**: ariki (kings), ivi atua (priests), matatoa (warriors), kio (farmers/servants). Whether these were rigid castes or contextual roles cannot be determined.

2. **King list**: 22 generations from Hotu Matua to Gregorio. This list DISAGREES with other recorded lists (Thomson, Jaussen, Métraux) in both names and order. Only 12 of 23 names overlap with Thomson; only 9 with Jaussen. "Few are given in the same order" (endnote 7). This inconsistency across informants recorded within a 25-year window (1869-1891) undermines the lists' reliability as historical records.

3. **Royal authority "absent or totally unrecognized"**: Roussel's own account contains an internal contradiction. He describes kings who "exercised absolute power" AND admits their authority "had passed completely into the hands of the matatoa" (p. 41). Both cannot describe the same functioning system. The "absolute power" is reported tradition; the matatoa dominance appears to describe what Roussel actually observed.

4. **First-fruits ceremony**: Roussel directly witnessed a procession bringing first yams to the child Gregorio, "the one and only true chief" and last surviving royal line member (pp. 39-40). This is the strongest direct evidence for chiefly prerogatives. However, Gregorio was a child in a devastated society of 650 people; the ceremony may have been performative or residual.

5. **Tangata manu**: Roussel describes the birdman competition as a mechanism for transferring chiefly authority to the matatoa (p. 47). This brief description has been cited as evidence for the tangata manu institution but provides minimal detail.

**Property**: Land ownership existed but was "in an almost inextricable tangle" due to displacement from wars and slave raids (p. 50). Plantations held communally within families. Rahui stones marked exclusive fishing rights. This describes a property system, not a redistributive chiefdom economy.

**Evidential type**: Mix of direct observation (a) and reported tradition (b), often not clearly distinguished. The king list, royal powers, and origin story are all reported tradition from survivors.

### Loti 1872

**Recorded**: 10 years after slave raids
**Population context**: ~100 people, concentrated at Hanga Roa
**Duration on island**: 5 days

**Political organization observed**: Only an informal "old chief" mentioned; no political structure described. Dutrou-Bornier's commercial operation dominates the political landscape. Most of the island is deserted.

**Evidential type**: Direct observation (a) of extreme demographic collapse. Loti is a literary observer, not an ethnographer. His value is in documenting conditions, not recording traditions.

### Pinart 1877

**Recorded**: 14 years after slave raids
**Population context**: 111 people (26 women); demographic nadir
**Duration on island**: 6 days

**Political organization observed**: Only colonial authority (Dutrou-Bornier's widow as "regent"). A trained ethnographer who records NO traditional political structure, NO social classes, NO chiefs. Islanders had "retained no memory at all" of earlier inhabitants (pp. 122-123). Rongorongo tablets used as fishing line spools.

**Evidential type**: Direct observation (a) by a trained scientist. The complete silence on traditional political structure from a professional anthropologist is itself significant evidence of how thoroughly traditional knowledge had been lost by 1877.

## Political Offices Described

### Ariki / Ariki Mau

**Roussel 1869**: Kings (ariki) formed the highest class. Hotu Matua was first king; succession by abdication when son married. Kings "regarded as gods" who "exercised absolute power" with first-fruits privileges and tapu on head and hands. BUT: their authority had "passed completely into the hands of the matatoa." The child Gregorio was "the one and only true chief" and last of the royal line. [REPORTED TRADITION from survivors, except the observation of Gregorio]

**Eyraud 1864**: No ariki mentioned. Authority described as personal influence without institutional basis.

**Loti 1872**: No ariki mentioned. Only an informal "old chief."

**Pinart 1877**: No ariki mentioned. Only colonial "queen" (Dutrou-Bornier's daughter).

**Assessment**: The ariki institution is described ONLY by Roussel, based on what survivors told him 3-6 years after the raids specifically targeted and removed ariki. The three other immediate post-raid observers describe no institutional hierarchy. This raises the question of whether Roussel's informants were describing a functioning institution or a memory of one, and whether that memory was accurate or idealized.

### Tangata Manu

**Roussel 1869**: Brief mention of birdman competition as mechanism for matatoa authority (p. 47). "Luck alone, the discovery of a few eggs of a seabird, was all that was needed to transfer the chief's authority to the happy nest-robbers."

**Other immediate post-raid sources**: Not mentioned by Eyraud, Loti, or Pinart.

**Assessment**: Roussel's description is the earliest post-raid reference. More detailed descriptions come from Thompson (1891) and Routledge (1919), recorded further from the events.

### Matatoa

**Roussel 1869**: Warriors who had seized effective political power from the ariki. "Decided everything and fought among themselves without prior consultations with His Majesty" (p. 41). At harvest festivities, matatoa "fight like vultures for everything that took their fancy" (p. 48). Matatoa as tangata manu winners ruled for one year, "limited to marauding day and night" (p. 47).

**Eyraud 1864**: Torometi behaves as a matatoa would (domination through personal coercion, theft, intimidation) but is not labeled as such by Eyraud.

**Assessment**: The matatoa system described by Roussel is the aspect of post-raid political organization most consistent with what Eyraud independently observed. This suggests that matatoa-style personal dominance was the functioning political reality, while ariki authority was a memory or narrative.

### Kio

**Roussel 1869**: "Farmers" or "domestic servants" (p. 41). Kio eat leftovers from festivities (p. 48). Defeated warriors kept as slaves, forced to plant for masters (pp. 43-44).

**Assessment**: Described only by Roussel. Whether kio was a hereditary class or a status imposed by warfare cannot be determined.

### Mata Groups

Not described by any of the four immediate post-raid sources. Mata group organization appears primarily in later sources (Thompson 1891, Routledge 1919, Métraux 1940).

## Cross-Cutting Patterns

### 1. Declining Knowledge Trajectory

Each successive observer encounters LESS traditional knowledge than the one before:
- Eyraud (1864): Language barrier limits what he can learn; still observes large population with festivals, cave-hiding, collective practices
- Roussel (1869): Records king lists, social classes, customs from survivors; but population declining
- Loti (1872): Finds ~100 people, no traditional structure visible, dancing on overturned moai
- Pinart (1877): Islanders have "retained no memory at all"; rongorongo used as fishing line spools

This trajectory continues to Thompson (1891) and Routledge (1919), who are further removed and must reconstruct from even more attenuated oral tradition.

### 2. Internal Contradiction in Roussel

Roussel simultaneously describes: (a) kings with "absolute power," first-fruits privileges, and tapu; and (b) a system where royal authority is "absent or totally unrecognized" and matatoa "decided everything." He does not reconcile these descriptions. Later scholars have treated (a) as describing the "traditional" system and (b) as a recent development, but Roussel provides no basis for this temporal assignment.

### 3. Collective Action Evidence

Eyraud's boat-building episode ("national cooperation... Nobody would think of not participating") provides direct evidence for community-wide collective action without chiefly command. This is consistent with the archaeological evidence for collective moai production (DiNapoli et al. 2019, 2020; Lipo et al. 2013).

### 4. Property Without Redistribution

Roussel describes land ownership, family-based plantations, rahui fishing rights, and first-fruits obligations, but NO centralized redistribution system. Property disputes arise from displacement, not from chiefly allocation. This is a property system, not the redistributive economy that chiefdom models predict.

### 5. Religious Vacuum

Both Eyraud and Roussel describe the near-complete disappearance of religious practice. Eyraud: "religion seems to occupy a very minor place" and statuettes treated with indifference. Roussel: "the idea of a religious cult disappeared with the last priests and priestesses, who died a long time ago." If priests (ivi atua) had constituted a distinct class with institutional power, their disappearance should have produced a political crisis, not the indifference both observers describe. This suggests the ivi atua were not a powerful political class in the period immediately preceding the raids.

### 6. The Roussel Access Problem

The simplest version of the argument from these four sources would be: "three of four observers describe no hierarchy, therefore Roussel is the outlier and his chiefdom-supporting claims are suspect." This framing is misleading because it ignores a real tension: Roussel is the outlier precisely because he had access the others lacked.

Roussel lived on the island for seven years. Eyraud stayed nine months but was linguistically limited and focused on conversion. Loti and Pinart were each there less than a week. Roussel had time to learn the language, build relationships with informants, and observe the society in sustained depth. If anyone was positioned to learn about political structure, it was Roussel. Pinart finding "no memory" of earlier inhabitants after a six-day visit tells us what was available to a brief visitor, not necessarily what survivors actually knew. The manuscript must acknowledge this directly rather than minimize it.

However, Roussel's privileged access cuts both ways:

**(a) Longer stay enabled framework imposition.** Roussel arrived from Mangareva, where the Sacred Hearts mission had established a rigid hierarchical order. He may have been primed to hear hierarchy in what informants told him, or informants may have offered the kind of political narrative they thought a European authority figure expected. A five-day visitor has less opportunity to impose interpretive frameworks; a seven-year resident has more.

**(b) The internal contradiction stands regardless of Roussel's credibility.** Even granting Roussel full credibility as an observer, his account says royal authority was "absent or totally unrecognized" and had "passed completely into the hands of the matatoa." His own evidence argues against the functioning chiefdom he simultaneously describes. The question is not whether to believe Roussel, but which part of Roussel to believe.

**(c) The observation/tradition distinction persists.** What Roussel directly observed (the first-fruits ceremony for a child, the matatoa dominance, the property tangle) is one kind of evidence. What survivors told him about how things used to be (the king list, "absolute power," the class system) is another. His long stay gave him better access to both, but the reported traditions remain reported traditions, transmitted through a demographic catastrophe that killed or removed the ariki and priests who would have been their primary custodians.

**(d) Eyraud's shorter stay may be more diagnostically useful on one specific question.** Eyraud arrived when approximately 1,200 people were still alive, just one year post-raids, before the further collapse that reduced the population to 650 by Roussel's census. If institutional hierarchy had survived the raids in any functional form, Eyraud should have encountered it despite his limited language skills. A formal chiefdom with hereditary authority, tributary obligations, and territorial jurisdiction is not the kind of institution that becomes invisible to a nine-month resident. Eyraud's inability to characterize chiefly authority despite living under Torometi's personal domination is evidence, even if his access to oral traditions was limited.

The honest framing is therefore: Roussel's long stay gave him access to survivors' accounts of what their society had been, and those accounts include claims about hierarchical political organization. The question is not whether Roussel accurately recorded what he was told, but whether what he was told can bear the inferential weight that chiefdom models place on it, given that (i) his own direct observations of actually functioning authority contradict the traditions he recorded, (ii) the traditions were transmitted through a demographic catastrophe, (iii) the king list he recorded disagrees with other independently recorded lists [CITE-CHECK: Métraux 1940:91], and (iv) the one ceremony he directly witnessed involved a child who was the last of his line in a society reduced to 650 people.
