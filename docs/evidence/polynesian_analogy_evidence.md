# The Polynesian Analogy: Evidence Compilation

## Key Questions

1. What is the structural basis of the Polynesian chiefdom comparative model (Sahlins 1958, Kirch 1984)?
2. On what island cases was the model primarily built, and what are the evidential bases for those cases?
3. How specifically was this model applied to Rapa Nui by Kirch (1984, 2010) and McCoy (1976)?
4. What assumptions of historical continuity and structural homology are required for the analogy to hold?
5. What features of Rapa Nui's history and ecology distinguish it from the islands on which the model was built?
6. Does Kirch himself acknowledge limits on applying the model to Rapa Nui?
7. Are there parallel critiques of the Polynesian chiefdom model from within Pacific archaeology (Dye 2010 for Hawaii; Kolb 1994)?

## The Comparative Model

### Sahlins's Polynesian Stratification Gradient

**Source**: Sahlins 1954 (PhD dissertation, Columbia); published as Sahlins 1958. Full claims extraction: `docs/references/claims/sahlins_1954_claims.md`.

**The framework**: Sahlins classified fourteen Polynesian societies into four stratification groups using qualitative assessment of twelve criteria (structural, economic, socio-political, ceremonial). He correlated stratification with productivity, measured primarily by the size and frequency of redistributive networks. The hypothesis: "Other factors constant, the greater the productivity, the greater the amount of stratification" (1954, p. 413).

**Rapa Nui's placement**: Group IIa (moderate-high stratification), alongside Mangareva, Mangaia, and Uvea. Within the group: Mangareva > Easter Island ≈ Mangaia > Uvea.

**The four groups**:
- Group I (highest): Hawaii, Tonga, Samoa, Tahiti
- Group IIa: Mangareva, Easter Island, Mangaia, Uvea
- Group IIb: Marquesas, Tikopia, Futuna
- Group III (lowest): Pukapuka, Ontong Java, Tokelau

**Evidentiary basis for Rapa Nui**: Sahlins's Easter Island section (pp. 99-106) cites only post-slave-raid sources: Metraux 1940 (~30+ citations), Metraux 1937b (~8), Thomson 1889 (~8), Routledge 1919 (~3), and Williamson 1924 (~3). No contact-era observers (Roggeveen, González, Cook, La Pérouse) are cited anywhere in the Easter Island material. The entire characterization depends on ethnographic accounts recorded 25-75+ years after the slave raids reduced the population from ~3,000 to fewer than 200.

**Evidentiary asymmetry**: Group I societies are documented through dozens of independent observers and ethnographers producing thousands of pages. Hawaii alone has Malo (native informant), Ellis (long-resident missionary), Handy (extended fieldwork), and many others. Tikopia (Group IIb) has Firth's extraordinary multi-year fieldwork. Rapa Nui has Metraux's 1934-1935 fieldwork, conducted among a post-raid, post-missionary population, supplemented by Thomson's 12 days in 1886 (filtered through Salmon) and Routledge's 17 months in 1914-1915.

**The redistribution gap**: The single most consequential finding. Sahlins admits: "With regard to distribution, there is little direct evidence that the ariki mau and the birdmen were focal points of accumulation and redistribution" (p. 101). He then fills this gap by Polynesian analogy: "We may surmise from comparative data that in most cases these members of the upper status level were acting as focal points in accumulation and redistribution." This is the moment the analogy replaces evidence. The absence of redistribution evidence for Rapa Nui is not treated as a negative finding (suggesting redistribution may not have existed) but as a data gap to be filled by comparison with better-documented islands.

**Productivity rating**: Easter Island receives a score of 6,000-8,000† (5th-6th of 14 islands). The score rests on Metraux's population estimate (3,000-4,000) treated as a single redistributive unit, multiplied by a frequency factor of 2 (sporadic, with acknowledged uncertainty). The circularity is built in: the productivity measure (redistributive network size) presupposes the existence of redistribution, which is precisely the feature whose evidence is lacking.

**Ramage classification**: In Part Two, Sahlins classifies Easter Island as a ramage society with primogeniture, seniority-based ranking, and a genealogical tree encompassing the whole island through ~10 mata. He acknowledges this is ecologically anomalous: Easter Island's concentrated coastal resources should predict descent line (not ramage) organization. He resolves this by invoking "historical alignments to eastern Polynesia" (p. 362).

**What Sahlins reports from his own sources that challenges the chiefdom reading**:
- Metraux's conclusion that government was "decentralized and localized in the hands of the tribal chiefs" (p. 104)
- Thomson's report that the ariki mau "was given no great homage" (p. 106)
- The ariki mau's only sanctions for tabu violation were supernatural, not secular (p. 105)
- "Very little information" on status-differentiated life crisis rites (p. 106)
- No evidence of forceful confiscation: "The customary obligation to give surplus production to the chiefs does not seem to have been enforced by confiscation" (p. 101)
- The matatoa claim "rests on tenuous evidence" (p. 284); Thomson denied professional warriors existed

**Moai claim**: Sahlins states that "Large-scale enterprises of various types, including the transportation of the famous large stone heads, were initiated by members of the chiefly status level" (p. 100). In the Appendix, he characterizes moai as communal labor "channelized into non-strategic realms" (p. 433), adding that this "may also imply that stratification, in particular instances, can precede communal labor." No archaeological analysis supports either claim; both derive from Metraux 1940.

**Significance**: This dissertation is the first step in the chain that produces the chiefdom attribution. Sahlins's Group IIa placement for Rapa Nui becomes the baseline assumption that Rapa Nui is a moderately stratified chiefdom when Service (1962) formalizes the typology. The redistribution gap, filled by analogy in 1954, is never subsequently re-examined with independent evidence. The post-raid source dependency is inherited by all subsequent scholarship in the chain.

### Sahlins's Environmental Channeling Hypothesis (1955)

**Source**: Sahlins 1955, "Esoteric Efflorescence in Easter Island," *American Anthropologist* 57:1045-1052. Full claims extraction: `docs/references/claims/sahlins_1955_claims.md`.

**The argument**: Easter Island's environment precluded most forms of communal and specialist labor common in central-eastern Polynesia (irrigation, bush clearing, canoe building, fortress construction, large-scale fishing). Because Easter Island's ramage organization was "suited to and reinforced by" communal labor, this capacity was "channeled into an esoteric domain" (p. 1047): stone image carving and transport.

**Why it matters for the Polynesian analogy critique**: The environmental channeling hypothesis simultaneously (a) applies the Polynesian analogy (assuming Easter Island's social organization was "identical in principle" to central-eastern Polynesia, p. 1050) and (b) undercuts the mechanism by which the analogy's chiefdom model operates. Sahlins himself notes that the reinforcement loop between chiefly power and communal labor is "most marked when communal or specialized labor is expended in productivity-increasing, subsistence activities" (pp. 1048-1049). When labor goes into non-strategic monument building rather than subsistence, this loop is broken. The chiefs cannot increase their surplus by directing statue construction; the mechanism linking redistribution to chiefly power over labor does not operate on Easter Island as it does on Hawaii, Tahiti, or Rarotonga.

**Progressive hardening of claims**: The dissertation's "little direct evidence" of redistribution (1954, p. 101) becomes a flat assertion: "The predominant method of extrahousehold distribution was redistribution" (p. 1049). The ecological anomaly of the ramage classification (1954, pp. 361-362) is dropped; the system was "clearly of the ramage type" (p. 1049). Two data gap admissions survive ("badly disrupted nature of the culture," p. 1049; "information is not conclusive," pp. 1049-1050), down from eleven in the dissertation. This pattern continues into the 1957 JPS article, where only the ecological anomaly survives and all other qualifications are absent.

**The stone carver passage (Metraux 1940:137)**: Sahlins quotes Metraux's informant Tepano: carvers "received orders from people who wanted a monument for their ahu" and "were paid in fish, lobsters and eels" (p. 1050). Sahlins infers the lineage head gave the order. But the passage describes: (a) "people" (plural) commissioning, not a single chief; (b) direct food payment, not redistributive allocation; (c) organization under a "master" craftsman, not a chief; (d) motivation as lineage pride ("a monument for their ahu"), not chiefly command. This is consistent with collective action at the lineage level and inconsistent with centralized chiefly redistribution.

**Sahlins dismisses the explanation his own evidence supports**: Metraux's "tribal pride and competitive instinct" explanation (1940, p. 307) is criticized as a constant that cannot explain Easter Island's uniqueness (p. 1046). But Sahlins's environmental channeling explains only why labor went into stone rather than canoes; it does not explain who organized the labor. The stone carver passage he cites describes competitive lineage-level commissioning, precisely the pattern Metraux proposed. Metraux's 1957 synthesis confirms this: transport organized by "heads of lineages" through competitive feast sponsorship.

### Kirch's Evolutionary Chiefdom Framework

**Source**: Kirch 1984, *The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms*, Cambridge University Press. Full claims extraction: `docs/references/claims/kirch_1984_claims.md` (121 claims).

**The framework**: Kirch proposes that all Polynesian societies inherited a chiefdom template from Ancestral Polynesian Society (APS), reconstructed from archaeological and linguistic evidence. The template includes: the conical clan with genealogical ranking, hereditary *ariki* chieftainship, *mana* and *tapu* as principles of political legitimation, first-fruits tribute, and ritually sanctioned control of production (Ch. 3, pp. 62-65; Table 6, p. 65). Rather than demonstrating that each island developed a chiefdom independently, Kirch argues the chiefdom was the default condition carried by every colonizing group (p. 281). Local transformations then diverged depending on ecological constraints, demographic trajectories, and competitive dynamics.

**Primary cases**: Tonga (Ch. 9, dual chieftainship, tribute networks, inter-island polity), Hawai'i (Ch. 10, irrigation-based intensification, peripatetic paramount, the "threshold" of statehood). These establish what Kirch considers the "standard" chiefdom against which Easter Island is measured.

**How Rapa Nui fits**: Easter Island (Ch. 11) is the third case study, chosen because it represents "a radical departure from Ancestral Polynesian Society" (p. 8). Kirch applies the framework as "a developed form of the Ancestral Polynesian segmentary ramage system" (p. 272), but acknowledges the ariki-mau "may have exercised more ritual than secular power" (p. 272).

**The APS inheritance argument** (p. 281): This is Kirch's key innovation. "Colonizing Polynesians in every case carried with them concepts of pyramidal social structure, of first fruits and tribute as obligatory to the chiefs, of chiefs as earthly representatives of ancestral deities, of differential access to resources, of domination by the chiefs over labour and the means of production, were present in all Polynesian societies from the beginning." And: "We simply cannot attribute the existence of hierarchical political systems in Polynesia to such functional explanations as the need for chiefs as re-distributors of geographically dispersed resources (cf. Sahlins 1958)." This reverses Sahlins's functionalist argument but raises a new problem: if the chiefdom was inherited rather than developed in response to local conditions, what is the archaeological evidence that it persisted on Easter Island specifically?

**Archaeological evidence Kirch presents for Easter Island chiefdom**: Notably thin. The archaeological evidence in Chapter 11 (ahu distribution, settlement patterns, moai production, mata'a, cave fortification) is interpreted through the ethnographic framework. The two passages that present direct archaeological evidence (pp. 270-271) actually describe decentralized, lineage-level production, not chiefly command. The chiefdom label is maintained by the APS inheritance assumption, not by archaeological demonstration.

**Table 2 and Goldman's "Open" classification** (p. 37): Kirch presents Goldman's (1970) classification alongside Sahlins's. Goldman classified Easter Island as "Open," defined as: seniority modified by military/political effectiveness, more strongly military than religious, status differences "no longer regularly graded but tend to be sharply defined" (p. 36). This classification matches the matatoa-dominated system described by Routledge (1919) and Métraux (1937, 1940). However, Kirch never discusses the Goldman classification in the Easter Island chapter.

**Redistribution revised** (pp. 167, 281): Kirch explicitly notes that as stratification increased across Polynesian societies, "the proportion of surplus actually redistributed to the people at large decreased" (p. 167). He reverses Sahlins's argument that chiefs arose because societies needed redistributors. This is significant: if redistribution decreases as chiefdoms develop, and if Easter Island's redistribution evidence was already lacking (Sahlins 1954, p. 101), the functional justification for calling Easter Island a chiefdom is doubly undermined.

**Key passages supporting our argument**:

1. "Virtually all ethnographic and archaeological authorities agree that the *ahu* were conceived, constructed, and used by local descent groups, and do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority" (p. 270).

2. Rano Raraku quarry suggests "the activities of many independently working crews, probably sent periodically by their kin groups, rather than the activities of a single work force under centralized direction" (Mulloy and Figueroa 1978:134, quoted pp. 270-271).

3. Ariki-mau "may have exercised more ritual than secular power" (p. 272).

**Significance**: The author of the Polynesian chiefdom framework himself provides evidence that the primary archaeological features of Easter Island (ahu/moai) were produced by descent groups without centralized command. The chiefdom label survives not on evidentiary grounds but on the assumption that the ancestral template persisted. This is the circularity our paper identifies: the interpretation precedes and shapes the reading of the evidence.

### Goldman's Counter-Classification: Easter Island as "Open" Society

**Source**: Goldman 1970, *Ancient Polynesian Society*, University of Chicago Press. Full claims extraction: `docs/references/claims/goldman_1970_claims.md` (141 claims).

**The framework**: Where Sahlins classified by productivity-stratification correlation and Kirch by inherited ancestral template, Goldman classified Polynesian societies by the relationship between status and power. His three types (Traditional, Open, Stratified) track a developmental sequence driven by status rivalry, not ecological determinism.

**Easter Island's classification**: "Open" (p. 94). Goldman defines Open as: seniority modified by military/political effectiveness; power dominant over status; stability maintained by exercise of secular powers rather than religious sanction; status differences sharply defined rather than graded (p. 20). Easter Island stands alongside Mangaia, Marquesas, Samoa, and Niue.

**The key claim**: Easter Island "had never evolved a central authority" (p. 94). This is the clearest statement by any Polynesian comparativist that the chiefdom label is inappropriate.

**Why Goldman's classification is significant for the analogy critique**:

1. **Independent assessment**: Goldman's framework was developed independently of Sahlins's productivity-stratification model and reaches the opposite conclusion about Easter Island. Two Polynesian comparativists using the same ethnographic sources (primarily Metraux 1940) but different organizing principles classify Easter Island differently: Sahlins as moderately stratified (Group IIA), Goldman as lacking centralized authority (Open).

2. **Goldman's findings align with the ethnographic evidence**: Goldman's "Open" classification, emphasizing matatoa dominance, ariki mau powerlessness, and decentralized redistribution, matches what Routledge (1919) and Metraux (1937, 1940) actually describe. Sahlins's Group IIA requires redistribution evidence Goldman explicitly locates with commoner heads, not the paramount.

3. **Goldman's redistribution finding is critical** (pp. 114-115): "on Easter Island the main role of economic redistribution or recirculation lay not with the ariki mau, but with heads of commoner groups." The ariki mau distributed only to "important old men." This directly contradicts Sahlins's (1954, p. 101) analogical inference that the chief served as redistributive center.

4. **Goldman's monument construction finding** (pp. 106-107): "There is no reason to believe, however, that a central authority ever undertook the construction of local monuments." Goldman proposes captive labor organized by military coalitions, not chiefly redistribution. This aligns with both Kirch's own acknowledgment of decentralized production (1984, pp. 270-271) and the archaeological evidence (Lipo et al. 2013, 2025).

5. **Kirch acknowledged but did not engage**: Table 2 in Kirch 1984 (p. 37) displays Goldman's "Open" alongside Sahlins's Group IIA. The Easter Island chapter (Ch. 11) never mentions Goldman's classification or addresses why it differs from the framework Kirch applies.

**Goldman's limitations**: Goldman relies on the same post-raid sources as Sahlins (primarily Metraux 1940) and does not raise the epistemological problem of post-raid evidence quality. He dismisses contact-era accounts as not recording "culture or traditions" (p. 96), missing their value as independent observations about political organization. His "Open" classification still assumes functioning local-level authority by matatoa and does not question whether the dual status system he describes was itself a product of the contact/post-contact period.

**Relationship to Sahlins**: Goldman cites Sahlins 1959 (the productivity-power thesis) and rejects the ecological-determinist framework (p. 23). He does not specifically engage with Sahlins's Group IIA placement of Easter Island. The two frameworks operate on different axes: Sahlins measures where a society falls on a stratification gradient; Goldman asks what kind of authority relationship exists. The same data yields different answers because the questions are different.

**Relationship to Kirch**: Goldman's analysis predates Kirch 1984 and provides the only published alternative classification by a major Polynesian comparativist. That Kirch includes it without discussion is itself evidence that the chiefdom attribution was maintained not by engaging counter-evidence but by treating the APS inheritance argument as dispositive.
