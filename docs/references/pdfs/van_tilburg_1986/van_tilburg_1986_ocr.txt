To date, no clear stylistic connection between
monolithic and portable sculpture has been established.
The portable anthropomorphic sculpture is scattered
among private and museum collections in several
countries, with the largest corpus of such material
collected and documented by Heyerdahl (1975). This
material largely lacks archaeological or "voyage"
provenience and the work as a whole is too unexamined
to allow valid comparisons. Further, it has been shown
that Rapa Nui involvement in making and selling
portable sculpture to tourists and members of
scientific expeditions dates at least to the early
1800's and probably earlier (Van Tilburg 1984c; see
also Campbell 1971 for "historical origins" of portable
sculpture). Consequently, comparisons or other
judgments on the perceived stylistic antecedents to
monolithic sculpture thought to be present in portable
sculpture (if they exist) must a _.t a thorough
documentation and analysis of the latter on the same
methodological level as that accomplished here.

Introduction

Prom the initial discovery of Easter [sland by the
Dutch in 1722 up to and including the years of Peruvian
slave raids on the helpless populace (1860-64), ships’
records tell a fragmented tale of a culture struggling
to cope with internal strife, environmental
impoverishment and social, material and sexual
exploitation from within and beyond the tiny island
(cf. Corney 1908; McCall n.d.).

The first observers (Corney 1908; Beaglehole 1955)
noted massive architectural monuments, a treeless but
fruitful landscape, and the behavioral evidence of
social stratification and religious practice. Cultural
dagradation was obvious in the abandonment of massive

Polynesian Art as Symbol

Throughout Polynesia, art served a socio-political
function. It was meant to communicate
institutionalized ideological principles, not all of
which were widely known, understood or even accessible
to the general populace. Sculpture contained
ideological content through stylized and controlled

262


--- Book page 263 (PDF page 288) ---

form, serving a politically legitimizing function in
avery Polynesian society.

Image makers were a favored class of specialists.
Their profession was circumscribed by secrecy. Their
task was highly charged with both value and danger.
Often they formed secret societies or "guilds" complete
with specialized knowledge and cult paraphernalia (cf.
Handy 1927; Henry 1982; Firth 1961, 1967a, 1967hb).
Prequently specialists in a given profession were also
chiefs, and their work was most often commissioned by
chiefs (Mead 1984).

Carvers favored conventionalism in their work
because of the belief that the carvings "represented
gods and spirits, not the external appearance of
particular pecple" (Barrow 1968:56). Portrait
sculpture is net a characteristic of Polynesian craft.

In general, there seems to have been a prevailing
tendency to reduce forms, "to modify them by
exaggerated and ‘fantastic’ treatment, and then to
stress certain emotional values" (ibid:187). There was
also a tendency to fragment those forms and use parts
symbolically.

The Polynesian preoccupation with lineage and the
belief in the continuing power of the ancestors
resulted in accomplished image making (ibid: 192).

263


--- Book page 264 (PDF page 289) ---

That image making was highly regulated. ‘The carving
process was governed by entrenched procedural
conventions, and ritual attended throughout.
Divergence from style required the abandonment of the
project. Occasionally such divergence was regarded as
so socially threatening that atonement or sacrifice was
required. "If errors creep into the carving, or if
there are willful deviations from the ancient
tradition, there is the risk that some dire harm will
befall the basic needs of man, and that must be avoided
at all costs" (Trowell and Nevermann 1968:206).

Words in the form of chants were often
accompanying formulae to various stages of carving or
placing of images, and words in themselves were
regarded as having magical power (Barrow 1968:37). A
failure of recitation required at the least the
abandonment of the project (Henry 1982).

Heads of carved images, whether of wood or stone,
were sometimes rendered as large as the bodies, and
they were almost always to some degree outsized because
of "the relative importance of the head in Polynesian
belief", which is attributed to the belief "throughout
Oceania and Southeast Asia that a man's head was...the
most taboo part of his body" (Barrow 1968:50).

The early pre-contact social structure seemingly
was tightly controlled, with power based upon control
of resources and hereditary status held by the ariki
mau. That power was conceptualized as all-
encompassing, and Goldman (1970:112) says that the
sacredness of the ariki mau's person, the concentration
of mana and associative power of tapu were "the most
widespread generalized personal sanctity in all
Polynesia" (ibid:113).

While the symbolism of the statues and the

my opinion, two possible suggestions. The first is the
conventional one, i.e. that the ahu were conceived,

the ariki mau, and are erected at his behest. This
alternative hypothesis has some scant ethnographic data
in its support.

The early explorer's, for example, were told that
the statues represented ariki (see Chapter II) and the
ethnography shows that the title ariki was reserved
only for the Miru (Metraux 1940:128). Metraux (ibid)

tribes other than the Miru." Elsewhere, he says that
"the Miru were the most important group on the island

themselves the title of ariki because the king was
always a Miru" (ibid:90).

The Miru traced their ancestry to Tongaroa and

including red scoria quarries. If the erection of

Conclusion

This paper has argued that the ahu/moai complex of
pre-contact Easter Island demonstrates, both
archaeologically and iconographically, the dominance of
an ideology which is defined as male, Lineage-based and
incorporating anthropomorphic and bird symbolism as
understocd within the Polynesian context. Ritual
beliefs and practices were originally based upon the
concept of inherent mana and ancestral authority. The

Lineage head was characterized as source of both

350


--- Book page 351 (PDF page 376) ---

secular and religious power which emanated initially
from the gods through the founding ancestor.

A subordinate but integrated subset of ritual
beliefs and practices is postulated to have developed
with the differentiation of the role of chief and
priest. This ritual subset continued to incorporate
anthropomorphic and bird symbclism, but evolved a
metaphysic of mana which moved from an emphasis placed
upon its inherent nature toward an equal or near equal
emphasis upon its acquired nature (demonstrated prowess
of any sort but which included mastery of esoteric
knowledge, carving, related stoneworking skills,
increase or fertility magic and which may have
ultimately focused largely upon warfare) .

Two other factors are postulated to have been
operative. The first is the major role played within
the system of social status and rank by carvers.
Demonstrated prowess in carving, moving and erecting
the generally progressively larger images was an
important way to demonstrate acquired mana, both for
the Lineage head who commissioned the statue and the
tufunga or expert who directed the work. The sacond is
the total or near-total control of esoteric knowledge
by the initiates of the priestly class, demonstrated in
part by its nearly complete loss after the decimation

351


--- Book page 352 (PDF page 377) ---

of the leadership following contact. tas of passage
and status-reversal ritual are postulated to have been
enacted and to have incorporated such knowledge, with
the emphasis on status-reversal ritual correlating with
the end of the statue-carving activity.

In sum, the evidence no longer allows the
suggestion, frequently advanced in the past, that two
separate, distinct and unrelated cults developed on
Easter Island. Neither is it possible to postulate the
sudden cassation of one activity (the ancestor or
statue cult) and the inexplicable (perhaps imported)
rise of the birdman cult. There is a clear integration
between the two, and the function of both was to
legitimize and objectify the social structure. The
evolutionary nature of social change is established
archaeologically and iconegraphically by the data base
presented here. .

Finally, while the ahu represent "the religious
fecus of an ancestor~based kin group" (Kirch 1984:271),
it is doubtful that the statues themselves commemorated
"illustrious ancestors of the various kin-groups, whose
mana was thought to bring benefit to the communities”
(Englert as quoted by Xirch, ibid). Instead, I think
it is more likely that the statues represent the
genealogical connection with the founding ancestor,

that the founding ancestor is the one actually being
objectified, and that the construction of the statues
is more like, for example, the construction of sacred
cances in Tikopia (Firth 1967a:55-55) .

Sacred canoes are "possessed by the chiefs, their
ritual elders, and other heads of the most important
kinship groups" (ibid:55}). The ownership of these
canoes is complex. "Theoretically they are the
property of the chief of the clan, despite the fact
that the immediate possessor in each case has ordered
the building of the craft, paid the craftsmen for it,
shelters it in his own canoe shed, has full rights of
use over it, and is fully responsible for its upkeep
and repair®™ (ibid). While the statues are, therefore,
the property of those who commission and construct
them, they are also the property of the paramount
chief, who uses whatever property he —_ (including
statues) for the benefit of the community as a whole
through sacred ritual and the activation of his
inherent mana.

The paramount chief is the principle link with the
ancestors and gods, and his “overlordship and control
are not only justified but natural, since it is he who
igs responsible for the people's welfare™ (ibid:56). [If
the statues are an objectification of that link through

the paramount chief and the Miru lineage, then the
toppling of the statues is seen not simply as the
result of warring chiefdoms, but also as an indirect
challenge to the position of the Miru within the
hierarchy. ‘The challenge, as we can see by the fact
that the Miru remained in tacit control throughout the
pre-contact history of the island, was only partially
successful.

Symbolic archaeology is an attempt to give meaning
and significance to a world seen otherwise as a
collection of meaningless objects. The aim of "a nore
adequate understanding of long-term changes as the
context for the study of relationships between social
and material culture, power and social strategies" is
sought (Miller and Tilley 1984:152). Different
subsystems and behavioral variability between sites
“are interpreted in terms of the principles and
concepts which play a part in all social and ecological
actions" (Hedder 1982:228). Studies and
interpretations of the past are designed to incorporate
symbolic principles and the "framework within which man
gives meaning to his actions" (ibid:229). It is, in
essence, & non-reductive approach, and such an attempt

is made in this study.

354

While “what is meant by concepts such as ideology,
legitimation, power, symbol and social structure must
be argued within the archaeological literature" (ibid),
this paper contributes, it is hoped, a reasonable
attempt to make manageable, and more important, to make
meaningful a body of data which is still rich in
potential. The monolithic sculptures of Easter Island
are defined here (definitively, I believe) as artifact,
and a first stage analysis of their meaning and
function has been undertaken. More work, as they
always say, remains to be done.

One conclusion emerges from the data, however
which seems irrefutable. That is that, while the
iconographic and emblematic symbols required to
rationalize and concretize power relations on Easter
Island can be traced in developmental patterns, much of
the ideological content remained essentially unchanged.
Thus it seems probable that one of the great cosmic
notions of Polynesian spiritual life, that of
mana/tapu, may have survived the thunder and dust of
falling stone.
