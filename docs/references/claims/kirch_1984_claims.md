## I. Intellectual Genealogy

### Who Kirch builds on

1. **Sahlins 1958** (*Social Stratification in Polynesia*): "most widely read" synchronic analysis of Polynesian societies (p. 4). Kirch acknowledges Sahlins's "differentiation by adaptation" concept (pp. 10-11) but proposes a diachronic archaeological approach to supplement the synchronic ethnographic one.

2. **Goldman 1970** (*Ancient Polynesian Society*): Status rivalry framework, "complementary perspective" to Sahlins's ecological determinism (p. 11). Goldman classified Polynesian societies into Traditional, Open, and Stratified types (p. 36). **Easter Island = "Open"** in Goldman's scheme (Table 2, p. 37).

3. **Sahlins 1972** (*Stone Age Economics*): "Domestic mode of production" and "strategy of underproduction" (pp. 40, 160-161). The relationship between chieftainship and surplus production: "at least mutual, if not largely the other way around" (Sahlins 1972:140, cited p. 161). "Leadership continually generates domestic surplus" (p. 161).

4. **Service 1962/1967**: Chiefdom typology acknowledged (pp. 2, 39). Kirch disclaims the neo-evolutionary stages but uses the term "chiefdom" throughout.

5. **Friedman & Rowlands 1978**: "Prestige-goods" model of socio-political transformation (pp. 12-13). Friedman's production function model adopted for Ch. 7 analysis (pp. 162-164).

6. **Earle 1978**: Hawai'i irrigation and chiefly competition (pp. 166, 175).

7. **Firth 1936, 1939, 1959, 1967**: Tikopia ethnography as ethnographic baseline; "Work of the Gods" (p. 165); ramage concept (p. 31).

### How Kirch positions himself

8. **The chiefdom disclaimer** (p. 2): "although I use the term 'chiefdom' to characterize the socio-political organization of Polynesian societies at the contact-era endpoints, this does not mean that I regard them as exemplars of some evolutionary 'stage', or that I subscribe to the 'neo-evolutionary' schemes popular in American anthropology during the 1960s and 70s. Indeed, for reasons given in detail below, I believe that a stadial or stagal approach to evolution in Polynesia is something of a 'dead horse', entirely inadequate as an explanatory framework."

9. **Yet uses "chiefdom" as the organizing concept**: The entire book is framed around "the evolution of the Polynesian chiefdoms." The disclaimer does not prevent the term from functioning as a typological category throughout.

10. **Preface (pp. xi-xii)**: Book conceived in Tikopia, 1978. Collaboration with "Marshall Sahlins" in Anahulu Valley, O'ahu, shaped Kirch's views on "structures of social reproduction in the evolution of Polynesian chiefdoms." Patrick McCoy read chapters of the draft.

## II. Theoretical Framework (Chs. 1-2)

### Method

11. **Direct historical continuity** (p. 5): The method treats ethnographic descriptions not merely as analogies but as "endpoints of indigenous developmental sequences." The ethnographic present provides the interpretive framework for reading the archaeological record backward.

12. **Three objectives** (p. 5): (1) Reconstruct Ancestral Polynesian Society, (2) analyze processes of differentiation, (3) three case studies: Tonga, Hawai'i, Easter Island.

13. **Easter Island as case study** (p. 8): Chosen because it represents "a radical departure from Ancestral Polynesian Society" in contrast to Tonga and Hawai'i.

14. **Polynesia as "laboratory"** (p. 2): Cites Suggs 1961:194, Sahlins 1963, Friedman 1981:275.

### Conical clan and ramage

15. **Conical clan** as organizational basis of Polynesian societies (p. 31): "an extensive group descended from a common ancestor, ranked and segmented along genealogical lines" (Kirchhoff 1955; Sahlins 1968).

16. **Ramification** (p. 31): Population growth leads to fissioning of local subgroups (ramages), each with its senior male as head. Head of original senior ramage remains chief of conical clan.

17. **Territorial divisions** (p. 33): Typical high island divided radially, each local group occupying a pie-shaped slice from coast to interior. Each radial segment includes all major resources.

18. **Ambilateral descent** (p. 33): Polynesian descent groups are "with few exceptions, *ambilateral*" (Firth 1957; Goodenough 1955). Affiliation may be through male or female links.

### Rank and stratification

19. **Continuous scale** (p. 34): Rank is "a continuous scale with the fundamental criterion of seniority of descent." Structural equivalents: father:son :: older brother:younger brother :: chief:commoner.

20. **Qualitative disjunction** (p. 34): "A qualitative disjunction exists between chief and people, a disjunction clearly marked by differential access to sumptuary items, by prescribed behaviour, and by distinctive ritual behaviour."

21. **Range of stratification** (pp. 35-36): From Pukapuka (minimal: chief was sacred but had same type of house, no elaborate tabus) through Futuna (more elaborate paramount, lineage chiefs) to Tonga/Society Islands/Hawai'i (extreme stratification).

### Table 2: Social stratification in Polynesian societies (p. 37)

22. **Easter Island**: Population 3,500; Sahlins Group IIA; Goldman "Open."

23. **Goldman's "Open" classification for Easter Island**: "In the second system, which I call 'Open', seniority has been modified to allow military and political effectiveness to govern status and political control. The Open system is more strongly military and political than religious and stability in it must be maintained more directly by the exercise of secular powers. In the Open, status differences are no longer regularly graded but tend to be sharply defined" (p. 36, summarizing Goldman 1970:20). [CROSS-REFERENCE: Goldman 1970 processed 2026-03-16. Goldman's full Easter Island chapter (pp. 93-122) finds: "had never evolved a central authority" (p. 94); redistribution lay "not with the ariki mau, but with heads of commoner groups" (pp. 114-115); no central authority undertook local monuments (pp. 106-107); ariki mau "probably never a powerful secular ruler" (p. 115); political tradition "separatist" (p. 119). See `docs/references/claims/goldman_1970_claims.md`.]

24. **Population figure discrepancy**: Table 2 (p. 37) gives Easter Island population as 3,500. Chapter 11 (p. 271) gives "about 7,000." The Table 2 figure may represent the "largest political unit" rather than total island population, or may be an earlier estimate. Either way, the discrepancy is unresolved in the text.

### Ritual, polity, and production

25. **Chief as divine representative** (p. 37): "Closest to the gods, the chief is their earthly representative, the means by which supernatural efficacy, *mana*, is conveyed to the society at large."

26. **Ritual control of production** (p. 38): "The wresting of food and material goods from nature was for Polynesians a fundamentally religious process, precisely because the natural world was the realm of the gods." Production "intimately tied to ritual sanction and control" (p. 38).

27. **"Work of the Gods"** (p. 38): "The chief, naturally, occupied the central role in this ritual regulation of production." In Tikopia, the annual religious cycle ("Work of the Gods") was a "ritual codification of the island's production system" (Firth 1967).

28. **Tapu as economic tool** (pp. 165-166): "*Tapu* associated with Polynesian *ariki* was not merely a passive indicator of rank, it was actively used as an economic tool." Chiefs imposed *rahui* (interdictions) on resources, raising domestic production and reserving yield for collective purposes.

### Redistribution

29. **Redistribution** (p. 39): "most theorists (Service 1967; Sahlins 1958, 1968; Fried 1967) have regarded [redistribution] as a fundamental characteristic of the chiefdom level of society." Tribute redistributed back to people in less stratified societies; invested in chiefly establishments in more stratified ones.

30. **As stratification increased, redistribution decreased** (p. 167): "As the degree of social stratification increased in various Polynesian societies (in part, as a function of the chiefly ability to control production and accumulate surplus), the proportion of surplus actually redistributed to the people at large decreased."

31. **Kirch reverses Sahlins's functionalist argument** (p. 281): "We simply cannot attribute the existence of hierarchical political systems in Polynesia to such functional explanations as the need for chiefs as re-distributors of geographically dispersed resources (cf. Sahlins 1958), or as managers of irrigation networks which in turn resulted from 'population pressure'. Rather, the argument must run, in part at least, the other way around."

### APS as inherited template

32. **APS as carried cultural baggage** (p. 40): "These basic structural elements, and the contradictions and oppositions inherent in them, were carried as part of the 'cultural baggage' of every canoe-load of Polynesian voyagers who searched the Pacific for new landfalls."

33. **KEY passage** (p. 281): "Colonizing Polynesians in every case carried with them concepts of pyramidal social structure, of first fruits and tribute as obligatory to the chiefs, of chiefs as earthly representatives of ancestral deities, of differential access to resources, of domination by the chiefs over labour and the means of production, were present in all Polynesian societies from the beginning."

## III. Ancestral Polynesian Society Reconstruction (Ch. 3)

### Archaeological evidence

34. **No direct archaeological evidence of stratification in APS** (p. 63): "Direct archaeological data, in short, imply only dispersed hamlet groups composed of several households each, with no direct evidence of any significant public architecture." No Ancestral Polynesian cemeteries with differential mortuary treatment have been found.

35. **APS as transformation, not progress** (p. 62): "The course of social and political change in Oceania was one of *transformation*, and not of some kind of 'progress' from lesser to greater complexity."

### Lexical reconstruction of political institutions

36. **\*ariki** = "chief" (Table 6, p. 65): Reconstructable Proto-Polynesian term. "First born in the senior line, who succeeds to the chieftainship of the lineage and has strong personal 'mana' and 'tapu'" (Pawley 1979:20). The original meaning of *ariki* was "first born of the chief" (Koskinen 1960), and was "more clearly sacerdotal" in character (p. 64).

37. **\*toa** = "warrior" (Table 6, p. 65): Reconstructable term. "Here is the germ of a potential conflict between *'ariki* and *toa* over their respective claims to the sources of *mana* or power. As we shall see in later chapters, this conflict was in some Polynesian societies to come to the forefront of political transformation" (p. 64).

38. **\*mana, \*tapu** = "supernatural power," "sacred, prohibited" (Table 6, p. 65): Reconstructable Proto-Polynesian terms.

39. **\*kainanga** = "corporate, land-holding descent group" (Table 6, p. 65).

40. **\*fono** = "assembly of people" (Table 6, p. 65).

41. **\*mala'e** = "ceremonial area" (Table 6, p. 65): Antecedent to East Polynesian *marae* and Easter Island *ahu*.

42. **Hereditary chieftainship already in APS** (p. 64): "The lexical data clearly indicate that Ancestral Polynesian Society had already developed the institution of hereditary chieftainship."

43. **But: chieftainship was primarily religious/sacerdotal** (p. 64): "The 'more clearly sacerdotal' character of the 'oldest form of Polynesian *ariki* chieftainship' was also evident to Koskinen (1960:148). An ideology of direct, male descent from the gods, and of associated *mana* and *tapu* was the original mode of legitimation for Polynesian chieftainship."

## IV. Process Chapters

### Population (Ch. 5)

44. **Easter Island population estimate** (p. 271): "Various authors have suggested a maximum population of about 7,000 persons (a density of about 49/km²) though this may be an underestimate." No specific source cited for the 7,000 figure.

45. **Table 2 population** (p. 37): 3,500 as population of "largest political unit." This may represent a single territorial district rather than the island total.

### Environmental degradation (Ch. 6 / Ch. 11)

46. **Prior forest cover** (p. 268): "Palynological investigations of the peat deposits in Easter Island's crater lakes, by Flenley (1979, 1981), have demonstrated that prior to Polynesian colonization the island supported a more extensive forest cover, quite different from the treeless landscape seen by the early European voyagers."

47. **Deforestation sequence** (pp. 274, 280): Forest cleared during Ahu Moai Phase, leading to erosion, soil loss, reduced crop yields. "The net effect must have been a decrease in crop yields" (p. 274, citing McCoy 1976:146). "Forest depletion further meant that there was a shortage of wood for canoe construction, 'resulting in the probable reduced intake of deep sea fish'" (p. 274).

### Production and the chieftainship (Ch. 7)

48. **Intensification as most significant evolutionary trend** (p. 160): "Of the several trends that we have isolated, this is doubtless the most significant for an evolutionary analysis of the Polynesian chiefdoms."

49. **Surplus definition** (p. 161): "By surplus we mean that portion of production which extends beyond the sphere of individual households, and which therefore finances the general public economy, and especially, the political actions of the chiefs."

50. **Production function model** (pp. 162-164): Kirch adopts Friedman's (1979) model dividing the production function into regions characterized by a hierarchy of constraints. Region I: maximal expansion; Region II: surplus declining relative to yield; Region III: surplus diminishing absolutely; Region IV: "over the hill."

51. **Easter Island placed in Regions III-IV** (implied, pp. 163-164, 280): Easter Island's ecological constraints meant the system reached the point where "surplus is now diminishing, even though demand is not, the tension between chiefs and the cultivators may become critical" (pp. 163-164). In the Epilogue: "In Easter Island, the political motivations of the chiefs ran up against the ecological reality of a too marginal environment" (p. 282).

52. **Chief's role in directing production** (p. 165): "The chief exercised significant power over production. The timing of agricultural calendars, for example, depended upon a seasonal cycle of ritual activity, determined by the chief."

53. **Corvée labor initiated by chiefs** (p. 166): Examples from Hawai'i (irrigation, fishponds), New Zealand (Maori public works), Society Islands (temple construction preceded by two-three year *rahui* to accumulate surplus). Easter Island is NOT cited as an example of chiefly corvée.

54. **Easter Island fowl production** (p. 179): "Easter Island provides an instance of the intensive production of fowl (pigs and dogs were unknown on the island, apparently not surviving the initial voyage of colonization)." Chickens raised for feasts and as social currency. *Hare moa* (stone chicken houses) as adaptation to prevent theft (McCoy 1976:23-6, 87).

### Competition and conflict (Ch. 8)

55. **Warfare as fundamental process** (p. 14, Ch. 1): "Trend 8: militarism" listed among nine dominant trends in Polynesian prehistory. Warfare is a "truly fundamental" dynamic.

56. **\*toa (warrior) as ancestral concept** (p. 282): "That the concept of warrior existed in Easter Island is something owed to the ancestral Polynesian heritage, but that *matatoa* should rise to such a position of local political prominence does have a great deal to do with the particular historical interplay of population and production in a marginal, constrained environment."

## V. Comparative Cases

### Tonga (Ch. 9)

57. **Dual chieftainship**: Sacred paramount (Tu'i Tonga) separated from secular ruler (Hau). This structural separation parallels the Hawaiian ali'i-'ai-moku / kalaimoku distinction (p. 262, Fig. 87).

58. **Tribute and inter-island networks**: Tonga's political economy relied on a "magazine economy" with intensive arboriculture and dryfield cropping (pp. 279-280). Storage and inter-island redistribution.

### Hawai'i (Ch. 10)

59. **The exemplary chiefdom**: Largest population (30,000 in Table 2), most elaborate stratification (Goldman: "stratified"). Irrigation, ahupua'a territorial system, peripatetic ali'i-'ai-moku.

60. **Irrigation and chiefly competition** (pp. 175-176): Hawaiian irrigation complexes demonstrate the link between agricultural intensification and chiefly political demands. "In both Halawa and Halele'a, it is clear that 'the right to hold land relied on an individual's ability to work for the konohiki'" (Earle 1978:187).

61. **Fishponds as chiefly symbols** (p. 180): "Fishponds became symbols of the chiefly right to conspicuous consumption and to ownership of the land and its resources. They were manifestations of the chief's political power and his ability to control and tap his resources" (Kikuchi 1976:299).

## VI. Easter Island Chapter (Ch. 11) — EVERY CLAIM

### Opening framing

62. **Tragic narrative** (p. 264): "Easter Island is the story of a society which — temporarily but brilliantly surpassing its limits — crashed devastatingly."

63. **Already declining by 1722** (p. 264): "by the time of Roggeveen's discovery in 1722 — already begun a downward spiral of cultural regression."

64. **Métraux as primary ethnographic source** (p. 264): "A brilliant job of salvage ethnography by Métraux (1940) and others."

### Environmental description

65. **Isolation** (p. 264): "3,703 km west of South America, and 1,819 km east of Pitcairn Island, Easter is among the loneliest, most isolated islands on earth."

66. **Ecological marginality** (pp. 264-265): Volcanic island, ~160 km²; no permanent streams; porous volcanic soil; crater lakes far from main settlement areas; rainfall 1,250-1,500 mm; no fringing reef; sea cliffs predominate; fishing limited (~140 species vs. ~450 in Hawai'i).

67. **Drought frequency** (p. 265): "drought years are not uncommon."

68. **Irrigation impossible** (p. 265): "Lacking permanent watercourses, irrigation was not technologically possible."

### Literature and archaeological history

69. **Sources cited** (pp. 265-266): Routledge (1919, 1920), Englert (1948, 1970), Barthel (1971, 1978), Lavachery (1939), Métraux (1940), Heyerdahl (1975), Norwegian Expedition (Heyerdahl and Ferdon 1961, 1965), Mulloy, Ayres (1973, 1975), McCoy (1976, 1979).

70. **Three-phase cultural sequence** (p. 266): Norwegian Expedition proposed Early, Middle, Late Periods. Ayres (1973) revised. Mulloy & Figueroa (1978:131-8) showed supposed Early-Middle distinction "not supported" by additional evidence. Ayres's revision preferred by Kirch.

### Colonization and early developments (pp. 266-269)

71. **Colonization date** (p. 267): "a date in the first few centuries AD would be entirely reasonable." Earliest ¹⁴C date (AD 235-615, Poike Ditch) "is not in direct association with any evidence for human activity."

72. **Linguistic evidence** (p. 267): Retention of Proto-Polynesian velar nasal indicates early departure from central East Polynesia.

73. **Reduced crop/animal roster** (p. 268): "the Polynesian crop and domestic animal roster was significantly reduced." Coconut, breadfruit, various fruit trees not transferred. Only chickens and rat. "This reduction in the variety of domestic carbohydrate and protein sources is particularly significant in light of the island's restricted natural food resources."

74. **Prior forest** (p. 268): Flenley's palynological evidence of prior forest cover. "In the initial stages of human occupation, one may suppose that an adequate supply of wood for fuel and industrial purposes existed."

75. **Ahu Tahai and Vinapu 2** (p. 268): Earliest constructions, "both dating to about the ninth century AD." Easter Island ahu is "a variant of the East Polynesian *marae* complex."

76. **Single continuously developing society** (p. 269): Mulloy and Figueroa (1978:137) concluded ahu sequence reflects "unbroken chronological progression such as might be expected from the architectural reflection of the activities of a single continuously developing society." Kirch concurs: "Early" and "Middle" Period distinction in culture history "no longer holds up."

### The Ahu Moai Phase (AD 1000-1500) — pp. 269-273

77. **Ahu Moai Phase defined** (p. 269): "By the beginning of the second millennium AD, the construction of *ahu* in various areas around the island's coast was in full swing (ultimately some 245 *ahu* were to be built)." Named after characteristic statues (*moai*). Ayres dates phase AD 1000-1680; McCoy suggests AD 1550; Kirch prefers AD 1500.

78. **Ahu architecture** (p. 269): Flat-topped platforms up to 60 metres long and 7 metres high, well-faced on seaward side, ramps descending to rectangular plaza. Significant solar orientations possible.

79. **Moai production** (p. 269): "About 324 statues were actually raised on *ahu*, or in the process of being transported, and another 200 in various stages of completion have been recorded in the quarries (Englert 1970:121-3)."

80. **KEY CLAIM: Ahu as descent group structures** (p. 270): "Virtually all ethnographic and archaeological authorities agree that the *ahu* were conceived, constructed, and used by local descent groups, and do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority."

81. **KEY CLAIM: Quarry as decentralized production** (pp. 270-271): Rano Raraku quarry "suggests 'the activities of many independently working crews, probably sent periodically by their kin groups, rather than the activities of a single work force under centralized direction' (Mulloy and Figueroa 1978:134)."

82. **Ahu as segmentary pattern** (p. 271): "The distribution of the *ahu*, and their integration with other components of the total settlement pattern (to be discussed below), further reflect a typical Polynesian segmentary pattern, with each *ahu* representing the religious focus of an ancestor-based kin group."

83. **Moai as ancestral commemoration** (p. 271): "The statues themselves probably commemorated 'illustrious ancestors of the various kin-groups, whose mana was thought to bring benefit to the communities' (Englert 1970:108)."

84. **Sahlins 1955 cited** (p. 271): "Sahlins (1955) proposed an hypothesis regarding the 'esoteric efflorescence' represented by the image-*ahu*, which also supports the interpretation of these structures as products of individual ramages or lineages." Sahlins's environmental channeling argument accepted.

85. **Population estimate of ~7,000** (p. 271): "Various authors have suggested a maximum population of about 7,000 persons (a density of about 49/km²) though this may be an underestimate." No specific author named for this figure.

86. **Intensive dryland cultivation** (p. 271): Sweet potato, yams, taro, bananas. "The accounts of early European explorers hint at the intensive nature of cultivation practices, though the ethnographic record on agriculture is, in general, rather poor."

87. **No field boundaries** (p. 271): "Curiously, there appear to be few stone or earthen bank field boundaries such as those described earlier for West Hawai'i." *Manavai* (walled garden plots) present.

88. **Fishing constraints** (pp. 271-272): Deep-sea fishing "largely a monopoly of the occupants of the western and northern areas, particularly the high-ranking Miru clan." Winter *tapu* on fish (Métraux 1940:173). Association of fishhooks with *ahu* crematoria (Ayres 1979).

89. **Settlement pattern** (p. 272): Coastal *ahu* marking territory of segmentary ramage. Radial *kainga* divisions. *Hare paenga* (boat-shaped houses) near coast for high-ranking members and priests (*ivi-atua*). *Hare nui* as secular community house. Commoners dispersed inland.

90. **"Developed form of the Ancestral Polynesian segmentary ramage system"** (p. 272): "During the Ahu Moai Phase, Easter Island political organization seems to have been dominated by a developed form of the Ancestral Polynesian segmentary ramage system, manifested so elegantly in the image-*ahu* themselves."

91. **Miru as maximal ramage** (p. 272): "One maximal ramage, the Miru — direct descendants of the colonizing chief Hotu-Matu'a — was recognized as of necessarily higher rank and provided a paramount chief, the *ariki-mau*."

92. **Ariki-mau's power qualified** (p. 272): "This individual may have exercised more ritual than secular power, however."

93. **Social categories** (p. 272): "Other important social categories included the priests, *ivi-atua* ('bones of the gods'), and the warriors, *matatoa*."

94. **Matatoa foreshadowed** (p. 272): "As we shall see, the succeeding phase of Easter Island prehistory was to witness a reversal of this political system, with the rise of the *matatoa* to frightening power and social control."

95. **Rongorongo** (p. 273): "an indigenous concept and quite probably dates to sometime in the Ahu Moai Phase." Close cognates elsewhere in East Polynesia: Mangareva, Marquesas.

### Decadent (Huri Ahu) Phase (AD 1500-1722) — pp. 274-278

96. **Overpopulation as proximate cause** (p. 274): "By all archaeological indications and in the view of most authorities, by the close of the Ahu Moai Phase Easter Island had reached a state of over-population, which was the proximate cause leading to the ensuing phase of chronic inter-tribal warfare and social disintegration."

97. **Environmental degradation** (p. 274): Population density linked to environmental deterioration in a "negative feedback cycle." Forest clearing, soil erosion, reduced crop yields, reduced deep-sea fishing due to wood shortage for canoes.

98. **Mata'a** (p. 274): Obsidian spearheads "suddenly appears in great profusion in sites and deposits of this Phase." Englert: "they used to be scattered over the surface of the island in endless numbers. Tremendous quantities of them must have been made" (1970:139).

99. **Cave fortification** (p. 274): Volcanic lava tubes and caves modified as "defensible, permanent habitations," sealed with fortification walls, single-file entry only.

100. **Statue toppling** (p. 275): "cessation of new image-*ahu* construction, and the deliberate destruction of the existing *ahu* and tipping over of the statues." Ayres (1973:132): "almost all the statues were tipped over forward toward the court."

101. **Ahu destruction as mana obliteration** (p. 276): "Englert plausibly speculates that this rampant destruction, which undid the toil of countless ancestors of the various lineages, was the work of enemy groups seeking to obliterate the *mana* of their opponents."

102. **"Class revolt" hypothesis** (p. 276): "Equally plausible is the possibility of a class revolt by the commoners against the excessive demands by an oppressive chiefly class, particularly as the ability to provide an agricultural surplus became increasingly difficult."

103. **Dual coalition** (p. 277): "The warring tribes and ramages organized themselves into a loose dual coalition, Tu'u in the west and Hotu-Iti in the east, the two groups constantly opposing each other."

104. **Ariki-mau decline** (p. 277): "The former secular power of the *ariki-mau* declined to the point where he became solely a religious figure, a necessary link between the gods and the declining fertility of the land."

105. **Matatoa ascendancy** (p. 277): Englert: "The most characteristic theme of the traditions of this period, as I have heard them, is that of the ascendancy to power of the matatoa... the fear they inspired and the prestige they gained enabled them to acquire ever greater control over the ariki and the priesthoods, who formerly provided through their mana the sources of social control" (1970:137-8).

106. **Kio class** (p. 277): "Victorious *matatoa* and their groups virtually enslaved those they conquered, creating the *kio* class of persons 'obliged to serve their conquerors or to pay tribute to them with the produce of their lands' (Métraux 1940:139)."

107. **Birdman cult** (p. 277): "*Manutara* or 'bird-man' cult revolved around the ceremonial centre of Orongo." Annual island-wide competition between *matatoa* factions to find first egg of sooty tern (*Sterna fuscata*). Winner declared *tangata manu*, "sacred cult-leader for the ensuing year, his group taking *de facto* control over the island."

108. **McCall quote on birdman** (p. 277): "Instead of a king for life, they opted for one elected by ordeal. Instead of many ancestors who descended from many ancestors, they propounded a single god [Makemake], whose image they carved in deserted rocks, on the bases of felled-toppled figures, and even around water-holes" (1980:38-9).

109. **Social order "as fragile as the shells"** (p. 278): McCall notes "the social order bestowed annually by the selection of the bird man at Orongo was 'as fragile as the shells of the eggs that were the focus of the annual quest' (1980:38)."

110. **Final passage** (p. 278): "through a combination of internal conflict and degeneration, exacerbated by foreign predation, the culture of Easter Island went full circle — from a small band of Polynesian colonists on the shores of a new land, through remarkable heights of cultural brilliance, to a once again small and pitiful band striving to surmount yet another challenge to their island lifeways."

### Sources cited for Easter Island claims

111. **Ethnographic sources**: Métraux 1940 (cited ~15 times in Ch. 11); Englert 1970 (cited ~8 times); Routledge 1919/1920 (cited ~3 times). No direct citation of Thomson 1891, Roussel 1869, or any contact-era primary accounts (Roggeveen, González, Cook, La Pérouse).

112. **Archaeological sources**: McCoy 1976, 1979 (cited ~6 times); Mulloy and Figueroa 1978 (cited ~4 times); Ayres 1973, 1975, 1979 (cited ~6 times); Heyerdahl and Ferdon 1961, 1965 (cited ~3 times); Flenley 1979, 1981 (cited ~2 times).

113. **Sahlins 1955**: Cited once (p. 271), for the "esoteric efflorescence" hypothesis.

114. **Goldman not cited in Easter Island chapter**: Goldman's "Open" classification for Easter Island (Table 2, p. 37) is not discussed in the Easter Island chapter itself.

### Bibliographic analysis: what the source base means

115. **The entire Easter Island chapter rests on three ethnographic sources and five archaeological ones.** Of the ethnographic sources, all three are post-slave-raid (Métraux 1940, Englert 1970, Routledge 1919/1920), recorded 50-75+ years after the 1862-1863 slave raids reduced the population from ~3,000 to fewer than 200. No contact-era primary account is cited anywhere in the Easter Island discussion.

116. **What is excluded by this source base.** Four European expeditions observed Easter Island between 1722 and 1786, collectively spending several weeks on the island. Their accounts are the only direct observations of Easter Island society before the catastrophic demographic disruption. These accounts record: no institutional chieftainship observed (all four expeditions); active search for chiefs yielding negative results (La Pérouse 1786); explicit confusion about political authority (Cook 1774, "confess myself quite ignorant"). Kirch does not engage with any of this material. The reader of Chapter 11 has no way to know that four independent European expeditions failed to identify the chieftainship the chapter assumes.

117. **Selective use of Routledge.** Routledge 1919 is cited only for the birdman cult (p. 277). Her broader political findings are absent: "no chiefs nor any form of government" (p. 224); the ariki-mau was "neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest" (p. 242); no kio class, no four-class hierarchy, no king list, no tribute. Routledge's is the most extensive early ethnography and the first not filtered through Alexander Salmon. The portions of her work that challenge the chiefdom interpretation are not cited.

118. **Selective use of Métraux.** Métraux 1940 is the dominant source (~15 citations), used for specific ethnographic details: fishing tapu (p. 173), kio class (p. 139), matatoa traditions, agricultural practices. But Métraux's overall political assessment is absent. Métraux concluded that the ariki-mau was "certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (1937, p. 58; 1940, p. 136), that government was "decentralized and localized in the hands of the tribal chiefs" (1940, p. 131), and that Roussel's claim of ancient absolute royal power was an "entirely gratuitous assumption" (1937, p. 58). None of these conclusions appear in Kirch's chapter.

119. **Métraux 1937 not cited.** Kirch cites only Métraux 1940, not the 1937 article "The Kings of Easter Island," which contains Métraux's most focused and critical assessment of the kingship question. The 1937 article explicitly evaluates and rejects the chiefdom-supporting claims from Roussel and Thomson; the 1940 monograph incorporates these conclusions but distributes them across a 463-page text where they are easier to overlook.

120. **Métraux 1957 not cited.** Métraux's popular synthesis contains his clearest formulations: monument transport organized by "heads of lineages" through competitive feast sponsorship ("immense pleasure party"); the ariki-mau's political authority "not clear"; matatoa had "much stronger" political position than Polynesian warriors elsewhere. These passages directly describe the decentralized, competitive lineage-level production model that our argument advances.

121. **Thomson 1891 not cited.** Thomson is the source for the "arbitrary monarchy" characterization that most chiefdom attributions ultimately rely on. But Thomson simultaneously qualified this: no tax, no homage paid, independent clans. His 57-king list is irreconcilable with Roussel's 22 names. Thomson's account was filtered through Alexander Salmon as translator and informant. By not citing Thomson, Kirch avoids both the chiefdom-supporting "arbitrary monarchy" claim and the qualifications that undermine it.

122. **Englert 1970 as unexamined authority.** Englert is the second most-cited ethnographic source (~8 citations). Father Sebastian Englert lived on Easter Island for decades and his *La tierra de Hotu Matu'a* (1948, revised 1970) is a major compilation of oral traditions. However, Englert was a Capuchin priest working with a post-missionary, post-raid population; his accounts of pre-contact political organization are reconstructions, not observations. Kirch treats Englert's reports of matatoa traditions and social disintegration (1970:137-8, 139, 142) as straightforward historical evidence without assessing the chain of transmission. Englert 1948/1970 is not yet in our pipeline, so we cannot independently verify Kirch's use of it.

123. **McCoy 1976 cited selectively.** McCoy is cited for settlement patterns, agricultural adaptation, and demographic stress, but his actual conclusion about the chiefdom question is absent from Kirch's discussion. McCoy concluded: "If there existed any tendencies toward the development of a chiefdom, the political flux caused by the ascendancy of the warrior class to positions of authority clearly retarded it" (1976:145). McCoy also noted that Sahlins himself treated Easter Island as an "exception" to his chiefdom model (1976:152). Kirch cites McCoy's settlement data while omitting his political conclusions.

124. **Goldman 1970 present but unengaged.** Table 2 (p. 37) displays Goldman's classification of Easter Island as "Open" alongside Sahlins's Group IIA. The "Open" category is defined as military/political effectiveness governing status, with status differences "no longer regularly graded but tend to be sharply defined" (p. 36). This classification aligns with the matatoa-dominated system every post-raid ethnographer describes. Yet Goldman's classification appears only in this comparative table and is never discussed in the Easter Island chapter.

125. **The source base creates a self-reinforcing loop.** By relying exclusively on post-slave-raid ethnographies read through the APS inheritance framework, and by not engaging with contact-era accounts that fail to find chieftainship, or with the political conclusions of his own ethnographic sources (Routledge, Métraux, McCoy), Kirch's Easter Island chapter presents a chiefdom interpretation that appears empirically grounded while depending on a curated subset of the available evidence. The sources that challenge the interpretation are either not cited or cited only for details that do not bear on the political question.

## VII. Epilogue (Ch. 12) — pp. 279-286

115. **Three divergent transformations** (p. 279): Tonga, Hawai'i, and Easter Island as "three independent pathways of technological, social, and political evolution which led in each instance to a divergent transformation of Ancestral Polynesian Society."

116. **Easter Island's severe constraints** (p. 280): "In contrast with Hawai'i and Tonga, isolated Easter Island posed far greater and more severe ecological constraints." Crop and domestic animal roster reduced; impoverished marine environment; irrigation barred. "The tension between ecological constraint and economic activity was acute, contributing ultimately to late prehistoric social and political disintegration."

117. **APS as inherited template (KEY)** (p. 281): Full passage at claim #33 above. "Neither the Tongans, Hawaiians, nor Easter Islanders invented the position of *ariki* or its related concepts of *mana* and *tapu*. Ancestral Polynesian Society was already hierarchically structured, and the demand for surplus was an integral part of the political economy."

118. **Political motivations vs. ecological reality** (p. 282): "In Easter Island, the political motivations of the chiefs ran up against the ecological reality of a too marginal environment, but not before a growing population had been induced to raise multi-ton monuments to the glory of ancestral gods."

119. **Moai as expression of competition** (p. 283): "could one on such bases, possibly explain why these people should have turned their gazes to a sheer volcanic cliff, and imagined in its matrix giant stone images, and furthermore proceeded to the immense task of carving, transporting, and erecting these monoliths as their unique expression of competition?"

120. **Competition and *toa*** (p. 282): "While competition can certainly be analysed in its ecological and demographic context, and while population increases and the political design of chiefs for aggrandizement of power and hegemony of power were real incentives to conquest, we must recognize that such functionalist explanations cannot fully account for the nature and importance of competition in Polynesia."

121. **Paradigm self-awareness** (p. 283): Kirch quotes Keesing (1982): "when we commit ourselves to a paradigm, perhaps powerful but inevitably sharply limited in scope, we try to pretend that it is global, even total" (1982:32).

## IX. Key Claims for Our Argument

### 1. Does Kirch acknowledge Sahlins's redistribution gap?

**No.** Sahlins's admission that there was "little direct evidence" of redistribution for Easter Island (1954, p. 101) is nowhere acknowledged. The redistribution framework is assumed to apply via APS inheritance (p. 281).

### 2. Does Kirch cite contact-era accounts directly?

**No.** No contact-era primary sources (Roggeveen, González, Cook, La Pérouse) are cited in the Easter Island chapter. All Easter Island claims are mediated through post-raid ethnographic sources (Métraux 1940, Englert 1970, Routledge 1919/1920) and archaeological reports.

### 3. How does he characterize the ariki-mau's power?

**Qualified**: "may have exercised more ritual than secular power" (p. 272). This is consistent with Métraux 1937 and Routledge 1919, but Kirch does not cite their stronger conclusions ("certainly not a ruler or civilian chief"; "no chiefs nor any form of government").

### 4. Archaeological evidence for chiefdom vs. inferred from ethnography?

**Almost entirely inferred from ethnography.** The archaeological evidence presented (ahu distribution, settlement patterns, moai production, mata'a, cave fortification) is interpreted through the ethnographic framework. The two passages that describe direct archaeological evidence (pp. 270-271) actually support decentralized, lineage-level production rather than chiefly command.

### 5. Does he acknowledge Routledge's "no chiefs" finding?

**No.** Routledge is cited only for the birdman cult (Orongo). Her explicit finding that there were "no chiefs nor any form of government" (1919:224) is absent.

### 6. Does he cite Métraux's "decentralized" conclusion?

**No.** Métraux's conclusion that government was "decentralized and localized in the hands of the tribal chiefs" (1940:131) is not cited. Kirch uses Métraux for specific ethnographic details but not for his overall political assessment.

### 7. What is his source for the 7,000 population estimate?

**Unspecified.** "Various authors" (p. 271). Sahlins used Métraux's 3,000-4,000. Table 2 (p. 37) gives 3,500. The 7,000 figure appears in Ch. 11 without specific attribution.

### 8. How does he describe moai production?

**Lineage-level, decentralized.** Kirch explicitly quotes Mulloy & Figueroa (1978:134) on "independently working crews, probably sent periodically by their kin groups" (p. 270-271). He also states ahu "were conceived, constructed, and used by local descent groups, and do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority" (p. 270). These passages directly support decentralized production.

### 9. The key passage on decentralized production (p. 270)

"Virtually all ethnographic and archaeological authorities agree that the *ahu* were conceived, constructed, and used by local descent groups, and do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority."

**Significance for our argument**: This is the author of THE chiefdom framework for Polynesia stating that the primary archaeological feature of Easter Island (ahu/moai) was produced by descent groups, not centralized authority. The chiefdom label is applied despite this explicit acknowledgment that the material record does not support centralized command.

### 10. How does he reconcile decentralized production with the chiefdom framework?

**Through the APS inheritance argument** (pp. 272, 281). Kirch argues that Easter Island was "dominated by a developed form of the Ancestral Polynesian segmentary ramage system" (p. 272), and that all Polynesian societies inherited chiefdom institutions from APS. The chiefdom label is maintained not because the archaeological evidence demonstrates it, but because the ancestral template is assumed to persist. The tension is never explicitly addressed.

## XI. Honest Complications

1. **Kirch is more honest than his framework about decentralized production.** The p. 270 passage explicitly states ahu were descent group structures, not centralized authority. The Mulloy & Figueroa quote on independently working crews (pp. 270-271) directly supports our argument. Kirch includes this evidence while still applying the chiefdom label.

2. **The Goldman "Open" classification is in the book.** Table 2 (p. 37) presents Goldman's classification of Easter Island as "Open" alongside Sahlins's Group IIA. This should have created tension with the chiefdom framing, but Kirch never addresses it in the Easter Island chapter.

3. **The ariki-mau qualification is significant.** Kirch's "may have exercised more ritual than secular power" (p. 272) is consistent with the fuller picture from Routledge and Métraux. But he does not draw out the implication: if the paramount exercised primarily ritual power, and the production system was decentralized to descent groups, what exactly makes this a chiefdom?

4. **The APS argument is sophisticated.** Kirch is not simply asserting that Easter Island was a chiefdom because it had big monuments. He argues that chiefdom institutions were carried to Easter Island as part of the ancestral cultural template (p. 281). This is a stronger argument than Sahlins's synchronic comparison, and requires a different response: we need to address whether inherited institutional categories can persist unchanged through the radical environmental and demographic transformation that Easter Island experienced.

5. **Kirch explicitly reverses Sahlins's functionalist redistribution argument.** The passage on p. 281 states that the argument "must run, in part at least, the other way around" from Sahlins's claim that chiefs arose because of the need for redistribution. Kirch argues chiefs and redistribution were already present in the ancestral template. This is actually closer to our position (that redistribution did not develop independently on Easter Island) than to Sahlins's (that redistribution must have existed because the chiefdom required it).

6. **The ecocide narrative is now largely overturned.** Kirch's central narrative for the Decadent Phase (overpopulation → environmental degradation → warfare → statue toppling) has been challenged by DiNapoli et al. 2020 (steady ahu construction tempo), Davis et al. 2024 (rock gardens 1/5 of estimates), Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 (no population collapse), and Mulrooney 2013 (settlement continuity). The chronological scaffolding for the three-phase scheme has been substantially undermined.

7. **The population estimate of 7,000 is unsourced and challenged.** Davis et al. 2024 finds agricultural carrying capacity of ~3,000-3,900. Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 genetic data consistent with gradual growth to ~1,500-3,000, not 7,000. The 7,000 figure that drives the overpopulation narrative lacks an identified source.
