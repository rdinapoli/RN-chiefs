## I. Intellectual Genealogy

### Who Stevenson et al. cite

1. **McCoy 1976**: Lithic assemblages, artifact typology; also cited for obsidian cores and blades, and for population nucleation pattern in areas inland of Hanga Poukura and Hanga Tee (pp. 120, 121, 124).

2. **Mulloy 1961**: Previous lithic assemblage research, Ceremonial Center of Vinapu (p. 120).

3. **Smith 1961**: Two habitation caves, previous lithic research (p. 120).

4. **Metraux 1940**: Cited for conflicts "near the end of the Expansion Phase (AD 1000-1680)" and for oral traditions stating that "around AD 1680, major transformations occurred in the socio-political structure" (pp. 120, 124).

5. **Stevenson 1984**: Self-citation for the dating of residential structures and the population growth curve used for obsidian consumption analysis (pp. 123, 124). This is the dissertation.

6. **Michels et al., in press**: Obsidian hydration rate (6.29U²/Yr⁻¹) used for all dating (p. 123).

7. **Crabtree 1972**: Lithic terminology, discoidal core definition (p. 121).

### Notable absences

- **No chiefdom terminology**: "Chiefdom" does not appear anywhere in the paper.
- **No Service 1962, Sahlins, Kirch 1984**: None of the chiefdom-model architects are cited.
- **No Goldman 1970**: Unlike the 1984 dissertation, Goldman is not referenced here, but the paper is purely technical.
- **No contact-era sources**: No Roggeveen, González, Cook, or La Pérouse.

## II. Factual Claims (Archaeological Data)

### Quarry description

8. Orito quarry is one of three obsidian outcrops on the southwestern corner of Easter Island (p. 120).

9. The quarry consists of shallow depressions or open-pit mines distributed across the slopes of Mt. Orito (p. 120).

10. No pits exceeded a metre in depth; the majority were shallower (p. 120).

11. The study area is on the north slope of Mt. Orito, but quarrying activity was noted around the circumference of the hill (p. 120).

12. A total of 33 pits were mapped in the survey area (p. 120).

13. Obsidian is commonly found as tabular slabs, larger pieces 20-30 cm long, usually about 10 cm thick (pp. 120-121).

### Excavation data

14. A single mining pit (Pit 1) was excavated via a 7 x 1 m trench through the center, down the slope (p. 120).

15. Surface configuration: shallow pit with debris thrown or pushed downslope to create a low ridge; same configuration as other mining pits (p. 120).

16. All flaking was done by percussion; hammerstones found among mining debris (p. 120).

17. Pit 1 excavation recovered: 6 mataa, 2 hammerstones, 11 block cores, 7 discoidal cores (p. 121).

18. Test trench contained 1,268 flakes with striking platforms and 3,171 fragments without striking platforms (p. 122).

### Lithic reduction sequence

19. Two core types produced, constrained by raw material dimensions: discoidal cores (from thin slabs) and block cores (from thick slabs) (p. 121).

20. Discoidal cores: bifacially worked, circular with lenticular cross-section, flakes removed from both faces and all directions; produced medium to small flakes for domestic tools (p. 121).

21. Block cores: unifacial, unidirectional or bidirectional, from thicker slabs; produced large broad flakes for mataa manufacture (p. 121).

22. Discoidal cores were prepared at the quarry, then transported to habitation sites where flakes were produced when needed (p. 121).

23. Mataa manufacture occurred primarily at the quarry locality, supported by recovery of 6 completed mataa and many others in final stages of manufacture on the surface (pp. 121-122).

24. Akahanga habitation debitage shows 75% tertiary flakes and low frequency of primary/secondary flakes >5 cm, supporting hypothesis of preliminary core shaping at quarry (p. 121).

### Obsidian hydration dates (Orito quarry)

25. 11 of 33 mines in the northeastern sector were chosen for dating; small hand-excavated test probes (25 cm³) taken from center of each; average 3 specimens per probe randomly selected (p. 123).

26. 33 hydration rim values obtained (Table 1), using rate of 6.29U²/Yr⁻¹ (p. 123).

27. Earliest hydration rim: AD 988 ± 81 (2.50U ± .1) (p. 123).

28. Latest date: AD 1675 ± 46 (1.39U ± .1), with exception of two very late dates (p. 123).

29. Dates indicate an extended period of obsidian mining and reduction at this locality (p. 123).

### Obsidian consumption data (Akahanga residential area)

30. 184 residential sites (66%) from Quadrangles 7 and 11, located inland of Akahanga, randomly selected and dated (p. 123).

31. Average of 9-10 obsidian specimens dated per site (p. 123).

32. Each obsidian date assigned to one of eleven 50-year phases, AD 1350-1864 (p. 123).

33. Consumption ratio = total frequency of dated specimens per phase / number of separate occupations per phase (p. 123).

34. **Consumption rises during first six phases (300 years) until around AD 1650**, then decreases slightly in Phase 7, then rises to higher consumption in Phases 8-10, then drops dramatically after AD 1850 (p. 123, Table 2, Fig. 6).

35. Phase-by-phase data (Table 2):
- Phase 1 (AD 1350): 42 occupations, 42 specimens, ratio 1.00
- Phase 6 (AD 1601): 102 occupations, 157 specimens, ratio 1.54
- Phase 7 (AD 1651): 90 occupations, 129 specimens, ratio 1.43
- Phase 8 (AD 1701): 113 occupations, 192 specimens, ratio 1.70
- Phase 9 (AD 1751): 112 occupations, 193 specimens, ratio 1.72
- Phase 10 (AD 1801): 108 occupations, 183 specimens, ratio 1.69
- Phase 11 (AD 1851): 40 occupations, 51 specimens, ratio 1.21
- Total: 935 occupations, 1,388 specimens, mean ratio 1.42

## III. Interpretive Claims

### Territorial control

36. "Territorial control over the three obsidian flows may have been a factor during the later parts of prehistory since there is documentation, both historical and archaeological, of conflicts arising near the end of the Expansion Phase (AD 1000-1680) (Metraux 1940)" (p. 120). Note hedged language: "may have been a factor."

37. Terminal date of AD 1675 ± 46 from the mines "may be misleading. It does suggest that the subsurface extraction of obsidian halted, at least in the northeast section of the quarry. Either open-pit mining continued at other localities on Orito or Rano Kau, or the acquisition of obsidian became less intensive" (p. 124).

### Post-AD 1680 changes

38. "Oral traditions recorded by Metraux (1940) state that, around AD 1680, major transformations occurred in the socio-political structure of the island population. Although the nature of these changes is currently unclear, they are correlated with shifts in religious orientation, interment practices, and the decline in architectural manifestations of differences in rank" (p. 124).

39. The slight decline in population (measured by obsidian consumption) about AD 1650-1700 "may reflect a higher death rate or slight population displacement from the central part of the island" (p. 124).

### Post-AD 1700 increase

40. Rise in obsidian consumption during Phases 8-10 corresponds with increased population within Quadrangles 7 and 11; "may reflect increased population nucleation in this late phase, a pattern also noticed by McCoy (1976)" (p. 124).

41. Two hypotheses offered for increased obsidian use post-1700 (p. 124):
- (a) Population increase coupled with nucleation may have resulted in larger residential units and increased intensity of domestic activities.
- (b) "Political and social instability may have resulted in the inability of a specific group to control access to resources, such as obsidian, within its own territory. Thus, greater access to the obsidian quarries may have promoted its use now that costs associated with acquiring the material had been reduced."

42. Both hypotheses explicitly left unevaluated: "These, and other hypotheses, await evaluation" (p. 124).

## IV. Claims Relevant to the Chiefdom Question

### What the paper does NOT say

43. **No chiefdom terminology**: The paper does not use "chiefdom," "chief," "hierarchy," "stratification," "redistribution," or any sociopolitical typological term.

44. **No centralized control over quarry**: The paper documents quarrying activity around the entire circumference of Mt. Orito (p. 120), with 33 mapped pits in just the northeastern sector. No evidence of restricted access, specialized production areas, or elite control over obsidian procurement is presented or implied.

45. **No evidence of restricted distribution**: The reduction sequence shows cores prepared at quarry and transported to habitation sites; the Akahanga comparison suggests this was a routine pattern available to habitation communities.

### What the paper DOES imply about resource access

46. The territorial control hypothesis is raised but immediately hedged ("may have been a factor") and is attributed to the later prehistoric period only, based on Metraux's oral traditions about post-1680 conflicts (p. 120).

47. The alternative hypothesis for increased post-1700 obsidian use (claim 41b) explicitly suggests that political instability may have *reduced* territorial control over obsidian, increasing access. This hypothesis treats territorial restriction as a prior condition that was subsequently relaxed, not as a chiefdom-era management system.

48. The paper is entirely consistent with the Stevenson 1984 dissertation framework: corporate descent groups with autonomous access to resources, no centralized authority, and the post-1680 period as one of political decentralization rather than chiefly consolidation.

## V. Methodological Notes

49. **Obsidian hydration dating**: All dates depend on the 6.29U²/Yr⁻¹ rate from Michels et al. (in press). This rate has been subsequently revised; specific dates should be treated with caution, but relative patterns (consumption curve shape) remain informative.

50. **Single pit excavation**: Only one of 33 pits was excavated. The reduction sequence is preliminary and explicitly presented as such.

51. **Akahanga comparison**: The habitation site debitage sample is described as "small" (p. 121); the quarry-habitation divergence in reduction activities is presented as a hypothesis requiring further testing.
