# Claims Extraction: Routledge 1919

## Source Information

**Author**: Katherine Routledge (Mrs. Scoresby Routledge)

**Period on Easter Island**: March 1914 to August 1915 (approximately 17 months). The Mana Expedition, arriving on the yacht *Mana*.

**Publication**: *The Mystery of Easter Island: The Story of an Expedition*. London: Sifton, Praed & Co., 1919. 395 pages (588-page archive.org scan).

**PDF file**: `docs/references/pdfs/post_slave_raid/routledge_1919/Routledge_1919.pdf`

**Context**: Routledge arrived approximately 52 years after the 1862-1863 slave raids. The population was approximately 250. She worked with about a dozen elderly informants from multiple clans (Miru, Tupahotu, Marama, Ureohei), cross-examined them separately over 17 months, and learned the native language. Her principal interpreter was Juan Tepano (Tupahotu, ~40 years old, had served in the Chilean army). By the end of her stay she could conduct interviews directly in the native language with Tepano clarifying.

Routledge is the first post-raid ethnographer NOT intermediated through Alexander Salmon. Thomson (1886) and Geiseler (1882) both relied on Salmon as translator and cultural broker; Routledge worked directly with Rapanui informants. This independence is methodologically critical: convergences between Routledge and Thomson/Geiseler cannot be attributed to Salmon's framework, while divergences may reflect Salmon's distortions in the earlier accounts.

**[POST-RAID]**: ALL claims in this file derive from a source recorded ~52 years after the 1862-1863 slave raids. The population had collapsed from ~3,000 to ~111 (1877 nadir) and partially recovered to ~250 by 1914. Routledge herself notes: "the last of the ariki, or chiefs" were among those carried off to Peru (p. 205). Her informants were elderly survivors who had been children during the pre-missionary period (pre-1868). All ethnographic information about political organization is reconstructed from memory, not description of lived experience under functioning institutions.

**Key informants**:
- **Te Haha**: Miru clan, ariki-paka in Ngaara's entourage; "a handsome old man, connected with the court of the chief Ngaara" (p. 240). Most detailed single source for ariki-mau functions. Had begun to learn rongorongo but was reassigned to chicken-tending. Firsthand memories of life at Anakena.
- **Juan Tepano**: Tupahotu clan, ~40 years old; principal interpreter. Younger generation, useful for "summing up the general impression" but knowledge "a step further from the original" (p. 282).
- **Viriamo**: Ureohei clan; oldest woman living. Detailed life history spanning three marriages into three different clans. Carried food to Orongo. Former poki-manu.
- **Jotefa**: "Oldest man in the village." Alternative accounts of rongorongo assembly and manu ceremony.
- **Kapiera**: "One of the most reliable old men." Parents lived at Anakena. Explained the tau system.
- **Tomenika**: "Last man acquainted with the tau." Ill in leper colony. Died two weeks after last interview.
- **Kilimuti**: Tangata manu who died during the expedition. "Recognised authority" on the arrival tradition. Personal experience as egg competitor.
- **Porotu**: Former hopu (servant who swam to the islets for the egg).

## Analytical Significance

Routledge 1919 is the most extensive ethnographic account of pre-Christian Rapa Nui society, based on the longest stay by any early researcher (17 months vs. Thomson's 12 days, Geiseler's 4 days, Roussel's 7 years but with missionary duties). Her methodology was the most rigorous: multiple informants from multiple clans, cross-examination, language acquisition, and explicit discussion of informant reliability. She is also the first researcher to question Thomson's account and to identify Salmon as a problematic intermediary.

For the chiefdom attribution, Routledge's account is paradoxical. She simultaneously provides:
1. The most explicit denial of centralized authority: "with the exception of the Miru, there were no chiefs nor any form of government" (p. 224)
2. The most detailed description of what the Miru ariki-mau actually did: "neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest; he can best be described as the custodian of certain customs and traditions" (p. 242)
3. The most detailed account of the tangata manu as a rotating competitive institution with minimal practical benefits: food gifts were "the sole practical advantage of victory" (p. 264)
4. Evidence of the ariki-mau's inability to exercise coercive power: unable to recover a stolen tablet from his own servant (p. 249), captured and held captive for five years by the Ngaure clan (p. 246)

The account describes a dual system in which a hereditary but powerless ritual office (ariki-mau, Miru only) coexisted with a rotating competitive institution (tangata manu/Ao system) that provided temporary sacred status to members of whichever clan currently held ascendancy. Neither institution corresponds to the redistributive paramount chiefdom of the Kirch/Sahlins model.

## Claims Extraction

### 1. Methodology and Source Assessment

**1.1. Early voyagers were unreliable** [Routledge's assessment]

"While attention has been drawn to the importance of these early narratives, it must be remembered that all the visits were of very short duration, and that the old voyagers were not trained observers. The Dutchmen, for instance, deliberately tell us that the statues have no arms." (p. 205)

**(c) Routledge's inference.** She explicitly qualifies the reliability of contact-era accounts.

**1.2. Slave raids removed the last chiefs** [POST-RAID]

"The natives say that one thousand in all were so removed from the island, and, unfortunately, there were amongst them some of the principal men, including many of the most learned, and the last of the ariki, or chiefs." (p. 205)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The slave raids removed "the last of the ariki," meaning no living informant had experienced functioning chiefly authority.

**1.3. Salmon as unreliable intermediary**

"He [Salmon] spoke the language of the island, and when the U.S.S. Mohican arrived in 1886, he was the source of much of the information which they subsequently published. It is an important but difficult matter to know how far the material thus gleaned thirty years ago was carefully obtained and reproduced." (pp. 208-209)

Routledge adds: Salmon "appears to have taken little interest in the surviving customs and failed to understand them" (p. 209).

**(c) Routledge's assessment.** She explicitly questions Thomson's ethnographic data by questioning Salmon's competence and interest.

**1.4. Cross-examination methodology**

"The witness had to be heard, and discreetly cross-examined without any doubt being thrown on his story, which would at once have given offence; then allowed to forget and again re-examined, his story being compared with that of others who had been heard meanwhile." (p. 213)

**(a) Routledge's description of her methodology.** Separate interviews, cross-checking, re-examination after intervals.

**1.5. Memory and imagination blending**

"Deliberate invention was rare, but, when memory was a little vague, there was a constant tendency to glide from what was remembered to what was imagined." (p. 212)

**(c) Routledge's assessment.** Critical observation about the reliability of elderly informants' accounts.

**1.6. Reliability caveat**

"Though great care has been exercised, it must be remembered, when a particular account is quoted, as, for example, that of Te Haha regarding the annual inspection of the tablets, while it is believed to rest on fact, its absolute accuracy cannot be guaranteed." (p. 213)

**(c) Routledge's own qualification.**

**1.7. Genealogical depth limited to ~4-5 generations**

"Only in one case was it possible to get back beyond the great-grandfather of our informant." (p. 227)

**(a) Routledge's direct finding.** This fundamentally limits what can be known about political succession from oral tradition.

**1.8. Thomson's easterly origin not confirmed**

"The statement made by him [Thomson via Salmon], and repeated by various travellers, probably from the same source, that Hotu-matua came from the east, was never met with by us." (p. 282)

**(c) Routledge's inference.** Thomson's version may derive from a single source (Salmon), and key details were not independently confirmed.

**1.9. Legends "cannot be regarded as more than suggestive"**

"It need hardly be said that, like all such legends, they cannot be regarded as more than suggestive; when the mysteries have been solved, it will no doubt be easy to see where they have been founded on fact, and where error has crept in, and essential points distorted or forgotten; meanwhile, the clues they afford can only be partial." (p. 277)

**(c) Routledge's explicit assessment.** Applies directly to any political organization claims derived from oral traditions.

**1.10. "Impossible" to construct connected history**

"It is impossible as yet to give with any certainty a connected account of the early history of Easter Island." (p. 298)

**(c) Routledge's summary judgment.** The most thorough early ethnographer concluded that a connected historical account cannot be constructed.

### 2. Political Authority: The Ariki Mau

**2.1. "No chiefs nor any form of government" except the Miru** [POST-RAID]

"With the exception of the Miru, of which more will be said, there were no chiefs nor any form of government; any man who was expert in war became a leader." (p. 224)

**(b) Reported tradition from informants of multiple clans.** THE MOST IMPORTANT SINGLE STATEMENT for the chiefdom question. Outside the Miru, there were no chiefs and no government. Leadership was achieved ("any man who was expert in war"), not ascribed. Directly contradicts Thomson's "arbitrary monarchy" with a king reigning "over the entire island" (Thomson p. 472).

**2.2. Miru as the only clan with a chief** [POST-RAID]

"The Miru were unique in other ways; they were the only group which had a headman or chief, who was known as the 'ariki,' or sometimes as the 'ariki-mau,' the great chief, to distinguish him from the 'ariki-paka,' a term which seems to have been given to all other members of the clan." (p. 241)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Routledge's inference.** Only one clan out of ten had a chief. If all Miru were ariki-paka, the title indicated clan membership, not individual rank.

**2.3. Ariki-mau succession: hereditary, endogamous, abdication** [POST-RAID]

"The office of ariki-mau was hereditary, and he was the only man who was obliged to marry into his own clan. It was customary when he was old and feeble that he should resign in favour of his son." (p. 241)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Succession by abdication when old, consistent with Roussel (abdication when son marries). Partially consistent with Thomson (hereditary). Not consistent with Geiseler (brother-to-brother). Mandatory clan endogamy for the ariki-mau is unique to Routledge's account.

**2.4. The ariki-mau was NOT a leader, judge, or priest** [POST-RAID]

"Ngaara held official position for the whole island, but he was neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest; he can best be described as the custodian of certain customs and traditions." (p. 242)

**(c) Routledge's characterization based on extensive informant testimony.** THE MOST ANALYTICALLY IMPORTANT CHARACTERIZATION of the ariki-mau in any source. Routledge explicitly denies military, judicial, and priestly authority. The ariki-mau was a "custodian of customs and traditions," a ritual/cultural figurehead, not a political leader.

**2.5. Ariki-mau's supernatural food-increase power** [POST-RAID]

"Members of this group [Miru] had, in the opinion of the islanders, the supernatural and valuable gift of being able to increase all food supplies, especially that of chickens, and this power was particularly in evidence after death." (p. 240)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Miru supernatural authority over food production, especially chickens. Skulls of Miru were marked as "puoko-moa" (fowl-heads) because they could make hens lay eggs (p. 240). This is supernatural, not political, authority.

**2.6. Ariki-mau directed food-increase magic through deputies** [POST-RAID]

"If, he said, people wanted chickens, they applied to the Ariki-mau, who sent him with maru [feathered sticks], and his visits were always attended with satisfactory results." (p. 242, Te Haha's firsthand recollection)

**(b) Reported tradition (Te Haha's personal memory).** People "applied to" the ariki-mau for food-increase magic, and he sent ariki-paka to perform it. This is a recognized role, but it operates through supernatural mediation, not political command.

**2.7. Ariki-mau directed rain prayers through deputies** [POST-RAID]

"The act most nearly approaching a religious ceremony was conducted under his auspices, though not by him personally. In time of drought he sent up a younger son and other ariki-paka to a hill-top to pray for rain." (p. 242)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The ariki-mau's most "religious" function was delegated to others. He directed but did not personally perform.

**2.8. Annual pua offering: island-wide acknowledgment** [POST-RAID]

"The Ariki was visited one month in the year by 'all people,' who brought him the plant known as pua on the end of sticks, put the pua into his house, and retired backwards." (p. 243)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Annual island-wide visitation with ritual deference (retiring backwards). This is the strongest evidence for island-wide recognition of the ariki-mau's office.

**2.9. Ariki-mau at house inaugurations** [POST-RAID]

"Ngaara himself attended the inauguration of any house of importance. The wooden lizards were put formally on each side of the entrance to the porch, and the Ariki and an ivi-atua, who 'went with him like a tatane,' were the first to eat in the new dwelling: only the houses with stone foundations were thus honoured." (p. 243)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Ceremonial role at house inaugurations, limited to houses with stone foundations.

**2.10. Ariki-mau presided over rongorongo gatherings** [POST-RAID]

"as many as several hundreds of them came together. The younger and more energetic of the population assembled from all districts in the island to look on." The ariki and his son Kaimokoi "sat on seats made of tablets, and each had a tablet in his hand; they wore feather hats, as did all the professors." (p. 245, Te Haha's personal recollection)

"The inhabitants of the neighbouring districts brought offerings of food to Ngaara, that he should be able to supply the multitude, and the oven was 'five yards along.'" (p. 245)

**(b) Reported tradition (firsthand memory).** Island-wide rongorongo assembly under ariki-mau's authority. Rongorongo men "arranged in rows, with an alley-way down the centre to the Ariki." Failed students were publicly corrected; the ariki stood on a platform borne by eight men and "gave them each a chicken" (p. 246). The food-offerings passage describes a form of redistribution: people from neighboring districts brought food TO the ariki-mau so he could feed the assembled rongorongo men. This is the closest to chiefly redistribution behavior in the entire account, but it was specific to the annual rongorongo assembly, not routine economic redistribution: people brought food to enable a particular gathering, not as regular tribute.

**2.11. Ariki-mau's lethal supernatural power** [POST-RAID]

Ngaara told Te Haha to bring ten coconuts after his hair was cut, "which he broke and put in pieces of the sacred tree, 'ngau-ngau'; the spell blasted the offender, who promptly died." (p. 242-243)

**(b) Reported tradition (Te Haha's memory).** The ariki-mau had believed lethal supernatural power. This operates through sorcery, not political authority.

**2.12. Ariki-mau UNABLE to compel return of stolen property** [POST-RAID]

Ngaara's servant Kaara stole the most valuable tablet (kohau-o-te-ranga) for his own clan (Tupahotu). "The Ariki seems to have taken a somewhat feeble line, and, instead of punishing his servant, merely tried to bribe him, with the result that he never again saw his kohau." (p. 249)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The ariki-mau could not compel obedience from his own servant. He resorted to attempted bribery and failed. This directly contradicts any model of chiefly command authority.

**2.13. Ariki-mau captured and enslaved** [POST-RAID]

"Ngaara, before the end, fell on evil days. The Ngaure clan was in the ascendancy, and carried off the Miru as slaves; the Ariki was taken to Akahanga on the south coast with his son, Kaimokoi, and grandson, Maurata. They were there five years in captivity." (p. 246)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The ariki-mau could be captured and enslaved by an ascendant clan. The Miru needed alliance with the Tupahotu to rescue him (p. 246). He died shortly after at his daughter's home in Marama territory, not at Anakena (p. 246). His authority was contingent on political circumstances, not guaranteed by institutional power.

**2.14. Ariki-mau absent from the bird cult** [POST-RAID]

Rongorongo men at Orongo "came at the command of Ngaara, but it is noteworthy that he himself never appeared at Orongo, though he sometimes paid a friendly call at Mataveri." (p. 260)

**(c) Routledge's observation.** The ariki-mau was absent from Orongo, the center of the island's most important annual ritual and political competition. He commanded the rongorongo men there but did not participate himself. This suggests the ariki-mau and tangata manu systems were separate, potentially competing spheres.

**2.15. Ariki required "pure descent," was "quite white"** [POST-RAID]

"the ariki, or chief, the only man who was necessarily of pure descent, is said to have been 'quite white.'" (p. 299)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Routledge's inference.** The ariki was required to be of "pure descent" and was physically distinguished. Connected by Routledge to Polynesian ancestry through the Miru clan.

**2.16. Ariki-mau's elaborate funeral** [POST-RAID]

"For six days after his death everyone worked at making the sticks with feathers on the top (heu-heu), and they were put all round the place. He was buried in the ruined image ahu at Tahai, his body being carried on three of the tablets, and followed through a lane of spectators by the rongo-rongo men; the tablets were buried with him." (p. 246)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Elaborate funeral with island-wide participation indicates recognized status. Tablet burial with the ariki is notable.

### 3. Mata Groups and Territorial Organization

**3.1. Ten clans with flexible territorial boundaries** [POST-RAID]

"They were divided into ten groups, or clans ('mata'), which were associated with different parts of the island, though the boundaries blend and overlap; members of one division settled not infrequently among those of another. Each person still knows his own clan." (p. 221)

**(b) Reported tradition, confirmed by (a) direct observation** (that each person still knew their clan). Ten clans, flexible boundaries, frequent inter-clan residence.

**3.2. Kotuu and Hotu Iti division** [POST-RAID]

"These ten clans were again grouped, more especially in legend or speaking of the remote past, into two major divisions known as Kotuu (or Otuu), and Hotu Iti, which correspond roughly with the western and eastern parts of the island. These divisions were also known respectively as Mata-nui, or greater clans, and Mata-iti, or lesser clans." (p. 221)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The dual division was spoken of "more especially in legend or speaking of the remote past," suggesting a conceptual/legendary framework rather than an active political division.

**3.3. Kotuu clans** [POST-RAID]

Six clans in the western division: Marama, Haumoana, Ngatimo, Miru, Hamea ("a lesser people" mixed with the Miru), Raa. (pp. 221-223)

**3.4. Hotu Iti clans** [POST-RAID]

Four clans in the eastern division: Tupahotu, Koro-orongo, Hitiuira/Ureohei, Ngaure ("a small group"). (p. 223)

**3.5. Clan names have Polynesian etymologies**

"'Raa' means the sun and 'Marama' the light... 'Haumoana' means the sea-breeze; 'Hitiuira' is probably 'hiti-ra' or sunrise... 'Koro-orongo' is doubtless from 'Koro-o-Rongo,' or the ring of Rongo (a well-known Polynesian deity), that is the rainbow." (p. 223, footnote, with linguistic analysis from Mr. Ray)

**(c) Routledge's inference with linguistic analysis.** "Hotu Iti" means "small hill" (referring to Rano Raraku), not a personal name.

### 4. Tangata Manu as Political Institution

**4.1. Bird cult as center of social existence** [POST-RAID]

"Knowledge of the tablets was confined to a few, and formed a comparatively small element of life in the island; the whole of social existence revolved round the bird cult, and it was the last of the old order to pass away." (p. 254)

**(c) Routledge's inference.** The bird cult, not the ariki-mau, was the center of social life. This directly challenges models that place the ariki-mau at the apex of a chiefdom hierarchy.

**4.2. The Ao (ascendant group): rotating clan dominance** [POST-RAID]

"Only those who belonged to the clan in the ascendancy for the time being could enter on the quest." (p. 258)

"Sometimes one group would keep it in their hands for years, or they might pass it on to a friendly clan." (p. 258)

"This selection gave rise, as might be expected, to burnings of hearts; the matter might be, and probably often was, settled by war." (p. 258)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The Ao system was a rotating political dominance. Clan ascendancy was variable in duration and transferable between allied clans. Succession could be violent. No overarching authority enforced orderly succession.

**4.3. Matatoa/matakio binary tied to Ao rotation** [POST-RAID]

Footnote: "The men of the ascendant clan are also often spoken of as the Mata-toa, or warriors, the other clans being the Mata-kio, or servants." (p. 259)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This is NOT a permanent class system. Matatoa (warriors) and matakio (servants) are descriptions of which clans currently held Ao status vs. all others. This rotating binary contradicts the permanent class system described by Roussel (ariki, ivi atua, matatoa, kio as fixed hereditary classes).

**4.4. Ao period at Mataveri: feasting and cannibalism** [POST-RAID]

The ascendant clan resided at Mataveri with "their wives and families" in "large huts with stone foundations." "The months passed at Mataveri were occupied by the residents in feasting as well as in dancing, and equally naturally the victims were human." (p. 259)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The Ao engaged in feasting AND cannibalism, indicating real dominance. Even the Miru were victimized: "it was to grace one of these gatherings, when the Ureohei were the Ao, that the mother of Hotu, the Miru, was slain" (p. 259).

**4.5. Miru role in Ao selection, violently contested** [POST-RAID]

"One year the Marama were inspired with jealousy because the Miru had chosen the Ngaure as their successors, and burnt down the house of Ngaara. This was, perhaps, the beginning of the fray when the old Ariki was carried off captive." (p. 258)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Routledge's inference.** The Miru apparently had some role in selecting the next Ao, but this could be violently contested. The ariki-mau's choice was not enforceable.

**4.6. Entry to competition required supernatural nomination** [POST-RAID]

"The privilege of obtaining the first egg was a matter of competition between members of the Ao, but the right to be one of the competitors was secured only by supernatural means." An ivi-atua "dreamed that a certain man was favoured by the gods, so that if he entered for the race he would be a winner." (p. 260)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The ivi-atua (prophet/seer) controlled access to the competition through dreams, making the ivi-atua a crucial political actor independent of both ariki-mau and clan leadership. Competitors could reject the nomination: a man might "say that 'the ivi-atua was a liar,' and retire from the contest" (p. 260).

**4.7. Tangata manu's practical benefits were minimal** [POST-RAID]

"Food was brought as gifts, especially the first sugar-cane, and these offerings seem to have been the sole practical advantage of victory; those who did not contribute were apt to have their houses burnt." (p. 264)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Routledge's inference.** "Sole practical advantage" directly undermines political-authority interpretations. However, house-burning for non-compliance indicates coercive capacity, even if limited.

Compare Thomson 1891: tangata manu received "no especial authority" (p. 483). Routledge adds the detail about house-burning, which Thomson omits, but her overall characterization ("sole practical advantage") aligns with Thomson's assessment that the tangata manu's status was primarily sacred/ritual.

Compare Roussel 1869: the birdman took "the chief's authority." Routledge's much more detailed account does not support this characterization.

**4.8. Tangata manu in seclusion, not governing** [POST-RAID]

"The victor did not wash, and spent his time in 'sleeping all day, only coming out to sit in the shade.'" (p. 264)

The tangata manu "remained for a year, five months of which were spent in strict taboo." Then "the new man then took the place of his predecessor, who returned to ordinary life." (pp. 264-265)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The tangata manu spent his tapu period sleeping and in seclusion, then returned to ordinary life. He was not governing. Annual rotation means temporary sacred status, not permanent authority.

**4.9. Tangata manu feared entering enemy territory** [POST-RAID]

"If, however, the winner belonged to the western clans, he generally went to Anakena for the next stage, very possibly because, as was explained, he was afraid to go to Hotu Iti." (p. 263)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The tangata manu's sacred status did not provide universal immunity. His power was circumscribed by clan politics.

**4.10. 86 bird-man names recorded** [POST-RAID]

"the request to be given the names of as many bird-years as could be remembered met with an almost embarrassing response, eighty-six being quoted straight away." (p. 265-266)

**(a) Routledge's direct observation.** 86 names suggests the institution operated for many generations.

**4.11. Cult degenerated in later years** [POST-RAID]

"The cult admittedly degenerated in later years. A new practice arose of having more than one bird-man, with other innovations." (p. 265)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Informants themselves acknowledged degeneration, meaning what they describe may itself be a late form.

**4.12. Last formal ceremony 1866-1867** [POST-RAID]

"The last year that the Ao went to Orongo, which is known as 'Rokunga,' appears to have been 1866 or 1867." (p. 265)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Competition continued for twelve more years in reduced form, surviving Christianization (completed 1868).

### 5. Social Structure and Rank

**5.1. No clan restrictions on marriage** [POST-RAID]

"In remembered times there were no group restrictions on marriage, which took place indiscriminately between members of the same or of different clans. The only prohibition had reference to consanguinity, and forbade all union nearer than the eighth degree or third cousins." (p. 221)

**(b) Reported tradition.** No endogamy, no exogamy, no ranked marriage rules. Inconsistent with hierarchical societies where rank is maintained through marriage restrictions.

**5.2. Viriamo married into three clans including the Miru** [POST-RAID]

Viriamo (Ureohei) married: (1) a man of the Ureohei; (2) a man of the Miru at Anakena; (3) a Tupahotu near Tongariki. (pp. 227-228)

**(b) Reported tradition.** An ordinary Ureohei woman married into the "chiefly" Miru clan with no described restrictions, barriers, or special status.

**5.3. No priests** [POST-RAID]

"There were no priests, but certain men, known as 'koromake,' practised spells which would secure the death of an enemy." (p. 239)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Explicit denial of a priestly class. Chiefdom models typically require religious specialists legitimating chiefly authority.

**5.4. Ivi atua as independent seers, not political officers** [POST-RAID]

"the class known as 'ivi-atua,' which included both men and women. The most important of these ivi-atua, of whom it was said there might be perhaps ten in the island, held commune with the aku-aku, others were able to prophesy, and could foresee the whereabouts of fish or turtle, while some had the gift of seeing hidden things, and would demand contributions from a secreted store of bananas or potatoes." (p. 239)

**(b) Reported tradition.** About ten ivi-atua on the island, both men and women. They were independent seers/diviners who "demanded contributions" on their own authority, not attached to a chiefly hierarchy.

**5.5. No sacrifice, no formal worship** [POST-RAID]

"There was no sacrifice; the invitation to the supernatural power was purely formal, or restricted to the essence of the food only." (p. 236)

Eyraud footnote: "Though I have lived in the greatest of intimacy and familiarity with them, I have never been able to discover them in any act of actual religious worship." (p. 236, quoting Annals of the Propagation of the Faith, Jan. 1866)

**(b) Reported tradition, confirmed by Eyraud's direct observation.** No organized state religion of the kind chiefdom models predict.

**5.6. Kio class NOT MENTIONED** [Significant absence]

The kio (servant/slave class described by Roussel and Thomson) does not appear in Routledge's account. If a hereditary servant class existed, one would expect 17 months of extensive interviews to reveal it.

**5.7. Rongorongo knowledge distributed across clans** [POST-RAID]

"Every clan had professors in the art who were known as rongo-rongo men ('tangata-rongo-rongo'). They had houses apart... Here they practised their calling, often sitting and working with their pupils in the shade of the bananas." (p. 244)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Every clan had its own rongorongo experts. Knowledge was distributed, not monopolized by the Miru.

**5.8. Non-Miru expert paralleled ariki-mau's function** [POST-RAID]

"Another savant on the south coast was said to be 'too big a man to have a school,' and also went about visiting and inspecting learned establishments in the same manner." (p. 246)

**(b) Reported tradition.** A non-Miru expert conducted independent inspections of rongorongo schools, paralleling the ariki-mau's function.

**5.9. Tablets contained no genealogies or king lists**

"The matters with which it would naturally have been supposed that the rongo-rongo would deal, such as genealogies, lists of ariki, or the wanderings of the people, were never mentioned." (p. 249)

**(c) Routledge's inference.** The rongorongo did not serve to legitimate hierarchical authority through genealogical records.

### 6. Property and Land

**6.1. Fish bartered for vegetables without chiefly mediation** [POST-RAID]

"Fish are not very plentiful, as there is no barrier reef, but they also were an article of diet, and were bartered by those on the coast for the vegetable products obtained by those further inland." (p. 218)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Direct horizontal exchange without redistribution through a chiefly center.

**6.2. No routine tribute or redistribution described**

Nowhere in Routledge's account does she describe regular tribute payments to chiefs, redistributive feasting organized by chiefs as a routine political function, or systematic extraction of surplus. The feasts described (paina, koro, kaunga) are organized by families for family members. The one passage approaching redistribution is the rongorongo assembly (p. 245), where "the inhabitants of the neighbouring districts brought offerings of food to Ngaara, that he should be able to supply the multitude." This describes food mobilization for a specific annual ceremonial event (the rongorongo gathering at Anakena), not the routine economic redistribution that chiefdom models require. The ariki-mau also gave each rongorongo man a chicken (p. 246), but this was payment for participation in knowledge assessment, not general redistribution.

[Significant absence of routine redistribution.] The rongorongo assembly represents the ariki-mau's closest approach to redistributive behavior, but its ceremonial specificity and annual limitation distinguish it from the systematic redistribution of the Kirch/Sahlins chiefdom model.

**6.3. Chicken houses more elaborate than human dwellings** [POST-RAID]

"chickens played an important role in native life, and the remains of the dwellings made for them are much more imposing than those for human beings." (p. 218)

**(a) Routledge's direct observation.** Chickens were a primary form of wealth, but no chiefly control over chickens is described.

### 7. Warfare

**7.1. Warfare as spasmodic raids by aggrieved persons** [POST-RAID]

"The warfare consisted largely of spasmodic and isolated raids; an aggrieved person gathered together his neighbours and descended on the offenders." (p. 224)

**(b) Reported tradition.** War leadership was ad hoc: an "aggrieved person" (not a chief) gathered neighbors. Inconsistent with organized campaigns directed by political authority.

**7.2. Intra-clan fighting** [POST-RAID]

"Legend tells of continual wars between Hotu Iti and Kotuu. In recent times general fighting seems to have been constant, and took place even between members of one clan." (pp. 223-224)

**(b) Reported tradition.** If fighting occurred even within clans, clan-level authority was insufficient to maintain internal order.

**7.3. Collective punishment of the Toa-toa fraud** [POST-RAID]

Six men at Toa-toa "were in the habit of going about after dark with their faces painted red, white, and black, and visiting houses, where they declared they were gods, and demanded food." When the fraud was discovered, "the people gathered together and killed them." (p. 224)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Collective enforcement, not chiefly justice. The use of supernatural claims to extract food was recognized as illegitimate.

**7.4. Last statues overthrown in warfare** [POST-RAID]

"It was in this sort of warfare that the last images were overthrown." (p. 225)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Routledge's inference.** Moai toppling occurred during inter-clan raids, not as an organized political upheaval. Statue toppling motivated by revenge for Ao-era cannibalism: "it was in revenge for another Mataveri victim that the last statues were thrown down" (p. 259).

### 8. Legends and Origin Traditions

**8.1. Hotu Matua arrival from Marae Renga** [POST-RAID]

Hotu-matua came from Marae Renga after losing a dynastic struggle with his brother. Six advance scouts preceded him. Two boats arrived (Hotu-matua's and Hinelilu's), with Hotu-matua using supernatural power to arrive first at Anakena. (pp. 277-279)

**(b) Reported tradition (Kilimuti as primary informant).** Major differences from Thomson: Thomson has Hotu-matua coming from the east (Routledge's informants never said this); Thomson omits Hinelilu; Thomson omits the triumph of Hotu-iti over Kotuu.

**8.2. Hotu-matua's sons as clan ancestors** [POST-RAID]

"Hotu-matua had many sons from whom the different clans are descended, and whose names they bear." (p. 279)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Standard Polynesian charter linking clan organization to the founding chief's genealogy.

**8.3. Hotu-matua abdicates after quarrel with eldest son** [POST-RAID]

"He quarrelled with the eldest, Tuumaheki, and with his own wife, Vakai; the two having behaved badly to him, he finally gave up his position to Tuumaheki and retired to the top of Rano Kao." (pp. 279-280)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Succession by abdication after personal conflict, not orderly hereditary transfer.

**8.4. Deathbed blessing favors youngest son** [POST-RAID]

"You are Hotu-iti, of Mata-iti, and your descendants shall prosper and survive all others." And to Kotuu: "your descendants shall multiply like the shells of the sea... but they shall die and shall not remain." (p. 280)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Mythological charter for Hotu Iti's eventual triumph over Kotuu, reversing normal primogeniture. Reads as retrojection from the victorious side.

**8.5. NO formal king list provided** [POST-RAID]

Routledge does NOT provide a numbered king list comparable to Thomson's 57 names or Roussel's 22. She mentions "all the lists" agreeing that Miru was the third ariki (p. 281) but notes fundamental confusion among informants about genealogical details: "The sons of Hotu-matua are not known exactly. Kotuu is sometimes identified with Ko Tuumaheki, and is sometimes a separate person." (pp. 281-282)

**(c) Routledge's assessment.** The genealogies are contradictory; a reliable king list cannot be constructed.

**8.6. Long Ears: maximum confusion about origins** [POST-RAID]

"On the most interesting point, namely, the origin of the Long Ears, there is the most vagueness." (p. 281)

Kilimuti: Long Ears were Hinelilu's crew. Three old men: Long Ears arose through the mana of the third ariki. Te Haha's wife dismissed them all: "they don't know anything about it," which Routledge judged "probably summed up the situation." (p. 282)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) direct observation of informant session.** The most ethnologically significant question produced the most contradictory answers. Routledge endorses the assessment that her informants did not actually know.

**8.7. Chronological impossibility in war legends** [POST-RAID]

Kapiera placed the Kotuu-Hotu Iti wars "during the time of Ngaara's grandfather," while involving Tuukoihu from the founding voyage. Juan "pertinently pointed out that this was inconsistent." (p. 289)

**(a) Direct observation of informant interview.** The internal chronology of the traditions is contradictory, undermining their use as historical evidence.

**8.8. War leaders were "great men," not ariki** [POST-RAID]

"Kainga was a great man, and he lived near Tongariki." (p. 283). Poie was "a big man" (p. 285). Neither is called ariki. Leadership in the war traditions is exercised by "great men" and "big men," not by hereditary chiefs.

**(b) Reported tradition.** Wartime leadership was personal and achieved, not institutional and ascribed.

### 9. Social Functions and Festivals

**9.1. Funerals as communal symmetric consumption** [POST-RAID]

"everybody took food and everybody ate." (p. 229)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Potlatch-style communal consumption, not redistributive feasting organized by a chief.

**9.2. No ceremony at burial** [POST-RAID]

"The missionaries were impressed with the fact that there was no ceremony of any kind at a burial." (p. 229)

**(b) Reported tradition attributed to missionaries.** No ritual differentiation of rank in death.

**9.3. Paina: family-organized festival at image ahu** [POST-RAID]

"The 'paina'... was given by the family as a testimonial of esteem to a father, or possibly a brother who might be either alive or dead; it was a serious matter, and the original direction for the celebration came from a supernaturally gifted individual known as an 'ivi-atua.'" (p. 233)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Organized by the family (not a chief), honoring a family member (not a political figure), initiated by an ivi atua (not a chief). Held at image ahu, connecting ahu to ongoing community life rather than exclusively to chiefly authority.

**9.4. Koro: extended house-party with food distribution** [POST-RAID]

"Presents of food were brought to the hero who distributed them to the party." (p. 234)

**(b) Reported tradition from an eyewitness.** The honoree distributed food, resembling redistribution. But organized by the family for a family member, not by a political office. The "hero" was the honored person, not a chief.

**9.5. Feasts timed by astronomical observation by "old men"** [POST-RAID]

"on the extremity of the eastern headland... the old men came to watch the constellations." (p. 235)

**(b) Reported tradition confirmed by physical remains.** Calendrical knowledge held by specialists ("old men"), not by political authorities.

### 10. Significant Absences and European Categories

**10.1. No routine tribute or redistribution** described anywhere in Routledge's account. The one exception is the rongorongo assembly (p. 245), where neighboring districts brought food to the ariki-mau to feed the gathered scholars; see claim 2.10. This was a specific annual ceremonial context, not routine economic redistribution.

**10.2. No succession rules** described, despite 17 months of fieldwork. Compare: Roussel says abdication when son marries; Thomson says eldest son; Geiseler says brother. Routledge's silence after the most thorough fieldwork suggests the rules were either forgotten, never standardized, or differently reconstructed by different informants. (Routledge notes in passing that the ariki-mau "should resign in favour of his son" when "old and feeble" [p. 241], partially consistent with Roussel's abdication.)

**10.3. No kio (servant/slave) class** mentioned.

**10.4. No chiefly role in monument construction** described. This absence is analytically significant because Routledge's Chapter XIV (pp. 175-199) provides the most detailed early account of moai quarrying, carving, and transport. She describes the quarry layout, carving techniques, the unfinished statues in various stages, and transport traditions in considerable detail, yet nowhere attributes any aspect of monument production to chiefly command, direction, or oversight. The chiefdom model assumes monument production was organized by chiefs; the most thorough early researcher documented the production process without finding evidence for this assumption.

**10.5. No king list** provided, despite collecting "all the lists" and noting contradictions.

**10.6. No priestly class** described.

**10.7. No organized religious worship** described.

## Cross-Reference: Succession Rule Comparison

| Source | Rule | Ariki-mau described as |
|--------|------|----------------------|
| Roussel 1869 | Abdication when son marries | Kings with "absolute power" (but power "passed into hands of matatoa") |
| Geiseler 1883 | Succession to the brother | "Almost despotic" regime (through Salmon) |
| Thomson 1891 | Eldest son nominated | "Arbitrary monarchy" (through Salmon) |
| Routledge 1919 | Resign when "old and feeble" in favor of son (p. 241) | "Neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest" (p. 242) |

Three different succession rules from four sources within 50 years, from an island of 150-650 people. Routledge's description partially overlaps with Roussel (abdication) and Thomson (son), but the trigger differs (old age vs. son's marriage vs. eldest son automatically). The fundamental disagreement across sources recorded from overlapping generations of the same small population demonstrates that succession rules cannot be reliably established.

## Cross-Reference: Tangata Manu Political Authority Comparison

| Source | Tangata manu political authority |
|--------|-------------------------------|
| Roussel 1869 | "Luck alone, the discovery of a few eggs of a seabird, was all that was needed to transfer the chief's authority to the happy nest-robbers" (p. 47) |
| Thomson 1891 | "No especial authority was vested in him" (p. 483) |
| Routledge 1919 | Food gifts were "the sole practical advantage of victory" (p. 264); spent five months "sleeping all day"; "returned to ordinary life" after one year (pp. 264-265) |

Thomson and Routledge agree that the tangata manu received no political authority. Roussel's brief description of "the chief's authority" being transferred is not supported by the more detailed later accounts. Routledge adds the detail of house-burning for non-compliance (p. 264), indicating some coercive capacity, but her overall characterization ("sole practical advantage") aligns with Thomson.

## Summary Assessment

Routledge's account, the most extensive and methodologically rigorous early ethnography of Rapa Nui, describes a political system fundamentally inconsistent with the redistributive paramount chiefdom of the Kirch/Sahlins model:

1. **Only one of ten clans had a chief.** The remaining nine had "no chiefs nor any form of government" (p. 224).
2. **That chief was explicitly not a leader, judge, or priest** (p. 242), but rather a "custodian of customs and traditions."
3. **The chief could not compel obedience** from his own servant (p. 249) and was captured and enslaved by another clan (p. 246).
4. **The tangata manu system was a rotating competitive institution** with minimal practical benefits (p. 264), in which the winner spent most of his term in seclusion and then "returned to ordinary life" (p. 265).
5. **The ariki-mau and tangata manu were separate institutions.** The ariki-mau was absent from Orongo (p. 260). The Miru were victimized when another clan held Ao status (p. 259).
6. **No routine tribute, redistribution, or taxation** described anywhere. The one passage approaching redistribution is the rongorongo assembly (p. 245), where neighboring districts brought food to the ariki-mau so he could feed the gathered scholars, but this was a specific annual ceremonial event, not the systematic economic redistribution that chiefdom models require.
7. **No succession rules, no king list, no priestly class, no kio class** described.
8. **Marriage was unrestricted** between clans (p. 221).
9. **Warfare was led by achieved "great men,"** not hereditary chiefs (pp. 224, 283).
10. **Social functions were organized by families,** not by political authorities (pp. 233-235).

The evidence that does support some form of chiefly institution is confined to the Miru: supernatural food-increase powers (p. 240), annual pua offering (p. 243), rongorongo assembly leadership (pp. 245-246), and elaborate funeral (p. 246). These describe a ceremonial/ritual office with recognized supernatural authority over food production and knowledge transmission, not a political administrator.
