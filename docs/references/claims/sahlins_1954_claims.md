## I. Intellectual Genealogy

### Who Sahlins builds on and what he takes from each

1. **Leslie A. White**: "Social systems are in a very real sense secondary and subsidiary to technological systems.... A social system is a function of a technological system" (p. 2, quoting White). Sahlins takes the materialist premise that technology determines social organization but modifies it by reintroducing environment as a variable White had averaged out. [White 1943, 1949]

2. **Julian Steward**: The "ecological" concept combining technology and environment. Sahlins credits Steward and Morton Fried as practitioners of this approach (p. 4). He takes Steward's multilinear evolution and cultural ecology framework. [Steward 1936, 1938, 1949, 1955]

3. **Karl Marx / Friedrich Engels**: Listed among the "technologists" who held that social systems vary as technological systems vary (p. 2). The materialist premise is foundational though Sahlins does not closely engage Marx's specific arguments. [Marx 1904; Engels 1942]

4. **Lewis Henry Morgan / E.B. Tylor**: Named as early proponents of the technology-determines-society view (p. 2). [Morgan 1877; Tylor 1898]

5. **V. Gordon Childe**: Cited alongside White for the premise that culture history is "a record of man's progressive control over nature" (p. 419). [Childe 1948a, 1948b]

6. **Paul Kirchhoff**: Key influence. His unpublished 1935 paper "The Principles of Clanship in Human Society" provides the distinction between the "equalitarian clan" (exogamous, all members equal) and the "conical clan" (non-exogamous, ranked by genealogical proximity to common ancestor) (pp. 10-12). This conical clan concept becomes the "ramage" in Sahlins's terminology and is foundational for the entire Polynesian chiefdom model. [Kirchhoff 1935 unpublished]

7. **Gunnar Landtman**: *The Origin of the Inequality of the Social Classes* (1938). Provided a precedent for ranking societies by amount of stratification "irrespective of the type of clan" (p. 12).

8. **Raymond Firth**: The "ramage" concept from *We, The Tikopia* (1936), adopted as the key organizational term. Also influential through his introduction to Williamson (1937) discussing factors in Polynesian chieftainship (p. 8). [Firth 1936, 1940, 1949, 1950]

9. **Karl Polanyi**: The "redistributive form of economic integration" provides the mechanism linking productivity to stratification (p. 411). Redistribution is the master variable in the entire framework. [Polanyi, not directly cited but concept used throughout]

10. **Karl Wittfogel**: Engaged extensively in the Appendix (pp. 427-434). Sahlins draws the general implication that communal labor reinforces stratification but rejects the specific hydraulic hypothesis in favor of a distribution-centered origin. [Wittfogel 1931, 1938]

11. **Robert W. Williamson**: The three-volume *Social and Political Systems of Central Polynesia* (1924) is the secondary compilation on which Sahlins most heavily relies. He describes Williamson's work as "masterful" (p. 15) and treats it as having "exhaustively summarized" historical sources.

## II. Theoretical Framework

12. **Core hypothesis**: "Other factors constant, the greater the productivity, the greater the amount of stratification" (p. 413, underlined in original). Social stratification is an adaptive response to techno-environmental conditions, mediated by redistribution.

13. **Redistribution as master variable**: "The differential roles of different statuses in the distribution of goods is perhaps the best point from which to begin to understand the relationship of the various aspects of stratification" (p. 22). "The greater the productivity the greater would be the economic, social and ceremonial distinction between distributors and producers" (p. 12).

14. **"Ethnological present" assumption**: "The dissertation is concerned with the Polynesian cultures in the ethnological present, that is at the time just prior to European influence. Any trait described in the ethnographic sources that can be attributed to European influence is excluded" (p. 14). [No discussion of how post-contact sources can recover this pre-contact state.]

15. **Three-stage evolutionary scheme** (pp. 419-425): (I) Reciprocal economies with egalitarian kin societies; (II) Redistributive economies with stratified kin societies; (III) States. This is the direct precursor to Service's band-tribe-chiefdom-state model. The word "chiefdom" does not appear as a type label.

16. **"Other factors constant" proviso**: "It should be stressed that the proviso, other factors constant, is not to be taken lightly" (p. 413). "It is possible that in particular cultures alternative means may be found to cope with the problems of surplus production -- means that may or may not lead to a high degree of social stratification."

## III. Methodology

### Criteria for measuring "amount of stratification"

17. **Structural criterion**: Number of hereditary status levels. Two to three in Polynesia (p. 27).

18. **Economic criteria**: (a) Differential access to strategic resources / land tenure stewardship; (b) Supervision of production; (c) Segregation of chiefs from subsistence production; (d) Roles in redistribution (collection and distribution of surplus); (e) Consumption differentials (food restrictions, insignia of rank) (pp. 28-31).

19. **Socio-political criteria**: (a) General statements about chiefly authority; (b) Power structure (how decisions emanate); (c) Application of sanctions / justice; (d) Intra-status marriage (p. 31-32).

20. **Ceremonial criteria**: (a) Mana-tabu complex; (b) Obeisance and ceremonial respect; (c) Differential life crisis rites (pp. 33-34).

21. **Rating system**: Qualitative, not quantitative. "It would be, of course, preferable to have some mathematical index of stratification based perhaps on behavioral measurements. But to apply such methods to written accounts of behavior over which we have no control as to what is selected to be observed is, of course, out of the question" (p. 22). Cultures grouped into four categories: Group I, IIa, IIb, III (pp. 34-35).

22. **What is omitted**: Position of women and *vasu* relationships explicitly excluded (p. 13).

### Productivity measurement

23. **Primary measure**: Size of largest redistributive network multiplied by frequency factor (3 = regular, 2 = sporadic, 1 = rare) (pp. 182, 189). [NOTE: This is circular: the size of the redistributive network is itself a feature of the stratification system being measured.]

24. **Secondary measure**: Specialization of labor. Sahlins himself calls this "pitifully inadequate" (p. 196).

25. **Tertiary measure**: Technology and environment charts, one per island (pp. 204ff). Qualitative assessment.

### Source basis

26. **Sources primarily ethnographical** (p. 15). Bishop Museum monographs and Williamson's compilations are the main data. Historical/missionary accounts "not heavily used" because of dubious nature, Williamson's exhaustive summaries, and Bishop Museum monographs' incorporation of historical materials (pp. 15-16).

27. **"Ideal culture" caveat**: "There is always the possibility, especially when dealing with sanctions, that the 'ideal' culture, rather than the actual workings of the society, has crept into descriptive materials. Such could lead to serious misinterpretations" (p. 34).

## IV. Easter Island: Every Claim

### Status Levels (pp. 99)

33. "Priests came from the tribe which supplied the paramount religious leader. The so-called 'servants' of the ariki mau were high-ranking relatives of his." (p. 99) [Source: Metraux 1937b, p. 49]

45. "There is little information as to what became of many of the goods the ariki mau received." (p. 101)

46. "The birdmen are said to have been entitled to the largest share of feasts, but the disposition of the share is not clear." (p. 101) [Source: Metraux 1940, p. 238]

47. **CRITICAL CLAIM (analogy fills gap)**: "We may surmise from comparative data that in most cases these members of the upper status level were acting as focal points in accumulation and redistribution." 48. "That this is so of other members of the upper status level, the heads of large kinship groups or tribes, is more certain. The occasions of these chiefly distributory activities were similar in nature to such occasions elsewhere in Polynesia." (p. 101)

49. "The customary obligation to give surplus production to the chiefs does not seem to have been enforced by confiscation." (p. 101) [Source: implied Metraux 1940]

51. "There is evidence that some upper status members were exempt from subsistence production. The birdmen evidently depended to a large extent on plunder and the obligations of others to provide sustenance." (p. 102) [Source: Metraux 1940]

Thomson, however, states to the contrary that there were no professional warriors, that every man was a soldier and that the tribal chiefs led the tribes to battle." 54. "The usual restrictions of certain types of food to the chiefs were present on Easter Island." (p. 102) [Source: Metraux 1940]

55. "Certain ornaments, as crescent-shaped breast ornaments, served as insignia of high rank. The paramount chief's attire included six of these, as well as a yellow tapa cloak, and a feather headdress." (p. 102) [Source: Metraux 1940]

### Part Two: Social Organization Classification (pp. 283-289)

97. **Ramage classification**: "A system of ramages encompassing the entire island with the usual customs of primogeniture, seniority of the line of eldest sons, elaboration of life crises rites for eldest sons, growth of ramages by fission, were all present on Easter Island." (p. 283) [Source: Metraux 1937b, 1940; Routledge 1919; Thomson 1889; Williamson 1924]

98. **Segmentary structure**: "The smallest was the household, a patrilocal extended family probably headed by the senior male and composed, at present, of about nine people. Aboriginally, households of thirty or more were apparently not uncommon. A number of households tracing descent to a common ancestor formed a 'sub-tribe' headed by the direct descendant of this ancestor. A number of similarly related 'sub-tribes' formed the tribe, mata, of which there were about ten, averaging 300 to 400 people each." (pp. 283-284)

99. **Genealogical basis**: "Most of the population traced descent to a common ancestor as many tribes were considered to be offspring of sons and grandsons of the first settler and chief. It is both Metraux' and Williamson's opinion that those mata that were not descendent from immediate relatives of the first settler were offshoots of mata that were, so that eventually the whole mata system could be phrased on a genealogical basis." (p. 284)

100. **Ranking system**: "This ramified structure was the basis of the genealogical ranking system. The mata descendent on the direct line from the first settler supplied the paramount chief of the island, the ariki mau, in the person of the eldest son of that line." (p. 284)

101. **Matatoa challenge**: "The assertion that there was a professional class of warriors rests on tenuous evidence. Missionary accounts and native traditions stressing the importance of matatoa, and a missionary dictionary distinguishing matatoa from paoa, 'ordinary soldiers,' seem to be the major supports for the interpretation and the conclusion regarding the powers of the matatoa. Although Metraux states that matatoa, as persons distinct from mata chiefs, ruled the mata, no attempt is made to indicate what position on the genealogical ranking system the matatoa might come from -- especially no indication that they weren't actually the tribal chiefs to begin with." (pp. 284-285) [NOTE: Sahlins challenges Metraux on this point]

102. **Thomson on matatoa**: "'Clans [mata] were always led to battle by the chief.' And: 'There was no class of professional fighters or soldiers; every able-bodied man was supposed to be a warrior and compelled to do duty in time of war.'" (p. 285, citing Thomson pp. 475-476)

103. **Sahlins's resolution**: The matatoa "might have been leaders of junior lines who usurped tribal chieftainship through military expression of differential ramage growth" (p. 286).

104. **Birdman as dual supremacy**: "The paramount chief, head of the genealogical hierarchy of the island did not participate in the egg hunt. The position of the bird-man, therefore, was one of coexistent supremacy with the ariki mau." (p. 286)

105. **Sacred vs. secular power**: "The ariki mau was a sacred ruler in the main. His divine powers were great but his temporal powers were nil, and he had next to no authority outside of his own tribe. There are hints that he once did have. But this was definitely so with regard to the bird-man who had widespread secular powers as well as religious privileges. The bird-man usurped some of the functions in the accumulation-redistribution system from the paramount chief." (p. 287)

### Ecological anomaly (pp. 355-356, 361-362)

106. "The distribution of resource areas in Easter Island evidently was a less scattered one than prevailed in other islands with ramified systems... The majority of the evidence indicates that agricultural possibilities, and settlements as well, were concentrated along the coast, and that the range of crops was not particularly great." (p. 355)

107. Settlement evidence from Routledge: "'The lower portions of the island were the most densely populated parts, especially those on the coast, and the settlements on the higher ground appear to have been few.'" (p. 356, citing Routledge 1919, pp. 221, 216)

108. Familial specialization evidence: "Fish are not very plentiful, as there is no barrier reef, but they were an article of diet, and were bartered [?] by those on the coast for the vegetable products obtained by those further inland." (p. 356, citing Routledge 1919, p. 218)

109. **Anomaly acknowledged**: "Only in Easter Island, among the ramified systems, and Uvea, among the descent line systems, was there room for doubt that the social organization was adapted to the 'proper' technological-environmental situation." (p. 361)

110. **Resolution by historical inheritance**: "In the case of Easter Island it would seem that its historical alignments to eastern Polynesia would prejudice the outcome under such conditions of slight selective pressure." (p. 362)

### Moai and communal labor (pp. 433-434, Appendix)

111. **CRITICAL CLAIM**: "In both Tonga and Easter Island, strategic communal production was inhibited by environmental factors -- by the absence of streams in Tonga, precluding irrigation, and by the absence of streams, reefs and forests in Easter Island, precluding irrigation, communal fishing and cooperative bush clearing. In both cases, communal production occurred, but it was largely channelized into non-strategic realms, the chiefly tombs in Tonga and the famous Easter Island stone heads." (p. 433)

112. "The fact that masses of manpower were devoted to these tasks -- without analogies provided by organizations of labor for subsistence production -- demonstrates that in a very real sense communal labor is related to and reinforces stratification. But it may also imply that stratification, in particular instances, can precede communal labor." (p. 433) [NOTE: This qualified claim becomes the unqualified "moai = chiefly command" in later literature]

### Warfare and leadership (p. 439)

113. "Secular leadership over all Mangaia and over Easter Island were spoils of war." (p. 439) [Source: Metraux 1940; Buck 1934]

## VII. Productivity Rating

Easter Island receives a productivity score of **6,000-8,000†** (Table II, p. 190), ranking 5th-6th of 14 islands.

The score rests on:
- Population of 3,000-4,000 (Metraux's estimate, itself a post-contact reconstruction)
- Frequency factor of 2 (sporadic, uncertain: "it is not clear whether what was accumulated was for his own support or was widely redistributed")
- The entire island treated as a single redistributive unit

## VIII. Comparative Context

### Group IIa summary (pp. 122-125)

Within Group IIa, Sahlins ranks: Mangareva (most stratified) > Easter Island ≈ Mangaia > Uvea (least stratified).

Key distinguishing features of Group IIa vs. Group I (pp. 122-125):
- "Sanctions for violations of economic tabus were usually supernatural" (vs. secular in Group I)
- "Chiefs could not usually confiscate the goods of others" (vs. could in Group I)
- "There was no elaborated system of obeisance" (vs. elaborate in Group I)
- "Dispossession of underlings by higher status members was possible but occurred under more limited conditions than in Group I"

Key distinguishing features vs. Group IIb:
- Group IIa had "tendency to form a third status level" (kio on Easter Island)
- Group IIa had "greater role of chiefs in redistribution" (though for Easter Island this was inferred by analogy)

## IX. Part Two Classification

Easter Island is classified as a **ramage society** (p. 243, 283) with:
- Primogeniture
- Seniority-based ranking
- Genealogical tree encompassing the whole island
- Ariki mau as paramount on the senior line
- ~10 mata (tribes) averaging 300-400 people

Sahlins acknowledges Easter Island is **ecologically anomalous** for the ramage category (pp. 355-356, 361-362): its concentrated coastal resources should predict a descent line system, but its eastern Polynesian heritage accounts for the ramage.

## X. Assumptions and Silences

114. **Ethnological present assumption**: Sahlins assumes ethnographic sources recover pre-contact society by filtering out European-introduced traits (p. 14). No discussion of whether post-slave-raid sources can accomplish this.

115. **No mention of the slave raids**: The 1862-1863 slave raids and subsequent demographic collapse are never discussed. The French missionary report of "anarchy" is noted but attributed to post-decimation conditions without exploring whether ALL the Easter Island ethnographic data might be similarly compromised.

116. **No contact-era accounts used**: Roggeveen, González, Cook, and La Pérouse are not cited for Easter Island. Sahlins relies entirely on secondary ethnographic works that themselves depend on post-raid informants.

117. **Redistribution gap filled by analogy**: The single most consequential move in the entire Easter Island section. Sahlins admits "little direct evidence" of redistribution, then states "We may surmise from comparative data" that it existed. This is the Polynesian analogy at its most explicit.

118. **No archaeological evidence considered**: There is no reference to any archaeological investigation of Easter Island. The material record (ahu, moai) is mentioned only in passing.

119. **Moai claim unexamined**: The claim that stone head transportation was "initiated by members of the chiefly status level" (p. 100) and involved "masses of manpower" (p. 433) is stated without archaeological analysis.

120. **Population estimate uncritiqued**: The 3,000-4,000 figure from Metraux is used without examining its basis or the contact-era population evidence.

## XI. Qualifications and Hedges

Sahlins himself notes the following uncertainties about Easter Island:

121. "there is little direct evidence" of redistribution (p. 101)
122. "we do not know who these were" (recipients of ariki mau's fish distribution, p. 101)
123. "There is little information as to what became of many of the goods the ariki mau received" (p. 101)
124. "the disposition of the share is not clear" (birdman's feast share, p. 101)
125. "We could not learn whether commoners could be freely dispossessed" (p. 100)
126. "political controls disintegrated shortly after European contact" (p. 103)
127. Government was "decentralized and localized" per Metraux (p. 104)
128. "No great homage" for ariki mau per Thomson (p. 106)
129. "Very little information" on status-differentiated life crisis rites (p. 106)
130. "it is not clear whether what was accumulated was for his own support or was widely redistributed" (p. 192, fn. 14)
131. Easter Island is ecologically anomalous for its ramage classification (pp. 361-362)
132. The matatoa claim "rests on tenuous evidence" (p. 284)
133. "The assertion that there was a professional class of warriors" is challenged by Thomson (pp. 284-285)
