## I. Intellectual Genealogy

### Who Stevenson et al. cite in 2001

1. **Earle 1997** (*How Chiefs Come to Power*): Cited for the claim that monumental architecture "bolster[s] ideological beliefs that are necessary for maintaining an integrated chiefly polity" (p. 17). This is a major chiefdom theory work providing the theoretical scaffolding for linking architecture to political hierarchy. NEW to the Stevenson citation network; not in 1984 or 1986.

2. **Ladefoged 1995**: Cited for the theoretical framework that uncertain environments produce higher organizational complexity and that polities "can often provide opportunities for resource buffering during periods of environmental perturbations" (pp. 17-18). Ladefoged is a co-author. NEW citation.

3. **Graves & Ladefoged 1995**: Cited alongside Ladefoged 1995 for the uncertainty-complexity framework (p. 17). NEW citation; already on checklist (#139).

4. **Ladefoged & Graves 2000**: Cited for evolutionary theory applied to Hawaiian dry-land agriculture (p. 17). The Hawaiian agricultural parallel that grounds the ahupua'a analogy. NEW citation.

5. **Dunnell 1999**: Cited for the "concept of waste" in evolutionary archaeology, supporting the argument that monument construction diverts energy from subsistence as a population-suppression mechanism (p. 17). NEW citation.

11. **Martinsson-Wallin 1994**: Cited for ahu construction dates, "many of the largest structures were built in the AD 1400s to AD 1600s" (p. 21). Already processed.

23. **Cristino et al. 1981**: Cited for settlement survey data (p. 20).

### What is NOT cited in 2001

26. **Service 1962 NOT cited.** The chiefdom typology source is absent, as in 1984 and 1986. However, "chiefdom" is now used freely throughout the paper.

27. **Kirch 1984 NOT cited.** *Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms* remains absent from Stevenson's bibliography, even though the chiefdom district framework it inspired is now the organizing structure of the paper.

28. **Sahlins 1958/1963 NOT cited.** The Polynesian stratification gradient that positioned Easter Island as a chiefdom in the first place is absent.

29. **Goldman 1970 NOT cited.** The "Open" classification, which in 1984 and 1986 provided the dual status system, is entirely absent. The paper discusses hierarchy without acknowledging Goldman's counter-classification.

30. **McCall 1979 NOT cited.** The primary ethnographic source for kinship structure in 1984 and 1986 is absent.

31. **Metraux 1940 NOT cited.** The foundational post-slave-raid ethnography is absent.

32. **Routledge 1919 NOT cited.** The other major post-slave-raid ethnography is absent.

33. **Fortes, Firth, Kirchhoff, Hayden & Cannon NOT cited.** The British social anthropology framework from 1984 remains absent, as in 1986.

34. **Stevenson 1986 NOT cited.** The published chapter in Kirch's volume is absent, replaced by the new settlement model citations (1997, 1999, 2002).

### Assessment: what the citation changes mean

35. **The ethnographic foundation has been replaced by an archaeological-theoretical one.** In 1984 and 1986, political organization claims derived from McCall 1979, Goldman 1970, Metraux 1940, Routledge 1919. In 2001, none of these are cited. Instead, the theoretical framework comes from evolutionary ecology (Ladefoged, Graves, Dunnell, Earle) and the empirical claims from Stevenson's own survey data (1997, 1999, 2002). The effect is that the post-raid source dependency is hidden: the chiefdom districts and elite-manager interpretations still derive from an ethnographic-analogical framework, but the 2001 paper presents them as if they emerge from archaeological survey data.

36. **Ladefoged as co-author imports the Hawaiian agricultural framework.** Ladefoged's comparative work on Hawaiian dry-land agriculture (Ladefoged & Graves 2000) and Rotuman political ecology (Ladefoged 1995) provide the theoretical apparatus. The Hawaiian ahupua'a model is explicitly applied to Rapa Nui (p. 21). This is Polynesian analogy operating through co-authorship rather than citation.

37. **The chiefdom is now infrastructure, not hypothesis.** In 1984, "chiefdom" was absent. In 1986, it was absent from Stevenson's text but present in the editorial framing. In 2001, "chiefdom" appears at least 8 times in Stevenson's own text: "chiefdom districts" (Figure 2 caption), "chiefdom complexity" (p. 20), "chiefdom districts" (pp. 18, 21), "chiefdom collapse" (p. 21), "chiefly sponsored" (p. 17), "chiefly polity" (p. 17), "chiefly houses" (p. 21). The term has become the default vocabulary.

## II. The Chiefdom Districts Map (Figure 2)

38. **Figure 2 caption**: "Rapa Nui chiefdom districts circa. AD 1500 (after Stevenson in press)" (p. 18). The map shows the island divided into named territorial units labeled as "chiefdom districts." The source is Stevenson 2002 "Territorial Divisions," cited as "in press."

39. **How the districts are introduced**: The districts appear as a comparison framework for rainfall patterns. The text reads: "If the rainfall pattern is compared with the distribution of island political units (Stevenson in press, Figure 2), we see that not all chiefdom districts had access to the wetter interior regions" (p. 18). The districts are presented as pre-existing "island political units" whose spatial distribution can be compared with environmental data. They are not introduced as hypothesized, modeled, or provisional.

40. **District names**: Ten districts are named: Heki'i, Mahatua, Tongariki, Hanga Poukura, Vinapu, Tahai (characterized as "dryer"), and Vaihu, Akahanga, Tepeu, 'Anakena (characterized as having "sizable upland regions") (pp. 18, 20).

41. **Where the boundaries come from**: The paper does not derive the district boundaries from its own data. The source is "Stevenson in press" (= Stevenson 2002). The 2002 paper in turn derives boundaries from Routledge 1919, Metraux 1940, and McCoy 1976, none of which are cited in the 2001 paper. The district boundaries thus rest on post-slave-raid ethnographic sources whose dependency is concealed by the citation chain: 2001 cites 2002, 2002 cites Routledge/Metraux/McCoy.

42. **The cartographic naturalization mechanism**: By placing district boundaries on a map as a spatial grid for analyzing agricultural data, the 2001 paper transforms the chiefdom district hypothesis into cartographic infrastructure. Subsequent researchers inheriting this map encounter the districts as spatial units, not as interpretive claims. The map caption ("circa AD 1500") dates the districts to the mid-prehistoric period without stating the evidentiary basis for this dating.

43. **Compare Stevenson 1984**: The 1984 dissertation explicitly stated: "it will not be possible to draw boundaries between political units" (p. 162) and "The use of an arbitrary criteria for establishing political divisions... is probably misleading" (p. 162). By 2001, boundaries are drawn, named, dated to "circa AD 1500," and used as the analytical framework for agricultural production data.

## III. Introduction: Theoretical Framework (pp. 17-18)

44. **Environmental uncertainty drives complexity**: "environments characterized by higher uncertainty (e.g., risk of crop failure) will exhibit a greater tempo of changing organizational forms and an overall higher level of organizational complexity" (p. 17). Cites Ladefoged 1995, Graves & Ladefoged 1995, Dunnell 1999, Ladefoged & Graves 2000, Kirch 2000, Hommon 2001, Hunt & Lipo 2001.

45. **Monumental architecture as population suppression**: "the construction of chiefly sponsored monumental architecture can suppress population numbers to below critical levels. Energy that could be expended on increasing agricultural production... is diverted into 'wasteful' activities, such as monumental construction" (p. 17). This framing treats monumental architecture as a chiefly management strategy. The "wasteful" framing derives from Dunnell 1999.

46. **Monumental architecture as ideological tool**: "monumental architecture can be used to bolster ideological beliefs that are necessary for maintaining an integrated chiefly polity (Earle 1997)" (p. 17). Earle's political economy framework is imported as theoretical scaffolding.

47. **Hierarchy required for supra-household production**: "labor intensive forms of agricultural intensification in response to marginal and uncertain environmental conditions require that the authority of the hierarchy be strong in order that production above the household level can be obtained" (p. 18). THIS IS A KEY CLAIM: it asserts that intensive agriculture requires hierarchical authority. The claim is presented as a general principle, not as a hypothesis requiring Rapa Nui-specific evidence.

48. **Research framing assumes hierarchy**: "we look at how the practice of prehistoric agriculture may have contributed to the development and maintenance of a hierarchical social organization" (p. 18). The research question presupposes that a hierarchical social organization existed and that agriculture contributed to it. The alternative question, whether agriculture was practiced without hierarchical organization, is not asked.

## IV. Landscape and Rainfall (p. 18)

49. **Rainfall variability**: Mean annual rainfall >1000 mm at coastal Mataveri station, high coefficient of variation (0.17-0.26), "no periodicity in these short-term rainfall records," dry periods "unpredictable" (p. 18). Data from 1972-1997 records analyzed by Hunt & Lipo 2001.

50. **Orographic rainfall model**: Honorato et al. 1991 predicts "up to double the amount of rainfall characteristic of coastal regions will fall in the upper regions of the three volcanoes beginning near the 200 meter elevation" (p. 18). Only about a sixth of the island benefits from higher rainfall.

51. **Offsetting factors at elevation**: Higher elevations have lower temperatures (0.6-1.0 C per 100 m rise), greater wind velocities, and increased evaporation, "partly offset[ting] the full benefit of increased rainfall amounts" (p. 18).

52. **Differential district access to moisture**: "not all chiefdom districts had access to the wetter interior regions" (p. 18). This is the critical analytical move: agricultural data is organized by chiefdom district, making the political framework the lens through which environmental data is interpreted.

53. **Dryer vs. wetter districts**: Heki'i, Mahatua, Tongariki, Hanga Poukura, Vinapu, Tahai characterized as "dryer"; Vaihu, Akahanga, Tepeu, 'Anakena as having "sizable upland regions" (pp. 18, 20). Tahai and Vinapu noted as having access to Rano Kau moisture but small productive zone.

## V. Agricultural Features (pp. 18-19)

54. **Six types of agricultural features** (pp. 18-19): (1) Mulched soils: small rocks mixed into subsurface sediment to 30-50 cm depth, conserve moisture, reduce temperature fluctuations. (2) Veneer surfaces: 20-30 cm rock layer spread over surface. (3) Stacked boulder concentrations: veneer + stacked rock windbreaks. (4) Pu: steep-sided planting depressions 50-60 cm diameter. (5) Manavai: 2-6 m stacked stone garden enclosures. (6) Planting circles: 1.0-1.5 m diameter stone rings.

55. **Feature identification history**: "a series of surveys and test excavation programs (Stevenson and Cristino 1986; Stevenson 1997; Stevenson and Haoa 1998; Stevenson et al. 1999; Wozniak 1998a, 1998b, 2000) has identified a variety of morphologically distinct agricultural features found throughout the island" (p. 18).

56. **Hypothesized crops**: Sweet potatoes in mulched soils; dryland taro, yams, and possibly sweet potatoes in veneer surfaces and stacked boulder concentrations; dryland taro and yams in pu; bananas in manavai; dryland taro, yams, and sweet potato in planting circles (p. 19).

---

## VI. Heki'i District (pp. 19-20)

57. **Three zones of production** (p. 19): Coastal (<30 m, within 1000 m of coast), Lowland inland (~50-80 m elevation), Lowland interior (~2 km from coast).

58. **Coastal zone**: "heavily settled coastal plain," numerous domestic settlements with "houses, chicken houses, earth ovens," associated with "small household gardens and larger field systems" of manavai, veneer surfaces, and stacked boulder concentrations (p. 19).

59. **Hilltop plantation claim**: "a large hilltop plantation is encountered on top of Maunga Puko Puhi. This flat hilltop is heavily mulched with small basalt stones that cover a shallow (50 cm deep) agricultural soil many hectares in size" (p. 19).

60. **Elite manager hypothesis**: "The houses of elite personnel and a small shrine (ahu) have also been identified in this zone. They are hypothesized to represent a managerial presence" (pp. 19-20). NOTE: "hypothesized" is used here, but the same claim is stated without qualification elsewhere (p. 20: "elite managed plantations"; p. 21: "managed by elite personnel").

61. **Lowland interior**: "Permanent domestic settlement with associated veneer surface and stacked boulder concentration gardens resumes although the overall density of settlement is lower than the near coastal region" (p. 20).

---

## VII. Akahanga/Vaihu District (pp. 19-20)

62. **Three zones**: Coastal (to ~50 m, 1.75 km inland), Lowland inland (50-150 m, 1.75-4 km inland), Upland interior (>150 m, 4-6 km inland).

63. **Upland Vaitea features**: "rectangular house foundations, rectangular platforms of crude field stone, u-shaped structures, cleared walkways with stone borders, circular enclosures with low perimeter walls and open hearths" (p. 20). Notably absent: "larger house patios, chicken houses, manavai and earth ovens."

64. **Rock gardens in protected locations**: "Prehistoric gardens of varying dimensions consisting of veneer surfaces and stacked boulder concentrations are located within the walled enclosures and in the protected areas at the base of slopes and between the basalt outcroppings" (p. 20).

---

## VIII. Chronology of Landscape Use (pp. 20-21)

65. **Settlement model**: "early (ca. AD 800) settlement for coastal regions and a gradual expansion from core areas to other near shore regions" (p. 20). First upland forays ca. AD 1250. By AD 1400-1500 uplands "routinely utilized" (Stevenson 1997). Early AD 1700s uplands abandoned.

66. **Heki'i coastal domestic sites**: "long-term and stable residence locations with occupational spans of over 500 years (AD 1300-1800)" (p. 20). Cites Stevenson et al. 1996.

67. **KEY CLAIM: "Apex of chiefdom complexity" and "elite managed plantations"**: "the heavily mulched hilltop fields located about a kilometer inland were only used for several centuries (ca. AD 1400-1700) and represent an effort to significantly increase production near the apex of chiefdom complexity by the introduction of elite managed plantations (Stevenson et al. 1999)" (p. 20). This sentence contains two critical claims: (a) the AD 1400-1700 period is characterized as the "apex of chiefdom complexity," and (b) the hilltop fields are characterized as "elite managed plantations." The evidence for both claims is the archaeological survey data (hilltop mulched fields with some higher-status architectural features). The interpretation that these are "elite managed" and that the period represents "chiefdom complexity" is not derived from the data but from the chiefdom framework applied to the data.

68. **Hilltop fields contemporary with upland gardens**: "These fields are in production at the same time as the moister upland regions and are a direct expression of an intensified agricultural system" (p. 20).

69. **Interior valley settlement**: "initial settlement was early and began in the AD 1100-1200s and continued until it too was abandoned in the early AD 1700s" (p. 20).

70. **Post-1700 coastal concentration**: "From this time on, virtually all of the island population was living on the island coastal plain" (p. 21).

## IX. Discussion: Hawaiian Ahupua'a Model (p. 21)

71. **Explicit Hawaiian analogy**: "It was based upon the concept of the Hawaiian ahupua'a where each sub-district population segment had access to a strip of land from the coast to the interior and the full range of resources contained within it" (p. 21). This is a direct application of the Hawaiian territorial model to Rapa Nui. The ahupua'a is a specifically Hawaiian institution involving chiefly management of land strips from mountain to sea. Applying it to Rapa Nui constitutes Polynesian analogy: the assumption that Easter Island's territorial organization resembled Hawaii's.

72. **Equal resource assumption**: "Implied in this model is that the resources available to each population segment were more or less equal" (p. 21). The paper then argues this assumption is violated by differential rainfall.

73. **Differential district favorability**: "On Rapa Nui the variation in elevation and moisture availability suggests that some chiefdom districts had more favorable habitats within their boundaries" (p. 21).

---

## X. Discussion: Political Organization Claims (pp. 21-22)

74. **"Chiefdom collapse"**: "prior to chiefdom collapse in the 17th century, the political center of each district was located near the largest ceremonial center (ahu)" (p. 21). "Chiefdom collapse" is presented as an established event. Compare Stevenson 1984 (p. 168), where the equivalent finding was "reverted to a non-ranked corporate structure," and Stevenson 1986 (p. 77), where it became "corporate solidarity weakened." By 2001, this is "chiefdom collapse."

75. **Chiefly houses**: "A cluster of chiefly houses was located immediately inland of the ahu" (p. 21). In 1984 and 1986, these were "elite residential complexes." In 2001, they are "chiefly houses."

76. **Elite managers overseeing production**: "We now know that the rectangular houses represented temporarily occupied dwellings, some of which may have been the residences of elite managers overseeing agricultural production (Stevenson 1997; Stevenson et al. 1999)" (p. 21). "We now know" is a strong epistemic claim. The supporting evidence is the identification of houses near agricultural features, which is interpreted as managerial presence through the chiefdom framework. "May have been" partially hedges this.

77. **Elite-managed plantations (Heki'i)**: "further inland on the edge of the coastal plain of the Heki'i District were large and labor-intensive plantations on low hilltops that were managed by elite personnel and where non-elite occupations did not require permanent surface architecture" (p. 21). "Managed by elite personnel" is stated without hedging here. The evidence is the presence of some higher-status architectural features near agricultural fields, which does not demonstrate management.

78. **Surplus production for chiefly use**: "These latter two areas served as the main production regions in Heki'i District where surplus food supplies were generated" (p. 21). Surplus production is assumed, not demonstrated. No evidence for food surplus beyond household needs is presented.

## XI. Discussion: Agriculture-Architecture Correlation (pp. 21-22)

79. **Correlation claim**: "the development of labor-intensive field systems correlates well with the occurrence of larger ceremonial complexes" (p. 21). Agricultural intensification post-AD 1400 correlates temporally with the largest ahu construction (AD 1400-1600, per Martinsson-Wallin 1994).

80. **Extensive to intensive transition**: "extensive forms of agricultural production were practiced in the coastal regions (from AD 800), in the lowland interior (from AD 1100) and in the upland interior (from AD 1250)" transitioning to "intensive forms of agriculture in each of these same zones occurred after AD 1400 with the later introduction of the sweet potato" (pp. 21-22).

81. **Sweet potato introduction**: "This tuber reached east Polynesia around AD 1000 (Kirch et al. 1995) and was later taken to outlier islands where its cultivation produced yields well above that required for subsistence needs" (p. 22).

82. **Ahu construction supports surplus production**: "The presence of landforms in two parts of the island devoted to intensive agricultural production beginning around AD 1400 and ending around AD 1700 is evidence to support the proposition that corporate work efforts required a larger amount of food than could be produced at the household level" (p. 22). Note: "corporate work efforts" is compatible with both collective action and chiefly command. The paper assumes the latter.

83. **KEY CLAIM: "Ideologically strong and strictly ranked hierarchy"**: "We propose that the return on labor investment was very low and required an ideologically strong and strictly ranked hierarchy, one that emerged in only a few centuries after settlement" (p. 22). THIS IS THE PAPER'S STRONGEST POLITICAL CLAIM. It asserts: (a) agricultural return was very low; (b) low returns required a "strictly ranked hierarchy"; (c) this hierarchy was "ideologically strong"; (d) it emerged "in only a few centuries after settlement." The logical structure is: marginal agriculture -> hierarchy needed -> hierarchy must have existed. The argument is functional: the conditions required hierarchy, therefore hierarchy existed. No independent evidence for the hierarchy is presented. The claim that low agricultural returns REQUIRE hierarchy rather than, say, household-level or community-level cooperation is an assumption, not a demonstrated principle.

## XII. Progressive Hardening: 1984 -> 1986 -> 2001

84. **"Chiefdom" usage**:
- 1984: Absent. Corporate descent groups as analytical frame.
- 1986: Absent from Stevenson's text; present only in Kirch's editorial framing.
- 2001: At least 8 uses in Stevenson's own text. "Chiefdom districts," "chiefdom complexity," "chiefdom collapse," "chiefly sponsored," "chiefly polity," "chiefly houses."

85. **Political boundaries**:
- 1984: "it will not be possible to draw boundaries between political units" (p. 162); "arbitrary criteria... probably misleading" (p. 162).
- 1986: No boundary maps; political organization described without spatial boundaries.
- 2001: Ten named chiefdom districts mapped with boundaries, dated to "circa AD 1500" (Figure 2).

86. **"Elite" terminology**:
- 1984: "elite residential complex," "elite village complex."
- 1986: Same as 1984.
- 2001: "elite managed plantations," "elite personnel," "elite managers overseeing agricultural production," "chiefly houses." The shift from residential description to managerial function.

87. **Late-period transformation**:
- 1984: "reverted to a non-ranked corporate structure" (p. 168). Structural flexibility.
- 1986: "corporate solidarity weakened" (p. 77). Degradation from a norm.
- 2001: "chiefdom collapse in the 17th century" (p. 21). Full adoption of the collapse narrative.

88. **Theoretical framework**:
- 1984: British social anthropology (Fortes, Firth, Kirchhoff). Corporate descent groups as principled alternative to chiefdom typology.
- 1986: Method without theory. Same corporate descent group analysis, stripped of theoretical infrastructure.
- 2001: Evolutionary ecology (Ladefoged, Graves, Dunnell, Earle). Hawaiian ahupua'a model. The theoretical framework is now explicitly compatible with the chiefdom model.

89. **Post-raid source dependency**:
- 1984: McCall 1979, Goldman 1970, Metraux 1940, Routledge 1919 all cited. Ethnographic data to be used "selectively and with caution" (p. 23).
- 1986: Same post-raid sources cited, caveat weakened.
- 2001: None of these sources cited. The chiefdom districts derived from Routledge/Metraux/McCoy via Stevenson 2002 are presented as "island political units" without acknowledging the ethnographic origin.

## XIII. Circular Argumentation

90. **The agriculture-chiefdom circle**: The paper's central argument has a circular structure:
- Premise: Chiefdom districts existed (from Stevenson 2002, based on ethnographic sources).
- Observation: Agricultural features are differentially distributed across these districts.
- Conclusion: Agricultural production was organized by chiefdom-level authority ("elite managed plantations," "hierarchy required").
- The conclusion (chiefdom organization of agriculture) is then presented as evidence for the premise (chiefdom districts existed). Agricultural data organized by chiefdom districts becomes evidence for chiefdom-level organization of agriculture.

91. **The architecture-hierarchy circle**: The argument that intensive agriculture correlates with large ahu construction (p. 21-22) is presented as evidence for surplus production supporting "corporate work efforts." But the interpretation of ahu as products of chiefly-directed corporate work is itself the chiefdom framework that the agricultural data is supposed to support.

92. **The elite-manager circle**: Houses near agricultural features are identified as "elite managers overseeing agricultural production" (p. 21). The "elite" identification derives from the chiefdom framework (in a chiefdom, agricultural fields have elite managers). The managerial function is inferred from the location (near fields) plus the framework (chiefdoms have managers). The "evidence" for elite management is the proximity of houses to fields interpreted through the chiefdom model.

## XIV. Post-Raid Source Dependency

93. **Hidden dependency**: The chiefdom districts mapped in Figure 2 derive from Stevenson 2002, which derives territorial boundaries from Routledge 1919, Metraux 1940, and McCoy 1976 (who maps Routledge/Metraux boundaries). None of these post-raid sources are cited in the 2001 paper. The ethnographic foundation of the chiefdom district framework is invisible to the reader. [POST-RAID]

94. **No direct citations of contact-era or post-raid sources**: Roggeveen, Gonzalez, Cook, La Perouse, Thompson, Routledge, Metraux, and Englert are all absent from the bibliography. The paper presents its claims as emerging from archaeological survey data and evolutionary ecological theory, concealing the ethnographic-analogical chain on which the chiefdom framework depends.

## XV. Assessment Against Current Evidence

95. **Davis et al. 2024 contradicts agricultural extent claims**: The paper assumes extensive agricultural field systems across the island. Davis et al. 2024 found rock gardens cover only 0.76 km2, approximately one-fifth of prior estimates. Maximum carrying capacity ~3,900, average ~2,000-3,000. The "large-scale" intensive production assumed by Stevenson et al. 2001 was never as extensive as believed.

96. **Lipo et al. 2025 contradicts centralized production**: The paper assumes centralized, elite-managed production. Lipo et al. 2025 found 30 redundant quarry workshops with varied methods, consistent with autonomous group-level production, not centralized management.

97. **DiNapoli et al. 2019 contradicts territorial interpretation**: The paper organizes agricultural data by chiefdom districts assumed to represent territorial political units. DiNapoli et al. 2019 found ahu placement explained by freshwater access, not territorial control.

98. **DiNapoli et al. 2020 contradicts the ahu construction timeline**: The paper dates the largest ahu to AD 1400-1600 and links them to agricultural surplus. DiNapoli et al. 2020 found steady ahu construction tempo with no pre-contact cessation, complicating the "apex then collapse" narrative.

99. **Mulrooney 2013 contradicts upland abandonment**: The paper states uplands were "abandoned" in the early AD 1700s. Mulrooney 2013 found settlement and land-use continuity, not abandonment: "inland agricultural areas (1-3.5 km from coast) were not abandoned prior to European contact."

100. **Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 contradicts population collapse**: The "chiefdom collapse in the 17th century" claim assumes a population decline. Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 (ancient DNA) rejects a 1600s population collapse: population grew steadily through European contact.

101. **The "ideologically strong and strictly ranked hierarchy" claim**: No independent evidence for strict ranking or ideological authority is presented. The claim rests entirely on the functional argument that marginal agriculture required hierarchy. Collective action theory (Blanton & Fargher, Ostrom) provides an alternative: community-level cooperation can achieve supra-household production without hierarchical command. The evidence for "elite managers" (houses near fields) is equally consistent with seasonal labor camps, extended family farming stations, or community work parties.

## XVI. Honest Complications

102. **The agricultural data are valuable regardless of framework.** The six-type classification of agricultural features, the three-zone settlement model, and the chronological framework are useful empirical contributions independent of the chiefdom interpretation. The problem is not the data but the interpretive framework imposed on them.

103. **The environmental risk argument has merit.** Rainfall variability is real, orographic effects are real, and differential access to moisture by geographic location is real. These observations do not require a chiefdom framework.

104. **"Hypothesized" is used in some places.** The elite-manager interpretation is called "hypothesized" at first (pp. 19-20) before being stated without qualification later (pp. 20, 21). This suggests awareness that the claim needs support, even though the paper does not consistently hedge it.

105. **The ahupua'a model is explicitly labeled.** Unlike some Polynesian analogies that operate implicitly, the Hawaiian ahupua'a model is named and its application to Rapa Nui is acknowledged (p. 21). This makes the analogical reasoning visible, which is methodologically better than concealing it.

106. **The chronological data may support a different narrative.** The temporal correlation between intensive agriculture (post-AD 1400) and large ahu construction (AD 1400-1600) is consistent with collective action models: more food enables more communal projects, without requiring chiefly extraction of surplus. The data are compatible with, but do not require, the chiefdom interpretation.
