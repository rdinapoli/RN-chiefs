# Claims Extraction: Mulrooney 2013

**Source**: Mulrooney, Mara A. 2013. "An island-wide assessment of the chronology of settlement and land use on Rapa Nui (Easter Island) based on radiocarbon data." *Journal of Archaeological Science* 40: 4377-4399.

**Type**: Journal article (radiocarbon chronology assessment)
**Accessed**: PDF (`mulrooney_2013.pdf`)
**Extracted**: 2026-03-16

## I. Core Argument and Framing

1. "The popular narrative of societal collapse prior to European contact relies on chronometric data from the late pre-European contact period and also cites major settlement shifts as evidence for societal collapse and socio-political reorganization." (p. 4377, abstract)

2. "The results of this study suggest that Rapa Nui settlement and land use exhibit continuity rather than punctuated, detrimental change during the late pre-European contact period." (p. 4377, abstract)

**Assessment**: The central finding directly undermines the collapse narrative that is intertwined with the chiefdom-to-warfare transition model (Stevenson and Haoa Cardinali 2008). If settlement and land use show continuity rather than disruption, the supposed breakdown of chiefly authority in the late prehistoric period lacks its primary archaeological correlate.

3. "The story of Rapa Nui's demise has been reified into orthodoxy in the archaeological literature and popular media, and authors have gone so far as to use this small, isolated island as an analogy for the planet in their interpretation of the 'ecodisaster' that the Rapa Nui people brought upon themselves in the late pre-European contact period (e.g., Bahn and Flenley, 1992; Flenley and Bahn, 2002)." (p. 4377)

4. "Although some scholars have begun to challenge this narrative, Rapa Nui continues to be widely perceived as 'the clearest example of a society that destroyed itself by over-exploiting its own resources' (Diamond, 2005:118)." (p. 4377)

---

## II. Critique of Existing Chronological Models

### The Stevenson and Haoa Cardinali (2008) 5-phase settlement model

5. Stevenson and Haoa Cardinali (2008) proposed a 5-phase model for island-wide settlement. According to this model, "areas in the uplands of Maunga Terevaka and some other localized interior areas were completely abandoned beginning at AD 1680" (p. 4378, describing Stevenson and Haoa Cardinali 2008, Fig. 1).

6. The 5-phase model was based on Heyerdahl and Ferdon's (1961) 3-phase cultural chronology. "Heyerdahl and Ferdon's 'Middle Period' (AD 1100-1680) was subdivided into the 'Expansion and Development' (AD 1100-1425) and 'Chiefdom Integration' (AD 1425-1680) periods and their 'Late Period' (AD 1680 onwards) was divided into 'Warfare and Fragmentation' (AD 1680-1750) and 'Post-Contact Decline' (AD 1750-1868)." (p. 4378)

**Assessment**: The very naming of the phases ("Chiefdom Integration," "Warfare and Fragmentation") embeds the chiefdom narrative into the chronological framework. Mulrooney's data challenge this periodization empirically.

7. "Island-wide shifts in settlement have also been noted as evidence for a pre-European contact societal collapse. Stevenson (1997), Vargas Casanova (1998) and others have proposed the abandonment of inland areas late in the pre-European contact (pre-AD 1722) sequence as evidence for collapse. According to these researchers, inland areas containing intensified agricultural field systems were abandoned with the breakdown of the chiefly economy." (p. 4377)

8. Stevenson and Haoa Cardinali (2008:171) stated: "If this pattern [the abandonment of the more upland area of Maunga Tari on Maunga Terevaka] were an island-wide phenomenon that correlated with the disintegration of the chiefdom reported to have occurred in the late 1600s, then it should be manifested in the Hanga Ho'onu area as well." (p. 4378)

**Assessment**: This explicitly ties the settlement shift hypothesis to the chiefdom model. Mulrooney's finding that the settlement shift did not occur as predicted therefore undermines the archaeological evidence claimed for the chiefdom's disintegration.

### Problems with prior chronological models

9. "These chronological models have not considered the reliability of the chronometric data on which they are based. Many have heavily relied on unreliable radiocarbon (14C) determinations as well as obsidian hydration dates processed in the 1980s, 1990s and 2000s, which are questionable due to methodological limitations (see Hunt and Lipo, 2009; Mulrooney, 2012)." (p. 4378)

10. "Although Hunt and Lipo (2006), Martinsson-Wallin and Crockford (2002), and Wilmshurst et al. (2011) have assessed radiocarbon dates to explore the initial colonization and settlement of the island, to date, no inclusive analysis of the large corpus of radiocarbon dates from Rapa Nui has been attempted." (p. 4378)

## III. The Radiocarbon Database

11. "The overall database contains 453 previously published 14C determinations (298 from archaeological contexts, 3 from museum objects, and 152 from palaeoecological contexts) and 15 additional determinations from recent stratigraphic excavations in the HHPA (Mulrooney, 2012)." (p. 4378)

12. A total of 313 determinations from archaeological contexts were isolated for analysis. Dates from palaeoecological contexts (122 dates from sediment cores and 30 dates from landforms) were excluded, as were 3 dates from artifacts in museum collections. (p. 4379)

### Sampling bias

13. "Overall, there has been a sampling bias towards coastal contexts, with 228 dates (72.8% of the total sample) coming from excavated contexts situated less than 500 m from the coast (Table 1)." (p. 4379)

14. "Over 1/4 of the total sample (89 of 313 dates) of dates from archaeological contexts come from the area of Hanga Rau in 'Anakena on the north coast, which highlights the spatially restricted nature of most archaeological research endeavours on the island." (p. 4379)

15. "If we exclude the 89 dates collected from Hanga Rau in 'Anakena, over sixty per cent (139 of 224; 62.1%) of the corpus of dates come from coastal areas situated within 500 m of the coast." (p. 4381)

**Assessment**: The heavy coastal sampling bias means that claims about inland abandonment are based on a comparatively thin dataset, which makes Mulrooney's finding that even this thin inland dataset does NOT show abandonment all the more significant.

16. "Researchers have tended to focus on coastal locales that contain large ceremonial architecture as well as extensive remains of residential features. The vast majority of dates come from landscape contexts, including excavated areas that are not stratigraphically associated with architectural features, but are associated with other cultural materials such as portable artifacts." (p. 4381)

---

## IV. Reliability Classification

17. Three major reliability classes: "1) dates collected from secure cultural deposits that include wood charcoal from identified short-lived species, palm endocarps, and algal nodules; 2) dates from secure cultural deposits on unidentified wood charcoal (Spriggs and Anderson's criteria L); and 3) generally unreliable dates that are not reliable due to problems of mixed isotopic fractionation (Spriggs and Anderson's criteria I), dates from the Gakushuin Laboratory (Spriggs and Anderson's criteria A), dates on coral and bone that are questionable due to calibration issues (Spriggs and Anderson's criteria B), and dates from questionable contexts (Spriggs and Anderson's criteria D, F, G)." (p. 4380)

18. "In total, 51 dates were classified as Class 1 dates, which are the most reliable. Class 2 includes 200 dates, and 62 dates were placed into Class 3, which includes the least reliable dates." (p. 4381)

19. "In sum, over two-thirds of the dates come from ceremonial and residential contexts, and dates from agricultural contexts account for less than 15% of the total sample. All other general functional classes combined account for less than 1% of the sample." (p. 4381)

---

## V. Calibration Curve Effects

20. "Although the Class 1 dataset (n = 51) contained less dates than the artificial dataset of continuous dates (n = 79), while the Class 2 dataset (n = 200) contained more dates, the three summed probability distributions were similar in their overall distributions, which may suggest that the archaeological datasets exhibit continuity through time." (p. 4382)

21. "When summed, the artificial dataset contained dips in the frequency of calibrated dates from the latter halves of the 15th and 17th centuries and the archaeological and logical datasets mimicked this trend. These results suggest that these decreases in the frequency of calibrated dates from these time periods may not reflect behaviourally meaningful patterns, but instead reflect the nature of the calibration curve for these time periods." (p. 4382)

**Assessment**: This is a critical methodological point. Apparent gaps in activity that previous researchers interpreted as evidence for cultural disruption or collapse may be artifacts of the calibration curve, not real behavioral changes. This further undermines the chronometric basis for the collapse scenario.

## VI. Results: Testing the Collapse Scenario

### Overall temporal distribution

22. "Class 1 and 2 dates from contexts located 1-2 km from the coast (n = 37) display a somewhat continuous temporal distribution. These dates are mostly from landscape contexts and cover most of the cultural sequence, beginning by at least the 14th century, and suggest continuous use of these areas until the post-contact period." (p. 4383)

23. "Dates from contexts situated 2-3.5 km from the coast (n = 10) account for less than 4% of the overall Class 1 and 2 sample, but do contain a relatively even distribution of dates from the mid-15th century until the post-contact period." (p. 4383)

24. "Those dates that come from contexts located more than 3.5 km from the coast also constitute a small percentage (ca. 3.2%) of the overall sample. All of these (n = 8) cluster to the 15th to early 17th centuries when calibrated." (p. 4383)

25. "It is difficult to discern whether the temporal distribution of these dates might reflect late prehistoric expansion followed by pre-European contact abandonment of these areas, or whether the more limited distribution of these dates is also the result of insufficient sampling." (p. 4383)

**Assessment**: For the far interior (>3.5 km), the data are too thin to distinguish between abandonment and sampling insufficiency. This is an honest acknowledgment that complicates both the collapse and continuity narratives for the most remote upland areas.

### Inland contexts compared to artificial datasets

26. "As shown in Fig. 5, the archaeological dates do not conform to the expectations of the collapse scenario, and instead exhibit a high probability of determinations dating to the second half of the 17th century and onwards that is broadly similar to the probability distribution of the artificial continuous dataset." (p. 4383)

27. "The post-AD 1680 distribution of summed archaeological dates suggests that these areas continued to be occupied at a continuous rate. This is extremely apparent when compared to the artificial collapse dataset, which drops off considerably when calibrated." (p. 4383)

**Assessment**: This is the central empirical finding. When inland dates are compared against an artificial dataset modeled on the expected collapse pattern, the archaeological data clearly do not match the collapse prediction. Instead, they match the continuous occupation prediction.

### Inland agricultural contexts (Class 1)

28. "Class 1 dates from agricultural contexts present the most limited sub-dataset of these data (n = 3). Nonetheless, these dates were individually calibrated using OxCal 4.1.7 (Fig. 6) and constrained by assigning a boundary condition at AD 1680, the date of the proposed collapse, in order to assess whether or not the probability distributions of these dates fell before this date, as would be expected based on the collapse scenario." (p. 4383)

29. "As these data show, all three dates from inland contexts have probability distributions that do not meet the expectations of the collapse scenario. When calibrated, the majority of the probability distributions at 1 SD for two of these dates fall fully within the post-contact period (Beta-254389: 69.8%; Beta-254391: 87.1%) and the same is true for a significant percentage of the 1 SD probability distribution of the third date (Beta-254390: 35.8%)." (p. 4384)

30. "These all come from excavated contexts in the inland portion of the Hiva Hiva area, situated 2.2-2.6 km from the coast on the west coast of the island." (p. 4384)

31. "Overall, it is more likely that the archaeological target events that the Hiva Hiva dates correspond to may actually reflect post-contact phenomena. As noted by Stevenson et al. (2008), this suggests that these intensified agricultural areas were not abandoned during late prehistory." (p. 4384)

### Inland agricultural contexts (Class 2)

32. "Class 2 dates from agricultural contexts encompass a slightly more robust dataset (n = 14). When calibrated (Fig. 7), they cover a wider range of the cultural sequence and include a Class 2b date that is markedly early (Beta-199324)." (p. 4384)

33. "All three of these dates contain probability distributions at 1 SD that fall mostly within the post-contact period (Beta-144309: 64.7%; Beta-238063: 70.2%; Beta 237461: 76.1%). This again suggests that it is more probable that these intensified agricultural areas were not abandoned in late prehistory." (p. 4384)

34. "Although these sampled areas are not in the far upland areas of Maunga Terevaka (>3.5 km from the coast), they are situated in localized situations that we may expect to have been abandoned according to the supposed breakdown of the chiefly economy according to the collapse narrative. This applies to both the intensified Maunga Puko Puhi plantation area in the HHPA and the inland portion of the Hiva Hiva area, which contains a high density of agricultural features (Stevenson et al., 2008)." (p. 4384)

**Assessment**: Mulrooney explicitly tests the prediction that follows from the chiefdom-collapse model: if the chiefly economy broke down, intensified agricultural areas should have been abandoned. The data do not support this prediction.

### Inland residential contexts

35. "Dates from inland residential contexts were also assessed. As these data (Figs. 8 and 9) show, a number of dates from inland residential contexts have probability distributions that do not meet the expectations of the collapse scenario." (p. 4384)

36. "The majority of the probability distributions of four residential Class 1 landscape dates from the interior areas of the HHPA also fell fully within the post-contact period (Wk-27378: 62.5%; Wk-24286: 69.3%; Wk-27380: 77.7%; Wk-24287: 74.1%)." (p. 4385)

37. "This means that overall, it is more likely that the target events that these seven, and possibly eight, dates correspond to are actually post-contact phenomena. This does not support the notion that these areas of the landscape were abandoned in late prehistory." (p. 4385)

## VII. Discussion: Settlement Continuity

38. "In examining the spatial and temporal distribution of 14C dates from across the island, it is clear that inland and coastal areas were continuously utilized. Beginning early on in the cultural sequence, and continuing right through to European contact and the post-contact period, most areas of the island appear to have been used by the ancient Rapa Nui based on the distribution of radiocarbon dates." (pp. 4385-4386)

39. "Except for the very far upland region (>3.5 km) this pattern contradicts the claims of previous studies (Stevenson and Haoa, 1998; Stevenson and Haoa Cardinali, 2008; Vargas Casanova, 1998; Vargas et al., 2006) for widespread abandonment of non-coastal areas during late prehistory." (p. 4386)

40. "Currently, the most intensively sampled area of the island is the HHPA, whose inhabitants intensified production in the Maunga Puko Puhi plantation area in the interior 'Hilltop zone' situated from 1 to 2.5 km inland from the coast, and this area does not show signs of abandonment according to the collapse scenario." (p. 4386)

41. "Overall, the results of the present analysis, although limited due to a small sample size of dates from inland contexts, show that the expected pattern according to the collapse scenario is not documented in the HHPA or other areas, including Hiva Hiva, Vaitea, and Poike." (p. 4386)

### Research bias

42. "The analysis undertaken in this project has elucidated the largely coastal bias of previous archaeological research endeavours on Rapa Nui. Nonetheless, this analysis has also shown that, despite this research bias, there is ample evidence of continued use of inland sampled locations and there is not sufficient evidence to support widespread abandonment of all inland areas of the island during the late pre-European contact cultural sequence." (p. 4386)

## VIII. Discussion: Chronology of Initial Settlement

43. "The earliest Class 1 date from 'Anakena, which has a 1 SD age range of AD 1054-1279." (p. 4386)

44. "The overall corpus of dates suggests permanent and widespread settlement on the island by at least AD 1200, with initial colonization possibly having occurred significantly earlier, as AD 1200 reflects the median age estimates of the earliest reliable Class 1 dates from 'Anakena and does not take the full probability distribution into account (see Shepardson et al., 2008)." (p. 4386)

**Assessment [LATE COLONIZATION]**: This finding is broadly consistent with Hunt and Lipo (2006), who proposed initial colonization ca. AD 1200. Mulrooney's corpus suggests settlement "by at least AD 1200" with possible earlier colonization, which is compatible with the late colonization model though not precisely confirmatory. The precision limits of the earliest dates prevent resolution of the early vs. late colonization debate.

45. "Interestingly, the Class 1 and Class 2 dates from the earlier portion of the cultural sequence are not predominantly from TPQ contexts, and are instead mostly from landscape and target event contexts. This means that these dates are appropriate for making a conservative estimate for the initial colonization and settlement of the island at this time because they do not pre-date the construction of archaeological features, but instead date samples that are temporally associated with specific events and/or the general use of the landscape." (p. 4386)

## IX. Discussion: Settlement and the Chiefly Economy

46. "The results of this analysis, especially in relation to dates from inland contexts situated 1-3.5 km from the coast which were isolated for analysis, empirically refute one component of the collapse scenario. If the chiefly economy collapsed during late prehistory, the reorganization of the sociopolitical structure would have potentially resulted in the complete abandonment of intensified agricultural areas situated away from the coast." (p. 4387)

**Assessment**: This is the most directly relevant passage for the chiefdom question. Mulrooney frames the inland abandonment pattern as a testable prediction of the chiefdom-collapse model and demonstrates that the prediction fails. The chiefly economy collapse, if it occurred, did not produce the settlement pattern changes it was supposed to produce.

47. "Currently, there is not evidence of widespread abandonment of inland locations prior to European contact. It instead appears that the ancient Rapa Nui continued to occupy and utilize most inland locations up to and following European contact." (p. 4387)

---

## X. Conclusion

48. "The overall temporal distribution of 14C dates from archaeological contexts on Rapa Nui appears to be reflective of continuous settlement and land use through time based on the results of this study." (p. 4387)

49. "Despite the fact that there has been a largely coastal bias in research endeavours, there is sufficient evidence to suggest that there was no widespread settlement shift during late prehistory as proposed by previous researchers utilizing a settlement pattern approach." (p. 4387)

50. "It is clear that these data are unsupportive of the idea that inland areas of the landscape were completely abandoned during late prehistory." (p. 4386)

## XI. Bearing on Chiefdom Attribution and Collapse Narrative

### Claims that support the project argument

- The collapse scenario, which depends on the chiefdom model (breakdown of chiefly economy leads to abandonment of managed agricultural zones), is empirically tested and found unsupported by the radiocarbon evidence.
- The chronological frameworks previously used to periodize Rapa Nui prehistory (including the "Chiefdom Integration" phase) are built on unreliable or uncritically assessed radiocarbon dates and obsidian hydration dates.
- Apparent gaps in activity attributed to cultural disruption may be artifacts of the radiocarbon calibration curve rather than real behavioral changes.
- The coastal sampling bias means that previous claims about inland abandonment were based on absence of evidence from areas that were never adequately sampled.

### Claims that complicate the project argument

- Data from the far upland region (>3.5 km from coast) are insufficient to determine whether those areas were abandoned or simply under-sampled. The continuity argument is strongest for the 1-3.5 km zone, not for the most remote interior.
- The paper does not directly address political organization; it addresses settlement patterns and land use. The inference from settlement continuity to the absence of political reorganization is our extrapolation, not Mulrooney's explicit claim.
- Small sample sizes for inland contexts (e.g., n = 3 for Class 1 agricultural dates) mean the continuity argument rests on limited data, even if those data consistently point in the same direction.

### Relationship to late colonization debate

- The corpus suggests permanent settlement by at least AD 1200, broadly consistent with Hunt and Lipo (2006).
- Earliest reliable dates are from 'Anakena with wide confidence intervals; the data cannot resolve the early vs. late colonization debate definitively.
- Class 1 and 2 early dates are mostly from landscape/target event contexts rather than TPQ contexts, making them appropriate for conservative colonization estimates.

---

## XII. Methodological Notes for Our Paper

Mulrooney's approach of comparing archaeological date distributions against artificial datasets (one reflecting continuity, one reflecting collapse) is a useful methodological model. It shows how the collapse scenario can be formalized as a testable prediction and then evaluated against data, rather than assumed as the interpretive framework.

The paper also demonstrates the importance of assessing chronometric data quality before building cultural chronologies. The fact that previous models (including Stevenson's 5-phase model with its "Chiefdom Integration" phase) were built on uncritically assessed dates, including unreliable ones, is relevant to our argument about the evidentiary foundations of the chiefdom attribution.
