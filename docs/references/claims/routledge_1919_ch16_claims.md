# Claims Extraction: Routledge 1919, Chapter XVI

## Source Information

- **Full citation**: Routledge, Katherine. *The Mystery of Easter Island*. London: Sifton, Praed & Co., 1919. Chapter XVI: "Native Culture (continued) - Religion and Bird Cult," pp. 236-271.
- **PDF file(s)**: PDF pages 369-428
- **Date of visit/observation**: 1914-1915 (Mana Expedition)
- **Duration on island**: Approximately 16 months
- **Context of visit**: Archaeological/ethnographic expedition. Routledge conducted extensive interviews with elderly Rapanui informants, several of whom had personal memories of pre-missionary life. Key informants for this chapter: Te Haha (a Miru, ariki-paka in Ngaara's entourage), Viriamo (elderly woman, carried food to Orongo), Jotefa (oldest man in village), Juan (Tepano), Kapiera, Tomenika (last tau expert, in leper colony), Porotu (former hopu), Kilimuti (tangata manu who died during expedition). [POST-RAID: All informants are post-slave-raid generation; Routledge's information about pre-contact institutions is reported tradition, not direct observation of functioning systems.]

## 8. CROSS-REFERENCE NOTES

### Routledge vs. Thomson 1891 on Tangata Manu

Thomson (1891): The tangata manu received "no especial authority" (the exact quote needs verification against Thomson claims file). Thomson emphasizes ceremonial/sacred aspects.

Routledge (1919): Food gifts were "the sole practical advantage of victory" but adds that "those who did not contribute were apt to have their houses burnt" (p. 264). This adds a coercive element Thomson does not mention.

**Assessment**: Routledge's account is more detailed and adds the house-burning coercion, but her overall characterization aligns with Thomson's that the tangata manu's status was primarily sacred/ritual rather than political-governmental. The key phrase "sole practical advantage" is Routledge's own interpretive summary.

### Routledge vs. Roussel 1869 on Chiefly Authority

Roussel describes a "chief's authority." Routledge provides much more detail but paints a picture of severely limited chiefly (ariki-mau) authority: no military, judicial, or priestly power; unable to compel obedience from his own servant; captured and enslaved by the Ngaure clan.

### The Ariki-mau / Tangata-manu Dual System

Routledge's account is the most detailed source describing what appears to be a dual system:
- The ariki-mau (hereditary, Miru-only) held supernatural food-increase powers, authority over rongorongo, and ceremonial prestige
- The tangata-manu (annual, rotating, any clan via Ao system) held temporary sacred status with limited practical benefits

These appear to be separate and potentially competing institutions. The ariki-mau "never appeared at Orongo" (p. 260), suggesting deliberate separation. The Miru could select the next Ao but could also be victimized by it (4.2.4, 4.2.8). The ariki-mau's capture by the Ngaure during their Ao period (2.16) demonstrates that Ao dominance could override ariki-mau status.

## 9. SUMMARY ASSESSMENT

### What This Chapter Can Tell Us

Routledge's Chapter XVI is the single most detailed ethnographic account of the tangata manu institution and the ariki-mau's functions. Her informants include individuals with personal memories of pre-missionary life (Te Haha, Viriamo, Jotefa), and she cross-checked accounts against multiple sources. The level of institutional detail, including spatial arrangements at Orongo and Motu Nui, ritual procedures, the Ao system, and the rongorongo gatherings, is unmatched in any other source.

### What This Chapter Cannot Tell Us

[POST-RAID] All of Routledge's informants were post-slave-raid generation. None could have witnessed the tangata manu or ariki-mau systems functioning in their undisrupted pre-contact form. The systems they describe were already late, possibly degenerate forms: Routledge herself notes the cult "admittedly degenerated in later years" (p. 265). The account of Ngaara's reign is a recollection of the final ariki-mau, a period already marked by inter-clan warfare and the imminent collapse of the old order. Whether Ngaara's limited authority was typical or reflected the institution in decline cannot be determined from this source alone.

### Key Findings for the Chiefdom Question

1. **The ariki-mau's authority was explicitly non-political**: "neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest" (p. 242). His authority was supernatural (food increase) and cultural (rongorongo oversight).

2. **The Ao/tangata manu system was a rotating competitive institution** in which clans took turns in ascendancy, with the tangata manu returning to "ordinary life" after one year (p. 265). This is fundamentally different from permanent hierarchical chiefdom.

3. **The tangata manu's practical benefits were minimal**: food gifts were "the sole practical advantage of victory" (p. 264). His five months of strict taboo seclusion, sleeping all day, not washing, suggests sacred rather than political authority.

4. **The ariki-mau lacked coercive power**: unable to recover a stolen tablet from his own servant (p. 249), captured and enslaved by the Ngaure (p. 246).

5. **Multiple clans participated in the system**: every clan had rongorongo experts (p. 244), the Ao rotated among clans (p. 258), the ivi-atua who controlled nominations operated independently (p. 260).

6. **There were no priests** (p. 239), no formal religious worship (Eyraud, p. 236), and no sacrifice (p. 236).

7. **The dual ariki-mau / tangata-manu system** was characterized by separation (ariki-mau absent from Orongo) and potential tension (Miru victimized when not holding Ao).

This evidence is more consistent with a decentralized, rotating competitive system than with a permanent hierarchical chiefdom. The ariki-mau held recognized but severely circumscribed supernatural and ceremonial authority; the tangata manu held temporary, annually rotating sacred status with minimal practical advantage. Neither institution, as described by Routledge, corresponds to the redistributive paramount chiefdom of the Kirch/Sahlins model.
