## I. Intellectual Genealogy

### Who Stevenson cites in 1986

1. **McCall 1979**: PRIMARY source for kinship structure. "Most of the information presented below derives from McCall (1979), who summarizes the local kinship structure" (p. 69). Five levels of segmentation, corporate group interpretation, boundary markers. Same dependency as 1984.

2. **Metraux 1940**: Oral traditions for founding king narrative (p. 69: "Metraux 1940, 121"). Also cited for Orongo and Makemake worship (p. 77). Same as 1984.

3. **Goldman 1970**: Dual status system (p. 70: "Goldman 1970, 109-10"). Hereditary chiefs vs. warrior chiefs (matatoa). Same as 1984, though engagement is briefer.

4. **McCoy 1976**: Settlement pattern surveys (p. 69); environmental deterioration and resource shortages (p. 70, 77). Same as 1984.

5. **Routledge 1919**: Ethnographic data (p. 69). Same as 1984.

6. **Brown 1974**: Corporate group attributes (p. 70). Same as 1984.

7. **Tiffany 1976**: Corporate group definitions (p. 70). Same as 1984.

8. **Cristino, Vargas, and Izaurieta 1981; Cristino and Vargas 1980**: Site surveys. NEW citation not in 1984 bibliography (Centro de Estudios data acknowledged in 1984 but formal citation added here).

9. **Michels et al. 1984**: Obsidian hydration rate re-evaluation (p. 69). NEW citation.

10. **Flenley and King 1984**: Radiocarbon dates from crater lake cores for deforestation, "990 ± 70 bp" (p. 77). NEW citation, not in 1984. Strengthens environmental deterioration narrative.

11. **Stevenson 1984**: Self-citation for population growth curve (p. 77) and semipyramidal construction dates (p. 76).

12. **Seelenfreund 1982**: Burial structure excavations (p. 74). Same as 1984.

### Ibo ethnographic sources (retained from 1984)

13. **Jones 1949, 1961; Ardener 1959; Udo 1965; Meek 1937; Onwuejeogwu 1981**: Full Ibo ethnographic apparatus retained. The African analogue occupies approximately 2.5 of the chapter's 9 pages.

### What is NOT cited in 1986

14. **Service 1962 still NOT cited.** The band/tribe/chiefdom/state typology remains absent.

15. **Kirch 1984 NOT cited**, despite this chapter appearing in Kirch's own edited volume. This is remarkable: the volume editor's major theoretical work is not referenced by the contributor.

16. **Sahlins 1958 NOT cited.** Still absent, as in 1984.

17. **Sahlins 1963 NOT cited.** Still absent.

18. **Fortes 1953/1959 NOT cited.** The British social anthropology theoretical framework that grounded the 1984 dissertation is entirely absent. This is a significant omission: the theoretical scaffolding that made corporate descent group analysis a principled alternative to neoevolutionary typology has been removed.

19. **Firth 1936/1957 NOT cited.** Polynesian kinship baseline absent.

20. **Kirchhoff 1955 NOT cited.** Conical clan concept absent.

21. **Hayden & Cannon 1982 NOT cited.** The corporate group archaeology framework that provided the methodological foundation for the 1984 dissertation is absent.

22. **Fried 1957 NOT cited.** Ranked society concept absent.

23. **Befu & Plotnicov 1962 NOT cited.** Descent group typology absent.

### Assessment: what the citation changes mean

24. **The theoretical infrastructure is gone; the method survives.** The 1984 dissertation was grounded in an explicit theoretical tradition (British social anthropology, corporate group theory) that constituted an alternative to the Sahlins-Service-Kirch neoevolutionary framework. In 1986, the theoretical sources (Fortes, Firth, Kirchhoff, Hayden & Cannon, Fried) are all absent, but the method (corporate group identification through ritual architecture, using Ibo analogy) is retained. The chapter presents the analytical approach as practical technique without the theoretical commitments that made it a principled alternative. This makes the corporate group analysis appear compatible with the chiefdom framework rather than constituting an independent intellectual tradition.

## II. Changes from 1984 Dissertation

### A. Terminology shifts

25. **"Chiefdom" does NOT appear in Stevenson's text.** The term is entirely absent from all 9 pages. The analytical vocabulary is: "socio-political structure," "corporate descent group," "lineage," "autonomous political units," "elite," "commoner." This confirms that Stevenson did not adopt the chiefdom label in 1986.

26. **"Corporate descent group" remains the primary analytical frame.** The chapter title itself says "socio-political structure," and the opening states: "the corporate group will serve as the unit of analysis" (p. 70). This is unchanged from 1984.

27. **"Elite" and "commoner" language retained.** "Elite village complex" (p. 74), "elite residential complex" (p. 74, 76), "elite and commoner population" (p. 77). Same as 1984.

28. **"Autonomous political units" retained.** "Four autonomous political units can be identified prior to AD 1700, each composed of an elite and commoner population" (p. 77). Same language as 1984.

29. **New term: "competitive strategy."** "The maintenance of corporate territories appears to have given way to a more competitive strategy based on the acquisition of land through warfare" (p. 77). This framing is not in the 1984 dissertation and introduces a competitive/warfare narrative that is compatible with (but not identical to) the ecocide framework.

### B. Hedging retained vs. dropped

30. **RETAINED: "preliminary reconstruction"** (p. 69): "This paper offers a preliminary reconstruction of the island's prehistoric socio-political structure." Parallels 1984's "preliminary propositions" (p. 20) and "preliminary model" (p. 181).

31. **RETAINED: "hypothesized"** used multiple times. "This relationship between the hereditary chiefs and the position of warrior chief is hypothesized to have arisen out of conditions of increasing social instability" (p. 70). "It is hypothesized that the corporate solidarity of these communities had been weakened" (p. 77).

32. **RETAINED: "proposed"** (p. 69): "It is also proposed that shrines or temples are focal points for corporate groups." (p. 74): "It is also proposed on the basis of obsidian dates..."

33. **RETAINED: "tentative"** for Type 5 ahu interpretation (p. 76): "the role of Type 5 ahu as secondary elite centers remains tentative."

34. **RETAINED: "Whether these groups were integrated at a higher level has not been detected archaeologically within the study area"** (p. 77). This important hedge, acknowledging the absence of evidence for supra-local integration, survives from 1984.

35. **DROPPED: Explicit rejection of unilinear evolution.** The 1984 statement (p. 50): "it is not assumed a priori that the prehistoric social system of Easter Island is headed full tilt on a course of increased social complexity, but that it may adopt alternate forms of social structure as conditions dictate" is entirely absent from 1986. This was the single most important methodological commitment distinguishing Stevenson's approach from the Sahlins-Service-Kirch framework.

36. **DROPPED: "tentative in nature and require verification through future research"** (1984, p. 20). The explicit statement that conclusions require verification is absent.

37. **DROPPED: "preliminary model which needs to be evaluated by additional research"** (1984, p. 181). The call for further verification is absent.

38. **DROPPED: "it will not be possible to draw boundaries between political units"** (1984, p. 162). The explicit acknowledgment that political boundaries cannot be drawn is absent.

39. **DROPPED: "The three models discussed above are not presented as an evolutionary trajectory"** (1984, p. 50). The anti-evolutionary framing of the three models is absent, along with the three-model framework itself.

40. **DROPPED: "The use of an arbitrary criteria for establishing political divisions... is probably misleading"** (1984, p. 162). The caution about arbitrary boundaries is absent.

41. **DROPPED: Three competing models (Types 1-3) of corporate group structure** (1984, pp. 47-50). The entire framework of alternative organizational forms, including the possibility that the system could move between types in any direction, is absent. What remains is a single narrative trajectory.

42. **Assessment of hedging changes.** Of the 11 explicit hedging/qualification statements documented in the 1984 claims file (claims 78-88), three general types of hedging are retained in 1986 ("preliminary," "hypothesized," "tentative," and the absence of higher-level integration) and six are dropped. The dropped items are not random: they are the theoretical commitments (rejection of unilinear evolution, models as non-evolutionary alternatives, boundaries as unmappable) and the calls for verification. What survives is hedging about specific interpretive details; what is lost is the methodological framework that constituted an alternative to evolutionary typology.

### C. Claims strengthened

43. **Environmental deterioration narrative strengthened.** The 1986 version adds Flenley and King 1984 radiocarbon dates (p. 77) and expands the degradation-to-warfare narrative. The 1984 dissertation mentioned environmental deterioration but did not cite palynological evidence or develop the narrative as fully. The 1986 version presents a more confident causal chain: population growth + environmental deterioration -> shortage of agricultural land -> corporate territory breakdown -> warfare and competitive strategy -> replacement of ancestral ahu by Orongo cult.

44. **Settlement trajectory presented as a single narrative arc** rather than as a test of competing models. The 1984 dissertation evaluated three alternative organizational models (Types 1-3) and assessed which the data supported at different phases. The 1986 chapter presents a linear narrative: settlement -> proliferation -> integration -> weakening. The analytical framework of alternative possibilities is replaced by a historical account.

45. **Elite village association with Type 1 ahu stated more confidently.** "It is also proposed on the basis of obsidian dates from the Akahanga elite houses (hare paenga)(fig. 8.2a), that the elite complexes were constructed at this time and coincided with the erection of Type 1 ahu along the southern coast" (p. 74). The 1984 version was more cautious about the contemporaneity.

### D. Claims dropped

46. **Three-model framework absent.** The 1984 Types 1-3 models of corporate group structure (non-corporate, lineage-specific, ranked) are entirely absent. Only the ahu architectural Types 1-5 survive. The conceptual apparatus for evaluating alternative organizational forms has been removed.

47. **"Reverted to a non-ranked corporate structure" (1984, p. 168) is reframed.** The 1984 finding that the system reverted to a non-ranked structure after ~1680 AD is replaced in 1986 by language about "weakened" corporate solidarity (p. 77). The 1984 phrasing implied organizational simplification (from ranked back to non-ranked); the 1986 phrasing implies degradation (from strong to weak). This is a subtle but significant shift: reversion suggests the system was flexible and could adopt simpler forms; weakening suggests decline from a norm.

48. **Population estimates absent.** The 1984 island-wide estimates (8,927-12,000) do not appear. The 1986 chapter reports only the population growth curve for the Akahanga area (fig. 8.3) without extrapolating to island-wide figures.

49. **"No institutionalized rank differences" absent.** The 1984 finding that "each of these groups does not exhibit internal or between group differences in ascribed social rank" (p. 180) is not stated in 1986. The closest equivalent is the statement that higher-level integration "has not been detected archaeologically" (p. 77), which is weaker.

50. **"Corporate group strength relatively weak" absent.** The 1984 characterization (p. 176) that corporate groups "exercised a significant amount of autonomy" and were "relatively weak" does not appear in the 1986 version.

51. **Post-raid evidence caveat weakened.** The 1984 statement that "Easter Island ethnographic data shall be used selectively and with caution" (p. 23) is replaced by: "Easter Island lacks a large corpus of ethnographic data and reliable early contact descriptions" (p. 69). The 1984 version acknowledged the problem and committed to careful use; the 1986 version notes the limitation but does not commit to a cautious methodology.

### E. Claims added

52. **Flenley and King 1984 deforestation evidence.** "Radiocarbon dates from cores in the crater lakes suggest that this process had begun by 990 ± 70 bp (Flenley and King 1984)" (p. 77). New empirical support for environmental deterioration, not in the 1984 dissertation. This strengthens the degradation narrative.

53. **Orongo cult as replacement for ancestral ahu.** "The regular activities and supplications conducted on behalf of the ancestors connected with the land became less important, and were superseded by the cult of Orongo, and worship of the creator god Makemake (see Metraux 1940)" (p. 77). While the 1984 dissertation mentioned the Orongo cult, the explicit replacement narrative (ancestral ahu -> Orongo) is more developed in 1986.

54. **"More competitive strategy based on the acquisition of land through warfare"** (p. 77). The warfare-competition framing is new in this explicit form. The 1984 dissertation described "conditions of increasing social instability" but did not frame the late-period transformation as a shift from cooperative corporate management to competitive warfare strategy.

## III. Political Organization Model (as stated in 1986)

55. **Five levels of segmentation retained from McCall 1979** (p. 69-70): island-wide -> two moieties (Tu'u Aro, Hotu Iti) -> eleven mata (descent groups) -> multiple lineages -> extended families -> nuclear families. Same as 1984.

56. **Mata as corporate units** (p. 70): "McCall (1979, 124-5) interprets these mata descent groups as corporate units. Each group had a territory defined by boundary markers (pipihoreko)." Same as 1984.

57. **Dual status system from Goldman 1970** (p. 70): "one status system was organized along traditional descent lines, with social position based on proximity to the founding ancestor. The second system was comprised of a set of warrior chiefs or matatoa." Same as 1984, though the "Open" classification is not named.

58. **Warrior-chief relationship hypothesized from resource competition** (p. 70): "This relationship between the hereditary chiefs and the position of warrior chief is hypothesized to have arisen out of conditions of increasing social instability which resulted from resource shortages brought on by environmental deterioration (McCall 1979, 132; see also McCoy 1976)." Same as 1984.

59. **Four politically autonomous groups** (p. 76): "the five multiple lineage centers reflect the presence of four politically autonomous groups which had developed by Phase 9, and continued unchanged until Phase 11." Same finding as 1984.

60. **No evidence for higher-level integration** (p. 77): "Whether these groups were integrated at a higher level has not been detected archaeologically within the study area." Same important negative finding as 1984.

61. **Phase 11+ transformation: corporate solidarity "weakened"** (p. 77): "It is hypothesized that the corporate solidarity of these communities had been weakened in response to a decline in the availability of productive agricultural land." Compare 1984: "reverted to a non-ranked corporate structure" (p. 168). The language shifts from structural reversion to gradual weakening.

62. **Akahanga fissioning** (pp. 76-77): "The unified multiple lineage center at Akahanga appears to have separated into two spatially discrete groups." The four groups of Phase 10 give way to five groups in Phases 11-12. This finding is in the 1984 dissertation but is more prominently narrated in 1986.

63. **Ahu fell into disuse and were replaced** (p. 76): "the coastal platform ahu fell into disuse and were replaced by newly constructed above ground interment structures." Same finding as 1984, but the 1986 version does not explicitly characterize this as evidence against ranked organization.

### Bibliography comparison: 1984 vs. 1986

65. **Citations added in 1986**: Cristino, Vargas, & Izaurieta 1981; Cristino & Vargas 1980; Michels et al. 1984; Flenley & King 1984; Stevenson 1984 (self-citation).

66. **Citations dropped from 1984**: Fortes 1953/1959, Firth 1936/1957, Kirchhoff 1955, Hayden & Cannon 1982, Fried 1957, Befu & Plotnicov 1962, Sahlins 1958, La Perouse 1799, Forster 1777, Herve 1770, Lisiansky 1814, Cordy 1981, Thompson 1891 (cited as 1889 in 1984), Englert 1948, Geiseler 1883.

67. **Pattern of citation changes.** The additions are empirical (new survey data, new dating method, new palynological evidence). The drops are theoretical (British social anthropology) and contextual (contact-era sources, Hawaiian analogues for population estimates). The net effect is a chapter that presents empirical findings without the theoretical framework that positioned them as an alternative to neoevolutionary typology.

### Post-raid source dependency

68. **Same post-raid sources used for political organization**: McCall 1979 (which synthesizes Metraux 1940, Routledge 1919), Goldman 1970. All claims about kinship structure, moiety division, mata territories, dual status system, and matatoa derive from these sources. [POST-RAID]

69. **Source limitation acknowledged but caveat weakened** (p. 69): "Easter Island lacks a large corpus of ethnographic data and reliable early contact descriptions. Information collected during the early part of this century (Routledge 1919; Metraux 1940) and more recent ethnographic work by McCall (1979) permit a partial reconstruction of the socio-political structure for the latest period of the prehistoric cultural system (post AD 1722)." Compare 1984 (p. 23): ethnographic data should be used "selectively and with caution."

70. **Polynesian ethnographic data characterized as limited** (p. 70): "much of the ethnographic data from Polynesia is limited and often anecdotal in nature, in my view hindering an examination of the relationships of interest. Therefore I have selected an ethnographic example from the African data." This passage, justifying the Ibo analogue over Polynesian comparisons, is significant: Stevenson explicitly chose not to use Polynesian models because the data were inadequate.

## V. Editorial Context

71. **Volume title frames chapter within evolutionary archaeology**: *Island Societies: Archaeological Approaches to Evolution and Transformation*. The words "evolution" and "transformation" position all chapters within a framework of developmental change, though Stevenson's own text does not use evolutionary language.

72. **Kirch as volume editor.** Kirch's *Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms* was published by the same press (Cambridge) two years earlier (1984). The volume represents Kirch's programmatic statement about applying evolutionary archaeology to Pacific island societies. A chapter in this volume is read within Kirch's chiefdom framework regardless of the contributor's own theoretical commitments.

73. **Chapter does not adopt the volume's framing.** Despite appearing in an "evolution and transformation" volume edited by the architect of the Polynesian chiefdom framework, Stevenson's chapter does not use "chiefdom," "evolution," "complexity," or any of the Sahlins-Service-Kirch vocabulary. The chapter was likely completed before or during the dissertation (1984) and submitted "In Press" (per the 1984 Vita, p. 359), meaning the text may predate the volume's editorial framing.

74. **The editorial context does the reframing work the text does not.** A reader encountering this chapter in Kirch's volume reads it as a contribution to the chiefdom-evolution literature. The four autonomous political units become evidence of "chiefdom-level" organization; the Type 1 ahu become "chiefly" centers; the elite villages become "chiefly residences." None of these readings are in Stevenson's text, but the volume's framing invites them. This is the mechanism of Step 3 of the circularity: the editorial context transforms the meaning of the data without the author needing to change a word.

## VI. Ahu Typology and Settlement Data

75. **57 platform ahu dated** (p. 69), vs. 65 ceremonial centers in 1984. The difference reflects a narrower count (platform ahu only vs. all ceremonial structures).

76. **24 above-ground burial structures** classified (p. 69). Three morphological types: semipyramidal, rectangular, amorphous.

77. **33 complete ahu analyzed in cluster analysis** (p. 74), producing five architectural types. Same analysis as 1984.

78. **Five ahu types retained from 1984** (pp. 73-74, Table 8.2):
- Type 1: Multiple lineage centers (N=6). Fullest architectural complement: fine/crude masonry, paved ramp, top knots, crafted front walls, cornices, crematorium.
- Type 2: Lineage centers (N=6). Medium platforms, crude masonry, wings, statues, no top knots.
- Type 3: Lineage centers without wings (N=8). Rectangular platforms, crude masonry.
- Type 4: Small secondary structures (N=10). Small platforms, wings, statues.
- Type 5: Secondary elite centers? (N=3). Miniature Type 1, located inland.

79. **13 one-hundred-year phases retained** (p. 74): AD 700-1864. Same as 1984.

80. **Phase 7 (1301-1400)**: First ahu at Vaihu, Ure Uranga te Mahina, Akahanga (p. 74). Same as 1984.

81. **Phase 8 (1401-1500)**: "Construction of a large number of platform ahu" (p. 74). Same as 1984.

82. **Phase 9 (1501-1600)**: Multiple lineage centers at Hanga Hahave, Hanga Poukura, Ure Uranga te Mahina, Akahanga. Elite houses (hare paenga) constructed (p. 74). Same as 1984.

83. **Phase 10 (1601-1700)**: Construction limited; Type 5 structures built inland (pp. 74-76). Same as 1984.

84. **Phases 11-12 (1701-1864)**: Platform ahu fall into disuse; semipyramidal interment structures replace them (p. 76). Same as 1984.

85. **Population growth curve retained** (fig. 8.3, p. 77): Rapid increase Phase 5-9, stable Phase 9-12. Same as 1984 (fig. 30). No island-wide extrapolation.

## VII. Key Claims for Our Argument

### 1. Does Stevenson use the chiefdom concept in 1986?

**No.** The term "chiefdom" does not appear. Service 1962 is not cited. Kirch 1984 is not cited. The analytical framework remains corporate descent groups. However, the theoretical infrastructure that made this an alternative to the chiefdom framework (Fortes, Firth, Kirchhoff, Hayden & Cannon, explicit rejection of unilinear evolution) has been stripped away. What remains is the method without the theory.

### 2. What changed between 1984 and 1986?

The changes fall into four categories:

**(a) Theoretical infrastructure removed.** All British social anthropology and corporate group theory citations dropped. The three-model framework (alternative organizational forms, non-evolutionary) dropped. The explicit rejection of unilinear evolution dropped.

**(b) Key qualifications dropped.** "Tentative in nature and require verification," "preliminary model which needs to be evaluated," boundaries cannot be drawn, models are not an evolutionary trajectory.

**(c) Reversion reframed as weakening.** "Reverted to non-ranked corporate structure" becomes "corporate solidarity weakened." Structural simplification becomes decline.

**(d) Environmental narrative strengthened.** New palynological evidence, more developed degradation-warfare-cult replacement narrative.

### 3. What stayed the same?

**(a) Core data unchanged.** Same study area, same ahu typology, same settlement phases, same population growth curve.

**(b) "Chiefdom" still absent.** No terminological adoption of the chiefdom framework.

**(c) Four autonomous groups, no higher integration.** The basic political organization finding is unchanged.

**(d) Ibo analogue retained.** Takes 2.5 of 9 pages.

**(e) Some hedging retained.** "Preliminary," "hypothesized," "tentative," and the important "has not been detected archaeologically."

### 4. What does this mean for the chiefdom attribution?

The 1984-to-1986 transition is more subtle than a simple adoption of the chiefdom label. Instead, it represents:

1. **Decontextualization**: The findings are separated from the theoretical tradition that positioned them as an alternative to the chiefdom framework.
2. **Recontextualization**: The same findings are placed in a volume whose editorial framing positions them within the chiefdom-evolution literature.
3. **Selective attrition**: The strongest anti-chiefdom commitments (rejection of unilinear evolution, reversion to non-ranked structure, models as non-evolutionary alternatives) are dropped while the data and some hedging survive.

This pattern, rather than explicit adoption, is how interpretive drift typically operates. The data do not change; the intellectual context does. And subsequent citations to "Stevenson 1986 in Kirch (ed.)" read the findings as chiefdom evidence because the volume frames them that way, even though Stevenson's own text does not.

## VIII. Honest Complications

86. **Space constraints explain some omissions.** A 377-page dissertation compressed to 9 pages necessarily loses material. The absence of the three-model framework, the theoretical citations, and some hedging statements may reflect editorial constraints rather than deliberate reframing. It is not possible to distinguish space-driven omission from intentional softening.

87. **The chapter may have been written before the dissertation was finalized.** The 1984 Vita lists the chapter as "In Press" in Kirch's forthcoming volume. If the chapter was drafted early in the dissertation process, some of the theoretical elaboration and qualification may not yet have been developed. However, this would mean the dissertation's stronger commitments represent the author's more considered position, making the published chapter a less mature version.

88. **Goldman engagement is present but reduced.** The dual status system is discussed (p. 70), but the "Open" classification is not named and the implications for the chiefdom question are not drawn. This may be a space issue or may reflect the volume's framing: naming Goldman's "Open" classification would be inconvenient in a volume organized around chiefdom evolution.

89. **The Ibo analogue remains a strong commitment.** Devoting 2.5 of 9 pages to an African ethnographic case study, while explicitly criticizing the adequacy of Polynesian ethnographic data (p. 70), represents a deliberate methodological choice. Stevenson is not using Polynesian chiefdom models; he is using an African corporate group model. This is retained from 1984 and represents genuine intellectual independence from the Kirch framework, even within Kirch's own volume.

90. **The "has not been detected archaeologically" hedge is significant.** The statement that higher-level integration has not been detected (p. 77) is a direct counter to the chiefdom interpretation's assumption of supra-local political authority. This hedge survives in the published chapter and constitutes evidence that Stevenson did not consider the chiefdom model demonstrated.
