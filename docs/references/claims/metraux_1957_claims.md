# Claims Extraction: Métraux 1957 *Easter Island: A Stone-Age Civilization of the Pacific*

## Source Information

- **Author**: Alfred Métraux (translated by Michael Bullock)
- **Title**: *Easter Island: A Stone-Age Civilization of the Pacific*
- **Publication**: André Deutsch, London, 1957
- **Fieldwork**: Based on 1934 Franco-Belgian expedition (same as 1940 monograph)
- **Source type**: Popular synthesis of the 1940 monograph for a general readership
- **Post-raid status**: [POST-RAID] Same data as 1940 monograph, reframed for popular audience
- **Relationship to other Métraux publications**: Written 17 years after the monograph. Same underlying data but with clearer, more accessible formulations. Represents Métraux's mature, settled views.
- **OCR note**: Text extracted via tesseract OCR at 200 DPI; occasional OCR artifacts but generally high quality

## A. Social Hierarchy and Political Authority (Ch. V "A Cannibal Society")

### Claim A1: Rigorous social stratification described
**Page**: p. 87
**Quote**: "A century ago, however, this society was as rigorously stratified as that of New Zealand and the Society Islands. At the top of the ladder was the ariki-maw, or great chief, whom we have referred to as the king of the island. Below him, it is said, came the priests (ivi-atua) and the ariki-paka or nobles."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's popular framing of Roussel's class system.
**Relevance**: The qualifier "it is said" is significant: Métraux attributes the hierarchical description to informant reports, not to his own observation or conclusion.

### Claim A2: King's essence was supernatural, not political
**Page**: p. 89
**Quote**: "The essence of royalty is best expressed in the association with the plants and turtles, that the king was a sacred being whose magic power influenced nature and assured his people a supply of food... the king made fertile the vegetable and animal kingdom... outcome of the king's mystic force, his mana, which had been transmitted to him by his divine ancestors."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's interpretive synthesis.
**Relevance**: Even in a popular account where simplification might flatten nuance, Métraux defines the "essence of royalty" as supernatural food-increase magic, not governance.

### Claim A3: Political authority "not clear" (KEY PASSAGE)
**Page**: p. 93
**Quote**: "The political authority of the ariki-mau is not clear. On this point we are reduced to conjecture. Although he was the civil leader of the Miru, he was not regarded by the other tribes as more than an extremely sacred personage. The reverence in which he was held did not spare him the fate of a captive when the Miru were defeated by their rivals."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's assessment.
**Relevance**: More direct than the 1940 formulation. Métraux explicitly states political authority is "not clear" and that the ariki-mau was viewed by other tribes as "an extremely sacred personage," not as a political superior. The capture incident is repeated.

### Claim A4: Political power in the hands of warriors
**Page**: p. 93
**Quote**: "Political power seems to have fallen into the hands of the mata-to'a, who formed an aristocracy of happy warriors. Royalty would doubtless have disappeared if the king had not been invested with the mana necessary for the continuance of natural processes and the prosperity of the island."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's interpretive summary.
**Relevance**: The clearest statement in any Métraux publication that political power belonged to the matatoa, not the ariki-mau. The king survived as an institution only because of his supernatural functions, not political ones.

### Claim A5: Matatoa stronger than warriors elsewhere in Polynesia
**Pages**: pp. 96-97
**Quote**: "The matato'a or 'warrior' is a familiar figure in historical legends. The word 'warrior' only partially expresses the role of these personages, for the matato'a was above all a warrior who inspired terror, the leader of a band. Like the South American caudillo, he was both a civil and military leader."
**Quote**: "The political position of the matato'a on Easter Island was much stronger than that of the warriors in the rest of Polynesia, where they were subordinate to the ariki. Once again, Mangaia affords a parallel; for there, just as on Easter Island, power might be in the hands of the warrior who had won a decisive victory."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's comparative analysis.
**Relevance**: NEW emphasis. The 1957 book makes a stronger comparative claim than the monograph: Easter Island's warriors had more political power than Polynesian warriors elsewhere because they were not subordinate to the ariki. The caudillo analogy (personal power through force, not institutional authority) is new and vivid.

## B. Warfare and Cannibalism (Ch. V)

### Claim B1: Warfare pattern
**Pages**: pp. 100-102
**Summary**: Warfare involved: mutual provocations and insults, stone-throwing as opening phase, javelin volleys, close combat until one side fled, pursuit and killing/capture of the vanquished, entry into enemy territory to burn huts and destroy crops, capture of women and children. Prisoners could be tortured.
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition, synthesized by Métraux for popular audience. [POST-RAID]

### Claim B2: Cannibalism not purely ritual
**Pages**: pp. 102-103
**Quote**: "The Easter Islanders' cannibalism was not exclusively a religious rite or the expression of an urge for revenge: it was also induced by a simple liking for human flesh that could impel a man to kill for no other reason than his desire for fresh meat. Women and children were the principal victims of these inveterate cannibals."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]

## C. The Great Statues (Ch. IX)

### Claim C1: Statues not idols; no evidence of worship
**Pages**: pp. 158-159
**Quote**: "No outward sign of any religious observances was noted by other voyagers... Modern natives, questioned as to the purpose of the statues, said that they were erected merely to decorate the mausoleums."
**Quote**: "None of these names are those of a Polynesian god, and many are descriptive terms—such as 'Twisted Neck', 'Tattooed One', 'Stinker', etc."
**Evidential type**: (a) Contact-era observations; (b) informant testimony; (c) Métraux's analysis.
**Relevance**: Confirms the 1940 monograph's conclusion. The statue names are prosaic descriptions, not deity names.

### Claim C2: Statues represented ancestors/chiefs, became noa without worship
**Pages**: pp. 159-160
**Quote**: "On the Marquesas Islands... the statues dominating the terraces of the sanctuaries represented famous chiefs or priests whose spirits had entered the ranks of the tribe's tutelary deities. It must have been the same on our island."
**Quote**: "The sanctity of the statues was, therefore, temporary and depended on rites that caused the advent of the deities. In the ordinary way, the image was simply an ornament, a lifeless block of stone. When, for lack of worshippers, the gods and ancestors were deprived of sacrifices, the images that were no longer visited finally lost all religious significance and ceased to be the object of any cult. They became noa—profane."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's inference from Marquesan parallel.
**Relevance**: NEW emphasis. Statues were only sacred during active ritual; otherwise "simply an ornament, a lifeless block of stone." This undercuts any argument that monumental statues necessarily indicate a religious system requiring centralized priestly or chiefly control.

### Claim C3: Lineage heads organized transport through feast sponsorship
**Pages**: pp. 164-165
**Quote**: "We must imagine the joyous tumult of the festivals when thinking of this effort. The heads of lineages who wished to raise a new bust on their ahu undoubtedly organized feasts that they prepared long beforehand... The guests harnessed themselves to the statue and strained their muscles to the sound of cries and chants recited by the priests to facilitate their task. A half-serious, half-humorous rivalry turned the undertaking into an immense pleasure party."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's reconstruction.
**Relevance**: CRITICAL for the monument-production argument. Métraux envisions statue transport as organized by "heads of lineages" (not a paramount chief), motivated by feast sponsorship and competitive display, executed by "guests" in a festive context. This is a collective action/competitive signaling model, not a chiefly-command model.

### Claim C4: Chiefs mobilized lineage resources
**Pages**: pp. 159-160
**Quote**: "Everything we know about the social organization of Easter Island suggests that the above system existed there in an almost identical shape. The chiefs had, therefore, the means of mobilizing the members of their groups and assigning to them the tasks which they considered useful."
**Quote**: "The Easter Islanders' spirit of enterprise therefore turned towards the only domain in which it was possible for them to expend their energy: the cult of the gods and the ancestors. The chiefs, who had vast resources at their disposal, employed them in maintaining and paying groups of craftsmen who carved statues to ornament the mausoleums belonging to the various lineages."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's reconstruction, drawing on Sahlins-era comparative Polynesian models.
**Relevance**: This is the passage closest to a chiefly-command model in all of Métraux's writings. However, the "chiefs" here are plural (lineage heads, not a paramount), the "resources" are deployed for lineage-level monuments ("mausoleums belonging to the various lineages"), and the mobilization operates through feast sponsorship (C3), not coercive authority. The passage describes competitive monument-building among autonomous lineages, not centralized state production.

## D. Religion and Bird Cult (Ch. VII)

### Claim D1: Makemake as supreme deity
**Pages**: pp. 127-128
**Quote**: "Makemake was probably the most important god in the Easter Island religion. He was the only one of whom the missionaries heard when they enquired about the pagan deities. He was the supreme atua and the Creator of the Universe."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]

### Claim D2: Religious knowledge lost with priests in 1862
**Page**: p. 128
**Quote**: "The Makemake cult has, however, left few traces in the island's traditions. Only the feast of the bird-man, which was held in his honour, can still be reconstructed in its broad outline. The disappearance of a large proportion of the priesthood during the slave-raid of 1862 would explain this sharp break in religious tradition and forgetfulness of the ancient cults."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's methodological assessment.
**Relevance**: Explicitly acknowledges that the slave raids destroyed the knowledge base needed to reconstruct pre-contact religious authority.

## E. The Demographic Catastrophe (Ch. III "The Tragic Story")

### Claim E1: 1862 as decisive break
**Page**: p. 47
**Quote**: "The year 1862 was decisive in the history of Easter Island. It saw the end of its civilization, most aspects of which have become for us, since the middle of the nineteenth century, as vague and far-off as though we were separated from them by the mists of time."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's framing.
**Relevance**: Métraux's popular formulation is even more emphatic than the monograph about the completeness of the cultural break.

### Claim E2: Social and religious system destroyed
**Page**: p. 48
**Quote**: "The social order had been undermined, the fields were left without owners, and people fought for possession of them. Then there was famine. The population fell to around six hundred. The majority of members of the priestly class disappeared, taking with them the secrets of the past. The following year, when the first missionaries settled on the island, they found a culture in its death throes: the religious and social system had been destroyed and a leaden apathy weighed down the survivors from these disasters."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's narrative synthesis.
**Relevance**: "The religious and social system had been destroyed" is Métraux's own assessment that post-raid sources cannot describe a functioning pre-contact system.

## F. Convergences and Divergences with the 1940 Monograph

### What's UNIQUE to the 1957 book:

1. **"The political authority of the ariki-mau is not clear"**: More explicit statement of uncertainty than the 1940 monograph.
2. **Caudillo analogy for matatoa**: New and vivid comparison to South American strongmen.
3. **Matatoa "much stronger" than Polynesian warriors elsewhere**: Explicit comparative claim about Easter Island's distinctive warrior power.
4. **Feast-sponsorship model of statue transport**: "Heads of lineages organized feasts... The guests harnessed themselves to the statue... A half-serious, half-humorous rivalry turned the undertaking into an immense pleasure party."
5. **Statues as "noa" (profane)**: Without active ritual, statues were "simply an ornament, a lifeless block of stone."
6. **"Vast resources" passage**: The closest Métraux comes to a chiefly-command model, but attributed to plural lineage chiefs competing, not a paramount.
7. **Explicit statement that priestly class "disappeared" in 1862**: Clearer than the monograph about the implications for religious knowledge.

### What's CONFIRMED from 1940:

1. Ariki-mau as sacred figure without clear political authority.
2. Warrior dominance over the ariki institution.
3. Capture of king by rival clan.
4. No evidence of statue worship.
5. Social/religious system destroyed by 1862 raids.

### Evolution in Métraux's thinking:

The 1957 book is, if anything, **more skeptical** about chiefly political authority than the 1940 monograph. The 1940 text states the ariki-mau was "certainly not a ruler or civilian chief"; the 1957 text says his political authority is "not clear" and that he was viewed by other tribes as "an extremely sacred personage," not a political superior. The warrior comparison with other Polynesian societies is stronger. The statue-transport model is explicitly cast as competitive feast sponsorship rather than centralized command. No evolution toward a chiefdom model is visible; if anything, the trend is toward greater emphasis on decentralization.
