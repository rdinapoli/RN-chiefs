### What is NOT cited

22. **Service 1962 NOT cited.** The band/tribe/chiefdom/state typology is absent as explicit framework. Van Tilburg works within the chiefdom model implicitly through Kirch 1984, not through Service directly.

23. **Sahlins 1954/1958 NOT cited** for Polynesian stratification gradient or redistribution claims. Sahlins's Easter Island analysis is absent.

24. **Sahlins 1963 NOT cited.** Big-man/chief distinction absent.

25. **Sahlins & Service 1960 NOT cited.** General/specific evolution framework absent.

26. **Feinman & Neitzel 1984 NOT cited.** Contemporary chiefdom critique absent.

27. **McCoy 1976's anti-chiefdom conclusion NOT cited.** McCoy's finding that chiefdom was "retarded" by warrior class (p. 145) does not appear despite McCoy being cited for other purposes.

28. **Stevenson 1984's anti-chiefdom commitments NOT cited.** Stevenson's explicit rejection of unilinear evolution and Goldman "Open" engagement are absent despite Stevenson being cited for settlement data.

### Assessment: what the citation pattern means

29. **Van Tilburg accepts Kirch 1984 as given scaffolding, not as a hypothesis to be tested.** The Polynesian cultural matrix is "assumed" (p. 299), not demonstrated. Kirch's chiefdom framework is the interpretive lens through which moai iconography is read. The alternative frameworks available in 1986 (Goldman's "Open" classification, Stevenson's corporate descent group model, McCoy's anti-chiefdom finding) are either not cited or cited only for non-chiefdom-relevant data.

30. **Post-slave-raid sources used uncritically.** Métraux 1940 and Englert 1948 are treated as reliable descriptions of pre-contact social organization. No acknowledgment of the 1862-1863 slave raids, the demographic collapse from ~3,000 to fewer than 200, or the consequent unreliability of these sources for pre-contact political organization. Van Tilburg does note that post-raid ethnographic data "must...be approached with extreme caution as it is projected backwards in time, or as it purports to represent pre-contact culture" (p. 39), but this caution is not applied in the interpretive chapters.

## II. The Four-Stage Power Shift Model (pp. 338-355)

### The model

31. **"The archaeological evidence on Easter Island suggests a probable power shift over time. The power shift was gradual and manifested itself in four stages of activity"** (p. 338). The model is presented as arising from archaeological evidence, but the interpretive framework is derived from ethnographic and analogical sources.

32. **Stage 1: Priest/chief differentiation through material distinction.** "The first stage of the power shift is objectified by the functional distinction between statues of red scoria not known to have been placed upon ahu and statues of Rano Raraku tuff erected upon ahu. I suggest that use of the two materials...may represent the differentiation of the priestly class from that of the chiefly class" (pp. 338-339). This interpretation rests on the assumption that material difference maps to social role difference, derived by Polynesian analogy.

33. **Stage 2: Shift from inherent to acquired mana.** "The second stage of the power shift is represented by the superimposition of red scoria pukao upon some of the statues of the largest lineage sites...It is suggested that the use of red stone in these ways represents a shift in emphasis from chiefly mana which is inherent in ancestral proximity to one of chiefly mana which is acquired and demonstrated through prowess" (pp. 339-340). "Prowess at this stage was demonstrated in the acquisition of secret, esoteric knowledge and initiation into the highest levels of cult activity" (p. 339).

34. **Stage 2 implies priestly hierarchy dominated by Miru.** "The highest ranked priests were part of the highest ranking lineage (i.e. the ariki paka of the Miru ramage) and...lesser priests officiated at lesser ceremonies at smaller sites. Thus we see that the priestly hierarchy may have been dominated by the Miru lineage just as the chiefly hierarchy seems to have been dominated by the Miru through the ariki mau" (p. 340). This is inferred from Polynesian parallels, not from archaeological evidence.

35. **Stage 3: Birdman replaces statue cult.** "The third stage of the power shift is the emergence of the birdman cult from the position of subordinate ideology to that of dominant ideology, replacing the statue cult as the pan-island religion. This implies a power struggle of a sort between priests and chiefs" (p. 341).

36. **Goldman's "Open" classification treated as Stage 3 endpoint only.** "The status-reversal rites of fertility and 'first fruits' which form the core of birdman cult power legitimation are carried to the ultimate, egalitarian evolutionary stage documented to have been operative in the early 1800's and which define the 'open' society suggested by Goldman (1970)" (pp. 342-343). Goldman's "Open" classification, which Goldman applied to Easter Island as a whole ("had never evolved a central authority," p. 94), is here reinterpreted as describing only the late/post-contact period, not the earlier system. This is a significant reframing.

37. **Stage 4: Proto-historic/historic re-use.** "This fourth and final stage of ritual activity is postulated to extend well into the late post-contact or proto-historic period, with remnants of it still visible into the early historic" (p. 342).

### Key interpretive claims within the model

38. **Monumentality as denial of time.** "The monolithic sculpture of Easter Island which stood upon ahu are thus seen to deny time, assert permanence, project continuity and stability" (p. 322, citing Tilley 1984). "The denying of their monumentality which was achieved by toppling them from their commanding positions on the platforms therefore asserted time, denied permanence and stability" (p. 322).

39. **Ideology as ruling-class misrepresentation.** Miller and Tilley's framework adopted: "the origin, purpose and function of ideology [is] the generation of ideas which misrepresent reality, presenting inherent social contradictions in the best interests of the ruling class" (p. 344). Applied to Easter Island: "Ideology legitimates power and symbols make ideology concrete" (p. 345).

40. **Miru control postulated as comprehensive.** "The non-localized nature of the Miru power base represented in its apparent dominance over or control of three key social roles" (p. 347): (1) hereditary mana through ariki mau, (2) control of significant food and resources, (3) control of esoteric knowledge through priestly class, plus (4) representation in the maori class of carvers (pp. 346-347). This synthesis draws from Métraux 1940 and Goldman 1970, both post-slave-raid. [POST-RAID]

41. **Sacredness of ariki mau cited as evidence of high stratification.** Goldman (1970:112-113) is cited: the sacredness of the ariki mau was "the most widespread generalized personal sanctity in all Polynesia." Van Tilburg then links this to Kirch (1984:38): "elaboration of chiefly sanctity parallels that of social stratification," inferring "a correlation of social stratification and chiefly sanctity on Easter Island which would be...on a very high level, indeed" (p. 347). This is a logical chain: Goldman's observation about sanctity -> Kirch's correlation between sanctity and stratification -> inference of high stratification on Easter Island. But Goldman's own conclusion was that Easter Island "had never evolved a central authority."

### The moai-as-paramount-chief hypothesis (pp. 348-349)

42. **Two hypotheses for who statues represent.** "There are, in my opinion, two possible suggestions. The first is the conventional one, i.e. that the ahu were conceived, constructed and utilized by individual lineages, and that the statues represent the individual heads of those lineages following their transmutation into ancestral gods (cf. Ayres 1973; Kirch 1984). The second accepts the ahu as described above, but holds that the statues represent instead the paramount chief, the ariki mau, and are erected at his behest" (p. 348).

43. **Moai-as-paramount-chief supported by ethnographic data.** "The early explorer's, for example, were told that the statues represented ariki (see Chapter II) and the ethnography shows that the title ariki was reserved only for the Miru (Metraux 1940:128)" (p. 348). "If the erection of statues upon lineage ahu was a kind of complex genealogical count and objectification of the Miru relationship with the gods which insured fertility and productivity of the land for the island as a whole, then the toppling of those statues was a direct challenge or affront to and rejection of the ariki mau" (p. 349). [POST-RAID for Métraux claim]

44. **Tikopia sacred canoe model applied.** Firth's Tikopia: "sacred canoes are 'possessed by the chiefs, their ritual elders, and other heads of the most important kinship groups'...The ownership of these canoes is complex. 'Theoretically they are the property of the chief of the clan, despite the fact that the immediate possessor in each case has ordered the building of the craft, paid the craftsmen for it, shelters it in his own canoe shed, has full rights of use over it, and is fully responsible for its upkeep and repair'" (p. 353). Van Tilburg then: "While the statues are, therefore, the property of those who commission and construct them, they are also the property of the paramount chief" (pp. 353-354).

45. **Paramount chief as "principle link."** "The paramount chief is the principle link with the ancestors and gods, and his 'overlordship and control are not only justified but natural, since it is he who is responsible for the people's welfare' (ibid:56). If the statues are an objectification of that link through the paramount chief and the Miru lineage, then the toppling of the statues is seen not simply as the result of warring chiefdoms, but also as an indirect challenge to the position of the Miru within the hierarchy" (pp. 353-354). This passage uses "warring chiefdoms" as if established.

### Acknowledgments that complicate the model

46. **Kirch 1984:270 quoted on descent group structures.** "Kirch (1984:270) says that 'virtually all ethnographic and archaeological authorities agree that the ahu were conceived, constructed, and used by local descent groups, and do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority'" (p. 349). Van Tilburg acknowledges this but then argues for "district or region-wide integration" beyond "simply that of individual, competing lineages" (p. 350).

47. **Mulloy & Figueroa on independent quarry work acknowledged.** "Such a hypothesis, while somewhat of a departure from the expected East Polynesian model, still allows for individual, lineage or kin groups of various sorts to work independently in, for example, Rano Raraku quarry as has been suggested (Mulloy and Figueroa 1978)" (p. 349).

48. **Not all lineages achieved pukao status.** "It is clear from the archaeological evidence that not all lineages which produced and erected statues upon ahu achieved the status exemplified by the red scoria pukao and facia. We thus see a clear division in the road to chiefly status developing between those who held their position solely due to heredity...and those who probably owed their status to demonstrated ability" (p. 341). This admission of differential lineage status is consistent with both chiefdom and collective action frameworks.

49. **"Sudden abandonment" thesis rejected.** "The notion of 'sudden abandonment' of the quarry and all activity associated with the statue cult (first suggested by Thomson 1891) should forever, itself, be abandoned" (p. 312). Van Tilburg argues for processual continuity and temporal overlap between statue and birdman cults.

## III. Cultural Correlates / Processual Development (pp. 299-312)

### Settlement and Development Phase (AD 400-1000)

50. **Polynesian cultural matrix assumed.** "The Polynesia cultural matrix into which the archaeological evidence firmly anchors Easter Island pre-contact culture is assumed to underlie and illuminate what is outlined by the typological and chronological evidence of the statuary" (p. 299). The word "assumed" is explicit.

51. **Ariki mau described from Métraux.** "Metraux (1940:136) tells us that the king was the man 'with the most aristocratic pedigree and the most exalted social position on the island. His person was overflowing with mana and his sacredness caused him to be feared and respected'" (p. 300). [POST-RAID]

52. **Settlement hierarchy from Kirch.** "Choice locations near the sea or special resources (water, for example or deep sea fishing grounds) were reserved for higher ranking households, while 'commoners' were situated inland" (p. 300, citing Ayres 1984 and Kirch 1984:273). [CITE-CHECK: Ayres 1984 vs. Ayres 1973?]

53. **Priest/chief combined in early period.** "The roles of chief and priest were largely filled, therefore, by one individual, the lineage head" (p. 301, citing Ayres 1973:335). Van Tilburg postulates increasing differentiation over time.

### Ahu Moai Phase (AD 1000-1500)

54. **Ahu as descent group products, per East Polynesian model.** "The East Polynesian marae model encourages the interpretation of Easter Island ahu as the product of discrete descent groups. This means that we would expect the ahu to be constructed and used by these individual groups, and would not conceptualize them as being produced by a single, island-wide political/ideological authority" (p. 304). This statement contradicts the moai-as-paramount-chief hypothesis advanced later (pp. 348-349).

55. **Deification of lineage heads.** "One would expect that the monolithic statues were carved, transported and erected on lineage ahu as part of the deification process whereby deceased chiefs (lineage heads) were transmuted into gods" (p. 305). This is the "conventional" interpretation Van Tilburg later offers as one of two options.

56. **Statues as requirement of "the power structure."** "The statue as symbol assumed the proportions it did (literally and figuratively) because it was required by the power structure, and the power structure was dominated by the Miru" (p. 305). No archaeological evidence is cited for Miru dominance of the power structure; the claim derives from ethnographic sources. [POST-RAID]

57. **Expert carvers "tightly controlled" by ritual.** "Master carvers or maori (tufunga) would have been tightly controlled by secret ritual custom" (p. 306). Inferred from Polynesian parallels, not from Easter Island archaeological evidence.

58. **Rongo rongo as "public display of the control of ritual knowledge."** "The chants and the objectification of the chants were a public display of the control of ritual knowledge, an affirmation of power and status" (p. 307). Draws on Kirch 1984:273 for dating.

59. **Ritual failure explains abandoned statues.** "Failure of ritual formulae would account for the abandonment of works in progress. Statues found 'in transport' or incomplete in the quarries may have thus been abandoned due not only to technical but possibly also to ritual difficulties" (p. 307). Alternative explanation to the conventional engineering-failure model; consistent with the Polynesian analogy but untestable.

### Decadent/Restructure Phase (AD 1500-1722)

60. **Population estimate of 9,659 by AD 1800.** "The population of Easter Island may have reached 9,659 by A.D. 1800, suggesting that overpopulation was a feature of the Decadent/Restructure Phase (Stevenson 1984:172)" (p. 309). This estimate is contradicted by Davis et al. 2024 (max ~3,900) and Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 (steady growth to ~3,000).

61. **Ecocide narrative adopted.** "Long-term stability of the socio-political system evidenced from settlement seems now to have been faced with severe ecological stress, including deforestation, soil depletion, and possibly periodic drought" (p. 309).

62. **Ariki mau decline narrative from Kirch.** Van Tilburg quotes Kirch (1984:277): "The former secular power of the ariki mau declined to the point where he became solely a religious figure, a necessary link between the gods and the declining fertility of the land" (p. 310). This is the ecocide-driven chiefdom-collapse narrative.

63. **Statue toppling as symbolic challenge.** "The power of the ariki-mau and of both the Miru ramage and the dominant Honga lineage was symbolically challenged in the toppling of the statues, just as the power of individual lineages was, in the same way, directly challenged" (p. 310).

## IV. Statue Iconography as Chiefdom Evidence (pp. 313-337)

64. **Dorsal design as "symbolic of sovereignty."** "We therefore see that the iconography of the dorsal design is interpretable as symbolic of sovereignty invested in the male heads of lineages, and that aspects of both human and sacred bird imagery are present" (p. 318).

65. **Hands as chief's tapu power.** "The life-giving or providing powers of the tapu hands of the chief are suggested by the manner in which this feature is placed and carved, and by the notion of rimaetua as the Supernatural" (p. 320). Derived from linguistic analysis (Churchill 1912) and Polynesian parallels.

66. **Monumentality explained by territorial control.** "Attempts to explain monumentality as a quality in many different types of structures in different areas of the world have centered upon 'the need to control resources such as land in the face of population pressure' (Tilley 1984:121 citing Renfrew and Chapman)" (p. 321). Applied to Easter Island moai.

67. **Small platforms attributed to "craft groups or guilds."** "Such structures...might have been platforms constructed and used by discrete aspects of the lineage community (craft groups or 'guilds', for instance)" (pp. 337-338). Tentatively: "I suggest that a tentative model of ahu use similar to that found in Tahiti is appropriate to Easter" (p. 338).

## V. Polynesian Analogy Claims (Chapter VI selections, pp. 255-267)

68. **Priest/chief differentiation linked to monumental architecture.** "The distinction between priest and chief as discrete social roles is best seen in those islands and island groups which constructed monumental architecture" (p. 255). This implicitly positions Easter Island (with its monuments) as having differentiated priests and chiefs.

69. **Religious structures "belonged to the chief, were built at his behest."** "It seems that the religious structures belonged to the chief, were built at his behest and were upon land of his lineage. The priest, on the other hand, officiated at ceremonies" (p. 255, citing Williamson 1924:420). Applied to Easter Island by analogy.

70. **Expert tufunga model from Tikopia.** "In Tikopia a tufunga marama was an acknowledged turmeric-making expert with recognized high status" (p. 258). Firth's Tikopia model of expert/chief cooperation is applied to Easter Island statue production: voluntary working parties under tufunga direction, supervised by paramount chief (pp. 258-259).

71. **Carvers as "privileged class" from Polynesian parallels.** "Carvers and stonemasons in Polynesia 'belong to the nobility and in general occupy a privileged position'" (p. 259, citing Buehler et al. 1968:97). "Image makers were a favored class of specialists. Their profession was circumscribed by secrecy" (p. 263).

72. **Stylistic conformity enforced by ritual sanctions.** "If errors creep into the carving, or if there are willful deviations from the ancient tradition, there is the risk that some dire harm will befall the basic needs of man" (p. 264, citing Trowell and Nevermann 1968:206). Applied to explain moai standardization.

## VI. Contact-Era and Ethnographic Source Use (Chapter II, pp. 22-50)

73. **Caution stated but not applied.** "The data must, however, be approached with extreme caution as it is projected backwards in time, or as it purports to represent pre-contact culture" (p. 39). This statement acknowledges the projection problem but Van Tilburg's interpretive chapters (VII) project Métraux 1940 and Englert 1948 backwards without applying this caution.

74. **Roggeveen account used for standing statues and active cult.** Description of statues on north coast, "objects of reverence," fire associated with statues, "organized priesthood" (p. 29). Van Tilburg does not note that Roggeveen's observations were made from aboard ship and during a single day ashore punctuated by violence.

75. **Spanish account: ministers as stonecarvers.** "The Spaniards here seem to be saying that 'ministers' were also stonecarvers. These stonecarvers thus produced figures on stone and the same tools and methods were used to carve statues" (p. 33). Van Tilburg infers a priest-carver connection from this, supporting the priest/expert differentiation model.

76. **Cook account: statues represent "areekees, or kings."** "It is also Cook's expedition which secured the information that the statues were 'monuments erected to the memory of some of their areekees, or kings' and that the ahu were primarily burying places" (p. 36). This contact-era claim is central to the moai-as-chief hypothesis.

77. **Stone carver guild from Métraux.** "Metraux (1940:137) says that 'the stone carvers were organized into a sort of guild. They were a privileged class, highly esteemed, and their profession was transmitted from father to son'" (p. 40). [POST-RAID]

78. **Expert carvers commissioned by "people who wanted a monument."** "Tepano told him 'that expert stone carvers (tangata maori anga moai maea) received orders from people who wanted a monument for their ahu. They worked under the leadership of a master (tangata honui maori) and were paid in fish, lobsters and eels'" (p. 40, citing Métraux 1940:137). This is the same passage Sahlins 1955 cited; it describes lineage-level commissioning, not centralized command. [POST-RAID]

79. **Tuu-ko-ihu as "king" and "expert."** "One 'Tuukoihu' is considered to be both 'king' and 'expert' in that his name is found on both Metraux's and Thomson's lists of kings and a legend credits him with carving the first statue" (p. 42, citing Métraux 1940:91). Used to support the chief-carver connection. [POST-RAID]

## VII. Claims Relevant to the Chiefdom Question

### Claims supporting the chiefdom interpretation

80. **Moai explicitly framed as evidence of chiefdom-level power.** The entire dissertation interprets moai production, iconography, and distribution as expressions of chiefly power operating within a chiefdom framework inherited from Kirch 1984. This is the earliest scholarly work to make this connection explicitly.

81. **Four-stage model assumes evolutionary trajectory.** The power shift model assumes development from combined chief-priest roles through differentiation to legitimation crisis and egalitarian outcome. This is an evolutionary trajectory applied to Easter Island political organization.

82. **Polynesian analogy treated as demonstrative, not hypothetical.** The Polynesian cultural matrix is "assumed" (p. 299) and used to "illuminate" the archaeological evidence. The analogies from Tikopia, Tahiti, Marquesas, Tuamotus, New Zealand are not tested against Easter Island evidence; they are applied to interpret it.

83. **"District or region-wide integration" beyond lineage level postulated.** "The extent of political integration on Easter Island was a great deal more complex than simply that of individual, competing lineages, and the Tahitian model, for example, is indicated" (p. 350). This claim goes beyond Kirch 1984, who maintained ahu as descent-group structures.

### Claims that complicate or undermine the chiefdom interpretation

84. **Ahu as descent-group structures acknowledged.** The "conventional" interpretation (Ayres 1973, Kirch 1984) of ahu as lineage-specific structures is quoted and not refuted (pp. 304, 349).

85. **Independent quarry work acknowledged.** Mulloy & Figueroa 1978 on independent groups at Rano Raraku is accepted as compatible with the model (p. 349).

86. **Goldman's "Open" classification acknowledged.** Goldman's finding that Easter Island reached "the ultimate, egalitarian evolutionary stage" is cited (p. 343), but reframed as describing only the late period.

87. **Ethnographic caution acknowledged.** Post-raid ethnographic data must be "approached with extreme caution" (p. 39).

88. **Not all lineages achieved pukao status.** Archaeological evidence shows differential lineage status (p. 341), compatible with collective action rather than requiring centralized chiefly command.

89. **"Sudden abandonment" rejected.** Processual continuity is emphasized (p. 312), consistent with DiNapoli et al. 2020 finding of steady ahu construction tempo.
