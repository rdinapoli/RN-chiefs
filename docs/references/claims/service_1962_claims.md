## Overview

This is the book that formalizes band/tribe/chiefdom/state as universal evolutionary stages in cultural evolution. The chiefdom is defined as a redistributional society with a permanent central agency of coordination (p. 144), intermediate between tribal society and the state. Service draws on Sahlins's Polynesian work as a primary illustration of chiefdom society, creating the feedback loop: Sahlins uses Polynesia to characterize stratified kin societies, Service formalizes these into the chiefdom stage, Kirch then applies the formalized stage back to Polynesia archaeologically.

**Easter Island is never mentioned.** Not once. The term "Easter" does not appear in the text. Polynesia is referenced generically (~20 times) as the exemplar region for chiefdoms, with Hawaii, Tahiti, Tonga, Fiji, Tikopia, Maori, and Samoa cited specifically. The chiefdom concept is constructed without any consideration of whether it fits all Polynesian societies equally.

## I. The Chiefdom as Evolutionary Stage

### Definition

1. **KEY definition** (p. 144): "A chiefdom occupies a level of social integration which transcends tribal society in two important respects. First, a chiefdom is usually a denser society than is a tribe, a gain made possible by greater productivity. But second, and more indicative of the evolutionary stage, the society is also more complex and more organized, being particularly distinguished from tribes by the presence of centers which coordinate economic, social, and religious activities."

2. **Term from Oberg** (p. 144): "The term 'chiefdom' seems more appropriate than the several alternatives because it focuses attention on the rise of this sociopolitical innovation. It has been used by Oberg (1955), and after him by Steward and Faron (1959), to designate a type of South American Indian society. The present usage somewhat simplifies this regional type and extends it to designate a stage in general cultural evolution."

3. **KEY: Redistributional definition** (p. 144): "Chiefdoms are redistributional societies with a permanent central agency of coordination."

4. **Specialization and redistribution** (pp. 143-144): "the great change at the chiefdom level is that specialization and redistribution are no longer merely adjunctive to a few particular endeavors, but continuously characterize a large part of the activity of the society."

5. **Environmental/ecological basis** (pp. 144-145): "the rise of chiefdoms seems to have been related to a total environmental situation which was selective for specialization in production and redistribution of produce from a controlling center."

### Sahlins as key source

6. **Sahlins on redistribution in Polynesia** (p. 144, fn. 1): "For a discussion of redistribution in relation to social and political organization in Polynesia see Sahlins (1958)."

7. **Sahlins-Service collaboration** (pp. viii-ix): "Some of the most important of these were precipitated out of discussions (and arguments) with Sahlins, with whom I have been collaborating off and on for some time. He also read the manuscript carefully and made helpful comments."

8. **General and specific evolution from Sahlins** (p. viii): "Marshall Sahlins explained this clearly when he defined General and Specific Evolution to delineate more explicitly their divergent theoretical uses (Sahlins and Service, 1960)."

### The redistributor becomes the chief

9. **KEY: How chief emerges** (p. 149): "He begins as redistributor because of prestige achieved as a contributor or some other role; finally, he holds a status because he is the redistributor. As the system develops and specialization and redistribution become a necessary and integral part of the social and economic scheme, this central office confers very high rank."

10. **Office vs. person** (pp. 155-156): "An 'office,' on the other hand, is a position in a sociopolitical structure that has ascribed functions and conventionalized attributes no matter who occupies it."

11. **Hereditary succession** (p. 156): "Succession to hierarchical offices in chiefdoms seems normally to be characterized by the rule of primogeniture."

12. **Pyramidal structure** (pp. 150-151): "A chiefdom is in a sense pyramidal or cone-shaped in structure, and the hierarchical nature of the totality becomes reflected in the smaller local organizations."

### Rank and inequality

13. **Chiefdoms are "profoundly inegalitarian"** (p. 150): "bands and tribes are egalitarian, chiefdoms are profoundly inegalitarian."

14. **Genealogical ranking** (p. 155): "Persons are then ranked above others according to their genealogical nearness to him [the chief]."

15. **Continuous gradation, not classes** (p. 158): An ethnographer on the Nootka: "if they have classes, then everyone is in a class by himself." Rank is continuous, not categorical.

16. **Conceptual class division** (pp. 158-159): "many well-developed chiefdoms seem to have had a conceptual class division." Tahitian Ari'i, Ra'atira, Manehune cited as example.

### Authority without force

17. **KEY: No true government** (p. 172): "Chiefdoms have centralized direction and problems of governance, but no true government to back up decisions by legalized force."

18. **Authority vs. power** (p. 160): Citing Hannah Arendt: "authority precludes the use of external means of coercion; where force is used, authority itself has failed."

19. **Sumptuary rules as governance substitute** (p. 159): "It may be that for this reason sumptuary rules and the emphasis on rank come to such a sudden prominence at the chiefdom stage."

### Economic features

20. **Chief controls labor** (pp. 149-150): "A further important feature lies in the chief's ability to plan, organize, and deploy public labor."

21. **Craft subsidization** (pp. 148-149): "Craftsmen of superior skills can become fully subsidized so that many items of greatly superior workmanship tend to characterize chiefdoms."

22. **Storehouses and monuments** (p. 171): "The direction of public labor also leaves highly visible results: in some chiefdoms, irrigation works and terracing of slopes; in others, temples and temple-mounds, pyramids, and other such monuments."

### Rise and fall cycles

23. **Rise and fall inherent** (p. 152): "The 'rise and fall' of chiefdoms has been such a frequent phenomenon that it seems to be a part of their nature."

24. **Expansion and disintegration** (p. 152): "new smaller chiefdoms are left to compete for their own expansion."

## II. Polynesia as Chiefdom Exemplar

### Generic Polynesian references

25. **Polynesian chiefdoms as most developed** (p. 153): "Chiefdoms are most typical and most highly developed on the Polynesian and Micronesian high islands, diminishing in size and complexity on progressively smaller islands."

26. **Fiji, Tikopia, Trobriands as lower-level** (p. 153): "In Fiji, Polynesian outliers (such as Tikopia), and a few Melanesian areas such as the Trobriands are low-level or proto-chiefdoms."

27. **Polynesian genealogical depth** (p. 157): "Polynesian chiefs are said to have been able to calculate their genealogies for as far back as fifty generations."

28. **Polynesian ranked lineages** (p. 150): "Some of the well-known chiefdoms, such as the Polynesians and some Indians of the Northwest Coast of North America, arrived at the point where every member of the society bore some precise hereditary status which could be calculated in terms of nearness to the chiefly line."

29. **Maori as reverted chiefdom** (pp. 147-148): "The Maori originally came from the central region of Polynesia where chiefdoms were highly developed, but when they colonized the huge, open environment of New Zealand they subdivided and scattered. In so doing they reverted to a less centralized and less organized form of society, eventually coming to resemble tribal society more than their original chiefdom form."

30. **Maori and Samoa as more "democratic"** (pp. 161-162): "the Polynesians who settled New Zealand found a wide-open environment to expand into, so that frontier-like pioneering was possible and a leader of low hereditary position could nevertheless by charismatic force gain a following and raise his status by achievement in carving out a new domain. Thus the Maori of New Zealand have been described as more 'democratic' than most of the other Polynesian chiefdoms. Samoa has also been described as allowing for achievement as a criterion of rank."

31. **Tahiti and Hawaii ascended to states with European help** (pp. 153-154): "chiefdoms may ascend to the level of primitive states with the help of the invaders. This consequence was strikingly demonstrated in the cases of Tahiti, Hawaii, and some of the West African coastal states."

32. **Goldman cited for status rivalry and Polynesia** (pp. 162, 170): Goldman 1955 cited for status rivalry and sociocentric terminology. Goldman 1960 is not cited.

33. **Tahitian class terminology** (pp. 158-159, 172): Tahitian Ari'i, Ra'atira, Manehune used as example of conceptual class division. Used again (p. 172) to contrast chiefdom social strata from state political classes.

34. **Polynesian taboo as economic tool** (p. 159): "taboos (a frequent practice in Polynesia)" used to create social distance and implement authority.

## III. Easter Island in This Book

35. **Easter Island is never mentioned.** The word "Easter" does not appear anywhere in the text.

36. **No Easter Island-specific sources cited.** No Metraux, Routledge, Thomson, or Heyerdahl in the bibliography for Easter Island purposes.

37. **The chiefdom concept is built without Easter Island data.** The chiefdom stage is defined from: Polynesia generically (Sahlins 1958), Circum-Caribbean societies (Steward 1946, Oberg 1955), Northwest Coast (various), steppe pastoralists, and African chiefdoms.

## IV. The Feedback Loop

38. **The circular chain documented in this book:**

Step 1: Sahlins 1954/1958 classifies Polynesian societies on a stratification gradient, treating them as kin-organized societies with varying degrees of redistributive centralization. Easter Island is placed in Group IIA with acknowledged data gaps.

Step 2: Service 1962 takes Sahlins's Polynesian characterization and formalizes it into a universal evolutionary stage called "chiefdom," defined by redistribution, hereditary office, and centralized coordination. The chiefdom becomes a necessary step between tribal and state organization.

Step 3: Sahlins 1963 (contemporaneous) creates the big-man/chief binary that places all Polynesia on the "chief" side.

Step 4: Kirch 1984 applies the formalized chiefdom concept back to Polynesia archaeologically, using Service's framework (while disclaiming neo-evolutionary stages). Easter Island is treated as a chiefdom because it is Polynesian and Polynesia is the chiefdom exemplar.

**The circularity**: Polynesian data (primarily from Hawaii, Tahiti, Tonga) is used to construct the chiefdom concept; the concept is then applied back to Polynesia as if it were an independent analytical tool. For Easter Island specifically, the circularity is compounded: Sahlins acknowledged the redistribution evidence was lacking for Easter Island (1954, p. 101); Service defines the chiefdom by redistribution; Kirch applies the chiefdom label to Easter Island without ever demonstrating redistribution.

39. **Service's chiefdom definition vs. Easter Island evidence:**

| Service's chiefdom criteria | Easter Island evidence |
|---|---|
| "Redistributional societies with a permanent central agency of coordination" (p. 144) | Goldman 1970 (pp. 114-115): redistribution lay with commoner heads, not the ariki mau. Sahlins 1954 (p. 101): "little direct evidence" of redistribution. |
| Hereditary office with primogeniture (p. 156) | Goldman 1970 (p. 120): only the Miru maintained hereditary succession; all other mata headed by warrior chiefs via military success. |
| Chief can "plan, organize, and deploy public labor" (p. 150) | Goldman 1970 (pp. 106-107): "no reason to believe" central authority undertook local monuments. Kirch 1984 (pp. 270-271): "independently working crews." |
| "Profoundly inegalitarian" with continuous genealogical ranking (p. 150) | Goldman 1970 (p. 116): Easter Island lacked rangatira rank. Only two genealogical ranks (ariki, huramana), not a continuous gradation. |
| Craft subsidization from the redistributive center (pp. 148-149) | Goldman 1970 (p. 113): carver "guild" hereditary but no evidence of chiefly subsidization. Sahlins 1955 (p. 1050): carvers paid directly in food by commissioners. |
| Monuments as evidence of directed public labor (p. 171) | Lipo et al. 2013: 18-person transport. Lipo et al. 2025: decentralized quarry, 4-6 carvers per moai. |

Easter Island fails every defining criterion of Service's chiefdom.

## V. Bibliography Analysis

### Sources for the chiefdom concept

**Primary theoretical sources:**
- Oberg 1955: coined "chiefdom" for South American societies
- Steward 1946, 1948: Circum-Caribbean framework
- Sahlins 1958: Polynesian stratification/redistribution
- Goldman 1955, 1957: Polynesian status rivalry (but not the 1970 book)

**Primary ethnographic sources for Polynesia:**
- Sahlins 1958 (*Social Stratification in Polynesia*)
- Firth 1957 (*We, the Tikopia*)
- Goldman 1955 ("Status Rivalry and Cultural Evolution in Polynesia")

**Specific Polynesian societies discussed:**
- Tahiti (Ari'i/Ra'atira/Manehune class terminology)
- Hawaii (ascended to state with European contact)
- Tonga (ascended to state)
- Maori (reverted from chiefdom to tribal)
- Samoa (democratic achievement of rank)
- Tikopia (low-level/proto-chiefdom)
- Fiji (transitional, Melanesian-Polynesian)

- No Metraux, Routledge, Thomson, Heyerdahl
- No Easter Island-specific source of any kind
- No Goldman 1960 or later works

## VI. Significance for Our Argument

40. **Service completes the conceptual infrastructure.** After this book, "chiefdom" is a universal evolutionary stage, not merely a Polynesian descriptor. When Kirch (1984) calls Easter Island a chiefdom, he is invoking Service's universal category, not merely Sahlins's Polynesian classification. The conceptual stakes are higher: Easter Island must be a chiefdom because that is the evolutionary stage between tribe and state, and it is too complex for a tribe.

41. **The redistribution definition is the chiefdom's Achilles' heel for Easter Island.** Service defines chiefdoms by redistribution. Sahlins admitted redistribution evidence was lacking for Easter Island. Goldman found redistribution lay with commoner heads, not the paramount. The foundational criterion is unmet. Yet the label persists because it is the only available evolutionary category between tribe and state.

42. **Service's acknowledgment of variation is important but unexploited.** Service notes Maori "reverted" to tribal levels (pp. 147-148) and that not all chiefdoms had fully hereditary offices (pp. 161-162). These concessions admit that being Polynesian does not automatically mean being a fully developed chiefdom. But neither Service nor subsequent scholars applied this logic to Easter Island, which Goldman had independently classified as lacking centralized authority.
