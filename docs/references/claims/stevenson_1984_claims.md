## I. Intellectual Genealogy

### Who Stevenson builds on

1. **Hayden & Cannon 1982**: Corporate group as the archaeological unit of analysis. Provides the methodological premise that corporate descent groups can be identified from archaeological settlement patterns (pp. 23-24).

2. **Fortes 1953**: Descent group theory from British social anthropology. Theoretical foundation for treating kinship organization as the structural basis of political organization.

3. **Kirchhoff 1955**: Conical clan concept. Cited for the hierarchical kinship model applied to Polynesian societies.

4. **Firth 1936/1957**: Polynesian kinship organization. Tikopia ethnography as baseline for Pacific kinship structures.

5. **Fried 1957**: Ranked society concept. Used alongside Befu & Plotnicov 1962 for the distinction between ranked and stratified political organization.

6. **Brown 1974**: Defines eight attributes of the corporation as a social unit (pp. 23-24). Foundational for Stevenson's operational definition of corporate descent groups.

7. **Tiffany 1976**: Supplements Brown 1974 on corporate group attributes (pp. 23-24).

8. **Befu & Plotnicov 1962**: Types of corporate unilineal descent groups. Used for structural distinctions among descent group forms.

9. **Sahlins 1958**: Cited for placing Mangareva and Uvea at the same complexity level as Easter Island (p. 22). Used for comparative context, not as the organizing framework.

10. **Goldman 1970**: Cited for the dual status system on Easter Island (pp. 16, 178): hereditary chiefs (*ariki*) had sacred but not secular authority; warrior chiefs (*matatoa*) controlled force. Goldman's "Open" classification is engaged, unlike in Kirch 1984.

11. **McCoy 1976**: Settlement pattern data for Easter Island. Primary archaeological source for site distributions.

12. **McCall 1979**: Extensive citations for kinship structure, territorial divisions, and five levels of segmentation (pp. 15-16). PRIMARY source for late-period kinship and political organization.

13. **Metraux 1940**: Primary ethnographic source for oral traditions, subsistence practices, burial customs, and boundary markers. [POST-RAID]

### What is NOT cited

14. **Service 1962 NOT CITED in bibliography.** The band-tribe-chiefdom-state typology is entirely absent from the theoretical framework. This is significant: the dissertation that produced the first systematic archaeological model of Easter Island political organization did not use the chiefdom concept as an analytical category.

15. **Kirch 1984 NOT CITED.** *The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms* does not appear in the bibliography, though Stevenson had a chapter "In Press" in Kirch's forthcoming *Island Societies* volume (per Vita, p. 359). The two frameworks were developed independently.

### How Stevenson positions himself

16. **Theoretical framework draws on British social anthropology and African ethnographic analogues** (Ibo, Dahomey), NOT neoevolutionary typology. The intellectual genealogy runs through Fortes, Firth, and corporate group theory rather than through White, Steward, Service, and cultural evolution.

17. **Does NOT use the term "chiefdom" as an analytical category.** The three competing models (Types 1-3) describe descent group organization, not typological stages. The organizing concept is "corporate descent group structure," not "chiefdom."

## II. Theoretical Framework

### Corporate descent group model

18. **Eight defining attributes of the corporation** (from Brown 1974 and Tiffany 1976, pp. 23-24): presumptive perpetuity, unique identity, rules for membership, empirical existence, common concerns, organization, autonomy of action.

19. **Three competing models of corporate group structure** (pp. 47-50):
    - **Type 1: Non-Corporate Descent Groups** — dispersed, egalitarian, minimal lineage activity
    - **Type 2: Lineage Specific Corporate Descent Groups** — organized along descent lines, lineage-focused ritual
    - **Type 3: Ranked Corporate Descent Groups** — hierarchically ordered lineages, regional centers

20. **Explicit rejection of unilinear evolutionary trajectory** (p. 50): "The three models discussed above are not presented as an evolutionary trajectory of corporate descent group structure... In this analysis it is not assumed a priori that the prehistoric social system of Easter Island is headed full tilt on a course of increased social complexity, but that it may adopt alternate forms of social structure as conditions dictate."

21. **Models describe alternative organizational forms, not stages.** The system can move between types in any direction, including reversal toward less complex organization. This framing is fundamentally different from the Sahlins-Service-Kirch evolutionary trajectory.

## III. Methodology

### Study area and sample

22. **Study area**: 2.5 km x 3.0 km, Quadrangle 7 and lower Quadrangle 11, southern coast (p. 19).

23. **Coastal strip**: 9 km from Hanga Hahave to Runga Va'e (p. 19).

24. **Sample size**: 65 ceremonial centers and 184 residential sites (abstract).

25. **Residential site sample**: 66% of residential sites dated (p. 19).

### Dating and phasing

26. **Obsidian hydration dating** re-evaluated with experimentally derived rates (Ch. 3). Stevenson developed new hydration rate constants rather than relying on existing ones.

27. **13 one-hundred year phases** (700 AD - 1983 AD) based on OHD error factor (p. 136). Finer temporal resolution than Ayres's five-phase scheme.

28. **Residential site phase assignment** by obsidian hydration dates directly (p. 137).

29. **Ahu phase assignment**: by associated obsidian where available, or estimated at 129 years earlier than associated residential structures (p. 137).

### Analytical methods

30. **Multivariate cluster analysis** for ahu typology and residential site typologies (pp. 19-20). Classification based on empirical clustering of attributes, not imposed categories.

## IV. Settlement Pattern Data

### Population trajectory

31. **Settlement minimal until ~1350 AD** (p. 170). Study area was occupied late relative to proposed initial island settlement.

32. **Phases 1-6 (700-1300 AD)**: 1-33 occupations, sparse and scattered (p. 138). Low-density, dispersed settlement.

33. **Phase 7 (1301-1400)**: Population doubles to 60; first platform ahu constructed at Vaihu, Ure Uranga te Mahina, Akahanga (pp. 138-140).

34. **Phase 8 (1401-1500)**: Doubles again to 106; large number of platform ahu constructed (p. 140).

35. **Phase 9 (1501-1600)**: Increases to 124; ahu construction becomes less frequent (p. 142).

36. **Phase 10 (1601-1700)**: Population levels off at 124; ahu construction "very limited" (p. 144).

37. **Phases 11-12 (1701-1900)**: Population increases slightly then declines to 114; platform ahu fall into disuse, semipyramidal interment structures replace them (pp. 146-148).

38. **Population increase of ~400%** from Phase 6 to Phase 9 (p. 144).

39. **Settlement concentrated within 1 km of coast** throughout all phases.

### Settlement of study area relative to island colonization

40. **Study area settled ~800-900 years after proposed initial island settlement** (p. 176). The southern coast was not among the earliest occupied zones.

## V. Ahu Typology

41. **Type 1 ahu = multiple lineage centers**: Large, elaborate platform ahu associated with elite residences. Interpreted as centers for multiple descent groups (p. 160).

42. **Type 2 ahu = individual lineage centers**: The most common type. Interpreted as ceremonial centers for single lineages.

43. **Type 3 ahu = same category as Type 2** but different building style. Cluster at eastern end of study area.

44. **Type 4 ahu = sub-lineage shrines**: Smaller structures interpreted as ritual sites for sub-lineage segments.

45. **Type 5 ahu = tentatively proposed as "secondary elite centers"** (p. 160). Classification uncertain.

## VI. Political Organization Model

### Four autonomous groups

46. **Four politically autonomous groups identified by Phase 9** (p. 163), continuing unchanged until Phase 11. These are identified by clusters of Type 1 platform ahu (multiple lineage centers) with associated elite villages.

47. **No internal or between-group rank differences**: "each of these groups does not exhibit internal or between group differences in ascribed social rank" (p. 180).

48. **Corporate group strength weak**: "the corporate group strength of the population appears to be relatively weak. Descent groups most likely exercised a significant amount of autonomy" (p. 176).

49. **Cannot draw group boundaries**: "it will not be possible to draw boundaries between political units" (p. 162). "The use of an arbitrary criteria for establishing political divisions... is probably misleading" (p. 162).

### Organizational trajectory

50. **Phases 5-6**: "lineage specific corporate descent groups, although it is organizationally weak" (p. 166). Matches Model Type 2.

51. **Phases 7-8**: "non-ranked corporate descent group lines" (p. 166). Still matches Model Type 2, but with more organizational elaboration through ahu construction.

52. **Phases 9-10**: Integration increases; four autonomous groups form with multiple lineage centers and elite villages. Ranking "suspected" but not demonstrated (p. 167). Approaching but not clearly matching Model Type 3.

53. **Phase 11+**: "the ranked corporate descent group structure is not present" (p. 168). Burial data "do not exhibit institutionalized differences in rank" (p. 168). System "reverted to a non-ranked corporate structure" (p. 168). Matches Model Type 2 again.

54. **System reverts to non-ranked structure after ~1680 AD.** This is the opposite of the Sahlins-Kirch expectation of progressive complexity. The archaeological evidence shows organizational simplification, not collapse from a higher level, but reversion to an earlier form.

## VII. Chronological Framework

55. **Ayres (1975) five-phase scheme adopted** (p. 10, Table 23 p. 170): Settlement and Development (400-1000), Expansion (1000-1680), Decadent (1680-1722), Protohistoric (1722-1868), Historic (1868-1983).

56. **"Decadent" phase based on oral histories from Metraux 1940** (p. 11). The periodization depends on post-raid ethnographic reconstruction. [POST-RAID]

57. **No "Chiefdom Integration" periodization in the 1984 dissertation.** This terminology appears in later Stevenson publications (1986, 1997, 2002) but is absent here.

58. **Stevenson's 13-phase system** (100-year intervals, 700-1983 AD) provides finer resolution than Ayres (p. 136). Used for the settlement pattern analysis rather than the five-phase scheme.

59. **First platform ahu in study area during Phase 7 (1301-1400 AD)** (pp. 138-140).

60. **Corporate group consolidation during Phases 8-9 (1401-1600 AD)** (p. 167).

## VIII. Population Estimates

61. **Study area at 1600 AD: 147 persons/sq km** (p. 172).

62. **Island-wide at 1600 AD: 8,927 persons** (p. 172). [cf. Davis et al. 2024: max ~3,900]

63. **By 1800 AD: 9,659 persons** (p. 172).

64. **Peak population: 11,000-12,000 suspected** (p. 173).

65. **0.65% annual growth rate** (p. 172).

66. **Based on 6 persons per household** (from Hawaiian data, Cordy 1981) **and 50% of island cultivable** (Routledge 1919). Both assumptions are now problematic: the persons-per-household figure is borrowed from a different island, and the 50% cultivable estimate has been substantially revised downward by Davis et al. 2024.

### Contact-era sources

76. **McCoy 1976, Ayres 1973/1975/1981, Mulloy 1961/1966/1978, Heyerdahl & Ferdon 1961**: Primary archaeological references. Settlement pattern and ahu data from these sources.

77. **Stevenson explicitly acknowledges post-raid evidence problem** (p. 23): "Easter Island ethnographic data shall be used selectively and with caution" due to post-raid disruption. This caveat is stated but the full implications are not drawn out: the political organization model still depends on post-raid sources for its kinship framework.

## XI. Key Claims for Our Argument

### 1. Does Stevenson use the chiefdom concept?

**No.** The term "chiefdom" does not function as an analytical category. Service 1962 is not cited. The framework is corporate descent group theory from British social anthropology (Fortes, Firth) and corporate group archaeology (Hayden & Cannon), not neoevolutionary typology. This is the first systematic archaeological study of Easter Island political organization, and it was built without the chiefdom concept.

### 2. Does the archaeological evidence support centralized authority?

**No.** "each of these groups does not exhibit internal or between group differences in ascribed social rank" (p. 180). "the corporate group strength of the population appears to be relatively weak. Descent groups most likely exercised a significant amount of autonomy" (p. 176). The four groups identified are politically autonomous, not hierarchically integrated.

### 3. Does the trajectory match neoevolutionary expectations?

**No.** The system shows increasing organizational complexity through Phases 7-10 (1301-1700 AD) but then reverts to non-ranked structure: "the ranked corporate descent group structure is not present" after ~1680 AD (p. 168). Stevenson explicitly rejects the assumption of unilinear progression toward complexity (p. 50).

### 4. What is the relationship to Stevenson 1986?

**Progressive hardening.** The 1984 dissertation contains at least 11 explicit hedging/qualification statements (claims 78-88 above). It does not use "chiefdom," does not cite Service 1962, and explicitly rejects evolutionary trajectory assumptions. The distance between this 1984 framework and the later "Chiefdom Integration" periodization (Stevenson 1986, 1997, 2002) represents a significant transformation of tentative, qualified findings into an established analytical framework. This parallels the Sahlins 1954-to-1958 hardening documented elsewhere in the project.

### 5. How does Stevenson handle Goldman's "Open" classification?

**Engages it directly**, unlike Kirch 1984. Goldman 1970 is cited for the dual status system (hereditary *ariki* with sacred authority, *matatoa* with secular/military authority) at pp. 16 and 178. This is significant because Goldman's "Open" classification implies exactly the kind of non-centralized, competitive political organization that Stevenson's archaeological data support.

### 6. What does the settlement data show about political integration?

**Four small, autonomous groups, not a unified polity.** Each group is identified by a cluster of Type 1 platform ahu with associated elite villages. There is no evidence of hierarchical integration between groups or of island-wide political authority. The scale is local, not regional or island-wide.

### 7. Does Stevenson acknowledge post-raid source problems?

**Yes, explicitly** (p. 23). But the acknowledgment is partial: the caveat is stated, yet the political organization model still depends entirely on post-raid sources (McCall 1979, Metraux 1940, Goldman 1970) for its kinship framework. Contact-era sources are cited only for subsistence and population, not for political structure.

## XIII. Honest Complications

1. **The four-group model still depends on post-raid ethnographic data.** Although the settlement pattern analysis is archaeological, the interpretation of ahu types as "multiple lineage centers" versus "individual lineage centers" depends on kinship categories derived from McCall 1979 and Metraux 1940. The archaeological patterns are real; the kinship interpretation is ethnographically mediated.

2. **Stevenson does describe "elite villages" associated with Type 1 ahu.** This implies some degree of social differentiation, even if not institutionalized rank between groups. The existence of elite residential sites associated with ceremonial centers is consistent with leadership roles, though not necessarily with chiefdom-level hierarchy.

3. **The "suspected" ranking at Phases 9-10 (p. 167) is an honest assessment.** Stevenson sees patterns suggestive of ranking but refuses to assert it without stronger evidence. This is methodologically sound and contrasts with later publications where ranking becomes an established feature.

4. **Population estimates are high but internally consistent.** The 8,927 figure at 1600 AD is based on explicit assumptions (6 persons/household, 50% cultivable land) that can be evaluated and updated. Davis et al. 2024 substantially revises the agricultural carrying capacity downward, which undermines the density extrapolation.

5. **The corporate descent group framework is substantively different from the chiefdom framework.** This is not merely a terminological difference. Corporate descent group analysis asks: what kind of kinship-based organization produced these archaeological patterns? Chiefdom analysis asks: what stage of political evolution does this society represent? The questions generate different interpretations of the same evidence. Stevenson's framework is closer to what collective action approaches would later propose.

6. **The reversion finding (Phase 11+) is potentially important for our argument.** If political organization simplified rather than collapsed, this challenges both the ecocide narrative (catastrophic failure of a complex system) and the chiefdom narrative (progressive evolution toward centralization). It suggests flexible organizational adaptation, consistent with collective action models.
