# Claims Extraction: Pollard et al. 2010

**Source**: Pollard, Joshua, Alistair Paterson, and Kate Welham. 2010. "*Te Miro o'one*: the archaeology of contact on Rapa Nui (Easter Island)." *World Archaeology* 42(4): 562-580.

**Type**: Journal article (archaeology of contact period)
**Accessed**: PDF (`pollard_et_al_2010.pdf`)
**Extracted**: 2026-03-16

## I. Framing: Contact as Creative Agency, Not Simply Decline

1. "Rather than seeing events on the island during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries as a simple trajectory of decline, we highlight how myriad new practices and social orders emerged through a creative agency that drew inventively upon the material and cosmological possibilities afforded by contact." (p. 562, abstract)

2. "There also exists a tendency to see events on the island during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries as a simple trajectory of decline, a sad postscript to a vibrant prehistoric sequence. We have highlighted the ways that myriad new practices and social orders emerged through a creative agency that drew inventively upon the material and cosmological possibilities afforded by contact." (p. 576)

**Assessment**: The paper's central framing challenges the collapse narrative but does not directly address the chiefdom attribution. It treats the contact period as a time of cultural innovation, not just disintegration.

## II. Political Organization and the Birdman Cult

### The tangata manu as erosion of hereditary chiefly authority

3. "The winner, or *tangata manu*, became paramount warrior chief for the year, though their resulting *tapu* status demanded that much of this time was spent residing in seclusion." (p. 566)

4. "The emergence of these rites reflects an erosion of traditional chiefly authority constructed around genealogically ascribed rank and its substitution with performative leadership structures controlled by warriors and priests (Thomas 1990: 120, 178-9), a pivotal shift in the way power relations were enacted." (p. 566)

**Assessment**: [CENTRALIZATION FLAG] Pollard et al. characterize the birdman institution as a *replacement* of hereditary chiefly authority with a performative, competitive system. This framing implies that the birdman cult represents decentralization or at least a shift away from ascribed-rank hierarchy. They rely on Thomas (1990) for this interpretation. This is significant for the chiefdom question: if the birdman cult replaced chiefly authority, the contact-period political system observed by Europeans was *not* a chiefdom in the Sahlins-Kirch sense.

### Chronology of the birdman cult

5. "While it is known that the last *tangata manu* ('birdman') ceremonies were held at 'Orongo in 1866 or 1867 (the eve of conversion), the period during which they emerged is somewhat ambiguous." (p. 566)

6. "Van Tilburg and Lee place the political changes that gave rise to the birdman ceremonies in the early to mid-sixteenth century (Van Tilburg 1994: 92; Van Tilburg and Lee 1987: 136). This chronology is based upon radiocarbon dates on bulk charcoal samples recovered during the 1955-6 Norwegian Expedition excavations at 'Orongo (Ferdon 1961: 221-55)." (pp. 566-567)

7. "Taking a critical stance, all that can be reliably inferred from the published sequence is that structure R-12 was created some time later, and potentially much later, than a charcoal lined-pit from which a date of AD 1412-1643 (T-194, at 95.4 per cent) was obtained on a bulk sample." (p. 567)

8. "Rather than lying within the later part of the prehistoric period, it could equally be argued that the stone structures which form Complexes B and C at 'Orongo, and which provided the focus for the *tangata manu* ceremonies, belong to a horizon of religious and political change that is post-contact." (p. 567)

**Assessment**: [CENTRALIZATION FLAG] This is a critical claim for the chiefdom question. If the birdman ceremonies are post-contact rather than late prehistoric, then the entire narrative of a "Decadent/Restructure Phase" in which competitive warrior chiefs replaced hereditary leaders in the prehistoric period is called into question. The political transformation may have been driven by contact, not by internal social dynamics.

9. "New dates from appropriate contexts are clearly needed. If new dates were to support a post-contact chronology, as we suspect, they would have profound implications for our understanding of the timing and mechanisms of changing politico-religious practices on the island, effectively moving key events out of the prehistoric sequence." (p. 567)

### Moai toppling and chiefly authority

10. "It cannot be doubted that major political and religious changes took place on Rapa Nui prior to AD 1800. The statue cult came to an end, with those *moai* on *ahu* platforms being toppled deliberately (Heyerdahl 1961: 39). This can be read as a material correlate of the decline of traditional modes of chiefly authority based around genealogically ascribed rank (Kirch 1984: 274-7)." (p. 564)

**Assessment**: Pollard et al. acknowledge the standard interpretation but frame it as one possible "reading" rather than a demonstrated fact. They cite Kirch 1984 for this interpretation.

11. "Toppled *moai* statues are a striking material manifestation of ideological changes that swept the island during the later eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. There existed a distinct technology to these acts of ancestral sacrilege, with *moai* being pulled forward onto their faces (Plate 1), exposing and violating the most *tapu* part of a chiefly person, the back of the head and neck (Linton 1925: 86; Van Tilburg and Lee 1987: 137)." (p. 569)

12. "Boulders were positioned in such a way as to break the statue necks upon their fall, thus severing their supernatural power (Ayers 1973: 132; McCoy 1979)." (p. 569)

**Assessment**: The deliberate technology of moai toppling indicates political intentionality, but the interpretation that this represents the decline of "chiefly authority" depends on the prior assumption that moai embodied such authority. The moai could equally represent lineage ancestors, clan prestige, or collective achievement without requiring a chiefly command interpretation.

## III. Contact-Era Observations and Political Authority

### Roggeveen 1722

13. "Following an on-board welcome by an individual of likely priestly status, Roggeveen was greeted by thousands of islanders offering gifts of food (Corney 1908: 133), the prime medium through which social relations were initiated." (p. 568)

14. The Roggeveen visit saw exchanges of "over 500 chickens and many vegetables" for "a length of cloth with coloured print 50-60 ells (c. 35-40m) in length" (p. 568, citing Corney 1908: 134).

### Gonzalez 1770

15. "Arriving nearly fifty years later, Gonzalez claimed the island for Spain with an incredible piece of political theatre that had considerable political impact (Corney 1908: 47-104). Three wooden crosses were carried in full procession, accompanied by drums and flying colours, and planted on the three hillocks of Poike on the far east of the island." (p. 568)

16. "The religious and political import of the ceremony was understood, albeit through the lens of eastern Polynesian cosmology. Van Tilburg makes the astute observation that the erection of upright poles and crosses would have constituted an act of domination, such objects being both symbols and implements of sacred and chiefly authority (1994: 31)." (p. 569)

17. "Contemporary Spanish accounts speak of 800 islanders (perhaps one quarter of the population) assembling at the event, of Rapa Nui assisting in carrying the crosses, singing, dancing, making offerings of barkcloth and chickens (Corney 1908: 100-4)." (pp. 568-569)

### Cook 1774

18. "Only four years later, Cook observed toppled *moai*, the material signature of internal conflicts that had been set in train by the politically destabilizing actions of the Spanish (Cook 1777: 281)." (p. 569)

**Assessment**: Pollard et al. suggest that the Gonzalez visit may have *caused* or accelerated moai toppling, attributing internal political conflict to external destabilization. If correct, this would mean the toppled moai observed by all subsequent visitors reflect contact-period disruption, not a prehistoric political transition.

19. "Perhaps in acknowledgement of the power seen to reside in these irregular strangers, his party was led in solemn procession around the island by individuals of chiefly or priestly status (Cook 1777: 281-3; Van Tilburg 1994: 31)." (p. 569)

## IV. Demographic Collapse and Source Reliability

20. "Rainbird and Hunto and Lipo attribute population collapse and political/religious change to the arrival of Europeans: i.e. collapse as a contact period process." (p. 565)

21. "Using the distribution of habitation sites dated by obsidian hydration (Stevenson et al. 2007), Hunt and Lipo note that the marked decline in population from its likely maximum of c. 3,000-5,000 occurs after c. 1750 (2010: 38-9)." (p. 565)

22. "Peruvian slave raids during the 1860s further depleted the population, with subsequent resettlement of the survivors leading to the introduction of smallpox (Fischer 2005: 87-91). By 1877, the population of Rapa Nui stood at 110 (Metraux 1940)." (p. 565)

**Assessment**: [POST-RAID] Pollard et al. accept the Hunt and Lipo demographic chronology, placing population decline in the contact period rather than the prehistoric period. This is relevant because ethnographic sources (Thompson 1891, Routledge 1919, Metraux 1940) were collected after this demographic catastrophe.

### On Routledge's oral history

23. Footnote 2: "As Van Tilburg has highlighted (2003), the conditions under which Routledge collected her oral history on pre-conversion practices were far from ideal, and so the detail should be treated with a degree of caution." (p. 577)

**Assessment**: [POST-RAID] This note applies to Routledge's accounts of the *miro o'one* ceremonies and, by extension, to her accounts of the birdman cult and other pre-conversion practices. If the detail of Routledge's oral history is unreliable, then birdman cult descriptions dependent on her work inherit that unreliability.

## V. 'Orongo and Chronological Questions

24. "Four trenches were dug within Complex B, the main area of *ana* stone buildings connected with the birdman cult. Trenches 1 and 2 produced deeply stratified deposits indicative of long sequences of activity that included clearance deposits (thick charcoal-rich soils) and settlement-related features (a series of pits in Trench 2), and it is from these that radiocarbon dates were obtained whose calibrated ranges span the thirteenth to twentieth centuries." (p. 567)

25. "The sequence for Trench 1, from which four dates came (K-520, K-514, K-506 and M-708), should stand alone. However, both the sequence and the dates were extrapolated to argue for a central date of c. AD 1540 for the construction of building R-12 in nearby Trench 2, and by extension for the whole complex (Ferdon 1961: 243), a tenuous assumption." (p. 567)

**Assessment**: Pollard et al. systematically undermine the standard c. AD 1540 date for the construction of 'Orongo's birdman ceremonial complex. If the stone structures are later, potentially post-contact, the entire chronological framework for the transition from hereditary chiefdom to birdman warrior-chief system must be reconsidered.

26. "Many dates used to define the chronology of key ceremonial sites, especially those from the 1950s' work of the Norwegian Archaeological Expedition (Heyerdahl and Ferdon 1961), are on bulked charcoal samples. Without controls on sample residuality and old wood effect, such determinations will tend to be earlier than the contexts they purport to date and are best treated with caution (Martinsson-Wallin and Crockford 2001)." (pp. 566-567)

## VI. The Statue Cult and Its End

27. "Good evidence exists from both archaeology and the accounts of early European explorers to support the views of Rainbird (2002) and Hunt and Lipo (2010). Most striking are the observations by early visitors, which reveal a trajectory of dramatic change from the eighteenth century. Notably, the statue cult appeared to be extant as late as 1770, as recorded by members of the Dutch and Spanish voyages led by Jacob Roggeveen and Don Felipe Gonzalez y Haedo (Corney 1908). Yet in 1774 Cook describes statues being toppled (Cook 1777: 281)." (p. 565)

28. "Over the next half century visitors described both toppled and standing statues; the Russian expedition of 1804 reported around thirty standing *moai* (Richards, R. 2008: 26). The last to stand may have been the giant Paro on Te Pito te Kura on the north coast of the island, which was pulled down in 1862-4 (Fischer 2005: 80; Thomson 1891: 489)." (p. 565)

**Assessment**: The trajectory from standing moai (1770) to toppled moai (1774 onward) places the end of the statue cult firmly in the contact period. This supports the argument that the political transformation was contact-driven, not a prehistoric "decline."

## VII. European Objects in Rapa Nui Cosmology

### How Europeans and their goods were understood

29. "Viewed from the island, early encounters with Europeans were profound events with the capacity to elicit cosmogonic crisis (Campbell 2003; Sahlins 1985; Van Tilburg 1994: 29-32)." (p. 568)

30. "Strangers from across the sea were often afforded a semi-divine status within Polynesian mytho-practice, and it is telling that words first used to describe Europeans implied their supernatural provenance (Campbell 2003: 71). High-ranking chiefs, who possessed divine linkage and were an embodiment of considerable *mana*, were regarded as strangers or invaders (Thomas 1991: 92)." (p. 568)

31. "As beings of extra-local origin, European crews were perhaps conceived as the embodiments of gods, divine chiefs or, at the very least, as very powerful beings." (p. 568)

**Assessment**: This framing suggests that Rapa Nui people may have treated European visitors *as if they were chiefs* because of Polynesian cosmological categories, which means that European observations of being greeted by "chiefly" or "priestly" figures may reflect the islanders performing hierarchical roles in response to what they perceived as a cosmologically significant encounter, rather than simply displaying their everyday political organization.

---

## VIII. Material Culture and Competing Clan Confederations

32. "The distribution of carvings contrasts with the south coast focus of depictions of European/hybrid ships, perhaps implying a division in ceremonial practices that reflects the existence at this time of competing clan confederations." (p. 574)

**Assessment**: [CENTRALIZATION FLAG] Pollard et al. note geographic differentiation in rock art types that may reflect competing political entities rather than a unified polity. Polynesian canoe petroglyphs concentrated on the north coast (Ra'ai to 'Anakena); European/hybrid ship images concentrated on the south coast. This spatial patterning is consistent with decentralized, competing groups rather than a single hierarchical chiefdom.

## IX. The Miro o'one Ceremonies

33. "During fieldwork on the island in 1914-15, Katherine Routledge recorded the oral testimony of a popular religious performance. She also discovered archaeological evidence for the ritual in the form of a 42m-long platform of rounded pebbles upon which a thatched house had stood." (p. 574)

34. "When Routledge enquired of its significance among her local guides she was told that it was a *hare a te atua* (house of the god or deified ancestor), and that the beings praised during ceremonies here were 'the men who came from far away in ships' (Routledge 1919: 239)." (p. 574)

35. "Surviving earthworks of *miro o'one* are known on the south side of the island at Hanga Poukura (site 6-14), on the ridge of Maunga Ori (site 6-411) and on low ground to the east of the latter at Miro O' one (site 7-252). In plan they are of boat form, c. 30-54m in length and c. 5m in width, dimensions close to those both of European sailing ships of the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries and of some of the larger Polynesian war canoes of the period (Hooper 2006: 18)." (p. 574)

**Assessment**: The *miro o'one* ceremonies represent a syncretic response to European contact: boat-shaped earth mounds associated with rituals honoring beings who "came from far away in ships." This is relevant because it shows the contact period producing new religious and political institutions that drew on both indigenous and European elements, rather than simply disrupting existing ones.

## X. Ships, Canoes, and Cosmological Possibilities

36. "Striking similarities also existed between the hierarchical order within European naval organization and that of Polynesian chiefdoms (Dening 1992): both communities shared a concern with status and its material performance through dress and prowess in conflict and voyaging." (p. 575)

37. "We would argue that it helps to collapse the distinction between canoes and ships, things that were Polynesian and things that were European. The world of culture contact is characterized by mutability and re-contextualization of practices and materials (Thomas 1991), and we should be wary of reading outward form as a simple index of a spurious inherent quality to any thing." (p. 575)

38. "When the Spaniards erected three crosses on the hillocks of Poike in 1770 the islanders recognized these as a material manifestation of a divine presence. Europeans did not always recognize how potent wooden poles could be, as objects of genealogical connection to the land and the divine." (p. 575)

39. "One might therefore wonder whether the triple masts of European vessels, repeatedly depicted in ship paintings and petroglyphs, were conceptually linked to the triple Poike crosses, to the poles that separated earth and sky and so to a sense of sacred origin." (p. 575)

**Assessment**: This interpretive framework suggests that Rapa Nui people incorporated European material culture into indigenous cosmological categories, including categories of political authority and sacredness. For the chiefdom question, this means contact-era performances of hierarchy (e.g., processions, deference to certain individuals) may reflect islanders performing roles within a cosmological framework activated by the European encounter, not displaying their permanent political organization.

## XI. On the Ecocide Narrative

40. "Rainbird (2002) and Hunt and Lipo (2010) have presented cogent arguments against the humanly induced ecological collapse model. Rainbird sees the Rapa Nui population as actors knowledgeably altering the island's environment, and points to instances elsewhere in the Pacific where ecological change was both intentional and enhanced subsistence potential." (p. 564-565)

41. "Hunt and Lipo highlight the impact on Rapa Nui of introduced rats, which devoured palm nuts and seedlings and may have been chiefly responsible for the decline of the giant palm." (p. 565)

42. "High food yields were also maintained following palm forest decline by innovative soil management technologies such as lithic mulching and the creation of rock veneer pavements (Stevenson et al. 2006)." (p. 565)

---

## XII. Contact Frequency and Its Implications

43. "The frequency of early contact events on Rapa Nui was not great. From 1722 to 1795 the island was visited on only seven occasions by Europeans: the first four were the exploratory voyages of Roggeveen (1722), Gonzalez (1770), Cook (1774) and La Perouse (1786); the last three were commercial sailings (in 1792, 1793 and 1795) out of Bristol. In total, they represent only around two weeks of contact over an eighty-year period." (p. 567)

44. "Between 1801 and 1861, eighty-five ships are reported to have visited the island, often stopping only a day or two (McCall 1990). Rapa Nui was not a favoured 'port of call'. The absence of abundant fresh water and pigs made it a poor location for provisioning; there was no natural harbour." (p. 567)

**Assessment**: The brevity of early contact episodes (approximately two weeks total over 80 years) means European observers had minimal exposure to Rapa Nui political organization. Claims about chiefly authority, hierarchical structure, or political organization based on these brief encounters rest on extremely thin observational foundations. Even the most attentive observers could only witness performances staged for the encounter, not daily political life.

---

## Summary of Claims Relevant to Chiefdom Attribution

Pollard et al. 2010 does not directly address the chiefdom attribution as such, but several of its claims are significant for the question:

1. **The birdman cult as replacement of hereditary authority**: The paper treats the *tangata manu* system as a competitive, performative leadership institution that *replaced* genealogically ascribed chiefly authority (p. 566). If correct, the contact-period political system was not a chiefdom.

2. **Post-contact chronology for 'Orongo**: The paper argues that the stone structures at 'Orongo and the birdman ceremonies themselves may be post-contact rather than late prehistoric (p. 567). This would collapse the "Decadent/Restructure Phase" of Van Tilburg's sequence into the contact period.

3. **Contact-driven political change**: Moai toppling may have been triggered by European contact (specifically Gonzalez's 1770 visit), not by internal prehistoric dynamics (p. 569).

4. **Cosmological framing of encounters**: Europeans were incorporated into Polynesian cosmological categories, meaning that observed performances of hierarchy during contact may not reflect everyday political organization (pp. 568-569).

5. **Competing clan confederations**: Geographic differentiation in rock art suggests competing groups, not a unified hierarchical polity (p. 574).

6. **Brevity of contact**: Total European contact before 1795 amounted to roughly two weeks, an inadequate basis for claims about political organization (p. 567).
