## I. Intellectual Genealogy

### Who Van Tilburg & Lee cite

1. **Van Tilburg 1986a** (the PhD dissertation): PRIMARY source. Cited at least 15 times. Provides the interpretive framework, statue typology, dating, and ideological claims. Pages cited: pp. 350, 353, 292, 269, 275, 467, 175, 339, 386a. This article is explicitly a published condensation of dissertation findings integrated with Lee's petroglyph data.

2. **Lee 1986a** (Georgia Lee's PhD dissertation, UCLA): Co-primary data source. Cited repeatedly for petroglyph project data, rock art classification, birdman symbol evolution. Lee's contribution is the ~1,000 petroglyph sites database.

3. **Kirch 1984**: Cited twice. First for settlement dating: "settlement from the Society or Marquesas Islands as early as A.D. 300 is postulated" (p. 137). Second for mana definition: "supernatural power or efficacy, Kirch 1984: 288" (p. 137). Not cited for chiefdom framework directly in this article, unlike the dissertation.

4. **Métraux 1940**: Cited for brushes/paint application (p. 138: "states that brushes were likely to have been made of feathers or pounded ends of sticks"), tapa images/tattoo comparison (p. 138), net-fishing device interpretation of cross-end piece (p. 145), ariki on platform (p. 146: "the ariki stood on a platform carried by eight men when addressing a group"), and ahu as "variation of the East Polynesian marae concept" (p. 134). [POST-RAID]

5. **Routledge 1919**: Cited for birdman cult description (p. 136), canoe-shaped burial cairns "ahu poe poe" (p. 146).  [POST-RAID]

6. **Thomson 1891**: Cited for birdman cult timing (p. 136), facia petroglyph illustration (p. 144: "510 fig. 19 for inaccurate impression of these designs"), canoe burial cases (p. 146). [POST-RAID]

7. **Hodder 1982**: Cited for definition of "symbol" (p. 133: "an object or situation in which a direct, primary or literal meaning also designates another indirect, secondary and figurative meaning," Hodder 1982:11).

8. **Stevenson 1986**: Cited twice. First as part of chronological references for statue-birdman transition (p. 136). Second, corrected for misidentifying ahu facia as "lintel" or "cornice" (p. 142: "incorrectly called 'lintel' or 'cornice' by Stevenson 1986: 73, 76").

12. **Gonzalez N., Van Tilburg & Vargas C. 1984**: Cited for cultural sequence (p. 136). This is the conference paper "The moai as socio-political feature."

**Bibliographic profile significance**: This article strips away the dissertation's theoretical infrastructure even more completely than Stevenson 1986 stripped Stevenson 1984. The post-processual framework (Miller & Tilley, Hodder, Tilley) that drove the dissertation's interpretive engine is reduced to a single Hodder citation for the definition of "symbol." The Polynesian comparative ethnography (Firth, Williamson, Handy) that supplied the social meanings is entirely absent. What remains is the interpretive conclusions without the theoretical apparatus that generated them.

## II. The "Symbolic Stratigraphy" Concept

14. **Central thesis**: "a 'symbolic stratigraphy', a temporal linkage between two ideologies, that of the statue cult and the birdman cult which overlapped and succeeded it in time" (p. 147).

15. **Statue cult temporal range**: "the statue cult functioned from at least A.D. 690 and probably earlier" (p. 134, citing Ayres 1973).

16. **Birdman cult emergence**: "An ideological shift to an ethnographically described manutara or birdman cult is first manifested archaeologically about A.D. 1500" (p. 136, citing Thomson 1891; Routledge 1919; Metraux 1940; McCoy 1973; Ayres 1973). [POST-RAID dependency for the ethnographic description]

17. **Birdman as in situ evolution**: "the birdman symbol evolved in situ" (p. 136, citing Lee 1986a). The article argues against the view that the birdman cult was "imported but definitely unrelated to the statue cult" (p. 136).

18. **Cults as variants on same theme**: "the statue and birdman cults are variants on the same cosmic theme" (p. 136).

19. **Non-linear transition**: "the non-linear nature of ideological transition from statue cult to birdman cult" (p. 136).

20. **Bird symbolism in statue design**: "documentation of bird symbolism inherent in anthropomorphic statue design" (p. 136, citing Van Tilburg 1986a).

## III. Petroglyph-on-Red-Scoria Interpretations

21. **Twenty-seven sites**: "A total of twenty-seven individual sites have been documented both within and outside the survey area which include both petroglyphs and statues" (p. 141). Twenty are ahu sites, one is Puna Pau quarry.

22. **Limited symbol range on red scoria**: "the repetitive presence of a limited range of petroglyph symbol types at six sites suggests purposeful, non-random, re-use of ahu and pukao quarry sites" (p. 141).

23. **Red scoria facia as late addition**: "The addition of red scoria facia to these sites was undertaken somewhat late in the Ahu Moai Phase" (p. 142). Applied only to "the largest and most elaborate of ceremonial centers" (p. 142).

24. **Petroglyphs post-date statue fall**: "Placement of the designs relative to the position of the pukao indicates that they were applied after the statues had fallen from ahu" (p. 142).

25. **Puna Pau pukao data**: "In Puna Pau quarry, twenty-two pukao are documented" (p. 143). Design range slightly more extensive than at ahu sites.

26. **Conformity across sites**: "The high degree of conformity between and among the designs at all six sites, however, is striking" (p. 143).

27. **Lunate forms as "boats"**: "The lunate petroglyph symbols described above are interpreted here as 'boats'" (p. 144). Identification based on form: "slight thickening at the base of the curve," "high ends, or ends with out-curving elements are typical of Polynesian sailing craft" (p. 144).

28. **Ahu Akahanga as key site**: "Ahu Akahanga (site 7-584) ('platform of the king') is a ceremonial complex located on the south coast and connected in legend with the death and burial of Hotu Matu'a, the founding ancestor" (p. 143).

## IV. Claims About Chiefdom/Power/Ideology

29. **Ahu/moai as male lineage ideology**: "the ahu/moai complex symbolized, both architecturally and iconographically, the dominance of an ideology defined as male, lineage-based and incorporating anthropomorphic and bird symbolism as understood within the Polynesian context" (p. 134, citing Van Tilburg 1986a:350).

30. **Statues as genealogical connection**: "The statues themselves probably represented the genealogical connection of the lineage with the founding ancestor" (p. 134, citing Van Tilburg 1986a:353).

31. **Statues as polysemic symbols**: "Statues were polysemic symbols utilized within a multi-dimensional social context, and were central to the functioning of a cult which centered upon ancestor worship, fertility and rites of passage ritual" (p. 134).

32. **Red color and chiefly power**: "the association of the color red (kura) with chiefly power and mana is well-established" (p. 142, citing Handy 1927; Burrows 1936; Firth 1967; Holdaway 1984; Van Tilburg 1986b). NOTE: This claim is framed as applying to "Polynesia as a whole" and then applied to Easter Island without island-specific verification.

33. **Pukao as shift in mana type**: "the superimposition of red scoria pukao upon some of the statues of the largest and most important lineage sites represents a shift in ideological emphasis from chiefly mana, which is inherent in ancestral proximity, to one of chiefly mana which is acquired and demonstrated through prowess" (p. 146, citing Van Tilburg 1986a:339; 1986c).

34. **Red scoria facia as status transfer**: "The superimposition of red scoria facia upon some of the same ahu sites allows for the lineage-specific transfer of increased status" (p. 146, citing Van Tilburg 1986c).

35. **Boat symbol as class/status marker**: "Considered within the Polynesian socio-political framework of differential access to important resources, the symbolic meaning of the boat may involve a class/status symbol (vaka vaero, the royal canoe, which was the only vessel allowed to take certain varieties of deep-sea fish)" (p. 146). [POST-RAID dependency: the vaka vaero claim depends on ethnographic accounts]

36. **Boat symbol and founding ancestor**: "The association of boats with journeying and settlement and thus with the founding ancestor and the Miru ramage is also implied" (p. 146).

37. **Boat symbol and death/burial**: "The association of boats/canoes with death and burial in Polynesia is well-established" (p. 146). Thomson (1891:474) reports two canoes used as burial cases. Routledge (1919:230): canoe-shaped burial cairns "ahu poe poe."

38. **Hare paenga as high-status dwelling**: "The hare paenga or boat-shaped house was a high-status dwelling located in close proximity to ceremonial sites" (p. 146).

39. **Ariki on boat-shaped platform**: "on Easter Island the ariki stood on a platform carried by eight men when addressing a group" (p. 146, citing Métraux 1940:391). [POST-RAID]

40. **"Boats" carry complex meanings**: The symbols "may serve to objectify underlying socio-ideological meanings which may include chiefly or priestly status; lineage or ramage affiliation; social and/or physical boundary definition (tapu); death and/or the power over death, including ritual associated with death or burial; inherent power or mana or an effort to negate (make noa) that power; healing or sacrificial ritual" (p. 147).

41. **Late ritual and secret knowledge**: "The effect of the interactive, multi-layered symbolism employed may be to partially define elements of late ritual activity associated with some aspect of the priesthood and secret esoteric knowledge" (p. 147).

## V. Claims That Extend or Differ from Van Tilburg 1986 Dissertation

42. **New data from Lee's petroglyph corpus**: The article integrates data from Lee's ~1,000 petroglyph sites with Van Tilburg's 745 statues for the first time. The 27-site overlap inventory (Table 1) and the specific petroglyph-on-red-scoria analysis (Table 2, 6 sites) are new data not in the dissertation.

43. **Birdman evolved in situ**: The claim that "the birdman symbol evolved in situ" (p. 136) is attributed to Lee 1986a. The dissertation discusses the transition but the in situ evolution claim is Lee's contribution.

44. **Puna Pau pukao petroglyph inventory**: Twenty-two pukao documented in Puna Pau quarry with petroglyphs (p. 143). This specific inventory appears new.

45. **Ahu Akahanga detailed description**: The article provides a detailed site-level description of petroglyphs at Ahu Akahanga (pp. 143-145, Fig. 3-6) that is more specific than the dissertation's treatment. Three platforms, seventeen statues, specific petroglyph panel locations (A-G), pukao with bird and birdman figures.

46. **Hoa Haka Nana Ia paint evidence**: "Hoa Haka Nana Ia, the basalt statue re-carved with late bas-relief petroglyphs and taken from Orongo, seems to have been at least partially painted in red and white pigment at the time of its removal from the island" (p. 139, citing Palmer 1868:177). This specific paint evidence is not prominent in the dissertation.

47. **Gypsum identification**: White substance on Tongariki moai and north coast petroglyph "proved to be gypsum" (p. 141, S. Williams personal communication 1984). Specific analytical finding.

48. **Painting of statues uncertain**: "it is not possible to state definitively whether or not the sculptures when raised upon ahu may have been periodically painted, perhaps during certain rituals" (p. 141). Cautious assessment.

49. **Cupules as ancient symbol type**: "These simple, circular pits have been produced by non-literate peoples since the Palaeolithic and may be one of the earliest symbols made by man" (p. 138, citing Hadingham 1979:58). Broader comparative framing of cupules not in the dissertation.

50. **La Pérouse illustration**: Discussion of La Pérouse illustration of "twelve seemingly anthropomorphic figures carved on what may be the facia" of an unnamed ahu (p. 142, via Heyerdahl and Ferdon 1961:62 fig. 7b). These "either are inaccurately represented, never existed or have been lost due to subsequent site damage" (p. 142).

51. **Goldman's "Open" classification not mentioned**: Unlike the dissertation (p. 343), this article does not cite or discuss Goldman 1970's classification of Easter Island as an "Open" society. Goldman is entirely absent from the bibliography.

52. **Moai-as-paramount-chief hypothesis not present**: The dissertation's strongest chiefdom claim (statues may represent ariki mau, "erected at his behest," pp. 348-349) does not appear in this article. The interpretation stays at the lineage level: statues represent "the genealogical connection of the lineage with the founding ancestor" (p. 134).

53. **No Miller & Tilley framework**: The dissertation's theoretical engine (ideology as ruling-class misrepresentation) is entirely absent. The article uses Hodder's simpler semiotic framework ("symbol" = literal + figurative meaning) rather than the critical Marxist model.

## VI. Statue Typology and Chronology (Data Claims)

54. **Nine statue types defined**: "Statistical analysis of statue attribute data has defined nine types of form" (p. 136, citing Van Tilburg 1986a).

55. **Earliest ahu**: "The earliest dated ahu structure is Tahai I (A.D. 690)" (p. 136, citing Ayres 1973:11; Van Tilburg 1986a:467).

56. **Type 1 dominance**: "Type 1 figures are the most numerous on the island, and constitute 73.8% of the total sample" (p. 136). Size range 1.51-9.94 m.

57. **Type 1 conventionalized by AD 1000**: "The conventionalization of the statue form to that of Type 1 (rectangular form of head and body) was accomplished at least by A.D. 1000 at Vinapu and probably earlier" (p. 136).

58. **Post-AD 1500 re-use**: "a significant number of them are associated with late (post A.D. 1500) ritual activity defined by re-positioning, re-carving and the application of petroglyphs and cupules" (p. 137, citing Van Tilburg 1986a:175).

59. **Rano Raraku quarry**: "93% of the statue corpus was quarried" from Rano Raraku (p. 136, citing Cristino et al. 1981; Van Tilburg 1986a:275).

60. **Pukao as late feature**: "Pukao are generally considered a late feature, and one may have been superimposed upon the statue at Ahu Ko Te Riku sometime around A.D. 1100-1300" (p. 134).

61. **Selective statue damage**: "Selective damage done to statues in the Marquesas was considered to be a deliberate effort to destroy the figure's 'power,' localized in a particular part of the statue" (p. 137, citing Linton 1925:86). Applied by analogy to Easter Island.

62. **Re-use in architecture**: "Re-use of statues in architecture dates to Tahai II and was probably practised earlier. Some 4% of the total statue data base...was incorporated into ahu construction" (p. 137).

63. **Mana transfer in temple rebuilding**: "The purpose was to ensure a transference of mana (supernatural power or efficacy, Kirch 1984:288) based upon genealogical continuity from the older structure to the newer" (p. 137, citing Handy 1927:181, 191). Polynesian analogy applied to Easter Island without island-specific evidence.

## VII. Methodological Notes

64. **Data integration goal**: The article explicitly aims "to integrate, rather than fragment, the enormous corpus of survey-generated data" (p. 133). Seeks "a symbolically meaningful context for the analysis of two site types (statues and petroglyphs)."

65. **Combined fieldwork**: "a cumulative time of more than 30 months in the field has resulted in a data base which includes 745 statues and nearly 1,000 petroglyph sites" (p. 133).

66. **Rock art temporal difficulty acknowledged**: "Establishing temporal relationships is one of the most difficult tasks of rock art research" (p. 137).

67. **Superimposition as dating tool**: Temporal sequencing relies on superimposition relationships and manufacturing technique differences (incised vs. pecked vs. abraded vs. bas-relief, pp. 137-138).

68. **Post-fall petroglyph placement**: "Placement of the designs relative to the position of the pukao indicates that they were applied after the statues had fallen from ahu" (p. 142). This is a firm relative dating observation.

69. **Poor carving quality noted**: "the symbols are poor examples of the carver's art. They are in no instance equal to the level of expertise illustrated on numerous sites elsewhere" (p. 145). Contrasts with "complex meanings" interpretation.

70. **Boat identification hedged**: "It is also possible that the circular elements on some of the end extensions may suggest carved 'figureheads'" (p. 145). "Simple, curved half circles which lack the thickening at the base of the curve are problematical" (p. 145).

## VIII. Assessment for Chiefdom Attribution Argument

The article is a moderately useful source for the chiefdom attribution argument. It documents the published version of key Van Tilburg 1986 dissertation claims, making them citable in the journal literature. However:

1. **The chiefdom framework operates more subtly here than in the dissertation.** Instead of Miller & Tilley's ideology-as-ruling-class-legitimation, the article uses Hodder's semiotic framework and presents interpretive claims as archaeological observations.

2. **The strongest dissertation claims are absent.** No moai-as-paramount-chief hypothesis. No explicit "chiefdom" terminology. No Goldman engagement. The interpretation stays at the lineage level.

3. **The "chiefly mana" framing persists without explicit chiefdom labeling.** Claims about "chiefly power and mana" (p. 142), "chiefly mana" (p. 146), "class/status symbol" (p. 146), and "differential access to important resources" (p. 146) embed the chiefdom interpretation without naming it.

4. **Polynesian analogy is the mechanism.** Every interpretive claim about symbolic meaning is imported from Polynesian parallels (Marquesas statue damage, Polynesian canoe symbolism, East Polynesian temple rebuilding, Polynesian color symbolism), not derived from Easter Island evidence.

5. **Post-slave-raid dependency remains.** Political organization claims rest on Métraux 1940, Thomson 1891, and Routledge 1919. The vaka vaero, ariki-on-platform, and Hotu Matu'a claims all derive from post-1862 testimony.

6. **New data contribution (Lee's petroglyph corpus) is valuable.** The 27-site overlap inventory, the red-scoria-specific analysis, and the detailed Ahu Akahanga description are genuine data contributions independent of interpretive framework.
