# Claims Extraction: Mulloy & Figueroa 1966

**Source**: Mulloy, William T., and Gonzalo Figueroa G. 1966. "The Archaeological Heritage of Rapa Nui (Easter Island): Its Character, Description, and Conservation." Report prepared for UNESCO, Contract No. CLT-0768. 111 pp.

**Type**: UNESCO conservation and documentation report; comprehensive survey of archaeological monuments with proposals for investigation, conservation, and restoration

**Date processed**: 2026-03-16

---

## I. Settlement Pattern and Social Organization

### A. Kin-Group Settlement

1. "The distribution of the examples of this architecture suggest that at this time the people were scattered widely along the coasts and adjacent interior areas of the island in relatively small independent or partially independent kin groups of some sort. Local traditions which have survived, suggest this sort of arrangement." (pp. 12-13)

   **Note**: Settlement described as dispersed, coast-to-interior, organized by small kin groups. No mention of centralized authority, territorial hierarchy, or chiefly command. The qualifier "of some sort" indicates uncertainty about the exact nature of these groups.

2. "Each of these groups possessed its own religious structure or structures which were variants of a widely diffused kind of Polynesian marae locally called ahu." (pp. 13-14)

3. "Typically inland of the shoreside ahu were the domestic establishments of other members of the kin group which sometimes extended a considerable distance into the interior." (pp. 14-15)

4. "Thus the typical settlement pattern of a group appears to have included a segment of coast and its adjacent inland zone." (p. 15)

5. Settlement pattern description: "one or more ahu near the coast. To landward of each ahu is a large plaza. To landward of the plazas are series of boat shaped houses, traditionally said to have been occupied by a priesthood. Farther toward the interior, are found isolated domestic establishments in gradually diminishing numbers." (pp. 34-35)

   **Note**: Settlement described as coast-oriented with ahu, plaza, hare paenga, and then scattered domestic sites inland. No mention of chiefly compounds, elite residences, or administrative centers.

### B. No Political Hierarchy Described

6. No terms of political hierarchy appear in the entire 111-page document. The words "chief," "chiefdom," "hierarchy," "rank," "stratified," "paramount," "redistribution," and "tribute" do not appear in the text.

   **Note**: This is a systematic absence across a comprehensive 111-page report on the archaeological heritage of the island. The report describes settlement, ahu, domestic structures, caves, petroglyphs, and Orongo without any reference to chiefly authority or hierarchical political organization.

7. Service (1962), Sahlins (1954/1958), and Goldman (1970) are not cited. The theoretical framework is empirical/descriptive, not neoevolutionary.

## II. Ahu Description and Variation

### A. Ahu Variation as Kin-Group Expression

8. "Within the framework of this general plan, the ahu ruins vary greatly in size and individual characteristics and typically complicated sequences of rebuilding are present in which characteristics have been changed and innovations added." (p. 15)

9. "Many of these individualized characteristics undoubtedly have chronological significance. Others may have resulted from individual preferences of kin groups and a sense of competition among them." (pp. 15-16)

   **Note**: Ahu variation attributed to two factors: chronological change AND "individual preferences of kin groups and a sense of competition among them." Competition is between kin groups, not directed by centralized authority. This is the passage Kirch 1984:270-271 quotes when discussing decentralized production.

10. "It is likely that the size and ostentation of features of many ahu reflected the numbers and capacity of the kin groups that built them." (p. 16)

    **Note**: Ahu size explicitly attributed to the "numbers and capacity" of kin groups, not to chiefly power or centralized command. This is a kin-group capacity model, not a political authority model.

11. "Rebuilding in many cases may have resulted from increased capacity of a kin group and a desire for a more impressive structure or structures." (p. 16)

### B. Ahu Types and Scale

12. "In all there are probably about 350 ahu on the island." (p. 37)

13. "Probably about 150 have from one to fifteen now-fallen statues." (p. 37)

14. "Some are remarkable for their size (platforms over 150 M. long), others for the number or size of statues (one about ten meters high), others for their height and the size of the stones in their platform walls, and still others for the precision of the stone fitting in their masonry." (pp. 15-16)

15. Ahu described as "a long, narrow dry masonry platform with a sloping ramp on one side which might or might not have a slightly elevated central platform and lateral wings. Facing the ramp was frequently an artificially flattened plaza beyond which was an irregular row of long narrow boat shaped houses of thatch with precisely fitted stone foundations." (p. 14)

16. "Most of the remains of these ahu are located along the shores of the island with plazas on the inland side, while some ahu occupy inland locations." (p. 14)

### C. Statues and Ancestor Veneration

17. "Traditionally, these statues are said to have represented ancestors and to have possessed supernatural power." (p. 15)

18. "In general, through time, they increased in size and stylization until a highly uniform type developed." (p. 15)

## III. Moai Production and Transport

### A. Quarry Operations

19. "Though some statues were carved from stone obtained in a variety of locations, it appears that early in the statue building period, a single source became highly dominant. This was the southwest exterior and interior slopes of the volcanic cone of Rano Raraku." (pp. 15-16)

20. "Here intensive quarry operations were carried out with statues being almost completely carved while still attached to the living tuff." (p. 16)

21. "When detached, they were slid down the slopes of the volcano on their backs and transported to the ahu by means that can be speculated upon but are not known." (pp. 16-17)

22. "Traditions state that the statues walked to their ahu locations." (p. 17)

23. "Many statues remain partly finished in the quarry and examples may be seen in every stage of construction." (p. 17)

   **Note**: No attribution of quarry production to centralized command. No mention of labor organization, chiefly oversight, or administrative coordination of quarry work. Transport mechanism described as unknown. The walking tradition is noted without interpretation.

## IV. Orongo Ceremony

### A. Competitive, Nonhereditary, Annual

24. "The village, according to tradition, was occupied in prehistoric times for only a short period each year during a part of the month of September by representatives of the various lineage groups which occupied the various parts of the island." (p. 52)

   **Note**: Orongo ceremony described as (a) periodic, not permanent, (b) attended by representatives of lineage groups, and (c) competitive. No mention of centralized authority, chiefly administration, or permanent political office associated with the ceremony.

25. "A ceremony was held which involved, among other things, a race in which the contestants descended the cliff to the sea and swam to the islet of Motu Nui some 1400 meters away. The first to return to the village with an egg of the manutara (Sooty Tern) was declared winner and his sponsor then occupied during the following year a position of special ceremonial importance." (pp. 52-53)

   **Note**: The tangata manu position is described as (a) won through competition (not inherited), (b) lasting one year (not permanent), and (c) of "special ceremonial importance" (not political power). This is consistent with Routledge's (1919) and Métraux's (1940) characterizations. No chiefdom-related terminology.

## V. Cultural Chronology

### A. Environmental Narrative

26. "It seems likely that this architectural and artistic activity reached its greatest intensity at a time shortly before historic contact when the culture as a whole reached its optimum population and greatest economic productivity." (pp. 16-17)

27. "Such an effective equilibrium rarely lasts long and it seems likely that this one began to be destroyed shortly before the beginning of historic times by over-population resulting in a scarcity of food and eventually fomenting internal warfare." (p. 17)

28. "One of the most characteristic goals of [warfare] was the overthrowing of the statues of enemy groups and the destruction of their ahu." (p. 17)

   **Note**: The pre-contact decline narrative (population peak -> resource scarcity -> warfare -> statue toppling) is present in Mulloy & Figueroa 1966, predating Kirch 1984 by 18 years. However, it is framed as warfare between kin groups ("enemy groups"), not as chiefdom collapse. Compare with Mulloy 1979, Claim 18, which proposes ahu destruction may have been by "elements of the societies of their owners" (internal, not external).

### B. Population and Early Chronology

29. "The earliest radio-carbon date of unquestioned reliability is that from Ahu No. 2 at Vinapu and is 857 A.D. ± 200." (p. 10)

30. "An earlier date from the Poike Ditch of 380 A.D. ± 100 has been seriously questioned and opinion must be reserved on it." (p. 10)

31. "A probably small group, possibly reenforced by other arrivals from time to time, did appear on the island before the date indicated and gradually increased in numbers until they included several thousand people at the time of their first historic contact in 1722." (p. 11)

### C. Slave Raids and Knowledge Loss

32. "The most important raids were by Peruvians between 1859 and 1862 when about a thousand islanders were carried off to work on the guano islands off the coast of Peru." (p. 18)

33. "The dead appear to have included all of the maori rongo rongo, or the local priesthood and as a result much important religious and other esoteric information, including the technique of reading the Rapa Nui written language, was lost." (p. 18)

   **Note**: Mulloy & Figueroa recognize the catastrophic knowledge loss from the slave raids, confirming the post-raid source problem.

## VIII. Terminology Audit

**Terms present**: "kin groups," "lineage groups," "priesthood," "religious building compulsion," "competition," "independent or partially independent," "class" (used only for monument categories, NOT social classes)

**Terms absent**: "chiefdom," "chief" (as political term), "hierarchy," "rank," "stratified," "redistribution," "tribute," "paramount," "Service," "Sahlins," "Goldman," "ramage," "big man," "matatoa," "ariki"

**Significance**: A comprehensive 111-page UNESCO report on the archaeological heritage of Easter Island, written by the two most experienced field archaeologists on the island, contains no chiefdom-related terminology. Political organization is described entirely through kin-group frameworks. The absence of "ariki" and "matatoa" is notable: this is a report focused on archaeological heritage, not political organization, but the complete absence of political hierarchy vocabulary is consistent with the pre-chiefdom-model baseline.
