# Claims Extraction: Thompson 1891

## Source Information

**Author**: Paymaster William J. Thomson, U.S. Navy

**Period on Easter Island**: December 19-31, 1886 (12 days). Thomson was aboard the USS Mohican; expeditions were organized to survey the island, excavate platforms, and collect traditions.

**Publication**: *Te Pito te Henua, or Easter Island*, published in the Report of the National Museum, 1888-'89, pages 447-552 (with plates XII-LX). Washington: Government Printing Office, 1891.

**PDF file**: `docs/references/pdfs/post_slave_raid/thompson_1891/Thompson_1891.pdf`

**Context**: Thomson visited Easter Island approximately 24 years after the 1862-1863 Peruvian slave raids. The population had collapsed from roughly 3,000 to a nadir of ~111 (1877) and had partially recovered to 155 at the time of his visit. Mr. Alexander P. Salmon, a long-term foreign resident married into the Tahitian royal family, served as translator and primary intermediary. Much of the ethnographic content was collected during evening sessions at Salmon's residence at Vaihu, with traditions dictated by informants and translated by Salmon. The old man Ure Vaeiko, who claimed to understand the rongorongo tablets, was coerced into cooperating through a combination of alcohol and subterfuge (pp. 514-515). Thomson also obtained traditions from Kaitae, described as "nearest descendant of the last king" (p. 461).

**[POST-RAID]**: ALL claims in this file derive from a source recorded ~24 years after the 1862-1863 slave raids. The population at the time of Thomson's visit was 155, having recovered from a bottleneck of approximately 111 in 1877. The slave raids "carried away" all of "the officials and persons in authority" (p. 514). Thomson's informants were survivors and descendants of survivors of this catastrophe, and the traditions they reported had passed through the demographic bottleneck.

---

## Analytical Significance

Thomson's account is the most extensive 19th-century treatment of Easter Island, combining direct observation of archaeological remains with an extended collection of traditions, legends, king lists, and tablet translations. It has been heavily cited in subsequent literature. For the chiefdom attribution, the critical sections are: (1) the "Government" section (p. 472), which describes an "arbitrary monarchy"; (2) the king list of 57 names (pp. 533-534), the longest recorded; (3) the tangata manu description (pp. 482-483); (4) the origin tradition with land division among six sons (pp. 527-528); and (5) the Vinapu tradition describing clan warfare triggered by insult at a feast (p. 512).

Thomson's account is significant for the paper because it represents the fullest expression of the "hereditary monarchy" narrative that was later absorbed into the chiefdom model. However, it also contains internal contradictions and important qualifications. The "Government" section simultaneously claims hereditary monarchical authority AND states that "no tax was exacted of the people" and that "it does not appear that any great homage was paid the king" (p. 473). The king list of 57 names is far longer than Roussel's 22 generations and disagrees substantially in both names and order, undermining both lists' reliability.

## Claims Extraction

### 1. Government and Political Authority

**1.1. "Arbitrary monarchy"** [POST-RAID]

"The ancient government of Easter Island was an arbitrary monarchy. The supreme authority was vested in a king and was hereditary in his family. The person of the king was held sacred. Clan fights and internecine struggles were common, but the royal person and family were unmolested. The king reigned over the entire island and was not disturbed by the defeat or the victory of any of the clans." (p. 472)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Thomson is relaying what informants told him about the "ancient government." He did not observe a functioning monarchy. The phrase "arbitrary monarchy" is Thomson's characterization, applying a European political category. The description of the king as immune from clan warfare echoes Roussel's similar claim (1.6 in Roussel claims).

**1.2. Chiefs of districts** [POST-RAID]

"The island was divided into districts having distinct names and governed by chiefs, all of whom acknowledged the supremacy of the king. The title of chief was also hereditary, and descended from father to son, but the king reserved the right to remove or put to death any of them and of naming a successor from the people of the clan." (p. 472)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This describes a hierarchical system with a paramount king and subordinate district chiefs. The claimed royal prerogative to remove chiefs and name successors implies centralized political control. No evidence is provided for this system actually having functioned. The claim that the king could "put to death" district chiefs and name replacements is the strongest assertion of centralized authority in any post-raid source.

**1.3. No confederation** [POST-RAID]

"There was no confederation, each clan being independent of all the rest, except as the powerful are naturally dominant over the weak." (p. 472)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This passage contradicts the picture of centralized monarchy in 1.1. If each clan was "independent of all the rest," the king's authority was nominal at best. The qualifier "except as the powerful are naturally dominant over the weak" describes personal dominance, not institutional hierarchy.

**1.4. Chiefs' insignia** [POST-RAID]

"The chiefs wore peculiar feather hats to denote their rank, and they presided at feasts and councils in the absence of the king." (p. 472)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The feather hats are consistent with Polynesian markers of rank. Chiefs presiding "in the absence of the king" implies the king was the normal presider when present.

**1.5. No tax, no great homage** [POST-RAID]

"It does not appear that any great homage was paid the king, and no tax was exacted of the people." (p. 473)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Author's inference.** THIS IS THE MOST ANALYTICALLY IMPORTANT QUALIFICATION IN THOMSON'S ACCOUNT. A monarchical system without taxation and without "great homage" is a monarchy in name only. Chiefdom models typically require tribute or redistribution as a defining feature of centralized authority. Thomson's own description negates the structural requirements of a chiefdom: no tax, no homage, clans independent of each other.

**1.6. Disputes settled by king or chief** [POST-RAID]

"Personal security and the rights of private property were little regarded, and disputes were settled by king or chief without regard to law or justice. There was no code of laws, the people avenged their own injuries, and persons who incurred the displeasure of the ruler were marked as victims for sacrifice." (p. 473)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This passage contains contradictory elements: disputes were settled by the king or chief, yet "the people avenged their own injuries." If people settled their own disputes through personal vengeance, the king's judicial role was minimal. The claim about human sacrifice for those who "incurred the displeasure of the ruler" has no supporting evidence in Thomson's account or in other sources.

**1.7. Post-raid collapse of authority** [POST-RAID]

"Maurata, the last of a long line of kings, together with all of the principal chiefs of the islands was kidnapped by the Peruvians and died in slavery. Since that time there has been no recognized authority among the natives; every man is his own master, and looks out for his own interests." (p. 473)

**(a) Direct observation** (regarding the absence of authority in 1886) combined with **(b) reported tradition** (regarding Maurata's kidnapping). The statement that "every man is his own master" describes what Thomson actually observed. Whether this represented a post-raid collapse of functioning authority or merely the removal of a nominal figurehead cannot be determined.

**1.8. Kaitae as nearest descendant of the last king** [POST-RAID]

"The last king was kidnaped by the Peruvians and died in captivity, but his nearest descendant is a sturdy old fellow called Kaitae, about eighty years of age." (p. 461)

**(a) Direct observation** (of Kaitae) combined with **(b) reported tradition** (of his genealogical relationship to the last king). Kaitae held no political authority; his significance was genealogical only.

### 2. King List and Succession

**2.1. Genealogy of 57 kings** [POST-RAID]

Thomson records a king list of 57 names from Hotu-Matua to Maurata (pp. 533-534). The list is explicitly presented as tradition: "Hotu-Matua, driven from his kingdom to the eastward by the rebellion of his subjects, landed with a chosen band of followers at Easter Islands."

**(b) Reported tradition.** This is the longest king list recorded from any source. Roussel's 1869 list has 22 generations (23 names). The enormous discrepancy between 57 and 22 generations for the same supposed dynasty is a fundamental problem for treating either list as historical record. As noted in Altman's endnotes on Roussel: "Twelve of the 23 names on Roussel's list are included in Thomson's list; Roussel and Jaussen have nine names in common. Few are given in the same order" (Roussel claims, endnote 7).

**Critical assessment**: If the dynasty had 57 kings (Thomson) or 22 (Roussel), one or both lists is substantially incorrect. At approximately 20-25 years per generation, 57 kings would span 1,140-1,425 years, while 22 generations would span 440-550 years. The lists are irreconcilable in both length and content, and both were recorded from post-catastrophe informants by outsiders working through translators.

**2.2. Succession by eldest son** [POST-RAID]

"The king nominated his eldest son as his successor (Tuumae-Heke), and it was ordained that the descent of the kings should always be through the eldest son." (p. 527)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This describes primogeniture, consistent with some Polynesian succession systems. However, Roussel's account describes a different succession rule: abdication when the son married (Roussel claims 2.2). The disagreement between Thomson and Roussel on the basic mechanism of succession further undermines the reliability of both.

**2.3. Division of land among six sons** [POST-RAID]

Thomson records that Hotu-Matua divided the island into six districts, one for each of his sons: Tuumae-Heke (Anekena to Mounga Tea-tea), Meru (Anekena to Hane toa), Marania (Akahanga to Vinapu), Raa (Hanga-Toe), Korona-ronga (Anekena to Rana-Roraku), and Hotu-iti (east side) (pp. 527-528).

**(b) Reported tradition.** This origin narrative for territorial divisions has been influential in later interpretations of mata/clan organization. The six-district system is presented as originating from the founding king, naturalizing territorial political units as hereditary possessions. This is precisely the kind of origin charter that legitimizes existing arrangements rather than recording historical events.

**2.4. Two co-reigning kings** [POST-RAID]

Thomson's footnote at the 36th and 37th kings states: "These two kings reigned at the same time. The son rebelled against his father, and finally killed him." (p. 534)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This is the only acknowledgment in the king list of contested succession. A single instance of rebellion in 57 reigns implies extraordinary stability; more likely, the list omits contested or parallel claims.

**2.5. Tuu Koiho and the first image** [POST-RAID]

"Mahuta Ariiki had a son named Tuu-Koiho, who made the first stone image on the Island. This son died before his father." (p. 534, footnote)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This attributes the origin of moai-making to a member of the royal line, linking monument production to chiefly authority. This is the specific claim that connects moai production to the political hierarchy; it has no independent archaeological support.

### 3. Tangata Manu (Bird-Egg Ceremony)

**3.1. The bird-egg festival** [POST-RAID]

"According to the ancient custom, the fortunate individual who obtained possession of the first egg and returned with it unbroken to the expectant crowd, became entitled to certain privileges and rights during the following year. No especial authority was vested in him, but it was supposed that he had won the approval of the great spirit 'Meke Meke' and was entitled to receive contributions of food and other considerations from his fellows." (pp. 482-483)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Thomson's description of the tangata manu explicitly states "No especial authority was vested in him." The winner received food contributions and spiritual approval, not political power. This contradicts interpretations that treat the tangata manu as a mechanism for transferring chiefly authority. Compare with Roussel's description, where the birdman took the name "matatoa" and exercised "the chief's authority" for one year (Roussel claims 4.4). The two accounts disagree on whether the winner gained political authority.

**3.2. The king's role in the ceremony** [POST-RAID]

"The race for the distinguished honor of bearing off the first egg was an occasion of intense excitement. The contestants were held in check at Orongo until the auspicious moment arrived, and the scramble commenced at the word 'go,' pronounced by the king, who was about the only able-bodied man on the island who did not participate." (p. 483)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The king's role was to start the race; he did not compete. This is consistent with a ritual/ceremonial role for the ariki rather than political authority. The king authorized the start of the competition but was excluded from the contest that determined who received "contributions of food."

### 4. Clans and Warfare

**4.1. Numerous and powerful clans** [POST-RAID]

"all agree in the statement that the different districts were peopled by numerous and powerful clans who were constantly at war with each other." (p. 460)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The emphasis on clan independence and constant warfare is difficult to reconcile with the centralized monarchy described in the "Government" section. An effective paramount king would presumably be able to prevent or mediate between "constantly" warring clans.

**4.2. No professional fighters** [POST-RAID]

"There was no class of professional fighters or soldiers; every able-bodied man was supposed to be a warrior and compelled to do duty in time of war. Fighting men were not trained or drilled." (pp. 475-476)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The absence of a warrior class is significant. Chiefdom models often include specialized military roles supporting chiefly authority. Thomson describes a universal military obligation with no specialization.

**4.3. Clans led by chief** [POST-RAID]

"The clans were always led to battle by the chief, but there was no particular formation. Every man acted in accordance with his individual fancy, or as occasion demanded, relying upon skill and strength alone." (p. 476)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Chiefs led in battle but exercised no tactical command. This describes a war leader, not a political administrator.

**4.4. Clan warfare despite single king** [POST-RAID]

"In view of the fact that the islanders all acknowledged the authority of one king, their wars were surprisingly numerous, barbarous, and unrelenting." (p. 476)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Author's inference.** Thomson himself notes the paradox: a single king, yet constant warfare. If the king had effective authority, the perpetual warfare would be inexplicable. Thomson calls it "surprising," which is an understatement; it is contradictory.

**4.5. Vinapu feast tradition** [POST-RAID]

"The wife of the chief was of the Tongariki clan and during the ceremonies this 'lady' was slighted in the division of 'long pig,' but whether intentionally or otherwise does not appear. ... The insulted individual immediately sought the protection of her own clan, who arose en masse to vindicate the Tongariki honor. Long and bloody wars followed. Image-builders and platform-makers were drawn into the conflict from all parts of the island and, in a spirit of revenge, platforms were destroyed and images thrown down whenever opportunity offered." (p. 512)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This tradition attributes the destruction of platforms and images to inter-clan warfare triggered by a ritual insult. The image-builders and platform-makers are described as a distinct group "drawn into the conflict," consistent with specialized craft production. The tradition does not describe chiefly command over production; it describes artisans caught up in factional warfare.

**4.6. Hotu-iti clan wars** [POST-RAID]

"Kaina, the chief of the Hotu-iti clan, and a descendant of the sixth son of the first king, proved himself a valiant warrior, and his possessions were increased by encroachments upon the domain of his neighbors. He died and was succeeded by his son, Huriavai." (p. 529)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This passage describes a clan chief expanding territory through warfare, not through royal prerogative. The "king" is absent from this account of territorial politics. Chiefly power here is military, personal, and local.

### 5. Land, Property, and Economy

**5.1. Land division at founding** [POST-RAID]

See 2.3 above. The island divided into six districts by Hotu-Matua for his sons.

**5.2. No tax** [POST-RAID]

"no tax was exacted of the people" (p. 473). See 1.5 above.

**5.3. Image-makers exempt from other work** [POST-RAID]

"In the time of Atua Ure Rangi, the seventeenth king, the image-makers were exempt from all other kinds of work, and the fishermen were taxed for their chief support." (p. 533)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This is the only passage in Thomson suggesting a taxation system, and it specifically taxes fishermen to support image-makers, not a general tribute to the king. The contradiction with 1.5 ("no tax was exacted of the people") is notable. If read together, the two passages suggest that image production was supported by a specific work arrangement between craft specialists and food producers, not by a general redistributive economy.

**5.4. Rongorongo knowledge restricted** [POST-RAID]

"A knowledge of the written characters was confined to the royal family, the chiefs of the six districts into which the island was divided, sons of those chiefs, and certain priests or teachers, but the people were assembled at Anekena Bay once each year to hear all of the tablets read." (p. 514)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This describes restricted literacy as a marker of elite status, with an annual public reading. If accurate, it suggests a political and religious elite with specialized knowledge. However, Thomson also notes that Ure Vaeiko, who claimed this knowledge, could not actually read the tablets (see 7.1).

### 6. Thieving and Social Control

**6.1. Community enforcement of property rights** [POST-RAID]

"Retaliation for theft could be enforced by the weak and feeble against the strong and powerful, and any resistance would call to their aid the entire community." (p. 465)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) direct observation.** This describes a collective enforcement mechanism, not one mediated by chiefly authority. The community as a whole enforces property rights, and the weak can mobilize collective action against the strong. This is evidence for collective action, not hierarchical authority.

**6.2. God of thieving** [POST-RAID]

"They had a god of thieving, and successful operations were believed to be accomplished under his patronage." (p. 465)

**(b) Reported tradition.** A deity that sanctions theft is inconsistent with a strong centralized authority that would have an interest in protecting property.

### 7. Significant Absences and Qualifications

**7.1. Ure Vaeiko could not read the tablets** [POST-RAID]

"Ure Vaeiko's fluent interpretation of the tablet was not interrupted, though it became evident that he was not actually reading the characters. It was noticed that the shifting of position did not accord with the number of symbols on the lines, and afterwards when the photograph of another tablet was substituted, the same story was continued without the change being discovered." (p. 516)

**(a) Direct observation.** Thomson directly observed that Ure Vaeiko could not read the tablets. The old man recited memorized traditions associated with named tablets but could not identify individual characters. This demonstrates that by 1886, the knowledge system that the traditions describe (restricted literacy among the elite) had been completely destroyed.

**7.2. Thomson's method and its limitations**

Thomson's traditions were collected primarily through Mr. Salmon as translator during evening sessions. The sessions with Ure Vaeiko involved providing the elderly man with alcohol "as the night grew old and the narrator weary" (p. 515). Thomson spent 12 days on the island, with most time devoted to archaeological survey and excavation. The tradition-collection was fitted into evenings. This is not systematic ethnography; it is opportunistic recording under time pressure.

**7.3. Population at time of visit**

"Mr. Salmon took a complete census of the people just before the arrival of the Mohican, and we were furnished with a list containing the names of every man, woman, and child on the island. The total number of natives is at present 155. Of these 68 are men, 43 women, 17 boys under fifteen years of age, and 27 girls of corresponding age." (p. 461)

**(a) Direct observation.** The population of 155 had recovered from a nadir of approximately 111 in 1877. These 155 people were the entire community from which Thomson's informants were drawn; they were the survivors and descendants of survivors of a demographic catastrophe that had reduced the population by approximately 96%.

**7.4. Images as effigies of chiefs**

"it is pretty well established that they were intended as effigies of chiefs or distinguished persons." (p. 508)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Author's inference.** Thomson treats this as settled, but provides no evidence beyond informant statements. The claim that moai represent specific chiefs has been influential in chiefdom attributions, linking monumental production to political hierarchy. However, the interpretation is circular: if moai are assumed to represent chiefs, their scale is then taken as evidence of chiefly power.

### 8. European Categories Imposed

**8.1. "Arbitrary monarchy"** The term "arbitrary monarchy" (p. 472) is a European political category applied by Thomson. There is no Rapanui equivalent. The description that follows systematically undermines what "monarchy" implies: no tax, no great homage, independent clans, people avenging their own injuries.

**8.2. "King" vs. "chief"** Thomson uses "king" for the ariki mau and "chief" for district leaders, imposing a hierarchical distinction that may not reflect indigenous categories. The Rapanui term ariki appears in the king list (Mahuta Ariiki) and in the artifact catalogue (the club called "Ariiki" was "the baton of the king and used only by him," p. 535).

**8.3. Projection of European governmental structure** Thomson's "Government" section (p. 472) imposes European categories: monarchy, districts, hereditary succession, right to appoint and remove, sacrifice of the disloyal. This reads as a European model of feudal governance projected onto reported traditions about a society that Thomson himself acknowledges lacked taxation, regular homage, and effective control over perpetually warring clans.

### 9. Speculative/Uncertain Claims

**9.1. Origin from the east** [POST-RAID]

The tradition that Hotu-Matua came from "the land in the direction of the rising sun" (p. 526) contradicts the standard model of Polynesian migration from the west. Thomson himself notes: "It is difficult to account for the statement, so frequently repeated throughout the legends, that Hotu-Matua came from the eastward and discovered the land by steering towards the setting sun, because the chart shows no islands in that direction which would answer the description" (p. 532).

**9.2. Long-eared race** [POST-RAID]

"The suddenness with which the tradition jumps into the warfare between the descendants of the first king and the 'long-eared race' is startling, because no previous reference has been made to such a race on the island." (p. 532)

**(c) Author's observation.** Thomson himself identifies this narrative discontinuity. The "long-eared" tradition may represent a memory of conflict with a different population, or a mythological charter for the destruction of earlier monuments.

**9.3. Cannibalism** [POST-RAID]

"Several of the older natives acknowledge that they had frequently eaten human flesh in their youth." (p. 472)

**(a) Direct testimony** from living informants about their own past practices. This is among the most direct evidence in Thomson's account, though its reliability depends on the accuracy of Salmon's translation and the informants' motivations.
