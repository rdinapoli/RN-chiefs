- **Date of visit**: March 13-16, 1774
- **Duration on island**: 4 days (landed March 14, departed March 16)
- **Context of visit**: Cook's Second Voyage (1772-1775) aboard HMS *Resolution*. Cook was searching for the Southern Continent. The crew was in poor condition after months at sea with scurvy, rheumatism, and cholera. By this point in the voyage, Cook had extensive experience with Polynesian societies including Tahiti (two visits), the Society Islands, Friendly Islands (Tonga), New Zealand, and the Marquesas. The naturalists Johann Reinhold Forster and his son Georg Forster accompanied the expedition. The Tahitian man Mahine also traveled with them and served as a linguistic intermediary.

## Note on Sources

Cook's own narrative of the Easter Island visit is now directly available from the 1846 compiled edition (*The Voyages of Captain James Cook*, Vol. I, London: William Smith, MDCCCXLVI). The Google Books scan (Princeton University Library copy) had no embedded OCR; pages 477-495 (PDF numbering) were OCR'd locally at 300 DPI. The Easter Island material occupies Chapter VII (book pp. 448-452) and Chapter VIII (book pp. 452-455). OCR quality is high with only minor garbles. Page references to Cook 1846 use the book's own page numbers.

Note on edition: The 1846 compiled edition is an abridged narrative derived from the official account published by order of the Admiralty. Cook's journal text was edited for publication; the Beaglehole edition (1961) is the scholarly standard. The 1846 text is reliable for Cook's direct observations and characterizations but may differ in phrasing from the manuscript journals. For critical passages, cross-referencing with Beaglehole would be ideal when that edition becomes available.

The Forster accounts are accessed through: (a) von Saher's (1992) extensive excerpts from Georg Forster's *Voyage* and Johann Reinhold Forster's *Resolution Journal*; (b) Jakubowska's (2014) transcription and translation of the Forster *Memoire sur Waihou*; and (c) the book-length translation in Jakubowska 2014 (*Still More to Discover*). Page numbers for Forster materials refer to the PDF journal/book pages as published. Where the original Forster publication page is cited by von Saher or Jakubowska, it is given in brackets.

The *Memoire* is attributed primarily to Georg Forster as material author, with Johann Reinhold Forster as intellectual co-author (Jakubowska 2014, RNJ p. 84-85). It was likely composed shortly after the voyage when memories were fresh (Jakubowska 2014, RNJ p. 85).

## Claims Extraction

### Direct Observations of Political Authority / Social Differentiation

1. **First contact: no evident leader in initial canoe approach.** Two nearly naked men wearing "a sort of turban" paddled a canoe out to the ship, "waved a large green leaf and called out 'tayo'" (a greeting of friendship). They tied bananas to a rope lowered from the ship. No indication of hierarchical status among them. (von Saher 1992 Part I, RNJ 6(1) p. 3, quoting Georg Forster)

2. **Landing reception: approximately 150 inhabitants gathered, unarmed, with no evident authority figure directing the reception.** "When we finally landed, we were very kindly received by about 150 inhabitants who gathered on the shore. The majority were naked, except for a loincloth made from mulberry bark... All the multitude hurried to see us, surrounded us and followed us everywhere from the very moment we set our feet on the ground. All the island people, who were unarmed, except for two or three of them that held staffs about six feet long, were constantly offering us sugar cane, ripe bananas, sweet potatoes and pumpkins, as well as several roasted chicken." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* pp. 76-77, translating *Memoire*)

3. **Small number of women observed from the beginning.** "What struck us from the beginning was a small number of women, as we saw no more than about a dozen of them among the crowd of men." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 76, translating *Memoire*; confirmed by von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 36)

4. **Differentiated headgear observed but described as variable, not strictly hierarchical.** Von Saher quotes Georg Forster on head coverings: "most of the men wore a ring about two inches thick, strongly and curiously plaited, of grass and sitting close round the head. This was covered with great quantities of the long black feathers which decorate the neck of the man-of-war bird. Others had huge bunchy caps of brown gull feathers, which were almost as large as the full-bottomed wigs of European lawyers; and still others wore a simple hoop of wood, round which a number of the long white feathers of a gannet hung nodding, and waved in the wind. The women wore a great wide cap, made of very neat mat-work." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 35)

5. **Some individuals observed wearing robes described as "principal people."** "Some were dressed in a bright yellow or orange cloth. Most, however, were naked and it was assumed those wearing robes were principal people." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 34, paraphrasing Georg Forster's description of the approach from ship)

6. **One individual identified as having "some authority" encountered during afternoon walk.** Cook's party "encountered an islander who seemed to have some authority and who accompanied the Captain: 'He was not so timorous as the rest of his countrymen, but walked boldly along with us, whilst the others were alarmed at the least motion which appeared unusual to them.'" (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 36, quoting Georg Forster)

7. **Ko Toheetai identified as "aree" or king.** "...the natives told us their *aree*, or *hareekee*, or king was coming toward us. Several men came on before him, and distributed sugar-canes to us all in sign of friendship, at the same time pronouncing the word *heeo*, which signifies friend. We now saw the king standing on a hill, and walked up to him, Mr. Pickersgill and myself making him some presents. We asked for his name, which he told us was Ko Toheetai, adding that he was *aree* or king. We were desirous of knowing whether he was the only chief of a district, or of the whole island; upon which he spread out his arms, as it were to include the whole island, and said *Waihu*. To shew that we understood him, we laid out hands on his breast, and calling him by name, added his title, king of *Waihu*, at which he expressed very great satisfaction, and conversed a great deal with his people on that subject." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 38, quoting Georg Forster)

8. **Physical description of Ko Toheetai, with differentiated dress.** "He was a middle age man, rather tall; his face and whole body strongly punctured. He wore a piece of cloth made of mulberry bark, quilted with threads of grass, and stained yellow with turmeric; and on his head he had a cap of long shining black feathers, which might be called a diadem." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 38, quoting Georg Forster)

9. **Ko Toheetai wanted to prevent further exploration into interior.** "When we wanted to continue our march he seemed to dislike it, and desired us to return, offering to accompany us; but seeing our officer determined to proceed...he desisted and went with us." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 38, quoting Georg Forster)

10. **Ko-toheetai distributing sugar cane during the Forster party's march.** "Ko-toheetai, the chief of the island, was dispatching his people who brought plenty of sugar cane and distributed it among us while we were passing." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 81, translating *Memoire*; also Jakubowska 2014, RNJ p. 72)

11. **Man distributing food from hut, offering potatoes to each member of the party.** "On the day when we went to explore a great part of the island, we met a man coming out of his hut with a big sack full of sweet potatoes cooked under the earth, according to the custom of this country; he hurried to the head of our file and presented [a person] who marched first with a big potato and repeated it with everyone of us. Not satisfied with offering us that meal, he found out that there were some potatoes left [in his bag], he ran once more to the head of our file and again offered each of us a potato till he ran out of them." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* pp. 80-81, translating *Memoire*; also RNJ p. 72)

12. **No humiliating homage observed toward the chief.** "We did not observe that he was held in a very high esteem or that he was paid a humiliating homage, as it is demanded by despots from Asiatic islands." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 82; also RNJ p. 73)

13. **Chief characterized as paternal figure, not absolute ruler.** "He seemed rather a father of a family than an absolute ruler of this tribe; his commands were treated as advices of a tender father who cares about a true happiness of his children, and that is why they were executed immediately, what left us with no doubt that there existed a mutual attachment between the Chief and his subjects." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 82; also RNJ p. 73)

14. **Maroowahai/Mahine interaction: islander aboard ship.** "One bold islander returned to the ship with the boat; Forster describes him as: '...about five feet eight inches high, and remarkably hairy on the breast and all over the body. His colour was a chestnut brown, his beard strong, but clipped short, and of a black colour as was also the hair of his head, which was likewise cut short.'" He spent the night on the *Resolution*, was given cloth from Tahiti, and "was pleased to find inhabitants who spoke a language similar to his own." This man, later named Maroowahai, provided linguistic information. (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 34)

15. **Trading behavior: no centralized exchange system.** Trading was individualistic and chaotic. Islanders offered sweet potatoes, bananas, sugar cane, and chicken. "Cook had had little success trading for provisions, and concluded the people had very little to spare." The people "always threw away" beads "with contempt." Iron tools and Tahitian cloth were valued. "The Taheitean and European cloths were valued in the next degree, according to the size of the pieces; and iron-ware bore an inferior price." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) pp. 36-37)

16. **Theft was common and not controlled by any authority figure.** "The greatest part of the natives who traded with us instantly ran off with the cloth, nut-shell, or the nail...as if they were apprehensive that we might repent of our bargain." Also: "We soon discovered that they were experts when it comes to filching and cheating. Without hesitation they went to fields of their neighbors to look for sweet potatoes which they later came to sell us; the owners of those fields were doing everything possible to protect them from the depredation done by their countrymen." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 37; Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 77)

17. **No weapons of consequence observed; people generally unarmed.** "We saw but few arms among them; some however had lances or spears, made of thin ill-shapen sticks, and pointed with a sharp triangular piece of black glassy lava... One of them had a fighting club, made of a thick piece of wood about three feet long, carved at one extremity; and a few others had short wooden clubs, exactly resembling some of the New Zealand *patoo-patoos*, which are made of bone." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 35)

18. **Condition of the people: poverty and scarcity.** "Their want of clothing and a great eagerness to obtain our goods without offering anything in return, seems altogether to be sufficient marks of poverty." "The deplorable condition of the natives became more and more conspicuous. The scarcity of cloth among them was extremely great, most of the people being forced to go naked." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 37)

19. **Occupation houses distinguished from commoner sleeping areas.** "Forster comments on the dearth of occupation houses and concluded that they must either be crammed full or they were used for chiefs with the commoners sleeping in the open." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 36)

20. **Second day reconnaissance: about 200 inhabitants assembled, only 14-15 women.** "George went with Cook and several officers and upon landing found about 200 inhabitants assembled--but only 14 or 15 of them women." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 36)

21. **Monuments identified as *areekee* or kings.** "At sun set, on their way back to the boat, they passed the single statue, and asked questions about the nature of these stones: '...from what we could understand, we concluded that they were monuments erected to the memory of some of their *areekees*, or kings. This led us to believe that the pedestal was perhaps to be considered as a burying place and on looking carefully around it, we found a number of human bones, which confirmed our conjecture.'" (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 36)

22. **Names of individuals commemorated by statues.** "They were repeating enthusiastically the names: Obeena, Moraheena, Oomareeva, Quiperea, Moui and Manga-toto, at the same time pointing at the monuments dedicated to their memory." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 88, translating *Memoire*; also RNJ p. 75)

23. **Statue range named.** "The general appellation of this range was *Hanga Tebow*; *hanga* being the word which they prefix to every range. The names of the statues were *Ko-Tomoai, Ko-Tomoceree, Ko-hoo-oo, Moraheena, Oomarecva, Weenaboo, Weenape*." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 37, quoting Georg Forster)

24. **Shooting incident: sailor fires at thief.** "One of our sailors, who carried my plant bag, in which were a few nails, etc., being less careful of his bundle...a native snatched it up and ran off with it. None of us saw it, except Lieutenant Edgecumbe, who immediately fired his musket, loaded with small shot, at the thief... the native being wounded threw down the bag, which our people recovered, but he fell soon after; his countrymen took him up, and fled to a little distance, till we beckoned to them to return, which almost all of them did." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 38)

25. **Population estimate: 800-900 or never more than claimed.** The *Memoire* states: "Carl Friedrich Behrens, a sergeant major of Roggewein's fleet, from Mecklenburg, maintains that thousands of inhabitants of Easter Island gathered at the anchorage place and brought various fruits of their country for the Dutch. On the other hand, Spanish voyagers state that they counted about 3000 inhabitants of both sexes, whereas we, although we stayed there for four days and realized a reconnaissance tour of over 25 English miles, having seen, in consequence, a large part of the island, could not estimate the population to be more than nine hundred. Therefore I conclude that either the number of inhabitants decreased over fifty years from various thousands to 800 or 900 individuals, or the island's population never was so numerous as it was claimed." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* pp. 80-81, translating *Memoire*; also RNJ p. 72)

### Cook's Own Observations (from Cook 1846)

The following claims are drawn directly from Cook's first-person narrative in the 1846 compiled edition. Cook remained at the landing place on the second day due to illness and received the inland party's report secondhand from Lieutenants Pickersgill and Edgecumbe and from the astronomer William Wales. Claims 26-40 supplement and cross-reference the Forster accounts above.

26. **Canoe approach with plantains, no authority figure.** "Here a canoe conducted by two men came off to us. They brought with them a bunch of plantains, which they sent into the ship by a rope, and then they returned ashore. This gave us a good opinion of the islanders, and inspired us with hopes of getting some refreshments, which we were in great want of." No leader or intermediary; a spontaneous act of contact. (Cook 1846, p. 449)

27. **Landing: hundreds assembled, entirely unarmed.** "We landed at the sandy beach, where some hundreds of the natives were assembled, and who were so impatient to see us, that many of them swam off to meet the boats. Not one of them had so much as a stick or weapon of any sort in their hands." (Cook 1846, p. 449)

28. **Expert theft, no authority intervening.** "We presently discovered that they were as expert thieves, and as tricking in their exchanges, as any people we had yet met with. It was with some difficulty we could keep the hats on our heads, but hardly possible to keep anything in our pockets, not even what themselves had sold us; for they would watch every opportunity to snatch it from us, so that we sometimes bought the same thing two or three times over, and after all did not get it." No chief or authority figure is described as controlling or discouraging this behavior. (Cook 1846, p. 449)

29. **Plantation owner stops potato theft: direct evidence of private property.** "We had at one time a pretty brisk trade with them for potatoes, which we observed they dug up out of an adjoining plantation; but this traffic, which was very advantageous to us, was soon put a stop to by the owner (as we supposed) of the plantation coming down, and driving all the people out of it. By this we concluded that he had been robbed of his property, and that they were not less scrupulous of stealing from one another than from us." This describes an individual property holder defending his own resources with no recourse to any higher authority. (Cook 1846, p. 450)

30. **Man with spear and white flag leads the inland party.** "They had not proceeded far, before a middle-aged man, punctured from head to foot, and his face painted with a sort of white pigment, appeared with a spear in his hand, and walked alongside of them, making signs to his countrymen to keep at a distance, and not to molest our people. When he had pretty well effected this, he hoisted a piece of white cloth on his spear, placed himself in the front, and led the way with his ensign of peace." This man is not identified as a chief; Cook describes him by physical appearance only. He exercised situational authority over the crowd. (Cook 1846, p. 450)

31. **Hut owners individually distribute food and water.** "They also passed some huts, the owners of which met them with roasted potatoes and sugarcanes, and placing themselves ahead of the foremost of the party, (for they marched in a line in order to have the benefit of the path,) gave one to each man as he passed by. They observed the same method in distributing the water which they brought; and were particularly careful that the foremost did not drink too much, lest none should be left for the hindmost." This describes individual household-level hospitality with methodical equity, not centralized redistribution. (Cook 1846, p. 450)

32. **Simultaneous theft and generosity by different individuals.** "But at the very time these were relieving the thirsty and hungry, there were not wanting others who endeavoured to steal from them the very things which had been given them." This captures the absence of any overarching social control. (Cook 1846, p. 450)

33. **After the shooting: guide restores order, not through authority but persuasion.** After the shot hit a thief, "they presently saw the man who had hitherto led the way, and one or two more coming running towards them; but instead of stopping when they came up, they continued to run round them, repeating in a kind manner, a few words, until our people set forwards again. Then their old guide hoisted his flag, leading the way as before, and none ever attempted to steal from them the whole day afterwards." Pacification through personal intervention, not command. (Cook 1846, pp. 450-451)

34. **Man "seemingly of some note" identified as "the chief of the island."** "On a hill a number of people collected together, some of whom had spears in their hands; but, on being called to by their countryman, they dispersed; except a few, amongst whom was one seemingly of some note. He was a stout, well-made man, with a fine open countenance; his face was painted, his body punctured, and he wore a better Ha hou, or cloth, than the rest. He saluted them as he came up, by stretching out his arms with both hands clenched, lifting them over his head, opening them wide, and then letting them fall gradually down to his sides. To this man, whom they understood to be the chief of the island, their other friend gave his white flag; and he gave it to another, who carried it before them the remainder of the day." The identification as "chief" is the inland party's inference; Cook does not describe any commanding behavior. The man's authority is marked by better clothing, a distinctive greeting, and the delegation of the flag. (Cook 1846, p. 451)

35. **Fallen statues on the east side with precise measurements.** "On each had stood four of those large statues; but they were all fallen down from two of them, and also one from the third; all except one were broken by the fall, or in some measure defaced. Mr. Wales measured this one, and found it to be fifteen feet in length, and six feet broad over the shoulders. Each statue had on its head a large cylindric stone of a red colour, wrought perfectly round." (Cook 1846, p. 450)

36. **Massive standing statue casting enough shadow to shelter thirty people.** "Having measured one which had fallen down, they found it very near twenty-seven feet long, and upwards of eight feet over the breast or shoulders; and yet this appeared considerably short of the size of one they saw standing; its shade, a little past two o'clock, being sufficient to shelter all the party, consisting of near thirty persons, from the rays of the sun." (Cook 1846, p. 451)

37. **Quarry identified by Wales on the highest part of the island.** "In a small hollow on the highest part of the island, they met with several such cylinders as are placed on the heads of the statues. Some of these appeared larger than any they had seen before... Mr. Wales, from whom I had this information, is of opinion that there had been a quarry here, whence these stones had formerly been dug, and that it would have been no difficult matter to roll them down the hill after they were formed. I think this a very reasonable conjecture, and have no doubt that it has been so." This is significant: Cook's account via Wales confirms the quarry sighting, aligning with the *Memoire* but contradicting Georg Forster's *Voyage* claim that no quarries were found. (Cook 1846, p. 451)

38. **Population estimate: six or seven hundred.** "The inhabitants of this island do not seem to exceed six or seven hundred souls; and above two-thirds of those we saw were males." (Cook 1846, p. 453)

39. **Only three or four canoes on the whole island.** "Not more than three or four canoes were seen on the whole island; and these very mean, and built of many pieces sewed together with small line." Cook found one board six to eight feet long and marveled at its provenance given the total absence of large timber. (Cook 1846, p. 454)

40. **Three different names obtained for the island.** "Without pretending to say which, or whether any of them is right, I shall only observe, that the last [Teapy] was obtained by Oedidee, who understood their language much better than any of us; though even he understood it but very imperfectly." This underscores the severe communication barrier. (Cook 1846, pp. 454-455)

### Interpretive Statements About Political Organization

1. **Government described as "neither complicated nor troublesome."** "The civil government of the inhabitants of this island is neither complicated nor troublesome, their Chief bears no other distinction than a more complete costume and a sort of diadem made of frigate pectoral feathers." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 82; also RNJ p. 73)

2. **Chief compared to a "father of a family" rather than a despot.** "We did not observe that he was held in a very high esteem or that he was paid a humiliating homage, as it is demanded by despots from Asiatic islands. He seemed rather a father of a family than an absolute ruler of this tribe; his commands were treated as advices of a tender father who cares about a true happiness of his children." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 82; also RNJ p. 73)

3. **Conflict resolution attributed to family heads, not centralized authority.** "If by any chance [a quarrel] breaks up, heads of families, with their wisdom and authority, can easily end them and soon restore the peace between their countrymen." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 83; also RNJ p. 73)

4. **Self-sufficiency of individual subsistence emphasized.** "On this arid island everyone is in need of seeking their subsistence and they must do it using only the strength of their own hands." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 83; also RNJ p. 73)

5. **Peaceful disposition inferred from small numbers and poverty.** "The disposition of these people is far from being warlike; their numbers are too inconsiderable, and their poverty too general, to create civil disturbances amongst them. It is equally improbable that they have foreign wars." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 39)

6. **Young man's ability to simply occupy fallow land and establish household.** "Since the island is presently not very populated and there are so many fallow lands, if any young man wants to live on his own, he may simply occupy a [plot of] land and clear it out with a tool made of hard wood, in form of a pointed stake, which is used here instead of spade; his family and friends will not refuse him some sweet potatoes that he will divide into cuttings to multiply them; others will give him some shoots of sugar cane or banana tree, and there he is, ready to become head of a family and to feed himself, his wife and children." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 83; also RNJ p. 73)

7. **Unity, peace, and wellbeing attributed to simplicity.** "Everything [here] seems to be a promise of unity, public safety, peace and wellbeing of the islanders." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 83; also RNJ p. 73)

8. **Resemblance to other Polynesian governments noted.** Georg Forster concluded that the Easter Islanders' government "reiterates on the resemblance of culture, language, and government to that of the rest of Polynesia: '...[the language] being a dialect which, in many respects, resembles that of New Zealand, especially in the harshness of pronunciation and the use of gutturals, and yet, in other instances, partakes of that of Taheitee.'" Also: "The religion of the Easter Islanders is still wholly unknown to us, because abstract ideas are not to be acquired in so short a time as our stay. The statues, which are erected in honour of their kings, have a great affinity to the wooden figures, called *Tee*, [tiki] on the chief's *marai's* or burying places at Taheitee." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 39)

9. **Statues interpreted as memorials to chiefs and distinguished persons, NOT deities.** "Now I could not convince myself to ascribe that work to superstitions, as the islanders struggled to disillusion us in this regard, assuring that [these monuments] were not symbols of divinity, but statues erected to commemorate their chiefs, heroes and distinguished members of their nation." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 88; also RNJ p. 75)

10. **Statues motivated by good deeds of leaders, not coerced labor.** "What power must reside in the idea of good deeds that those great men performed for their people! It seems that those good deeds are permanently engraved in their hearts. Those good deeds, present today in their memory, infuse them with a noble ardor to perpetuate the cherished remembrance of their princes, through works that almost surpass their force; this feeling helped them execute a tough and costly plan, motivated them to carve, transport and erect a statue that always bore the favorite name of a chief or a hero, great by his good deeds performed for his countrymen and still alive in their memory." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 88; also RNJ p. 75)

11. **Honor given only after death, when authority no longer influences.** "The islanders from Waihu honor their great men only after their death, when their authority can no more influence the spirit of the contemporary. The merits of a man can be judged with the greatest equity only after his death." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 88; also RNJ p. 75)

12. **Present population judged incapable of building monuments.** "If we consider the immense mass of stone, 27 feet high by eight [feet] wide, that probably was pulled out of quarries that we find only on the hills, in the middle of the island, where we saw during our [reconnaissance] trip some hats and some unfinished statues; and if we imagine the ceaseless dedication, the patience, the effort to execute even the least refined of these clumsy monuments, realized by people who, before the arrival of the Europeans, had no knowledge of iron or any other metal and, in consequence, [could use] only coral stones and big shells to execute a shape roughly resembling a human figure, this is enough to give us the idea of the present depopulation of this colony." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* pp. 87-88; also RNJ p. 75)

13. **Island formerly happier, more populous, more creative.** "However, there was no stronger proof of a period when the island was happier, the population more numerous, the nation's spirit more active and its genius more creative than the funeral monuments that we saw standing in various places of the island and overturned in other." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 87; also RNJ p. 74)

14. **Georg Forster's conclusion on Easter Islander character.** "'Their behavior towards me was wholly inoffensive, agreeably to the general character of the nations of the South Seas. From the expressions of the historians of Roggewein's voyage, it should seem that the Dutch very wantonly fired upon the natives, who gave no provocation, and killed a considerable number of them, intimidating the rest to a great degree.'" (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 37)

15. **Jakubowska-Vorbrich on the *Memoire*'s political-philosophical dimension.** The *Memoire* "describes a happy, honest, benevolent community... living under the rule of a good, benevolent Chief..." and uses this as a contrast with European despotism, fitting into Enlightenment political philosophy (Jakubowska-Vorbrich 2019, p. 200). "This vision, contrary to the previous quotation, of a good, benevolent community is, in spite of its reporting, cases of 'theft' or 'cheating,' created... [as] an Arcadian, (mis)interpretation of the Rapanui society" (Jakubowska-Vorbrich 2019, p. 200).

### Cook's Own Interpretive Statements (from Cook 1846)

16. **Cook's critical admission: "quite ignorant" of chiefs' power.** "I have no doubt that all these plantations are private property, and that there are here, as at Otaheite, chiefs (which they call Areekes) to whom these plantations belong. But of the power or authority of these chiefs, or of the government of these people, I confess myself quite ignorant." This is the most important single statement in any contact-era account for the chiefdom question. Cook, an experienced Pacific navigator who had observed hierarchical chieftainships in Tahiti and Tonga, explicitly states he could not determine the nature of political authority on Rapa Nui. He infers the existence of chiefs from analogy with Tahiti ("as at Otaheite") and from the islanders' use of the term "Areekes," but acknowledges he has no direct knowledge of what their authority entailed. (Cook 1846, pp. 454-455)

17. **Statues not idols; burying-places for tribes or families.** "The gigantic statues so often mentioned are not, in my opinion, looked upon as idols by the present inhabitants, whatever they might have been in the days of the Dutch; at least, I saw nothing that could induce me to think so. On the contrary, I rather suppose that they are burying-places for certain tribes or families." Cook distinguishes between what the statues may have been and what they are "in my opinion" at the time of his visit. (Cook 1846, p. 455)

18. **Present inhabitants "most certainly had no hand" in the statues.** "The present inhabitants have most certainly had no hand in them, as they do not even repair the foundations of those which are going to decay." Cook uses this to argue for a formerly larger population. (Cook 1846, p. 455)

19. **"Moi" and "Areekee" as statue terminology.** "They give different names to them, such as Gotomoara, Marapate, Kanaro, Gowaytoo-goo, Matta Matta, &c. &c., to which they sometimes prefix the word Moi, and sometimes annex Areekee. The latter signifies chief, and the former, burying, or sleeping-place, as well as we could understand." This is Cook's own report that the islanders associated the statues with both the concept of burial/sleep and with chiefs, though he qualifies this with "as well as we could understand." (Cook 1846, p. 455)

20. **Platforms described as excellent masonry.** "They are built, or rather faced, with hewn stones of a very large size; and the workmanship is not inferior to the best plain piece of masonry we have in England. They use no sort of cement; yet the joints are exceedingly close, and the stones morticed and tenanted one into another, in a very artful manner." Cook admires the engineering without attributing it to any particular political system. (Cook 1846, p. 455)

21. **Construction method conjectured.** "The only method I can conceive, is by raising the upper end by little and little, supporting it by stones as it is raised, and building about it till they got it erect; thus a sort of mount, or scaffolding, would be made, upon which they might roll the cylinder, and place it upon the head of the statue." Cook's mechanical explanation requires persistence and ingenuity but not chiefly command. (Cook 1846, p. 455)

22. **Work of "immense time" showing "ingenuity and perseverance."** "Let them have been made and set up, by this or any other method, they must have been a work of immense time, and sufficiently show the ingenuity and perseverance of the islanders in the age in which they were built." Cook attributes monument construction to the islanders' character (ingenuity, perseverance), not to chiefly authority or command labor. (Cook 1846, p. 455)

23. **Island too resource-poor to attract visitors.** "No nation need contend for the honour of the discovery of this island, as there can be few places which afford less convenience for shipping than it does. Here is no safe anchorage, no wood for fuel, nor any fresh water worth taking on board. Nature has been exceedingly sparing of her favours to this spot." (Cook 1846, p. 452)

24. **Polynesian affinity recognized.** "In colour, features, and language, they bear such affinity to the people of the more western isles, that no one will doubt that they have had the same origin. It is extraordinary that the same nation should have spread themselves over all the isles in this vast ocean, from New Zealand to this island, which is almost one-fourth part of the circumference of the globe." Cook establishes the Polynesian connection but does not infer political similarity. (Cook 1846, p. 453)

25. **Not giants.** "In general, the people of this isle are a slender race. I did not see a man that would measure six feet; so far are they from being giants, as one of the authors of Roggewein's voyage asserts." Cook corrects Behrens directly. (Cook 1846, p. 453)

### European Categories Imposed

1. **"Aree" / "king" applied to Ko Toheetai.** Georg Forster records that "the natives told us their *aree*, or *hareekee*, or king was coming toward us." The term *aree* (ariki) is a Polynesian title familiar to the expedition from Tahiti. The islanders used this term themselves, but the equation with "king" is the European observers' framing. Actual observed behavior: Ko Toheetai stood on a hill, received presents, accepted the title "king of Waihu" with "very great satisfaction," wore differentiated clothing (mulberry bark cloth, feathered "diadem"), distributed food, and accompanied the party. (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 38)

2. **"Chief" used throughout the *Memoire*.** "Ko-toheetai, the chief of the island" (*le chef de l'isle*). Actual behavior: he dispatched people to distribute sugar cane and tried to show hospitality. (Jakubowska 2014, RNJ p. 72; *Still More to Discover* p. 81)

3. **"Princes" used to describe authority figures.** "The affability of the princes that wield power and use it to do Good, evokes respect [even] in the grossest people." This is philosophical language used by the *Memoire* author, not a direct observation of princely behavior. (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 82; also RNJ p. 72)

4. **"Subjects" used for the general population.** "a mutual attachment between the Chief and his subjects." This implies a political relationship of ruler-to-ruled that was inferred, not directly observed as a structured system. (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 82; also RNJ p. 73)

5. **"Sovereign" applied negatively (what he was not).** "If our sailors, instead of a sovereign deprived of everything and roughly shaped by nature, saw a prince lavishing them with favors, they would not react with more sensibility to his deeds as they seemed to [react] in the presence of Ko-toheetai." The term "sovereign" is used precisely to note his lack of sovereign attributes. (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 82; also RNJ p. 72)

6. **"Areekees, or kings" applied to those commemorated by statues.** "We concluded that they were monuments erected to the memory of some of their *areekees*, or kings." Actual evidence: human bones found at the base confirmed mortuary function; names were recited by islanders at the monuments. (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 36)

7. **"Chiefs, heroes and distinguished members" in *Memoire* interpretation.** "[These monuments] were not symbols of divinity, but statues erected to commemorate their chiefs, heroes and distinguished members of their nation." This is the islanders' reported explanation, but filtered through European categories. (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 88; also RNJ p. 75)

8. **Cook's "chiefs (which they call Areekes)" as analogical transfer.** Cook writes: "I have no doubt that all these plantations are private property, and that there are here, as at Otaheite, chiefs (which they call Areekes) to whom these plantations belong." The phrase "as at Otaheite" reveals the analogical reasoning: Cook interprets what he sees through the lens of Tahitian social structure. The islanders used the term "Areekes" (ariki), but Cook's equation of plantation ownership with chiefly status is his own inference from Polynesian comparison. He immediately qualifies: "But of the power or authority of these chiefs, or of the government of these people, I confess myself quite ignorant." (Cook 1846, pp. 454-455)

9. **Cook's inland party identifies a man as "the chief of the island."** "To this man, whom they understood to be the chief of the island, their other friend gave his white flag." The identification is the inland party's interpretation. The basis: better clothing, distinctive greeting, spear-bearers nearby. No commanding behavior is described. The man did not direct the party, control resources, or issue orders to the population. (Cook 1846, p. 451)

10. **"Areekee" glossed as "chief" in statue nomenclature.** "The latter [Areekee] signifies chief, and the former [Moi], burying, or sleeping-place, as well as we could understand." Cook translates *ariki* as "chief" following the established Polynesian framework. The qualification "as well as we could understand" acknowledges the communication barrier. (Cook 1846, p. 455)

### Significant Absences

1. **No humiliating homage or deference rituals observed.** "We did not observe that he was held in a very high esteem or that he was paid a humiliating homage, as it is demanded by despots from Asiatic islands." This is especially significant because Cook and the Forsters had visited Tahiti, Tonga, and the Society Islands where elaborate chiefly deference was observed. The explicit comparison to "Asiatic islands" (and implicitly to Tahiti/Tonga) indicates the observers were looking for such behavior and did not find it. (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 82; also RNJ p. 73)

2. **No evidence of redistribution networks.** No passage describes any system of tribute collection, centralized storage, or redistribution. The chief is observed distributing sugar cane during one walk, but this appears to be hospitality toward the visitors, not evidence of a redistributive system. Individual households offered their own food independently.

3. **No centralized exchange or market system.** Trading was entirely individualistic, with individuals offering goods and frequently absconding with payment. No one directed or organized trade. No centralized authority mediated exchanges.

4. **No large canoes or fleet observed.** "The boat which came off seemed to be a very wretched thing, patched together of several pieces, the head & stern high & the middle low; THERE WAS AN OUTRIGGER FIXED TO IT (my capitals), & each of the Men had a paddle made of more than one piece, which sufficiently proves the want of wood on this Isle..." (Johann Forster's journal entry for March 14, 1774, quoted in von Saher 1999, RNJ 13(2) p. 43). The poor quality of canoes contrasts sharply with the fleets of war canoes observed in Tahiti and Tonga.

5. **No quarries with active work found (contested).** Georg Forster wrote in his *Voyage*: "We neither met with any quarries, where they had recently dug the materials, nor with unfinished statues which we might have considered as the work of the present race" (Forster 1777:594, cited in Jakubowska 2014, RNJ p. 85). Johann Forster's *Journal* also "says nothing about it." However, the *Memoire* contradicts this, reporting they saw "the quarries in the interior of the island, but, apart from the 'hats,' noticed some unfinished statues, too" (Jakubowska 2014, RNJ p. 85). This discrepancy is significant; see Cross-Reference Notes below.

6. **No evidence for garden enclosures described by Roggeveen.** "Wandering through fields they saw no evidence for garden enclosures of the type described by Roggeveen." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 36)

7. **No musical instruments or organized entertainment.** Georg Forster stated: "We are unacquainted with the amusements of the people of Easter Island, having never seen them engaged in any kind of diversion, nor taken notice of a single musical instrument among them" (Forster 1777:600, cited in Jakubowska 2014, RNJ p. 85). However, this is contradicted by the *Memoire* which says "we saw them often laughing and playful, and several times even amusing themselves with singing and dancing" (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 83; also RNJ p. 73). [CITE-CHECK: Jakubowska 2014 considers this discrepancy potentially significant as social/political propaganda in the *Memoire*]

8. **No temples or religious structures identified.** The observers found no dedicated religious structures separate from the ahu/moai platforms. The *Memoire* explicitly states: "The religion of the Easter Islanders is still wholly unknown to us, because abstract ideas are not to be acquired in so short a time as our stay."

9. **No organized labor or command structure witnessed.** No passage describes anyone directing the labor of others, no work parties were observed, no evidence of corvee labor or organized production under chiefly command.

10. **Destitution noted as incompatible with chiefly surplus extraction.** "Their great disappointment in the island as a place of refreshment is clear; the only article of value was sweet potatoes, but even they were few in number. They collected less than fifty fowls, and found the water to be ill-tasting and in short supply." Georg Forster noted the islanders "generously shared their food, when obviously they were in need themselves." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 39)

11. **No warrior class or military organization.** "The disposition of these people is far from being warlike; their numbers are too inconsiderable, and their poverty too general, to create civil disturbances amongst them. It is equally improbable that they have foreign wars....This being premised, it is extraordinary that they should have different kinds of offensive weapons, and especially such as resemble those of the New Zeelanders; and we must add this circumstance to several others, which are inexplicable to us in their kind." (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 39)

12. **Coconut shells identified as "most valuable article of trade."** "The most valuable article of trade on our part were empty coconut shells, which we had received at the Society and Friendly Islands; but they were not current unless the hole in them was very small, or unless they had a cover." This reveals the island's resource poverty; coconut shells were more valued than the beads and trinkets that served as trade goods elsewhere in Polynesia. (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 37)

### Cook's Significant Absences (from Cook 1846)

13. **Cook's ignorance of government.** Despite four days on the island, extensive interaction, and decades of Pacific experience, Cook explicitly states: "of the power or authority of these chiefs, or of the government of these people, I confess myself quite ignorant." If there were a visible chieftainship with recognizable institutional features, Cook would have identified it; he did so routinely in Tahiti and Tonga. (Cook 1846, pp. 454-455)

14. **No religion identified.** "Nor are we better acquainted with their religion." Cook found no temples, no religious specialists, no ritual behavior he could identify as religious. (Cook 1846, p. 455)

15. **No commanding behavior by "chief."** Cook's narrative of the inland party describes the man "understood to be the chief" only in terms of appearance (better cloth, painted face, punctured body) and a distinctive greeting. No commands, no directing of labor, no control of resources, no deference by others is described. He simply accompanied the party. (Cook 1846, p. 451)

16. **No garden enclosures.** Cook describes plantations "prettily laid out by line, but not inclosed by any fence; indeed, they have nothing for this purpose but stones." This absence of enclosure is consistent with the accounts from the inland party, which observed open plantations. (Cook 1846, p. 455)

17. **No active monument construction.** "The present inhabitants have most certainly had no hand in them, as they do not even repair the foundations of those which are going to decay." Cook finds no evidence of ongoing monumental work. (Cook 1846, p. 455)

### Speculative / Uncertain Claims

1. **Forster's earthquake/volcanic catastrophe theory for depopulation.** "Since the islanders live underground and their women are busy with housework... while the men work outside, it is very likely that one of those big, terrible natural disasters suddenly buried a great number of inhabitants, and, above all, a proportionally greater number of women. This highly probable cause of depopulation, I think, devastated the island very shortly before our arrival, as we were observing everywhere traces of soil clearing that would disappear completely in less than ten years." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* pp. 86-87; also RNJ p. 74)

2. **Religious principles initially considered as motivation for statue construction, then rejected.** "At first I considered it right to suppose that religious principles, that lead the human spirit much further than any other circumstances, may have contributed [to this phenomenon]. However, now I could not convince myself to ascribe that work to superstitions, as the islanders struggled to disillusion us in this regard." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 88; also RNJ p. 75)

3. **Identification of Ko Toheetai as king of the whole island is uncertain.** The claim rests on Georg Forster's account that when asked if he was "the only chief of a district, or of the whole island," Ko Toheetai "spread out his arms, as it were to include the whole island." Communication was limited and relied partly on gesture; the question itself imposed European categories of territorial sovereignty. The islanders may have been confirming something different from what was asked.

4. **Houses interpreted as potentially for chiefs vs. commoners.** Forster "concluded that they must either be crammed full or they were used for chiefs with the commoners sleeping in the open." This is speculation based on the small number of visible houses relative to the population. (von Saher 1992 Part II, RNJ 6(2) p. 36)

5. **Population formerly much larger, inferred from abandoned cultivation.** "Having observed that formerly all the mountains were being cultivated, as we recognized the remains of the plantations alignment, and that at present the islanders are satisfied with clearing out plains and valleys, in the middle of which there are still many [plots of] fallow land, I came to the conclusion that formerly the population was greater." (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 86; also RNJ p. 74)

6. **War suggested as factor in some Forster texts but denied in others.** The *Memoire* attributes population decline to natural disaster and states the people are peaceful (Jakubowska-Vorbrich 2019, p. 199). In contrast, Johann Forster's *Journal* "cites war among possible factors that contributed to the population decrease" (Forster 1982:476, cited in Jakubowska 2014, RNJ p. 85). Georg Forster's *Voyage* "attributes it to the small number and poverty of the local tribe" (Jakubowska 2014, RNJ p. 85).

7. **Egyptian comparison for statues.** The *Memoire* compares Easter Island statues to those of ancient Egypt, especially the Thebaid, noting stylistic similarities (topped with a big hat, indication of arms, simpler lower portions). This is speculative analogy, not observation. (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* p. 89; also RNJ pp. 75-76)

## Cross-Reference Notes

### Discrepancies Between Forster Accounts

1. **Quarries and unfinished statues.** Georg Forster's *Voyage* (1777) states they found no quarries or unfinished statues of the present race. The *Memoire* reports seeing quarries in the interior and unfinished statues. Jakubowska (2014, RNJ p. 85) flags this as "one of the most astonishing differences between the French manuscript and other writings." Possible explanations: the Forsters deliberately kept strangers from the quarry area in published accounts (Cauwe 2011 theory); the *Memoire* was written earlier when memories were fresher and later publications "fossilized" a different version; or the observation was reinterpreted over time.

2. **Singing and dancing.** Georg Forster's *Voyage* (1777:600) states they were "unacquainted with the amusements of the people" and never saw them engaged in diversion or noticed musical instruments. The *Memoire* reports "we saw them often laughing and playful, and several times even amusing themselves with singing and dancing." Jakubowska (2014, RNJ p. 85) raises the possibility this is "social/political propaganda, so typical of the text, showing happy people under the command of a benevolent 'prince.'"

3. **Cause of population decline.** The *Memoire* attributes decline to natural disaster (earthquake/volcanic event). Georg Forster's *Voyage* attributes it to small numbers and poverty. Johann Forster's *Journal* mentions war as a possible factor. These are three different speculative explanations from the same expedition, reflecting different analytical priorities.

4. **Peaceful vs. warlike.** The *Memoire* emphasizes peacefulness and attributes population decline to wise governance. The *Voyage* notes possession of weapons resembling New Zealand types and finds this puzzling given their peaceful character. The *Journal* considers war as a depopulation factor.

### Cook vs. Forster Accounts

5. **Cook's explicit agnosticism vs. the Forsters' political characterization.** Cook writes: "But of the power or authority of these chiefs, or of the government of these people, I confess myself quite ignorant." The Forsters, by contrast, provide an elaborate characterization: government "neither complicated nor troublesome," chief as "father of a family," commands treated as "advices of a tender father." Cook's account is epistemologically more cautious; he acknowledges what he does not know rather than constructing a portrait of benevolent patriarchal governance. The Forsters' characterization may reflect their Enlightenment philosophical commitments (see Cross-Reference Note 7 below on the *Memoire*) rather than more direct evidence than Cook had.

6. **Cook's emphasis on poverty vs. the Forsters' Arcadian framing.** Cook's account of Easter Island is dominated by practical assessment: resource scarcity, brackish water, poor anchorage, nothing to attract ships, expert theft. The *Memoire*, by contrast, describes "unity, public safety, peace and wellbeing of the islanders" and presents food distribution as evidence of social harmony. Cook's tone is that of a naval commander evaluating a resupply stop; the Forsters' tone is that of natural philosophers constructing a political argument.

7. **Quarry identification: Cook via Wales confirms, Georg Forster's *Voyage* denies.** Cook reports: "Mr. Wales, from whom I had this information, is of opinion that there had been a quarry here, whence these stones had formerly been dug... I think this a very reasonable conjecture, and have no doubt that it has been so" (Cook 1846, p. 451). Georg Forster's *Voyage* (1777:594) claims they found "neither any quarries, where they had recently dug the materials, nor with unfinished statues." The *Memoire* aligns with Cook in reporting quarries. This confirms Jakubowska's finding that Georg Forster's published *Voyage* deliberately omitted or contradicted observations that appeared in both Cook's account and the *Memoire*. The discrepancy strengthens the case that the Forsters' published accounts were editorially shaped.

8. **Population estimate: Cook vs. Forsters.** Cook estimates "six or seven hundred souls" (Cook 1846, p. 453). The *Memoire* estimates "800 or 900" (Jakubowska 2014, *Still More to Discover* pp. 80-81). These are broadly consistent but not identical, suggesting independent estimation rather than one account copying another.

9. **"Chief" identification: Cook's party's report vs. Georg Forster's Ko Toheetai scene.** Cook's account of the inland party encountering "the chief of the island" (Cook 1846, p. 451) is notably sparse: a man with better clothing, a distinctive greeting, who accepted a flag and delegated it. Georg Forster's *Voyage* provides the elaborate Ko Toheetai scene (announced by retainers, sugar cane distributed, asked about his domain, "expressed very great satisfaction"). These may describe the same individual, but Cook's account lacks the political staging and dialogue that make the Forster version so quotable for chiefdom arguments. This difference may reflect what the two accounts' respective authors considered noteworthy.

10. **Von Saher's summary on George Forster's overall impression.** Georg Forster's conclusion: "Their behavior towards me was wholly inoffensive, agreeably to the general character of the nations of the South Seas." This is the most measured assessment, neither imposing strong political categories nor finding evidence of complex hierarchy.

### The *Memoire* as a Source

7. **Jakubowska-Vorbrich (2019) identifies the *Memoire* as having a dual purpose.** It is simultaneously a scientific report and "a philosophical, social and political manifesto, and also an open eulogy to the Kingdom of Prussia and its monarch, Frederick the Great" (Jakubowska 2014, RNJ p. 86). This ideological agenda may shape the political descriptions. The depiction of a benevolent chief ruling simple, happy subjects through wisdom rather than force corresponds to Enlightenment ideals of the philosopher-king, not necessarily to observed reality. The *Memoire*'s political descriptions must therefore be treated with particular caution as evidence for actual political organization.

8. **Authorship.** Jakubowska (2014, RNJ pp. 84-85) attributes the *Memoire* primarily to Georg Forster as material author, based on literary style analysis ("cohesion, fluency, the wistfully poetic nature of the language"), with Johann Reinhold as intellectual co-author. The Forsters "adopted a 'peculiar policy' regarding the true identity of the author of their works" (Vorbrich 2009:98). Jakubowska-Vorbrich (2019) considers Georg the "material author and intellectual co-author."

### Second-Pass Additions (2026-03-13)

**SP-1. Rotation of authority during the inland expedition: task-passing among apparent peers.**

Cook reports that his inland party received a flag from an islander, and the flag then changed hands: the flag-bearer passes his flag to the man "seemingly of some note," who then "gave it to another, who carried it before them the remainder of the day" (Cook 1846, p. 451). This is not delegation from superior to subordinate; it is rotation of a specific task (carrying the flag, leading the party) among apparent peers. In a chiefdom, such a task would remain with the chief or be delegated downward. Here, the object of authority (the flag) passes laterally among individuals without any described hierarchy determining who carries it. The man "seemingly of some note" neither retains the flag himself nor directs a subordinate; he simply passes it on.

**Assessment**: This passage is easily overlooked because it seems trivial. But the task-rotation pattern is analytically significant: it is inconsistent with hereditary chiefly command and consistent with situational or rotating leadership.

**SP-2. Simultaneous generosity and theft by different individuals: no unified social norm.**

Cook describes: "At the very time these were relieving the thirsty and hungry, there were not wanting others who endeavoured to steal from them the very things which had been given them" (Cook 1846, p. 450). Some islanders voluntarily provide food and water to the Europeans while other islanders simultaneously attempt to steal those same provisions. No unified social norm governs stranger-treatment; behavior ranges from generosity to predation within the same society at the same time. No authority controls either the generous or the predatory behavior. In a chiefdom, the paramount chief or his agents would enforce a consistent policy toward important visitors.

**Assessment**: This passage reveals the absence of enforceable social norms regarding outsiders, inconsistent with chiefly authority over community behavior.

**SP-3. Social response to the shooting: persuasion, not coercion.**

After someone in Cook's party fired a shot (apparently at an islander attempting to steal), the flag-bearer and companions responded by running toward the party "repeating in a kind manner, a few words" (Cook 1846, pp. 451-452). No demand for compensation, no retaliation, no escalation. Then the guide "hoisted his flag, leading the way as before, and none ever attempted to steal from them the whole day afterwards" (Cook 1846, p. 452). The response to violence is face-to-face communication and persuasion; the result is behavioral change through social influence, not institutional enforcement. The flag-bearer's authority operates through personal relationships and persuasive communication, not through coercive power or institutional sanction.

**Assessment**: Compare with Bouman's 1722 account of the post-shooting response: in both cases, the response to European violence is non-coercive social mediation, not organized retaliation or chiefly justice. This consistency across 52 years suggests a stable pattern of non-institutional conflict resolution.

**SP-4. The unused fertile southern land: anomalous under chiefly land allocation.**

Cook's inland party found the best soil on the island on the south end: "The country was now more improper for cultivation than that we had hitherto seen, but much of it was in actual cultivation. As they advanced, the country improved, the soil becoming of a reddish colour, and much superior to any they had seen" (Cook 1846, pp. 451-452). Yet despite this superior soil, "they saw neither house nor plantation" in the southern interior (Cook 1846, p. 452). If a chief controlled land allocation, the most productive land on the island would be in use, either by the chief himself or by favored subordinates. Its emptiness is anomalous under a chiefdom model. The unused fertile land is consistent with decentralized land tenure in which settlement patterns reflect kin-group territories, not centralized allocation.

**Assessment**: This is among the most striking observations in Cook's account for the chiefdom question. The fact that the island's best agricultural land was apparently unused suggests no centralized authority was directing settlement or land use.

**SP-5. Multiple independent cooking fires: household-level food preparation.**

Cook observes: "We frequently saw ten or a dozen, or more, such fires in one place, and most commonly in the mornings and evenings" (Cook 1846, p. 453). Multiple independent fires in one location imply household-level or small-group food preparation, not centralized communal cooking or chiefly feasting. In chiefdoms with redistributive feasting, food preparation is characteristically centralized; a chief's household or a communal kitchen prepares food for distribution. The pattern of many small simultaneous fires is consistent with autonomous household-level subsistence.

**Assessment**: This seemingly mundane observation about cooking patterns provides evidence about the scale of economic organization. Multiple independent fires in one place suggest a settlement of autonomous households, not a centralized communal economy.

**SP-6. No household utensils beyond gourds: universal poverty with no rank differentiation.**

Cook notes: "I saw no household utensils amongst them except gourds, and of these but very few" (Cook 1846, p. 454). This observation describes universal material poverty with no differentiation by rank. No prestige goods, no accumulated durable wealth, no material markers of elite status in domestic equipment. In chiefdoms documented elsewhere in Polynesia, chiefly households are distinguished by superior material culture (carved bowls, ornamental vessels, specialized cooking equipment, stored valuables). The universal absence of household goods beyond a few gourds is inconsistent with the material differentiation that chiefdoms produce.

**Assessment**: Combined with Cook's observations about the poverty of trade goods (coconut shells as "most valuable article of trade") and the scarcity of chickens, this passage describes a material environment in which the surplus accumulation necessary for chiefly redistribution simply does not exist.

## Summary Assessment

### What This Source Can Tell Us

The Cook 1774 expedition accounts, now including Cook's own narrative alongside the Forster materials, provide the most detailed early European observations of Rapa Nui society. The accounts come from multiple observers with extensive comparative Polynesian experience. Together they provide:

1. **Direct evidence of a named individual (Ko Toheetai) identified by islanders as "aree" (ariki) and acknowledged as a person of some distinction**, wearing differentiated clothing (feathered cap/diadem, mulberry bark cloth dyed with turmeric) and engaging in food distribution during the European visit. Cook's inland party independently encountered a man "seemingly of some note" wearing better cloth, whom they "understood to be the chief of the island" (Cook 1846, p. 451), possibly the same individual.

2. **Cook's explicit admission that he could not determine the nature of chiefly authority.** "But of the power or authority of these chiefs, or of the government of these people, I confess myself quite ignorant" (Cook 1846, pp. 454-455). This is the most epistemologically honest statement in any contact-era account. Cook infers the existence of "chiefs" from the Polynesian analogy ("as at Otaheite") and from the islanders' use of the term "Areekes," but acknowledges he cannot characterize their actual power.

3. **The Forsters' characterization of authority as paternal and non-coercive**, with explicit comparison to (and contrast with) the more hierarchical systems observed in Tahiti, Tonga, and "Asiatic islands." The observers found NO humiliating homage, NO elaborate deference rituals, and described the political system as "neither complicated nor troublesome."

4. **Direct evidence of individual property rights and household-level resource control.** Cook observes a plantation owner driving people from his land (Cook 1846, p. 450); hut owners individually distribute food (Cook 1846, p. 450); the Forsters describe subsistence as requiring "the strength of their own hands."

5. **Direct evidence that statues were identified by islanders as commemorating named chiefs/heroes** (not deities), with human bones found at the base confirming mortuary function. Cook independently reports islanders prefixed "Moi" (burying/sleeping-place) and appended "Areekee" (chief) to statue names (Cook 1846, p. 455).

6. **Confirmed quarry sighting.** Cook's account, reporting Wales's observation, confirms the *Memoire*'s claim and contradicts Georg Forster's published denial that quarries were found. This resolves a key discrepancy among the Forster accounts and demonstrates editorial shaping in Georg Forster's *Voyage*.

7. **Direct evidence of resource poverty, small population (600-900), and absence of features** associated with complex chiefdoms elsewhere in Polynesia (large canoe fleets, redistribution systems, warrior classes, organized labor, surplus production, religious specialists, elaborate deference behavior).

### What This Source Cannot Tell Us

1. **The accounts describe the political situation in March 1774, not pre-contact or prehistoric organization.** By 1774, the island had been contacted by Roggeveen (1722) and Gonzalez (1770). The Forsters themselves recognized evidence of depopulation and decline from a formerly more prosperous state. What they observed cannot be straightforwardly projected backward.

2. **Communication was severely limited.** Cook himself emphasizes this: Mahine (Oedidee) "understood their language much better than any of us; though even he understood it but very imperfectly" (Cook 1846, pp. 454-455). The expedition obtained three different names for the island. Political concepts could not be reliably communicated.

3. **The visit lasted only four days** with limited penetration of the interior. Cook remained at the landing place on the second day due to illness and received the inland party's report secondhand.

4. **The *Memoire* has an explicit ideological agenda** (Enlightenment political philosophy, praise of Frederick the Great's Prussia) that shapes its depiction of Rapa Nui governance as benevolent, simple, and wise. This makes it a less reliable source for political organization than it initially appears.

5. **Multiple versions of events exist** across Cook's narrative, Georg Forster's *Voyage*, Johann Forster's *Journal*, the *Observations*, and the *Memoire*, with significant discrepancies (quarries, singing/dancing, causes of depopulation, war vs. peace). Cook's account now provides an independent check on some of these, resolving the quarry question in favor of the *Memoire* and against the published *Voyage*.

6. **The 1846 compiled edition is not the critical Beaglehole text.** Cook's journal as published in 1846 was edited for a general audience. The Beaglehole edition (1961) provides the scholarly standard text with apparatus. For critical passages, cross-referencing with Beaglehole is advisable when that edition becomes available.

### Key Finding for the Chiefdom Question

The Cook 1774 accounts are **the most important single body of contact-era evidence** for political organization on Rapa Nui, and with Cook's own narrative now directly available, they are **even more clearly ambiguous** than the Forster accounts alone suggested.

The decisive new element is Cook's own epistemological position. Where the Forsters constructed an elaborate (if benign) portrait of paternal governance, Cook simply states: "I confess myself quite ignorant." This matters because:

- Cook was the most experienced Pacific observer of his generation
- He readily identified and described chieftainship in Tahiti, Tonga, and the Society Islands
- He inferred "chiefs" on Rapa Nui only by Polynesian analogy and native vocabulary, not from observed political behavior
- He could not characterize their authority after four days of direct contact

The combined evidence:

- A named individual acknowledged as *aree* with differentiated dress: consistent with some form of social differentiation
- Cook "quite ignorant" of the nature of chiefly power; Forsters describe authority as paternal and non-coercive: no evidence of hierarchical command
- Individual property rights asserted by plantation owners; household-level food production and distribution: consistent with decentralized organization
- Statues commemorating named leaders: consistent with both hierarchical and non-hierarchical models of prestige
- Extreme poverty, tiny population, no surplus extraction, no redistribution: inconsistent with functioning redistributive chiefdom
- No organized labor, no warrior class, no fleet: inconsistent with complex chiefdom
- Confirmed quarry but no active construction; present inhabitants judged incapable of building the monuments

The Forsters explicitly frame the political system as simpler and less hierarchical than what they observed in Tahiti and Tonga. Cook goes further: he does not frame it at all, because he cannot. This combination of the Forsters' "not much" and Cook's "I don't know" is the most honest assessment the contact-era record provides. The chiefdom attribution that rests on this evidence requires reading far more political structure into these accounts than the observers themselves were willing to assert.
