## I. Framing and Background

### The collapse narrative as context

1. "Rapa Nui (Easter Island, Chile) presents a quintessential case where the tempo of investment in monumentality is central to debates regarding societal collapse, with the common narrative positing that statue platform (*ahu*) construction ceased sometime around AD 1600 following an ecological, cultural, and demographic catastrophe." (p. 1, abstract)

2. "This narrative remains especially popular in fields outside archaeology that treat collapse as historical fact and use Rapa Nui as a model for collapse more generally." (p. 1, abstract)

3. "The claim of a pre-contact end to platform *ahu* construction stems from assumptions about a transition in Rapa Nui culture history from an 'Ahu Moai' phase, during which platform *ahu* were constructed and *moai* statues erected upon them, to a period of cultural and demographic collapse termed the 'Huri Moai' phase that saw the toppling of *moai* and destruction of platform *ahu*." (p. 7)

**Assessment**: The paper identifies the "Ahu Moai" to "Huri Moai" phase transition as the core chronological claim of the collapse narrative. This transition is foundational to the chiefly-consolidation model: if ahu construction stopped before European contact, it could be interpreted as evidence of political breakdown or chiefly overreach. Demonstrating that construction continued through contact undermines that interpretation.

4. "The occurrence and chronology of the Huri Moai phase are largely construction activities based upon Englert's (1948) conjecture of AD 1680 as the timing for the outbreak of a war described in oral traditions collected in the late 19th and early 20th centuries; however, archaeological evidence in support of this event is either lacking or has been debunked (Lipo and Hunt, 2009; Mulrooney et al., 2009)." (p. 7)

**Assessment**: The claimed AD 1680 "Huri Moai" date, central to the collapse narrative, rests on Englert's interpretation of post-slave-raid oral traditions, not on independent archaeological evidence. This is a clear instance of the circularity problem: ethnographic interpretations are treated as established chronological markers.

### Previous chronologies for ahu construction

5. "Heyerdahl and Ferdon's (Heyerdahl and Ferdon, 1961) Norwegian archaeological expedition to Rapa Nui in the 1950s provided the first modern attempts to build an absolute chronology for *ahu* construction. They built their chronology based on stratigraphic evidence derived from the excavation of numerous *ahu* complexes as well as 14C dates from *ahu* Vinapu, Te Peu, and several other important sites on the island. Based on this evidence, they argued that the island experienced an early period of *ahu* construction around ca. AD 800 and that this activity continued until AD 1600s." (p. 3)

6. "Martinsson-Wallin et al. (2013) later conducted a select sample of 14C dates from *ahu*, using summed probability distributions (SPD) to estimate the onset and cessation of *ahu* construction. In their visual interpretation of the *ahu* SPD, they suggest that *ahu* complexes 'were securely in place on Rapa Nui by ca. AD 1300-1400' and claimed that a 'destruction phase' for large platform *ahu* occurred around AD 1600 (Martinsson-Wallin et al., 2013, pp. 417, Figure 7)." (p. 3)

7. "This argument for a '[d]egeneration of ceremonial sites' (Wallin and Martinsson-Wallin, 2008, p. 154) during a destruction phase for platform *ahu* around AD 1600 assumes a transition from the 'Ahu Moai' phase to the 'Huri Moai' phase in Rapa Nui culture history (Kirch, 1984, 2017; Smith, 1961a; cf. Mulrooney et al., 2009; Lipo and Hunt, 2009)." (p. 3)

**Assessment**: The paper demonstrates that prior chronological estimates for ahu construction were based on ad hoc visual interpretations of SPD curves and calibrated date lists, not on formal statistical models. This is a methodological critique with direct bearing on the chiefdom question: the chronological framework that supposedly shows a political collapse (cessation of ahu construction ca. AD 1600) was never rigorously established.

8. "These estimates for initial *ahu* construction are limited, however, given that they are not based on formal statistical models but on *ad hoc* visual approximations of the calibrated date lists, or in the case of Martinsson-Wallin et al. (2013), visual approximations of an SPD." (p. 3)

## II. Colonization Estimates

9. "The single-phase colonization model using only short-lived samples estimates initial colonization of Rapa Nui in the range *1150-1290 cal. AD* (Amodel = 105.6, Aoverall = 101)." (p. 5)

10. "Our second model that incorporates unidentified charcoal samples and a *Charcoal Outlier* parameter suggests a slightly more precise colonization estimate of *1150-1280 cal. AD* (Amodel = 121, Aoverall = 120.5)." (p. 5)

**Assessment**: The colonization estimate of 1150-1280 cal. AD is consistent with other recent estimates (Hunt and Lipo 2006, Wilmshurst et al. 2011). This provides the baseline from which to measure the time lag before ahu construction begins.

## III. Ahu Construction Timing Estimates

### Onset of ahu construction

11. "Our earliest estimate comes from *ahu* Rongo 1, which has an initial construction estimate of *1305-1490 cal. AD*, estimated at some 55-285 years after colonization." (p. 6)

12. "The latest initial construction estimate is from *ahu* Tautira, estimated at *1505-1825 cal. AD*, some 260-610 years after colonization." (p. 7)

13. Table 1 (p. 5) provides initial platform construction estimates (95.4% HPD) for all 11 ahu:

14. "What we can confidently state is that initial platform *ahu* construction began sometime between the early-14th and mid-15th centuries AD. These model-based estimates for initial *ahu* construction are later than previous *ad hoc* interpretations of 14C samples (Martinsson-Wallin et al., 2013; Wallin et al., 2010, p. 43; Wallin and Martinsson-Wallin, 2008, p. 154), which suggested initial construction potentially as early as AD 1100-1250." (p. 7)

**Assessment**: The revised (later) onset dates are significant. If ahu construction did not begin until the early 14th to mid-15th centuries, there is a shorter window for the hypothesized "Ahu Moai" phase of chiefly consolidation. The standard culture-historical narrative requires centuries of competitive monument building before the proposed AD 1600-1680 political collapse. The revised dates compress this window substantially.

15. "Specifically, our revised estimates for initial *ahu* construction may be penecontemporaneous with temple (*marae*) construction in central East Polynesia." (p. 7)

**Assessment**: This finding challenges the assumption that Rapa Nui's monumental tradition was uniquely precocious or developed in isolation. If ahu construction timing is comparable to marae construction elsewhere in East Polynesia, the argument that Rapa Nui's monuments reflect a distinctively intense form of chiefly competition is weakened.

### Tempo of ahu construction

16. "The shape of the tempo plots using an AD 1838-1868 cutoff indicate a fairly rapid period of *ahu* construction from ca. AD 1350-1450, followed by a steady tempo of *ahu* construction that continues beyond European contact in AD 1722." (p. 5)

17. "The results indicate that *ahu* construction activities continue into post-contact times, with a flattening of the upper bound of 95% credible interval at ca. 1750." (p. 5)

**Assessment**: This is the paper's central empirical finding. The tempo plot shows no cessation of ahu construction at AD 1600 or any other pre-contact date. Construction continued at a steady rate through European contact in 1722 and likely into the post-contact era. This directly contradicts the collapse narrative's core chronological claim.

18. "The shape of tempo activity plot in Fig. 5 suggests that the rate of activity begins to slowly decline beginning around AD 1550 through the 18th century." (pp. 5-6)

**Assessment**: The decline in the *rate* of new construction beginning around AD 1550 is notable but must be distinguished from a *cessation* of construction. A slowing rate is consistent with many explanations (filling of available coastal locations, shifting investment patterns, demographic changes) and does not require a political collapse.

19. "Using an AD 1771 cutoff produces results that are essentially identical for time periods before AD 1700, but suggests a possible cessation of construction activities around the time of initial European contact in AD 1722 (based on the flattening of the upper bound of the 95% credible interval envelope, Fig. S15)." (p. 6)

20. "However, both iterations of the tempo plots suggest that *ahu* construction activities likely continued at least until European contact in AD 1722." (p. 6)

## IV. Evidence Against Collapse/Cessation Narrative

21. "The most significant results from our Bayesian analyses are the tempo plots for the duration of *ahu* construction activities (Figs. 4 and 5), which provide important falsifying evidence that directly challenges core components of Rapa Nui's collapse narrative. Previous chronologies for platform *ahu* have hypothesized that their construction ceased in the 17th century (e.g., Martinsson-Wallin et al., 2013; Stevenson, 1997; Wallin and Martinsson-Wallin, 2008)." (p. 7)

**Assessment**: The word "falsifying" is deliberately chosen. The tempo plots do not merely fail to support the collapse narrative; they provide positive evidence against the claim that ahu construction ceased before European contact. This is directly relevant to the chiefdom attribution because the collapse narrative is entangled with the argument that chiefly competition drove monument construction to unsustainable levels.

22. "These results suggest that the activities of the so-called 'Ahu Moai' phase that included statue platform construction and use likely continued up to and beyond European contact." (p. 7)

23. "This conclusion is bolstered by the fact that in AD 1722 the Dutch captain Jacob Roggeveen observed rituals being performed by islanders in front of statue platforms, and in 1770 the Spanish also observed that statue platforms were still being used for ritual activity (Corney et al., 1967). For example, Roggeveen (Corney et al., 1967, p. 15) states 'what the form of worship of these people comprises we were not able to gather any full knowledge of, owing to the shortness of our stay among them; we noticed only that they kindle fire in front of certain remarkably tall stone figures they set up; and, thereafter squatting on their heels with heads bowed down, they bring the palms of their hands together and alternately raise and lower them.'" (p. 7)

**Assessment**: The ethnohistoric evidence corroborates the Bayesian modeling results. Roggeveen in 1722 and the Spanish in 1770 both observed active ritual use of ahu, indicating these monuments were still functioning components of the social landscape. This is significant for the chiefdom question because it shows the monumental tradition was not a relic of a collapsed political system by the time of European contact.

24. "As others have argued (Boersema, 2015; Mulrooney et al., 2010), this direct observation suggests that platform *ahu* were still the focus of ritual activity at the point of, and following, European contact. This conclusion suggests that the observations made by the Dutch in AD 1722, and likely the Spanish in AD 1770, were relatively accurate depictions of Rapa Nui communities and their traditions." (p. 7)

## V. Implications for the Resilience Model

25. "Our results demonstrate a lack of evidence for a pre-contact 'collapse' and instead offer strong support for a new emerging model of resilient communities that continued their long-term traditions despite the impacts of European arrival." (p. 1, abstract)

26. "These findings are significant as they highlight the resilience of Rapa Nui communities following the devastating demographic impacts following European arrival (e.g., Fischer, 2005; Hunt and Lipo, 2011; Peiser, 2005; Rainbird, 2002)." (p. 7)

27. "Indeed, the steady continuous nature of construction of *ahu* features in the history of Rapa Nui strongly supports an emerging model in which this group-level activity served as a vital component of communities necessary for long term sustainability on this tiny and remote island (DiNapoli et al., 2019, 2018; Hunt and Lipo, 2018, 2011)." (p. 7)

**Assessment**: The paper explicitly frames ahu construction as "group-level activity" necessary for "long term sustainability," not as a product of chiefly command. The resilience model posits that monument construction was a community-sustaining practice, not one driven by elite competition that led to societal overextension. This framing is directly opposed to the chiefly-consolidation-leading-to-collapse narrative.

## VI. Implications for the Ecocide/Collapse Debate More Broadly

28. "Our results also question recent claims by Rull (2016) and colleagues (Rull et al., 2018) that a major drought ca. AD 1600 caused an end to *moai*/*ahu* construction." (p. 7)

29. "The results of our tempo plots indicate a rapid period of *ahu* construction between ca. AD 1350-1450 with a steady period of construction events that continue into the early historic era." (p. 7)

30. "The results of our Bayesian models, along with recent dates from the Rano Raraku statues quarry (Sherwood et al., 2019; Simpson et al., 2018), indicate there was not a pre-contact 'collapse' in *ahu* or *moai* construction, but that monument activity continued into the post-contact era." (p. 7)

31. "These findings add to the growing corpus of independent lines of evidence contradicting the traditional 'collapse' narrative for Rapa Nui (Hunt and Lipo, 2011; Lipo et al., 2016; Mulrooney, 2013; Mulrooney et al., 2010; Simpson and Dussubieux, 2018), and thus question the results of a broad range of interdisciplinary research on societal collapse that assume the occurrence of this event with certainty." (pp. 7-8)

**Assessment**: The paper positions the tempo plot results as part of a broader convergence of evidence against the collapse narrative. The significance for the chiefdom question is that if the supposed pre-contact collapse never occurred, then the culture-historical framework that organizes Rapa Nui's past into "Ahu Moai" and "Huri Moai" phases, and treats the transition between them as evidence of political transformation, loses its chronological basis.

## VII. Methodological Claims

### Formal Bayesian approach

32. "Resolving the tempo of 'collapse' events, however, is often fraught with ambiguity given a lack of formal modeling, uncritical use of radiocarbon estimates, and inattention to information embedded in stratigraphic features." (p. 1, abstract)

33. "We integrate radiocarbon dates, relative architectural stratigraphy, and ethnohistoric accounts with the recently developed 'tempo plot' technique (Dye, 2016) to provide rigorous, model-based estimates for when monument construction begins, the rate of change in monument construction events, and the most likely timing for the cessation of these activities." (p. 2)

34. "We construct these models using OxCal v.4.3.2 (Bronk Ramsey, 2017). For clarity, we capitalize and italicize OxCal commands." (p. 3)

### Sample selection and model structure

35. "We focus our study on the ca. 150 known platform *ahu*, also called 'image-*ahu*' or *ahu moai*, which are the largest and most common form of pre-contact *ahu* (Martinsson-Wallin, 1994)." (p. 3)

36. "In these models, the determinations from pre-platform, platform, and post-platform events are grouped into unordered *Phases* within an ordered *Sequence*." (p. 4)

37. "For the end of the *Sequence* we input a uniform calendar date range of AD 1838-1868 (*Date(U(1838, 1868))*) in OxCal to serve as a cutoff point for the construction estimates. This choice of AD 1838-1868 is based on historic European accounts of the last time a *moai* statue was recorded as still standing upright on an *ahu* platform, which serves as a conservative estimate for the time period after which we assume no platform *ahu* were built." (p. 4)

### Use of tempo plots

38. "Tempo plots utilize the raw output of OxCal's Markov-Chain-Monte Carlo (MCMC) procedure (using the *MCMC_Sample* query) to summarize the joint posteriors of multiple estimated events and to visualize the Bayes estimate and credible interval of the cumulative temporal distribution of the specified events (Dye, 2016, p. 2). Thus, the tempo plot is a summary of 'how many events took place before each date in a specified range of dates' (Dye, 2016, p. 2), where the slope of the curve relates to the rate at which events occur: steeper and flatter shapes of the curve indicate more rapid or slower frequency of events, respectively." (p. 4)

39. "We also use the 'TempoActivityPlot' function of the ArchaeoPhases package (Philippe et al., 2019) to examine the patterns of *ahu* construction activity. The tempo-activity plot is similar to the normal tempo plot, but instead of plotting the cumulative number of events, it graphically displays the first derivative of the tempo plot curve. As such, the tempo activity plot shows the changing rate of construction events." (p. 4)

**Assessment**: The methodological innovation here is the application of formal Bayesian tempo plots and tempo-activity plots to ahu construction chronology. Previous attempts to characterize ahu construction timing relied on visual inspection of SPD curves or calibrated date lists. The tempo plot approach integrates stratigraphic relationships as prior information, which is critical for Rapa Nui where radiocarbon probability distributions overlap substantially.

## VIII. Charcoal and Inbuilt Age Issues

40. "Samples within the models for *ahu* Rongo 1, Motu Toremo Hiva, Ra'ai, Ature Huki, Akivi, Vai Teka, and Tautira are comprised of unidentified charcoal, and as such these estimates may be affected by inbuilt age." (p. 5)

41. "The most secure estimates come from *ahu* Nau Nau (*1410-1450 cal. AD*, 145-285 years after colonization), Heki'i (*1320-1445 cal. AD*, 70-260 years after colonization), and Te Niu (*1415-1615 cal. AD*, 165-405 years after colonization)." (p. 7)

42. "Given that many of the 14C dates from our sample of 11 *ahu* are from unidentified charcoal only strengthen this result, as their potential for inbuilt age may indicate that these activities occurred even more recently." (p. 7)

**Assessment**: The inbuilt age issue actually strengthens the paper's main finding. If unidentified charcoal dates are biased older, then the true construction dates may be even later than the model estimates, pushing ahu construction even further into the post-contact era. This means the evidence against a pre-contact cessation of construction is, if anything, conservative.

## IX. Limitations Acknowledged

43. "While our results are based on 11 of ca. 150 known platform *ahu* on Rapa Nui, this represents the largest possible sample of features with reliable chronological data and thus our results provide the most complete island-wide synthesis currently possible." (p. 7)

44. "It is possible, however, that initial *ahu* construction began earlier at locations not covered by our sample." (p. 7)

45. "In particular, our analysis lacks samples from Rapa Nui's south coast, which contains some of the highest densities of large platform *ahu* on the island (Martinsson-Wallin, 1994)." (p. 7)

46. "Given uncertainties with obsidian hydration dating on Rapa Nui and elsewhere (see section 4 in Supplementary Materials), these dates could not be included in our Bayesian models." (p. 7)

**Assessment**: The 11-ahu sample represents a genuine limitation. The south coast, with its dense ahu distribution, is not represented. However, the paper is transparent about this and argues that the results represent the best available synthesis. For the chiefdom question, the key finding (no pre-contact cessation of construction) is robust because it is based on the presence of ongoing construction events, not on a complete census of all ahu.

## X. Claims About Population Dynamics

47. "Current estimates suggest that Polynesian voyagers initially colonized the island around the 13th century AD (e.g., Hunt and Lipo, 2008, 2006; Lipo and Hunt, 2016; Wilmshurst et al., 2011)." (p. 2)

48. The paper's colonization estimate of 1150-1280 cal. AD "is in broad agreement with previous estimates based on short-lived samples (Hunt and Lipo, 2006; Lipo and Hunt, 2016; Wilmshurst et al., 2011)." (p. 6)

49. "These findings are significant as they highlight the resilience of Rapa Nui communities following the devastating demographic impacts following European arrival." (p. 7)

**Assessment**: The paper does not directly model population size, but the steady tempo of ahu construction through European contact implies that the communities building these monuments maintained sufficient social organization and labor capacity to continue monument construction. This is inconsistent with a narrative of pre-contact demographic collapse.

## XI. Relationship to Ahu as Evidence for Political Organization

50. "Monumental architecture is central to explanations of Rapa Nui culture history and the proposed collapse of its pre-contact society." (p. 2)

51. "*Ahu* construction and elaboration are commonly used as evidence for increasing social complexity and fission-fusion patterns among Rapa Nui's social groups (Stevenson, 2002, 1997, 1986; Wallin and Martinsson-Wallin, 2008)." (p. 2)

52. "In addition, numerous archaeological narratives for the island posit that an accelerated pace of monument construction, during the 'Ahu Moai' phase, led to an environmental and demographic collapse around the 17th century." (p. 2)

**Assessment**: The paper identifies the key assumption linking ahu construction to the chiefdom model: that increasing ahu elaboration is read as evidence for increasing social complexity, and that the cessation of construction is read as evidence for political collapse. By showing that construction did not cease before European contact, the paper removes the empirical basis for the second half of this narrative. The first half (increasing elaboration = increasing complexity) remains untested by this study but is addressed by the tempo plots, which show a rapid initial period of construction followed by steady activity, not an ever-accelerating escalation.

## Summary of Claims Relevant to Chiefdom Attribution

DiNapoli et al. 2020 provides the first formal Bayesian chronological model for ahu construction timing, integrating radiocarbon dates with architectural stratigraphy and ethnohistoric accounts. The central finding is that ahu construction began in the early 14th to mid-15th centuries AD and continued at a steady rate through European contact in 1722 and likely into the post-contact era.

Key implications for the chiefdom question:

1. **No pre-contact cessation of ahu construction.** The tempo plots provide "important falsifying evidence that directly challenges core components of Rapa Nui's collapse narrative" (p. 7). The hypothesized AD 1600-1680 end to monument construction, foundational to the "Ahu Moai" to "Huri Moai" phase transition, is not supported.

2. **The culture-historical phase model loses its chronological basis.** If there was no cessation of ahu construction before contact, the phase framework (Ahu Moai / Huri Moai) that organizes the chiefdom-consolidation-and-collapse narrative cannot be sustained in its current form.

3. **Steady tempo, not escalating competition.** The tempo plots show a rapid initial period (ca. AD 1350-1450) followed by steady construction, not an accelerating pace of monument building that would be consistent with intensifying chiefly competition.

4. **Construction framed as group-level activity.** The paper interprets ahu construction as community-level activity necessary for sustainability, not as a product of chiefly command. The word "community" is used throughout, not "chief" or "elite."

5. **Revised onset dates compress the timeline.** Initial ahu construction beginning in the early 14th to mid-15th centuries, rather than AD 1100-1250 as previously suggested, compresses the window available for the hypothesized period of chiefly consolidation through competitive monument building.

6. **Inbuilt charcoal age strengthens the finding.** Because many dates come from unidentified charcoal with potential inbuilt age, the true construction dates may be even later, pushing activity further into the contact and post-contact era.

7. **Previous chronological frameworks were methodologically inadequate.** The paper demonstrates that prior estimates relied on ad hoc visual interpretations of SPD curves and calibrated date lists, not on formal statistical models. The chronological basis for the collapse narrative was never rigorously established.
