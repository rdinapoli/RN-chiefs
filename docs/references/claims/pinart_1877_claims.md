# Claims Extraction: Pinart 1877

## Source Information

**Visitor**: Alphonse Pinart, French anthropologist and ethnographer, age 25. Arrived on the warship *Seignelay* under Commandant Lafontaine, accompanied by Captain Aube, ship's doctor Thoulon, surveyor Escande, and photographer Berryer.

**Date of visit**: April 1-6, 1877 (six days ashore).

**Primary source consulted**: Pinart's account as translated from French by Ann M. Altman, in *Early Visitors to Easter Island, 1864-1877*, Parts 4-5 (Easter Island Foundation). Printed pages 104-135. Referenced below as "Pinart/Altman" with printed page numbers.

**Context of visit**: By April 1877, the island was in the most extreme phase of post-contact demographic collapse. Dutrou-Bornier, the French settler who had dominated the island since the late 1860s, had died in August 1876 (murdered by islanders, though islanders told visitors he died falling from a horse). Most of the population had been removed to Tahiti by Dutrou-Bornier and the missionaries during their conflict in the early 1870s. Only 111 people remained, including just 26 women. Traditional political structure was completely gone. The "queen" and "regent" described by Pinart were Dutrou-Bornier's widow and daughter, a colonial-era imposition with no connection to traditional Rapanui authority. Pinart was a trained ethnographer whose account provides the most archaeologically detailed descriptions of any visitor in the Altman compilation, but his ethnographic observations are necessarily limited because there was almost no traditional society left to observe.

**[POST-RAID]**: All claims in this file describe conditions 14 years after the 1862-1863 slave raids reduced the population from approximately 3,000 to fewer than 200, and after the subsequent removal of most survivors to Tahiti. Nothing described by Pinart can be treated as evidence of traditional pre-contact Rapanui political organization.

## Claims Extraction

### 1. Political Authority (Colonial)

**1.1. Koreto as "regent" and her daughter as "queen"** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** When Pinart's party approached Mataveri, "almost all the inhabitants came out to meet us. Their cries of ia-ora-na were deafening. They were carrying bananas, which they distributed among us; they relieved our men of their loads to carry them themselves, and they took us directly to the home of the queen" (p. 124). Koreto, the widow of Dutrou-Bornier, received them formally: "She was wearing a wide piece of tapa cloth, in the Tahitian manner; a panama hat, and a piece of Scottish plaid around her shoulders. In bare feet, standing between her daughters, she was waiting for us at the door of her home" (p. 124).

**(b) Reported tradition.** "Her mother explained that this daughter was, in fact, the queen and that she, her mother, was only acting as regent" (p. 124). Koreto's older daughter, "a child of five or six," wore "a tiara of shiny base metal, the decoration of some wooden statue of a saint that the missionaries had taken away with them" (pp. 124-125).

**Assessment**: The "queen" status derives entirely from Dutrou-Bornier's self-appointed colonial authority, not from traditional Rapanui political structure. The tiara is a repurposed Christian artifact. The formal reception imitates European diplomatic protocol ("She took off her hat gravely with both hands to respond to our greetings, extended her hand and introduced her two daughters to us," p. 124), not any recorded Rapanui custom. This tells us about the colonial situation in 1877, not about pre-contact political organization.

**1.2. The "major domo" maintaining order** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "The entire village was assembled in the interior courtyard. A kind of major domo with a baton in his hand seemed to be maintaining order" (p. 124).

**Assessment**: Pinart uses the European term "major domo" for this individual. The role, maintaining order in Koreto's courtyard during the reception of French visitors, is entirely a function of the colonial household established by Dutrou-Bornier. Whether this individual held any traditional Rapanui status is impossible to determine from this description.

**1.3. Koreto's property claims and authority over resources** [POST-RAID]

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) Direct observation.** "She also told us that all the cows, sheep and goats on the island belonged to her and she was putting them at our disposal. She asked us not to touch the chickens since they were the exclusive property of the Kanacs and she also told us that all the pigs that we might encounter ought to be killed" (p. 125).

**Assessment**: Koreto claims ownership of Dutrou-Bornier's introduced livestock (cows, sheep, goats) while distinguishing them from the chickens, which belong to the islanders. This property regime is entirely colonial. The distinction between Koreto's livestock and "Kanac" chickens reflects Dutrou-Bornier's imposed economic arrangements, not traditional Rapanui resource allocation.

**1.4. Koreto's imitation of French customs** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "She imitated our table manners and copied every gesture, drinking and eating as we did; saying Mereti (merci = thank you) when she was served. She insisted that the knives and forks that she and her daughters used be changed, banging impatiently on the table when the sailor who was serving us did not follow her orders sufficiently promptly" (p. 125).

Pinart reflects: "It was very strange to observe the gestures and behavior of this native queen, now the regent of Easter Island, as she made an effort to imitate French customs, combining the ignorance of her primitive background with the inherent demands of her title" (p. 125).

**Assessment**: Pinart's observation captures Koreto performing a European-derived authority role. Her "title" and behavioral model are entirely imported. This passage is revealing about colonial imposition but tells us nothing about pre-contact political structures.

**1.5. Request for French protection and political preferences** [POST-RAID]

**(b) Reported tradition.** "Koreto told us how much she and her people wished to come under French protection, and she did not disguise her aversion for the Chileans, the Americans and the Germans, an aversion shared, moreover, we must immediately add, by the island notables who were present during this conversation" (pp. 125-126).

**Assessment**: Pinart uses the term "island notables" for individuals present during the diplomatic discussion. Whether these "notables" held traditional status or were simply senior men in the remnant community is unclear. Their shared preference for French protection and hostility toward other foreign powers reflects the post-Dutrou-Bornier political situation, not pre-contact political organization.

**1.6. Dutrou-Bornier's distribution of property before death** [POST-RAID]

**(b) Reported tradition.** Koreto's account of Dutrou-Bornier's death: "Before he died, the colonialist captain had burned his papers, bequeathed all his property to the queen and her daughters, and distributed his clothing among the most important Kanacs in the village" (p. 125).

**Assessment**: The distribution of clothing to "the most important Kanacs" implies recognized social differentiation within the remnant community. Whether this differentiation reflected pre-contact rank, age seniority, kinship position, or simply individuals who had been useful to Dutrou-Bornier cannot be determined.

### 2. Population and Settlement

**2.1. Total population: 111 people, 26 women** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "In all, the population consists of only 111 men, women and children. ... We only counted 26 in the entire population" (of women) (p. 129).

**Assessment**: This is the most extreme documented phase of population collapse. Compare with Eyraud 1864 (approximately 1,200 people), Roussel 1869 (approximately 650 people), Loti 1872 (approximately 100 people). The catastrophically skewed sex ratio (26 women out of 111) reflects the removals to Tahiti and earlier slave raids. A population of 111 with only 26 women cannot sustain any traditional political structure requiring multiple kinship groups, territorial divisions, or lineage-based authority.

**2.2. Village of Mataveri** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "The village of Mataveri consists of about thirty huts, all built along the same lines and forming a rectangle that surrounds the village square. The home of Dutrou-Bornier, now occupied by the queen, is on the left" (p. 127).

"The huts are mostly made of wood from shipwrecks and resemble huts in Europe except in so far as they lack windows. They have one opening, which serves as the door, which is 60 to 80 centimeters high. A few ancient reed huts, also with very small entrances, can also still be seen at this site" (p. 127).

**Assessment**: The rectangular village layout surrounding a central square is not a traditional Rapanui settlement pattern. The huts are built of European shipwreck timber. Only "a few ancient reed huts" (traditional hare paenga) survive. The settlement pattern is entirely post-contact.

**2.3. Dutrou-Bornier's house** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "Dutrou-Bornier had built himself a home of the type favored by planters in the Southern states, surrounded by a vast verandah around which a vigorous growth of vines was entwined. ... The garden was divided into squares, with paths bordered by arbors made of vines. It was full of fig trees, almond trees, peach trees and mulberry trees, all of which were flourishing" (pp. 127-128).

He had also "planted a vast plantation of vines" and in front of the house "the finest sugar cane that we had seen on the island" (p. 128). At Hanga Piko, Dutrou-Bornier "had built a small harbor where he could safely anchor the two-masted sailing boat that he had built for his excursions in the coastal waters of the island" (p. 128).

**Assessment**: This describes a colonial plantation establishment, not traditional Rapanui settlement. The introduced plants (fig trees, almond trees, peach trees, vines) and European-style architecture testify to Dutrou-Bornier's transformation of the landscape.

**2.4. Mission church at Vaihu** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "The mission church is a vast building made of planks from the United States, capable of holding six hundred people" (p. 123). Beside it, "a wall surrounds two little houses and a garden. One of the little houses is made of wood, the other has dry-stone walls. In the garden, which has been abandoned, there are a few vines, some fig trees, some Chinese peonies" (p. 123).

**Assessment**: A church built to hold 600 people for a population of 111 testifies to the scale of recent depopulation. The abandoned garden documents the missionary withdrawal.

### 3. Monuments and Archaeology

**3.1. Rano Raraku quarry: interior crater statues** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "There were about forty of them, spread out over the interior face of the crater in three separate groups, all facing north. They were all very similar; many were lying down; and one had been completely carved but had not yet been detached from the rock" (p. 113).

Pinart describes the crater itself: "Extending for six hundred meters in greatest diameter, the oval crater of Ronororaka has a shallow slope that extends for eighty to a hundred meters" (p. 113).

**Assessment**: The observation of approximately 40 interior statues in three groups, all north-facing, is a direct archaeological record. No information about political organization can be derived from this description.

**3.2. Rano Raraku quarry: exterior slope statues** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** On the southeast slope, Pinart saw statues "at various stages of completion" and noted "another natural amphitheater, identical to the first and close to other groups of statues that numbered eighty in all. These statues are upright and differ from those in the interior of the crater only insofar as they have longer noses or thicker lips" (p. 121).

**Assessment**: Approximately 80 exterior statues documented. The observation of morphological differences between interior and exterior statues (longer noses, thicker lips) is an archaeological detail.

**3.3. Pinart's theory of statue erection** [POST-RAID]

**(c) Author's inference.** Pinart proposes that sculptors chose steep slopes, carved statues in situ, detached them by cutting "a myriad of small parallel channels of about eight centimeters in diameter beneath the statue," then slid them downhill to prepared holes. The statue was gradually raised "with the help of fragments of rock that were pushed under it, forming an inclined plane" (pp. 114-115).

**Assessment**: This is Pinart's inference from the physical evidence, not a report of traditional knowledge. The islanders evidently provided no oral tradition about moai transport or erection methods, consistent with the complete loss of such knowledge documented in section 7 below.

**3.4. Statue measurements** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** Largest statue on the southeast slope: "seven meters high from the chest up" (p. 115). Another statue measured: "height of the forehead, 2.0 meters; length of the nose, 3.40 meters; distance from the nose to the lips, 0.75 meters; height of the chin, 2 meters; and length of the body, 12 meters" (p. 118). Another bust: "height of the forehead, 1.25 meters; length of the nose, 2.80 meters; length of the mouth and the chin, 1.75 meters; length of the rest of the body 8.50 meters" (p. 118).

**Assessment**: These are direct physical measurements. The 12-meter body length is among the larger statues documented by any visitor.

**3.5. Obsidian implements near statues** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation / (c) Author's inference.** "On the side of the volcano, near the statues, just as at other sites on the island where there are statues, we found a large number of obsidian blades, carved into the shape of scrapers, knives and spearheads etc. Could these be the tools with which the statues had been carved? Even if this suggestion might seem surprising at first, it does seem plausible especially if one recalls the limited hardness of the rock and the ease with which it can be worked" (pp. 115-116).

**Assessment**: The observation of obsidian implements near statues is direct and archaeologically significant. Pinart's inference that these were carving tools is incorrect; the actual carving tools were basalt toki, as subsequent archaeological work has established. The obsidian mata'a found near quarry sites had other functions.

**3.6. Ahu ("pakaopa") description at Opulu (probably Akahanga)** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** Pinart describes an ahu in detail: "this terrace must have been a platform of 50 meters high, 200 meters long and 10 meters wide" (p. 120). Altman's endnote 7 observes that "the cited dimensions of the pakaopa would have benefited from the attention of an editor with access to the actual dimensions, as recorded by Pinart. The dimensions given are not possible" (p. 134). The height of 50 meters is clearly an error, likely a misprint; the dimensions of length and width are more plausible.

"Its sides were inclined and were made of slabs that had been cut without great skill. The interior, as far as we could tell, was filled with fragments of rocks. Above it was built a second terrace that was 5 meters wide, 1.70 meters high and 1.50 meters long, built out of big slabs laid down side by side" (p. 120).

The platform had red scoria facings: "a sculpted ledge, also made of red lava and 1.40 meters long by 70 centimeters high, the perfectly flat face of which was decorated in bas relief with figures that had been rather finely carved" (p. 121). Altman's endnote 8 identifies this as possibly "the ahu at Akahanga with its red scoria facings that contain petroglyphs" (p. 134).

**Assessment**: Pinart's is the most detailed ahu description from this era. The two-tiered platform, red scoria facings with bas-relief carvings, and interior fill composition are all archaeologically significant observations. Altman's identification with Akahanga is based on Pinart's map and the distance description. The term "pakaopa" that Pinart uses for ahu may reflect a misunderstanding; Altman notes (endnote 6, p. 134) that the word properly refers to a specific statue, not to ahu as a category.

**3.7. Burial chambers within ahu** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "The interior of the terrace contained burial chambers of quite considerable size, made of slabs placed one on top of the other, such that the ones on the top sealed each sarcophagus hermetically. The chambers were, on average, 2 meters long and 80 centimeters wide. A rather large number of cadavers seemed to have been placed there in no particular order" (p. 121).

**Assessment**: The presence of multiple burials "in no particular order" within ahu chambers is an archaeological observation. Pinart does not describe differential burial treatment that might indicate rank.

**3.8. Pukao ("cylindrical hats of red lava")** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** Pinart describes fallen pukao near the ahu at Opulu: "the crowns or headdresses of the statues from the pakaopa, which had rolled this far when the statues had been pushed over. They consisted of cylinders of red lava and were partly buried in the ground. It seems plausible that they had been balanced on the heads of the statues, which are generally flat. These cylinders were seventy to eighty centimeters high, with a diameter of fifty to sixty centimeters" (pp. 120-121).

At Puna Pau, Escande "set off towards Tauatapu, the site from which the natives carved the cylindrical hats of the statues and where he saw a considerable number that had already been completed" (p. 131).

**Assessment**: Pinart's observation that the pukao had "rolled" from the ahu when the statues were toppled is consistent with the archaeological evidence. His measurements and Escande's observation of completed pukao at Puna Pau are direct archaeological records.

**3.9. Petroglyphs and tattooed statues** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** On a statue at Rano Raraku: "a line of tattooing composed of little raised circles on the nose and the entire length of the body. The body itself was covered in sculpted bands" (p. 119). At the same location, "behind the head we collected a few calcified bones, which suggested that the statue might have been the focus of some ancient cult" (p. 119).

Another statue had "traces of tattoos that seemed to have been painted in red on its nose and chin" (p. 119).

On a cross-shaped rock engraving: "two signs, one representing a kind of bird and the other representing a person" (p. 118).

**Assessment**: These are direct observations of sculptural detail and associated finds. Pinart's inference about an "ancient cult" is speculative.

**3.10. Paved paths near quarry** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "In various places we found a type of paved path, bordered by carved stones of 1.20 meters in length, 15 centimeters high and 10 centimeters thick, with small round openings incised along a median line" (p. 121).

**(c) Author's inference.** "Perhaps they were also involved in ceremonies associated with the cult of the primitive islanders" (p. 121).

**Assessment**: The paved paths with carved border stones are an archaeological observation. Pinart's inference about ceremonial function is speculative.

### 4. Settlement Patterns

**4.1. Stone wall with burials behind Ovahe** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "Behind the village of Ovahe, we noticed a long wall of stones that had been piled with great regularity upon one another. The wall was about 50 meters long, 4 meters wide, and 1.5 meters high. There were bones mixed in with the construction materials and Monsieur Thoulon, the ship's doctor on the Seignelay was able to dig out twenty skulls and two complete skeletons" (pp. 110-111).

"Numerous little tumuli composed of carefully piled stones were distributed at regular intervals on the wall and what was remarkable about them was that, from a certain distance, they resembled crouching men. These tumuli, which were sometimes circular and sometimes pyramidal, had served as sepulchers" (p. 111).

**Assessment**: This wall-and-tumuli complex is an archaeological feature. Pinart describes it as containing burials throughout, with individual tumuli on top. The structure may correspond to what archaeologists would identify as an ahu or burial platform.

**4.2. Ruins of circular and rectangular huts** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "Ruins of circular and rectangular huts were distributed among these remains; they were made of fragments of lava and their roofs, most probably made of plant material, had disappeared entirely" (p. 111).

**Assessment**: Direct observation of abandoned settlement ruins. The circular and rectangular forms are consistent with known Rapanui house types.

**4.3. "Deep gardens" (manavai)** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "Spaced at relatively large intervals over the entire area, there were oval or circular pits of 1 or 2 meters in depth, with a diameter of 3 to 12 meters. There was a wall around the mouth of each pit and, at the bottom, banana trees, sugar cane and Dracaena terminalis (known to the natives as tii)" (p. 112).

**(c) Author's inference.** "The structure of these pits, which one might call deep gardens, can easily be explained by an examination of the soil, which is basically composed of volcanic ash and decomposed lava and is, thus, very porous and unable to retain the moisture required for growing plants. To overcome this problem, it was necessary for the natives to dig down to a certain level to reach the moisture that is indispensable for plant growth. Perhaps, also, the people who built the pits were trying to protect their plants from the incessant high winds that blow in from the sea" (p. 112).

On one side of each pit wall, "there was generally an opening that faced towards the south and permits entry into a chamber built of stones that probably served as a dwelling" (p. 112).

**Assessment**: These are manavai (stone-walled garden enclosures), a well-documented Rapanui horticultural feature. Pinart's functional inference about wind protection and moisture retention is consistent with modern archaeological understanding. The associated stone chambers may be related habitation or storage structures.

**4.4. Scattered village remains between Opulu and Vaihu** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "There are few remains of Kanac villages in this part of the island: a few dry-stone walls of 1.00 to 1.25 meters in height, arranged in circles or quadrangles, are the only indications left of the sites of earlier homes. Nonetheless, these walls remain intact" (p. 123).

**Assessment**: The sparseness of village remains along the south coast, contrasted with the density of monumental architecture (ahu, tumuli), is an archaeological observation. Pinart does not comment on what this pattern might imply about settlement organization.

**4.5. Tumuli and stone pyramids distributed throughout the landscape** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "We kept coming across tumuli and, on all sides, piles of rocks covered the remains of dead islanders. We were in the center of a vast necropolis" (p. 122). "Platforms, tumuli and elevated sites were all covered with them and they formed an impressive display that resembled an army of crouching men" (p. 122).

**Assessment**: Pinart describes a landscape densely covered with funerary monuments, consistent with the extensive ahu and burial platforms documented by later archaeological surveys along the south coast.

**4.6. Hotuiti plain: ruined village** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** From above, Pinart could "look out over the Hotuiti plain, with its ruined village, deep gardens, huts and tumuli" (p. 117).

**Assessment**: Brief reference to an abandoned settlement complex visible from Rano Raraku, including houses, gardens, and burial structures.

**4.7. Cave use for shelter and burial** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** At La Perouse Bay: "many hidden caves. The first thing that we did was to visit some of these caves and we could tell that the natives often took shelter here when they were in these parts. One of the caves contained a skeleton that was still enveloped in matting, as well as many skulls" (p. 108).

The Kanacs they encountered "after spending the night in the caves on the cliff, were waiting to escort us" (p. 110).

**Assessment**: Caves served both as shelter during travel and as burial sites. The continued use of caves for overnight shelter in 1877 indicates that settlement was concentrated at Mataveri, with the rest of the island traversed but not permanently inhabited by the remnant population.

**4.8. Contemporary burial practices** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "The natives took advantage of ancient graves to bury their dead, being content simply to remove a few stones and dig a shallow pit in which they laid the body which they would then cover, but only barely, with the stones that they had removed originally" (pp. 108-109).

Also: "Today the islanders take advantage, as we noted above, of any place that they can find to bury their dead, sometimes putting the bodies under fallen statues, sometimes in the pakaopas, and they content themselves with just removing a few stones to make space for the body" (p. 121).

**Assessment**: The expedient reuse of ancient burial structures for contemporary burials, with minimal effort, contrasts with the elaborate construction of the original ahu and suggests that the cultural practices and labor organization associated with monumental burial had been lost.

### 5. Material Culture

**5.1. Rongorongo tablets: loss of literacy** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation / (c) Author's inference.** "The signs on talking wood, as it is called by the islanders, are indecipherable by the present-day islanders. The very occasional tablets that one comes across are used by the natives to spool the cord that they use for fishing lines or nets and it is to this use that is responsible for the survival of such tablets as remain" (p. 130).

Pinart adds: "Many examples of these carvings, which are very rare today, are preserved at the museum in Santiago. Easter Island is the only Polynesian island on which such tablets have been found and they are very likely the work of the same people as the ones that erected the monumental statues" (p. 130).

**Assessment**: The rongorongo tablets being used as fishing line spools represents a total loss of the knowledge system they embodied. No one on the island could read them. This is among the most striking evidence of cultural discontinuity. Pinart's inference that the tablets were made by the same people who carved the statues is his own speculation.

**5.2. Moai kavakava (small wooden statuettes)** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "Many of the islanders wear small statuettes, generally carved from a type of mimosa that is quite common on the island. They value these statuettes highly, as we found when we tried, with difficulty, to get them to exchange them for tobacco. The figures represent both males and females. The Kanacs store them in little bags made of linen or cotton fabric. Some of the statuettes have necklaces and a huge head of carefully arranged hair. There are also statuettes made of stone that are exact miniature reproductions of the statues in the crater" (p. 130).

**Assessment**: The continued production, wearing, and high valuation of moai kavakava is one of the few areas where traditional material culture persisted in 1877. The use of linen or cotton bags (imported fabrics) for storage shows hybrid practices. Altman's endnotes identify the male figures as moai kavakava and the females as moai pa'a pa'a (endnote 13, p. 134), and the wood as Sophora toromiro, now extinct on the island (endnote 12, p. 134).

**5.3. Rei-miro (crescent ornament)** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "The Kanacs have, in addition, a crescent shape ornament, a kind of neck guard, whose purpose we were unable to determine" (p. 130).

**Assessment**: Pinart could not determine the function of the rei-miro, and his informants apparently did not explain it. Altman's endnote identifies it as a status symbol known throughout the Pacific (endnote 14, p. 135). Pinart's inability to learn its purpose is consistent with the general loss of traditional knowledge.

**5.4. Clothing: predominantly European** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "Almost everyone is dressed in European clothes but some, nonetheless, only have a type of jacket or overcoat around their shoulders and a piece of cloth between their thighs" (p. 129). The first Kanacs they encountered "were dressed in European clothes" (p. 108). Koreto wore tapa "in the Tahitian manner," a panama hat, and Scottish plaid (p. 124).

**Assessment**: European clothing had almost entirely replaced traditional dress by 1877. Even Koreto's tapa was worn "in the Tahitian manner," suggesting missionary-mediated Polynesian rather than specifically Rapanui practice.

### 6. Physical Anthropology

**6.1. Physical measurements and descriptions** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "The average height of the native men on the Easter Island is 1.57 meters and that of the women is 1.52 meters. Their chest is narrow and slightly concave, the clavicles are prominent, the head is relatively elongated, the forehead is non-protruding, the cheekbones are moderately prominent, the nose is rather slender with fairly wide nostrils, the lips are thick, the eyes are black, lively and expressive, and the skin is a bronzed brown" (pp. 128-129).

**Assessment**: These physical descriptions reflect Pinart's training as an anthropologist in the physical-anthropological tradition of his era. The average height of 1.57m (approximately 5'2") for men may reflect nutritional stress in the post-collapse population. These measurements describe the remnant 1877 population and cannot be extrapolated to the pre-contact population.

**6.2. Physical capacity despite weak constitutions** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "Even though they are not very muscular, they are able to carry fairly heavy loads on their heads. We saw Old Tago carry, in this way, a sack weighing about 35 kilograms from Ronororaka to Mataveri. These people are indefatigable walkers" (p. 129).

**Assessment**: The distance from Rano Raraku to Mataveri is approximately 18 km. Carrying 35 kg over this distance demonstrates considerable physical endurance despite Pinart's assessment of "weak constitutions."

**6.3. Diet and sobriety** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "Their diet, which is basically vegetarian and is composed almost exclusively of bananas and a type of watermelon, probably contributes to their weak constitutions. The men exhibit remarkable sobriety. They obstinately refuse any alcohol, even wine. By contrast, they are very keen to acquire European clothes and tobacco" (p. 129).

**Assessment**: The limited diet (bananas and watermelon) reflects the collapse of traditional agriculture. Pre-contact Rapanui diet included sweet potatoes, taro, yams, chicken, and marine resources. The refusal of alcohol is notable given that Dutrou-Bornier reportedly died while drunk; it may reflect a deliberate avoidance response.

**6.4. Women's conduct and men's childcare** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "The conduct of the women is irreproachable" (p. 129). "Both the men and the women demonstrate their great affection for their children. It is not unusual to see men acting as the most devoted caretakers of their seven- or eight-month-old infants" (p. 129).

**Assessment**: Pinart's observation about paternal childcare contrasts with some earlier visitors' descriptions of women being sent to ships. The "irreproachable" conduct of women may reflect the extreme scarcity of women (26 out of 111), making their behavior particularly scrutinized, or it may reflect genuine cultural norms about male caregiving.

**6.5. Tattoo patterns** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "Only one old man, Tago, had a full-body and very complex tattoo. Most of the women had tattooed faces. The designs consist of a circular blue line that extends from the temples almost to the eyebrows and ends near the center of the forehead, at the hairline. It is accompanied on the outer sides by a series of blue dots and another line, also blue, surrounds the mouth. Another type of tattoo represents a stone ax with a handle. The end of the handle starts at the ear lobe. The ax is drawn on the cheek such that the blade extends to the outer angle of the eye" (pp. 129-130).

"The ear lobe, which is pierced, hangs down to the level of the chin and is horribly deformed. The entire contours of the hole, and the cartilage, are decorated with a line of blue dots. Circular tattoos surround both the wrist and the ankle" (pp. 129-130).

**Assessment**: The fact that only one old man had full-body tattooing suggests this practice had died out. Women's facial tattoos persisted more widely. The detailed descriptions of specific tattoo designs (the ax motif, circular lines, dot patterns) are among the most precise in the ethnographic record. These surviving tattoo patterns may reflect traditional designs but describe a practice in terminal decline.

**6.6. Ear elongation** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** Ear lobes "pierced" and hanging "down to the level of the chin," described as "horribly deformed" (p. 130).

**Assessment**: Ear elongation is documented consistently across earlier contact-era accounts (Roggeveen 1722, Cook 1774). Its persistence in 1877 is one of the few bodily practices connecting the remnant population to the pre-contact tradition.

---

### 7. Absence of Traditional Knowledge

**7.1. Complete loss of historical memory** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation / (c) Author's inference.** "The present inhabitants of the Easter Island, as we had seen, had retained no memory at all of those who had come before them. This total absence of traditions suggests, perhaps, that the sculptors of long ago might not even have been the ancestors of the present-day Kanacs and that these generations had disappeared entirely, perhaps because of insufficient space on the island, perhaps as a result of emigration, perhaps even as a result of destruction by an invasion by Polynesians, from which the natives at Vahou appeared to have been descended" (pp. 122-123).

**Assessment**: The observation that islanders "had retained no memory at all of those who had come before them" is Pinart's single most important ethnographic finding. His inference from this, that the statue builders may have been a different people, is a common 19th-century trope now rejected by archaeological and genetic evidence. The correct explanation is that the slave raids of 1862-1863 killed or removed the knowledge holders (including the ariki, tangata rongorongo, and other ritual specialists), and the subsequent depopulation and missionary conversion destroyed the remaining channels of cultural transmission. This passage demonstrates that by 1877, the islanders could provide no oral traditions about their own monumental past, making post-1877 ethnographic accounts (Thompson 1891, Routledge 1919, Metraux 1940) dependent on whatever fragments were later recovered or reconstructed, not on a continuous tradition.

---

### 8. Death of Dutrou-Bornier

**8.1. Islanders' account of Dutrou-Bornier's death** [POST-RAID]

**(b) Reported tradition.** Islanders told Pinart on arrival that Dutrou-Bornier "had died from falling off his horse while drunk" (p. 109).

**(a) Direct observation.** Pinart noted that "the way the Kanacs drew our attention to their clothes seemed to suggest something abnormal and we were not far from thinking that they might have murdered Dutrou-Bornier" (pp. 108-109). However, he adds: "We learned later on that our suspicions had been unfounded" (p. 109).

**(b) Reported tradition.** Koreto's version: his death "had followed a fall from his horse" (p. 125).

Altman's endnote 2 provides the actual circumstances from Mouly (1935, p. 147): "This version, circulated for the benefit of foreigners, hides a tragedy. The exasperated population had responded to violence with more violence; the islanders had killed the foreigner by stabbing him with his own bayonette." Mouly adds: "According to Father Bienvenido, a man called Papiano appears to have protected the two little daughters of Dutrou-Bornier from the wrath of his compatriots by hiding them in his oven" (pp. 133-134).

**Assessment**: The islanders' coordinated cover story (horse fall while drunk) was told consistently to Pinart by multiple people, including Koreto herself. The actual murder by stabbing, confirmed by Mouly's later account, demonstrates that the islanders could organize collective deception toward outsiders. The protection of Dutrou-Bornier's daughters by Papiano suggests that the killing was directed at the colonist specifically, not at his children. The capacity for collective violence against an oppressor and the subsequent coordinated false narrative implies some degree of communal decision-making, though whether this was organized by specific leaders or emerged from collective action cannot be determined.

**8.2. Pinart's favorable assessment of Dutrou-Bornier** [POST-RAID]

**(c) Author's inference.** Pinart describes Dutrou-Bornier as "a devout and courageous sea captain who had not been afraid to come and live on an inhospitable island with the aim of making it a better place" and credits him with showing "the Kanacs a future full of promise" (p. 109). He calls him "a man of remarkable energy who had been blessed with many talents" and praises his agricultural achievements (p. 128).

**Assessment**: Pinart's positive assessment of Dutrou-Bornier is strikingly at odds with what is known about the man's conduct. Dutrou-Bornier forcibly removed most of the population to Tahiti, seized their land, and was eventually murdered by the surviving islanders. Pinart's French patriotic framing ("his name, evoking for us our distant motherland") and colonial perspective prevented him from recognizing the oppressive nature of Dutrou-Bornier's regime. This colonial bias should be noted when evaluating all of Pinart's observations and interpretations.

### 9. Cross-Reference Notes

**9.1. Population trajectory**

The population documented by visitors in this period shows a catastrophic trajectory:
- Eyraud 1864: approximately 1,200 people
- Roussel 1869: approximately 650 people
- Loti 1872: approximately 100 people
- Pinart 1877: 111 people (26 women)

Pinart's count of 111 is the most precise figure (he was a trained ethnographer visiting a community small enough to count) and slightly higher than Loti's estimate five years earlier, possibly reflecting a modest stabilization after the worst of the removals to Tahiti had ceased.

**9.2. Comparison with earlier contact-era accounts on political authority**

Where Roggeveen (1722) observed a "particular one who seemed to be in authority," Gonzalez (1770) identified an "Indian chief," Cook (1774) identified individuals exercising some authority, and La Perouse (1786) described "the chief of the district," Pinart's 1877 account identifies no traditional Rapanui political authority whatsoever. The only authority figures are Koreto (Dutrou-Bornier's widow, acting as "regent") and a "major domo" in her household. This is not evidence that traditional authority had been weak in the pre-contact period; it is evidence that the slave raids, depopulation, and colonial domination had completely destroyed whatever political structures had existed.

**9.3. Material culture continuity and loss**

Some material culture persisted in recognizable form between the contact era and 1877: moai kavakava (small wooden statuettes), rei-miro (crescent ornaments), ear elongation, and certain tattoo patterns. Other material culture had been entirely lost: rongorongo literacy, moai production, ahu construction, and the associated ritual practices. The persistence of portable, personal items (statuettes, ornaments) alongside the total loss of monumental-scale and knowledge-intensive practices (writing, statue carving) is consistent with the selective destruction caused by the slave raids, which specifically targeted knowledge holders and community leaders.

**9.4. Comparison with Loti 1872**

Pierre Loti visited five years before Pinart and described a similarly desolate scene. Both document Dutrou-Bornier's household as the center of island life, the concentration of the remaining population at Mataveri, and the abandonment of the rest of the island. Pinart provides more archaeological detail, while Loti provides more literary/emotional description. Neither describes any functioning traditional political authority.

**9.5. Implications for post-Pinart ethnographic sources**

Pinart's documentation of complete knowledge loss in 1877 has direct implications for the reliability of later ethnographic sources. Thompson (1891, 14 years later), Routledge (1919, 42 years later), and Metraux (1940, 63 years later) all collected oral traditions about pre-contact political organization, chiefs, and territorial divisions from a population that, according to Pinart, had "retained no memory at all of those who had come before them." Whatever traditions Thompson, Routledge, and Metraux recorded were either (a) fragments that were recovered after Pinart's visit from returning Tahiti exiles, (b) reconstructions or inventions by later informants, or (c) information that existed in 1877 but that Pinart's brief visit and limited communication did not elicit. Option (c) cannot be ruled out, but the rongorongo tablets being used as fishing line spools strongly suggests that the knowledge loss was genuine and comprehensive, not merely hidden from Pinart.

---

## Summary Assessment

Pinart's 1877 account documents the most extreme phase of Rapa Nui's demographic and cultural collapse. With 111 people, 26 women, no functioning traditional political authority, no readable rongorongo, no memory of the statue builders, and a colonial household substituting for indigenous governance, the island described by Pinart bears no resemblance to the society that produced the monumental landscape he so carefully measured.

For the chiefdom question, the Pinart account is significant in three ways. First, it provides the best pre-20th-century archaeological descriptions of ahu, quarry features, and settlement remains, offering a baseline for subsequent archaeological work that is independent of any political interpretation. Second, it documents the complete absence of traditional political knowledge among the surviving population, raising serious questions about the reliability of political information collected by later ethnographers. Third, it shows that the only functioning authority structure on the island in 1877 was an imposed colonial regime centered on the household of a dead French settler, with no continuity to pre-contact Rapanui political organization.

Nothing in Pinart's account can be used as evidence for or against a pre-contact chiefdom. What his account demonstrates is the scale of the cultural and demographic catastrophe through which all subsequent ethnographic knowledge about Rapanui political organization must have passed.
