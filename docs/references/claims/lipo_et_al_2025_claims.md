## I. The Research Question: Centralized vs. Decentralized Production at Rano Raraku

### Framing

1. "Ethnohistoric and recent archaeological evidence suggest that Rapa Nui (Easter Island, Chile) was a politically decentralized society organized into small, relatively autonomous kin-based communities across the island. The more than 1,000 monumental statues (*moai*) of Rapa Nui thus raise a critical question: was production at Rano Raraku, the primary *moai* quarry, centrally controlled or did it mirror the decentralized pattern found elsewhere on the island?" (p. 1, Abstract)

**Assessment**: This directly frames the paper's central question in terms relevant to the chiefdom attribution. The paper tests whether quarry production was organized hierarchically or whether it replicates the decentralized pattern documented in settlement, lithic, and transport evidence.

2. "If *moai* production was centrally controlled, we expect to find evidence of standardized production methods, hierarchical spatial organization, and restricted access, indicating top-down management of this critical resource. Alternatively, if the decentralized pattern observed elsewhere on the island extended to the quarry, we would expect to find multiple groups working simultaneously in discrete areas, employing varied techniques, and maintaining the competitive yet culturally unified dynamics known ethnographically." (pp. 2-3)

**Assessment**: The paper defines clear archaeological expectations for both the centralized and decentralized hypotheses. This is methodologically important because it specifies what evidence would look like under each model before presenting results.

3. "For this study, we define 'centralization' as a system of authority consolidated in a single political unit that exerts exclusive control over access to critical resources and labor organization at the island-wide or supra-local community scale. By contrast, 'decentralization' refers to multiple autonomous groups retaining control over production without evidence of a dominant authority imposing its will on production." (pp. 2-3)

**Assessment**: The explicit definitions are important. The paper acknowledges that kin groups could centralize their own efforts at localized scales; the analysis evaluates island-wide or supra-local centralization specifically.

## II. Prior Documentation Problems

### Inconsistent and incomplete records

4. "Despite extensive documentation efforts spanning over 140 years, systematic, accessible data for Rano Raraku remain piecemeal and inconsistent, with varying counts, incomplete publications, and inaccessible databases hampering comprehensive research and cultural heritage management." (p. 4)

5. "More critically, we know little about the contextual relationships of *moai* within the quarry: their orientation relative to geological bedding planes, the spatial organization of carving activities, the sequential patterns of production, or the technological decisions that guided where and how statues were carved from the tuff." (p. 4)

**Assessment**: This establishes that prior claims about quarry organization (including claims that it required centralized coordination) were made without comprehensive spatial documentation. Previous researchers "could only speculate about quarry organization based on limited observations" (p. 13).

---

## III. Methods: 3D Documentation

### UAV photogrammetry

6. "Between June 2023 and January 2024, we conducted a series of low-elevation (30 and 50m) flights using DJI Mavic 3 Enterprise UAVs equipped with 20-megapixel CMOS sensors and 24 mm lenses." (p. 5)

7. "We captured 11,686 photographs with 80% overlap and sidelap, combining nadir and 45-degree oblique orientations with double grid patterns to ensure robust photogrammetric reconstruction." (p. 5)

8. "The resulting images have a pixel resolution of 0.86-1.12 cm, which is sufficient to document subtle archaeological features." (p. 5)

**Assessment**: The resolution and comprehensiveness of the 3D model distinguishes this study from all prior quarry documentation. The sub-centimeter pixel resolution allows identification of production features not visible in previous surveys.

## IV. Results: Comprehensive Inventory of Production Features

### Feature counts

9. "From our analysis, we have recorded 426 features representing *moai* in various stages of completion, 341 trenches cut to outline blocks for carving, 133 quarried voids where statues were successfully removed, and five bollards that likely served as anchor points for lowering *moai* down slopes (Table 1)." (p. 6)

10. Table 1 (p. 8) provides the full inventory:
- Sculpted objects: 426
- Trenches: 341
- Quarried voids: 133
- Quarrying foci: 30
- *Pu*: 13
- *Taheta*: 7
- Bollards: 5
- Other features: 17 (including 11 areas of quarried waste material, 3 *umu* [earth ovens], 1 *hare paenga* [house foundation], 1 built earth and stone ramp, 1 rock alignment)

**Assessment**: The 426 sculpted objects and 133 quarried voids represent the most comprehensive inventory of the quarry to date. The presence of *umu* and a *hare paenga* within the quarry indicates on-site habitation and food preparation by carving teams, consistent with semi-autonomous work groups provisioning themselves rather than a centralized labor camp.

### Previously undocumented features

11. "The model also revealed features, including quarrying areas on the crater's exterior slope (Fig 6) and a system of bollards associated with *pu* (large bedrock pits) that likely facilitated the transport of *moai* down steep terrain (Figs 4 and 7)." (p. 6)

**Assessment**: The *pu makari* features (carved holes) and anchor stones "facilitated the movement of statues by small work groups, eliminating the need for centralized coordination of large labor forces" (Fig. 4 caption, p. 7). This is infrastructure for decentralized transport, not for centralized labor mobilization.

## V. Results: Multiple Carving Methods

### Three distinct quarrying procedures

12. "The model demonstrates that at least three distinct quarrying procedures were employed across the volcanic crater landscape (Fig 8, Table 2). The most common method (143 examples) involved defining facial details before outlining the head and body. In 120 cases, blocks were completely outlined before detailed carving began. In five instances where cliff faces were near vertical, carvers worked sideways into the cliff." (p. 6)

13. Table 2 (p. 10) provides the breakdown:
- Face Outline method: 143 (*moai* are carved in a supine position; face and head outlines are carved in detail, followed by the body)
- Block method: 120 (blocks of volcanic tuff are outlined first; then body outlines are carved, followed by faces)
- Lateral method: 5 (carving is conducted into the vertical faces of the cliff)

14. "Most *moai* were quarried and carved oriented in supine position, with the majority carved from the top down, while a smaller number were extracted and shaped from the side. This diversity of approaches suggests adaptation to local geological conditions rather than a standardized, centrally managed production system." (p. 7)

**[KEY CLAIM FOR CHIEFDOM QUESTION]**: The diversity of carving methods across the quarry is evidence against standardized, centrally managed production. Centralized control would predict uniform procedures imposed from above. The variation is instead consistent with autonomous groups adapting to local geological conditions.

15. "While variation in carving techniques does not preclude centralized organization, stylistic or procedural diversity can emerge through workshop traditions, family units, or individual artisanship. What distinguishes the Rano Raraku case is the spatial redundancy of multiple workshop foci operating contemporaneously without evidence of overarching standardization, restricted access, or hierarchical oversight. We suggest this distributed pattern is more consistent with coordination at the level of clans than with a single dominant polity." (p. 7)

**Assessment**: The paper acknowledges that carving variation alone does not preclude centralization, but argues that the combination of varied techniques and spatially redundant workshops operating simultaneously does distinguish the decentralized pattern. This is a measured claim that does not overstate the evidence.

## VI. Results: Scale of Moai Production (30 Quarrying Foci)

### Identification of 30 distinct workshop areas

16. "Significantly, our model reveals at least 30 distinct quarrying foci distributed across the crater landscape (Figs 9 and 10). These workshops are identified as spatially continuous areas of redundant quarrying activity, typically delineated by unquarried bedrock or natural discontinuities. Each focus contains a combination of voids where statues were removed, and figures abandoned in various stages of completion." (p. 7)

**[KEY CLAIM FOR CHIEFDOM QUESTION]**: Thirty distinct quarrying foci, each functionally redundant, distributed across the crater. This is the paper's core empirical finding bearing on the chiefdom question. The spatial pattern matches decentralized, clan-based production rather than centralized, hierarchically managed production.

### Physical constraints on workshop size

17. "Physical constraints within each workshop area limited the number of carvers who could work simultaneously, suggesting relatively small production teams [1], with perhaps 4-6 active carvers engaged directly on a single *moai* at one time, with additional personnel (perhaps 10-20 more) supporting activities such as tool and rope production, and provisioning." (pp. 7-8)

**Assessment**: The labor estimate of 4-6 active carvers per moai, with 10-20 support personnel, is consistent with small kin-group work teams. This estimate is constrained by the physical space available in each workshop area, not merely by theoretical labor calculations.

### Contemporaneous operation and redundancy

18. "Thus, while the carving locus itself restricted active workers, broader production crews likely operated at a somewhat larger scale, consistent with kin-based groups rather than an island-wide workforce. The redundancy of these work areas, combined with the relative stylistic uniformity of the statues, points to contemporaneous rather than sequential operation by multiple independent groups." (p. 8)

**[KEY CLAIM FOR CHIEFDOM QUESTION]**: The redundancy of workshop areas indicates contemporaneous operation by multiple independent groups. If production were centrally controlled by a single authority, sequential use of optimal quarry faces would be more likely than redundant, simultaneous production across 30 foci.

19. "Such a scale of *moai* production by clan-based groups (or 'tribes,' *mata*) conforms to patterns of social organization described by earlier researchers [1-3]." (p. 8)

**Assessment**: The paper explicitly connects the quarry organization pattern to the ethnographically documented *mata* (clan/tribal) divisions described by Routledge, Metraux, and Englert. This linkage is appropriate but should be handled carefully in the manuscript: the ethnographic sources are post-slave-raid, so the connection to prehistoric organization is an inference, not a demonstrated fact.

## VII. Discussion: Decentralized Production Network

### Convergence with broader evidence for decentralization

20. "Our findings suggest a decentralized network of production, consistent with a sociopolitical organization comprising multiple small, interacting kin-based communities rather than a more unified authority structure, aligning with broader ethnographic and archaeological evidence from the island." (p. 9)

21. "As we have outlined, the *moai* transport routes show that paths radiated outward from Rano Raraku in patterns suggesting separate group efforts rather than a coordinated system [6]. Investigations of statue transport mechanisms demonstrate that relatively small groups could efficiently move even the largest statues without centralized coordination." (pp. 9-10)

22. "Morrison's [4] analysis of community architecture reveals spatially clustered, functionally redundant complexes of limited scale, consistent with organization around small, relatively autonomous kin groups [4-7, 12]." (p. 10)

**Assessment**: The paper situates the quarry findings within the broader convergence of evidence for decentralized organization: transport roads show radial patterns (Lipo and Hunt 2005), transport required small groups (Lipo et al. 2013; Lipo and Hunt 2025), settlement patterns show redundant community clusters (Morrison 2012), obsidian tools show localized distribution (Lipo et al. 2010), and population modeling indicates semi-isolated communities (Lipo et al. 2021). The quarry analysis adds the production center itself to this pattern.

### Rapa Nui not unique in Polynesia

23. "Rapa Nui is not unique in this regard, as elsewhere in Polynesia we find aggregative productive activities organized through distributed networks of semi-autonomous groups, without the direction of a paramount authority. For example, the early phases of large-scale agricultural field systems of Kona and Kohala, Hawai'i Island, likely emerged in the absence of centralized control [41, 42]." (p. 10)

**Assessment**: This is an important comparative point. Even in Hawaii, where chiefdoms are well documented, some large-scale productive activities emerged without centralized direction. The paper cites Allen (2004) and Field et al. (2011) for the Hawaiian field system case. This complicates the assumption that monumental scale requires chiefly command.

### Standardization without central control

24. "While communities maintained independent artistic expression in architectural forms and material culture across the island, the statues carved at Rano Raraku during peak production converged on standardized forms. This standardization within clan-based production suggests information sharing and cultural connectivity between groups in the absence of centralized hierarchical control." (p. 10)

**[KEY CLAIM FOR CHIEFDOM QUESTION]**: Moai form standardization coexisted with decentralized, clan-based production. Standardization does not require centralized management; it can emerge through horizontal information sharing and cultural tradition in the absence of hierarchical oversight.

25. "Our findings thus support archaeological and biological evidence indicating high local cultural retention with limited island-wide community interaction [12]." (p. 10)

### Challenging the monumentality-hierarchy assumption

26. "Rapa Nui provides a case that challenges the assumption that monumental architecture requires strongly hierarchical political structures. This finding contributes to growing evidence that complex productive activities and large-scale cooperation can emerge through horizontal social networks rather than vertical power structures [7, 11, 44-46]." (p. 10)

**[KEY CLAIM FOR CHIEFDOM QUESTION]**: The paper explicitly challenges the assumption that monumentality implies hierarchy. The quarry evidence demonstrates that one of Polynesia's most intensive monumental production programs was organized through horizontal networks, not vertical power structures.

### Decentralized production as adaptive

27. "Moreover, a decentralized production network of *moai* production suggests a sociopolitical system that successfully coordinated monumental construction while avoiding a central concentration of power that could potentially lead to resource overexploitation." (p. 11)

28. "This decentralized production system may have been advantageous, preventing the concentration of power that could lead to resource overexploitation while enabling flexible responses to environmental and social challenges." (p. 12)

**Assessment**: The paper suggests that decentralized production was not merely an absence of centralization but may have been positively adaptive. This connects to resilience arguments but should be cited carefully: the adaptive-advantage interpretation is speculative and goes beyond what the spatial pattern directly demonstrates.

## VIII. Discussion: Broader Implications

### Monumental construction from heterarchical arrangements

29. "The Rano Raraku evidence thus contributes to a growing recognition that complex cooperative behaviors and monumental achievements can emerge from heterarchical rather than hierarchical social arrangements." (p. 12)

30. "Our results demonstrate that societies can successfully coordinate large-scale productive activities through distributed networks of semi-autonomous groups, maintaining both competitive dynamics and cultural cohesion." (p. 12)

### Pattern echoed at Orongo

31. "Interestingly, a pattern of competitive groups is echoed in the structure of the archaeological record in Orongo, the site of the historic *Tangata Manu* (Birdman) competition in the southwest corner of the island [47]." (pp. 10-11)

**Assessment**: The connection to Orongo is suggestive but not developed. The Birdman competition involved competitive groups, consistent with the multi-group pattern at the quarry, but the Birdman phenomenon is historically late and may post-date the period of peak moai production.

## IX. Conclusions

### Summary of evidence against centralized production

32. "The identification of 30 distinct quarrying foci, each containing redundant production features and employing varied carving methods, demonstrates that multiple groups worked simultaneously at the quarry without apparent centralized control." (p. 11)

33. "This pattern directly parallels the territorial divisions, autonomous community clusters, and competitive dynamics described by Routledge [1], Metraux [2], and Englert [3], extending the evidence for decentralized organization to the island's most significant production center." (p. 11)

34. "The spatial organization of these workshop areas, physically constrained by natural boundaries and unquarried bedrock, suggests that a controlling authority did not restrict access to the quarry but instead negotiated among multiple participating groups." (p. 11)

**[KEY CLAIM FOR CHIEFDOM QUESTION]**: Access to the quarry was not restricted by a controlling authority. The delineation of workshop areas by natural boundaries rather than imposed barriers indicates that access was negotiated among groups, not allocated by a central authority.

35. "The diversity of carving techniques, from face-first to block methods, indicates technological autonomy rather than standardized procedures that would be expected under hierarchical management." (p. 11)

36. "Yet, despite this organizational independence, the stylistic convergence of *moai* forms demonstrates the powerful role of cultural tradition and information sharing in coordinating production without centralized oversight." (pp. 11-12)

## X. Methodological Contribution

### 3D documentation advances hypothesis testing

37. "Where previous researchers could only speculate about quarry organization based on limited observations, we can now quantify the distribution of workshop areas, analyze production strategies, and identify organizational patterns that directly address hypotheses about sociopolitical structure." (p. 13)

**Assessment**: This is an important methodological point for the manuscript. Prior claims about quarry organization and what it implied about political structure were made without systematic spatial data. This study provides the first empirical basis for testing those claims.

38. "This study demonstrates that computational approaches to archaeological documentation can fundamentally reshape our understanding of ancient production systems and their socio-political implications." (p. 13)

## Summary of Claims Relevant to Chiefdom Attribution

Lipo et al. 2025 provides the first comprehensive, spatially explicit documentation of moai production at Rano Raraku using high-resolution 3D photogrammetry. The central finding is that the quarry was organized into 30 distinct quarrying foci, each containing redundant production features and employing varied carving methods, consistent with contemporaneous operation by multiple autonomous groups.

Key implications for the chiefdom question:

1. **30 redundant workshop areas**: The spatial redundancy of production foci indicates that multiple groups worked simultaneously at the quarry, consistent with clan-based production rather than centralized control (p. 7).

2. **No evidence of restricted access**: Workshop boundaries follow natural bedrock features, not imposed barriers. Access was negotiated among groups, not allocated by a central authority (p. 11).

3. **Varied carving techniques**: Three distinct quarrying methods were employed (face-first, block, lateral), indicating technological autonomy rather than standardized procedures imposed by hierarchical management (pp. 6, 11).

4. **Small production teams**: Physical constraints limited active carvers to 4-6 per moai, with broader crews of perhaps 10-20 additional support personnel, consistent with kin-group scale labor (pp. 7-8).

5. **Standardization without central control**: Despite organizational autonomy, moai converged on standardized forms through horizontal information sharing, not hierarchical direction (p. 10).

6. **Convergence with broader evidence**: The quarry pattern parallels decentralized patterns in settlement (Morrison 2012), transport (Lipo and Hunt 2005), lithic distribution (Lipo et al. 2010), and community interaction (Lipo et al. 2021) (pp. 9-10).

7. **Monumentality does not require hierarchy**: The paper explicitly challenges the assumption that monumental architecture requires strongly hierarchical political structures (p. 10).

8. **Prior claims were untested**: Previous assertions about quarry organization were based on limited observations without systematic spatial documentation. This study provides the first empirical basis for evaluating those claims (p. 13).

**Own-work note**: As co-authored work by Lipo, Hunt, and Pakarati, this paper should be cited with the same rigor as any other source. What the paper directly demonstrates is: (a) 30 spatially discrete quarrying foci with redundant production features, (b) three distinct carving methods, (c) physical constraints on team size, and (d) delineation by natural rather than imposed boundaries. What it infers from these findings is that production was decentralized and clan-based. The inference is well supported by the spatial evidence, but the paper does not directly demonstrate: (a) that specific clans used specific workshop areas, (b) the temporal relationships between workshops (whether truly contemporaneous or sequential), or (c) the specific social mechanisms by which quarry access was negotiated. The connection to ethnographically described *mata* divisions relies on post-slave-raid sources (Routledge, Metraux, Englert) and should be noted as such when cited in the manuscript.
