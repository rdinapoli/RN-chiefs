# Claims Extraction: Métraux 1937 "Easter Island Sanctuaries"

## Source Information

- **Author**: Alfred Métraux
- **Title**: "Easter Island Sanctuaries (Analytic and Comparative Study)"
- **Publication**: *Etnologiska studier* 5, Göteborg, 1937, pp. 104-153
- **Fieldwork**: 1934 Franco-Belgian expedition
- **Source type**: Archaeological and comparative study with ethnographic field observations
- **Post-raid status**: [POST-RAID] for any claims derived from informant testimony (72 years post-raids); architectural descriptions based on direct observation of ruins

---

## A. Ahu Typology and Classification

### Claim A1: Four-type ahu classification
**Page**: p. 106 (PDF p. 3)
**Quote**: "Ahus may be classified roughly into four types: I, image ahus, which are the largest and most elaborate; 2, rectangular ahus or ahu-avanga; 3, ahu-poepoe which differ from the preceding types in having one or both ends sloping more or less toward the center or outer extremity; 4, semi-pyramidal ahus or ahu-paepae, simplest of the four types."
**Evidential type**: (a) Direct observation and archaeological classification.

### Claim A2: Common structural principle across all types
**Page**: p. 106 (PDF p. 3)
**Quote**: "All of the structures, to whatever type they belong, have a common character: they are all mounds of stones. The only differences are in the number of stones piled up, and in the degree of care taken in the disposition of the stones."
**Evidential type**: (a) Direct observation.

### Claim A3: Each lineage had its own ahu
**Page**: p. 105 (PDF p. 2)
**Quote**: "Each lineage distinctly remembers an ahu of its own where the dead of the group were buried."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition from informants. [POST-RAID]

## B. Distribution Patterns

### Claim B1: Coastal concentration near coves and settlements
**Pages**: pp. 105-106 (PDF pp. 2-3)
**Quote**: "The ahus form almost an uninterrupted line along the coast, but they are rare on the headland of Poike... and absent on the steep cliffs of the southwest of the island." "They are particularly numerous around coves and other places which were economically the most advantageous."
**Evidential type**: (a) Direct survey observation.

### Claim B2: Ahu count estimates
**Page**: p. 105 (PDF p. 2)
**Quote**: "Routledge (27, p. 166) estimated the number of ahus for the island at 260, of which 20 were inland. The list made by Lavachery (21, p. 286-287) includes only 183 ahus."
**Evidential type**: (a) Archaeological survey data.

### Claim B3: La Pérouse on ahu-district correlation
**Page**: p. 105 (PDF p. 2)
**Quote**: La Pérouse cited: "The most probable conjecture concerning the government of these islanders is that they compose but a single nation divided into as many districts as there are morais; for we observed that the villages are built near these cemeteries."
**Evidential type**: La Pérouse's direct observation (1786), cited by Métraux. Notes ahu as "an outward indication of independent political unity."
**Relevance**: La Pérouse's observation implies decentralized, community-level organization corresponding to ahu locations, not a centralized polity.

## C. Construction Techniques

### Claim C1: Image ahu construction method
**Page**: p. 108 (PDF p. 4)
**Quote**: "Two unfinished ahus clearly show how the constructions were started. First the ground was leveled with stones and slabs, then the workmen erected the first course of the seaward wall by placing large slabs on edge."
**Evidential type**: (a) Archaeological observation of unfinished structures.

### Claim C2: Ahu Vinapu as masterpiece of stone-working
**Page**: p. 108 (PDF p. 4)
**Quote**: "There the lava is dressed into rectangular slabs, fitting one against the other. The surfaces have been perfectly smoothed by grinding with sand and water. The corners of the central portion are rounded in elegant curves."
**Evidential type**: (a) Direct observation.

### Claim C3: Natural quarries supplied material
**Page**: p. 108 (PDF p. 4)
**Quote**: "Most of the material comes from natural quarries. On many parts of the Island the lava cooled in regular beds, supplying stones for construction which require no further shaping."
**Evidential type**: (a) Geological observation.

### Claim C4: Stone-cutting developed locally in Polynesia
**Pages**: pp. 123-125 (PDF pp. 12-13)
**Summary**: Métraux argues cut-stone techniques developed locally across Polynesia based on available materials, not through diffusion from outside. "Stone cutting seems to have developed locally under differing circumstances. The suitability of available material is one of the predominant factors in the development of stone cutting throughout Polynesia."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's comparative analysis.

## D. Major Ahu Descriptions

### Claim D1: Ahu Hanga-o-honu dimensions
**Page**: p. 109 (PDF p. 4)
**Quote**: "The seaward wall, 84 meters long and 5 meters high, is made of rectangular blocks of stone. The salient, 30 meters long and 4 meters wide, forms the stage on which the statues stood... On the platform once stood six statues."
**Evidential type**: (a) Lavachery's field measurements.

### Claim D2: Ahu Te Pito-te-kura (via Skottsberg)
**Pages**: pp. 110-111 (PDF pp. 5-6)
**Summary**: Described via Skottsberg's measurements: central part 23.5 m long, protruding 4 m; wings 31.2 m and 32 m giving total length 86.7 m. Single statue (Paro), "said to have been one of the last erected on the island" and "the last one to be pulled down," probably about 1840.
**Evidential type**: (a) Skottsberg's measurements, (b) informant tradition for dating. [POST-RAID for the tradition]

### Claim D3: Ahu Vinapu masonry
**Pages**: pp. 125-126 (PDF pp. 12-13)
**Quote**: "The masterpiece of Easter Island stone work is the ahu Vinapu. The seaward wall is made of two rows of marvelously smooth slabs whose edges join with mathematical accuracy."
**Note**: Métraux explicitly rejects the Inca-connection hypothesis: "Before such a hypothesis was advanced, it should have been remembered that ahu Vinapu is unique on Easter Island, not at all characteristic of the ordinary stone work of the island."
**Evidential type**: (a) Direct observation; (c) Métraux's analytical rejection of diffusionist theory.

## E. Burial Function

### Claim E1: Burial vaults integrated into ahu
**Pages**: pp. 107-108 (PDF pp. 3-4)
**Summary**: Burial vaults (slab-lined) were located under the stage or more often under the sloping terrace. Some were built during construction, others added later. Thomson noted at Tongariki that "the oldest of these tombs appear to have been sealed up before the structure was completed."
**Evidential type**: (a) Direct archaeological observation.

### Claim E2: Ahu-poepoe still in use after European contact
**Page**: p. 115 (PDF p. 7)
**Quote**: "Near one of the skeletons were glass beads, an important discovery since it shows that ahus of this type were still being made after contact with Europeans at the end of the eighteenth century or in the beginning of the nineteenth century."
**Evidential type**: (a) Archaeological evidence (glass beads as terminus post quem).

## F. Polynesian Comparative Framework

### Claim F1: Ahu-marae linguistic connection
**Pages**: pp. 117-118 (PDF pp. 8-9)
**Summary**: "Ahu" is cognate with terms across Polynesia for raised stone platforms at one end of a marae. The Easter Island sanctuary retained the platform form (ahu) without developing the associated court (marae) found elsewhere, representing an earlier stage of the religious structure complex.
**Evidential type**: (c) Comparative linguistic and architectural analysis.

### Claim F2: Mangarevan marae closest parallel
**Pages**: pp. 118-119 (PDF pp. 8-9)
**Quote**: "The steps on the court face of the raised platform and the presence of burial vaults in the platform connect Mangarevan maraes with Easter Island ahus more closely than with other stone structures in Polynesia."
**Evidential type**: (c) Comparative archaeological analysis.

## G. Transportation of Stone Statues

### Claim G1: Walking statues legend dismissed as myth
**Pages**: pp. 131-132 (PDF pp. 15-16)
**Quote**: "The Easter Islanders have never been able to explain how the big stone images were transported to and erected on the platforms. To satisfy inquirers they invented a myth which tells how the statues moved to the ahu by magic."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's assessment.
**Relevance**: Métraux explicitly identifies the "walking statues" legend as a myth invented to satisfy European inquirers, not as a genuine tradition about transport method.

### Claim G2: The Roussel legend, full text
**Page**: p. 132 (PDF p. 16)
**Quote**: Roussel (1868): "The great chief of that time, who, like his predecessors, was very powerful, ordered the statues to walk. Hearing his words, all the statues set out and picked out the places which were the most convenient to them. We may believe that on occasion of a feast all the tribes gathered and each one took its family gods and dragged them with hau ropes to their respective districts."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition via Roussel. [POST-RAID]
**Relevance**: Two critical elements: (1) The magical walking is explicitly mythological. (2) Roussel's own explanation is that "all the tribes gathered and each one took its family gods and dragged them... to their respective districts," implying decentralized, clan-level organization of transport, NOT centralized chiefly command.

### Claim G3: Palmer identifies the "great chief" as Tuu-ko-ihu
**Page**: p. 132 (PDF p. 16)
**Quote**: Palmer "informs us that the 'great chief' was Tukuiu (Tuu-ko-ihu)."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition.

### Claim G4: Butaye version attributes movement to priests
**Page**: p. 132 (PDF p. 16)
**Quote**: "When the people wanted the statues to go somewhere, they appealed to the priests, who ordered them to go to a certain place. The huge blocks moved for a distance and then stopped. Each day the priests repeated the charms until the images had reached the spot for which they were destined."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition (late 19th century). [POST-RAID]

### Claim G5: Zumbohn version attributes movement to Makemake
**Page**: p. 132 (PDF p. 16)
**Quote**: "the statues moved toward their respective ahus at the will of the god Makemake."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]

### Claim G6: Weight of statues overestimated
**Page**: p. 133 (PDF p. 16)
**Quote**: "The weight of the Easter Island images has usually been overestimated." Density of Rano-raraku tuff: 2.48. "Statues 4 or 5 meters tall weigh about 4 or 5 tons at the most. I doubt whether the weight of the giant statues which stand near the quarry exceeds 30 tons."
**Evidential type**: (a) Scientific measurement (density by Professor Delacroix); (c) Métraux's estimate.

### Claim G7: Polynesian parallels for moving heavy stones
**Pages**: pp. 133-135 (PDF pp. 16-17)
**Summary**: Métraux provides multiple Polynesian parallels: Marquesan platforms include boulders of 3-10 tons moved by sling-and-pole methods; Maori hauled 60-ton vessels back to sea and transported massive canoe logs; Tongan trilithon pillars of 30-40 tons were quarried, dragged on wooden skids, and erected by combined muscular power with ropes, levers, and blocking wedges.
**Quote on problem**: "This problem has been unnecessarily complicated by those who want to add mystery to the Island."
**Evidential type**: (c) Comparative analysis drawing on published ethnographic sources.
**Relevance**: Métraux explicitly argues statue transport requires no special explanation beyond known Polynesian techniques, and no centralized authority to organize it.

### Claim G8: Geographical factors facilitated transport
**Pages**: pp. 135-136 (PDF p. 17)
**Quote**: "To realize how the Easter Islanders carved and transported huge statues we must keep in mind the geographical features of the island. Rano-raraku, the tuff quarry, is at the end of a large plain extending across the island."
**Evidential type**: (a) Geographic observation.

## H. Stone Statues: Origins and Comparisons

### Claim H1: Statues evolved from Polynesian stone uprights
**Pages**: pp. 131, 148-149 (PDF pp. 15, 22)
**Quote**: "The Easter Island stone statues may have evolved from the stone uprights on the back of the Tuamotuan and Society ahus."
**Summary**: Métraux traces a developmental sequence from stone uprights on marae platforms (Tuamotus, Necker Island) through increasingly representational carvings to the large Easter Island moai. "Stimulated by the abundance of tuff and the ease with which statues could be transported across open country, they resorted to larger and larger creations."
**Evidential type**: (c) Comparative analysis.

### Claim H2: Large stone images in only three Polynesian locations
**Page**: p. 129 (PDF p. 14)
**Quote**: "In the entire Polynesian area, stone images of great size were carved only in the Marquesas, Raivavae, and Easter Island."
**Evidential type**: (c) Comparative survey.

## I. Significant Absences

### Claim I1: No claims of chiefly command over ahu construction
**Entire article**
Throughout this detailed 50-page study of ahu architecture, typology, construction, distribution, and stone image transport, Métraux makes NO claims about political authority directing, organizing, or commanding monument construction. Construction is described in terms of techniques, materials, and Polynesian parallels, never in terms of who ordered or organized the labor.
**Evidential type**: Significant absence in a comprehensive architectural study.

### Claim I2: No centralized labor organization described
**Entire article**
Métraux describes construction techniques, estimates weights, provides Polynesian parallels for moving heavy stones, but never attributes the labor to a centralized political authority. The closest approach is Roussel's legend, which Métraux dismisses as myth and whose own explanation (each tribe dragging its family gods to its district) implies decentralized organization.
**Evidential type**: Significant absence.

### Claim I3: Ahu decay attributed to family extinction, not political change
**Page**: p. 114 (PDF p. 7)
**Quote**: "Their decay was noticed by Cook. Probably no care was taken of the ahu because it belonged to a family which had died out; and intertribal wars may have contributed not a little to the destruction of the monuments."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's inference from Cook's observation.
**Relevance**: Maintenance of ahu was a family/lineage responsibility, not a centralized state function. When the family died out, no political authority maintained the monument.

## J. Historical Descriptions Cited

### Claim J1: Cook on ahu masonry quality
**Page**: p. 112 (PDF p. 6)
**Quote**: Cook: "They are built, or rather faced, with hewn stones of a very large size; and the workmanship is not inferior to the best plain piece of masonry we have in England."
**Evidential type**: (a) Cook's direct observation (1774).

### Claim J2: Bernizet's detailed ahu description (La Pérouse expedition)
**Pages**: pp. 112-113 (PDF p. 6)
**Summary**: Bernizet (geographical engineer of the La Pérouse expedition) provides the "most complete and clear description of an ahu, made when the old culture was still functioning," including platform, steps, burial vaults, statues, and red stone hats.
**Evidential type**: (a) Direct observation (1786).

## Summary of Relevance to the Paper

This article is significant for the monument-production argument in several ways:

1. **Comprehensive ahu study without chiefly attribution**: The ethnographer who wrote the most detailed analytical study of ahu architecture and construction makes no claim that these monuments required or evidenced centralized political authority.

2. **Clan-level organization of monument transport**: Roussel's own explanation (dismissed by Métraux as myth in its magical elements) describes each tribe dragging its family gods to its own district, implying decentralized, clan-level organization.

3. **Distribution correlates with settlement, not centralized planning**: Ahu distribution follows coves and settlements (economically advantageous locations), consistent with community-level construction rather than top-down command.

4. **Each lineage had its own ahu**: The association between lineage and ahu suggests community-level ownership and maintenance, not centralized state monuments.

5. **Transport demystified**: Métraux systematically demystifies statue transport through weight corrections, Polynesian parallels, and geographic analysis, arguing it required no special explanation beyond known Polynesian techniques.

6. **Ahu maintenance as family responsibility**: When the owning family died out, the ahu decayed, demonstrating that monument maintenance was a lineage responsibility, not a state function.
