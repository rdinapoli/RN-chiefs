## I. Ahu Distribution and Settlement Pattern

### General distribution

1. "Image-*ahu* were the focal points of Rapa Nui's precontact communities. Overall, the Rapanui settlement pattern is characterized by relatively dispersed communities distributed along the coastline as redundant sets of domestic features ca. 100-200 m inland from *ahu*." (p. 4)

2. "While image-*ahu* have a primarily coastal distribution and, based on limited historical accounts, were community gathering locations for ritual activity, explanations for why they occur in their specific locations or additional social roles these monuments may have served remain largely unanswered." (p. 4)

3. "When compared to CSR *ahu* appear highly clustered at nearly all spatial scales, though when accounting for the spatial inhomogeneity of *ahu* (Fig 3B) they are neither clustered nor dispersed, except for dispersion at distances greater than ca. 1500 m." (p. 12)

**Assessment**: The finding that ahu are inhomogeneously but not clustered/dispersed once the coastal distribution is accounted for is important. It means the clustering is a first-order property (driven by environmental covariates like coastline and freshwater) rather than a second-order property (driven by inter-ahu attraction/repulsion processes). This is consistent with ahu placement being driven by resource access rather than by competitive territorial dynamics between polities.

4. "Areas of domestic activity are slightly disassociated from *ahu* and tend to occur 100-200 m inland." (p. 18)

5. "Morrison [59] found that clustered sets of co-occurring domestic activity areas were spatially segregated from the locations of *ahu*. However, at larger spatial scales, groups of these redundant sets of domestic features, while located further inland, are generally clustered around one or more *ahu*. In other words, while *ahu* were indeed the focal points for the settlement pattern, they are spatially offset from them, and directly adjacent to freshwater sources." (p. 18)

**Assessment**: The spatial offset between ahu and domestic features, with ahu directly adjacent to freshwater and domestic features slightly inland, is key. It means ahu are positioned at freshwater access points, not at the center of residential areas. This is more consistent with ahu marking shared resource access than with marking the center of a chief's residential territory.

### Study area and data

6. "The 93 image-*ahu* and their locations derive from the comprehensive *ahu* survey conducted by Martinsson-Wallin [10]." (p. 7)

7. Analysis was restricted to the eastern portion of the island "where we have near-complete coverage documenting the distribution of these three resources." (p. 2)

## II. Freshwater and Ahu

### Freshwater scarcity on Rapa Nui

8. "Freshwater is a limited resource of critical importance that is infrequently discussed for Rapa Nui. As noted above, the island receives a moderate amount of rainfall but also experiences frequent droughts and, due to soil and bedrock permeability, has no permanent streams." (p. 6)

9. "The highly permeable bedrock allows water to rapidly transfer to the island's unconfined aquifers, and the water table is generally only a few MASL near the coast." (p. 6)

10. "Except where a few springs occur, the island's geology makes inland groundwater inaccessible without the aid of modern drilling equipment." (p. 6)

### Coastal groundwater discharge (CGD)

11. "Cook, for example, noted that the islanders drank coastal water, commenting that the water given to them by the Rapanui was 'brackish and stinking' which was only 'rendered acceptable by the extremity of their thirst' and later saying that they were even given 'real salt water' and that the Rapanui 'drank pretty plentifully' from the sea. From these descriptions, it is likely that Cook experienced the Rapanui using coastal groundwater discharge (CGD) whereby groundwater seeps up in many locations along the island's coastline." (pp. 6-7)

12. "Coastal seeps, or coastal groundwater discharge (CGD), locations were identified using EXTECH EC170 salinity meters, which measure the concentration of dissolved ions in coastal water expressed in parts per thousand (ppt). Salinity measurements were taken every 10 m along the coastline within our survey area and within one hour of low-tide when CGD is at its maximum." (p. 9)

13. "Coastal seeps were defined as locations where the salinity of coastal water was substantially less than the average salinity of seawater (ca. 35 ppt), in this case 28 ppt or less. This value represents a 20% reduction in salinity and therefore signals areas of substantial CGD that could be used as freshwater sources." (p. 9)

### The freshwater-ahu correlation (central finding)

14. "*Ahu* are significantly closer to both marine resource locations (D+ = 0.65, p < 0.0001) and freshwater sources (D+ = 0.59, p-value < 0.0001) than expected under CSR." (p. 14)

15. "Both AIC and BIC indicate that *ahu* locations are best explained by an additive model with the combined effects of distance from the coastline and distance from freshwater sources (model 5) with a ΔBIC of 0 and a BIC weight of 0.675 and a ΔAIC of 0 and an AIC weight of 0.35." (p. 14)

16. "Negative values of the covariate estimates indicate that *ahu* intensity decreases with distance from the coast and freshwater sources, i.e., the inhomogeneous intensity of image-*ahu* is greatest near these resources." (p. 14)

17. "The results indicate no significant deviations between model 5 and the data. To further validate the model, we also used a MAD and DLCF test [139]. The results of these tests indicate that model 5 is a good fit to the data (MAD = 171.91, p = 0.22; U = 41528000, p = 0.18). This finding indicates that the inhomogeneous spatial intensity of image-*ahu* is sufficiently explained by the model 5 covariates and that no second-order interaction parameters (e.g., clustering/dispersion) are warranted, thus pointing to the significance of freshwater sources and their association with *ahu* locations." (pp. 14-16)

**Assessment**: This is the paper's core finding. The spatial distribution of ahu is most parsimoniously explained by proximity to freshwater and the coastline. No clustering or repulsion processes need to be invoked. This is a formally tested, quantitative result using information-theoretic model selection, not an informal correlation.

18. "This result is significant, as it likely explains the previously unresolved issue of why Rapa Nui's monuments occur primarily along the coast--one the most abundant sources of freshwater, coastal seeps, occurs primarily in coastal locations. The fact that our model 5 is a much better fit than the simpler model 1, which only includes distance from the coastline, offers compelling support for the claim that *ahu* are related to freshwater locations." (p. 18)

## III. Evidence Against the Agricultural-Territorial Model

### Ahu are NOT explained by rock mulch garden locations

19. "*Ahu* are not significantly closer to the minimal rock mulch classification than expected under CSR (D+ = 0.083, p = 0.26); however, there is a significant spatial association for both the medial (D+ = 0.20, p = 0.0004) and maximal classifications (D+ = 0.24, p < 0.0001) from Ladefoged et al. [28]." (p. 14)

20. "Our findings do not support the claims that *ahu* are related to competition over or monitoring of agricultural resources, at least in the sense that the distribution of image-*ahu* are not explained by the locations of rock mulch gardens, which were the source of intensified sweet potato production (e.g., [67-70])." (p. 16)

**Assessment**: This directly contradicts Stevenson and colleagues' longstanding argument that ahu placement tracks agricultural field systems and that ranked persons managed field systems under their authority. The rock mulch variable contributes nothing to the best-fitting model.

21. "While the construction of Rapa Nui's monuments may be coeval with the establishment of rock mulch field systems, image-*ahu* do not appear to be built in locations to mark control or territoriality over these resources. This result is supported by other evidence indicating a lack of control over other resources, in particular fine-grained basalt, *moai* tuff, and red scoria (e.g., [78,79]; cf. [77])." (p. 16)

**Assessment**: The paper notes converging evidence from lithic sourcing studies (Simpson and colleagues) that raw material access was not controlled. This reinforces the pattern: multiple independent lines of evidence fail to show territorial resource control.

### Marine resources are a spurious correlate

22. "While our exploratory SKS analyses suggest a strong and significant spatial association between *ahu* and marine resource locations, our PPM and multi-model selection suggests this variable is not particularly meaningful. It is likely that marine resource locations appear correlated with image-*ahu* due to their geographic proximity to the more meaningful explanatory variables (coastline, freshwater)." (pp. 16-17)

23. "Significantly, our results offer little support to recent claims that *ahu* were preferentially built at locations that mark control over marine resource locations (e.g., [18])." (p. 17)

**Assessment**: This directly challenges Kirch's (2017) argument that image-ahu "are found at the best embayments around the island" to "visually control" marine resources. The apparent ahu-marine correlation is an artifact of both being coastal; marine resources add nothing once coastline and freshwater are accounted for.

## IV. Implications for Social Organization

### The costly signaling / territorial display interpretation

24. "One interpretation of these results is that *ahu* were preferentially built near freshwater sources to demarcate community access/control over these resources. This interpretation draws on the logic of costly signaling theory, whereby Rapa Nui's monuments are hypothesized to serve as conspicuous displays of community access/control over the island's limited subsistence resources." (p. 18)

25. "Recently, such a costly signaling model for *ahu* has been proposed (e.g., [8,12,16,17]), which predicts that, if Rapa Nui's monuments did serve a costly signaling function, then there should be a spatial association between them and the underlying quality they are potentially signaling, such as the limited and vitally important, freshwater resources. These predictions are quantitatively supported by our present results." (p. 18)

**Assessment**: The paper interprets the freshwater-ahu association through costly signaling theory, which is a collective action / community-level framework, not a chiefly-command framework. The signaling is by communities, not by individual chiefs. This interpretation is compatible with the collective action model and does not require hierarchical control.

26. "McCoy [19], who conducted the first large-scale settlement pattern analysis on Rapa Nui, suggested that warfare in precontact times would have likely been over freshwater. It is interesting to note that McCoy [19] also suggested a signaling function for *ahu* and argued that, '[e]laboration of. . .ahu. . .would, from present knowledge, provide a rough index of success in competition between lineages. . .based ultimately on the free time that could be allotted to such non-vital activities.'" (p. 18)

27. "Several emerging and independent lines of evidence show that there is little empirical support for violent warfare, including little evidence for the production of lethal weapons [75,146], limited instances of lethal skeletal trauma [147], and a lack of fortifications [17,148-150]." (p. 18)

**Assessment**: The paper explicitly notes the absence of evidence for violent warfare, which undercuts models of inter-polity territorial competition driven by chiefly command.

### What monument construction does NOT require

28. "A key finding is that the construction and transport of the island's *moai* and *pukao* (red scoria 'hats') required neither large numbers of individuals nor trees [54,57]. The implications of this are far-reaching, particularly in that they question the common assumptions that monument construction necessarily involved complex social organization and labor management or that it necessarily led to environmental degradation." (p. 19)

**Assessment**: This is a critical claim for the chiefdom question. If moai/pukao construction did not require large labor forces, then the monuments cannot be used as evidence for the labor-mobilization capacity of chiefs. This removes one of the primary inferential props for the chiefdom model.

### How ahu relate to community formation

29. "We see our spatial analyses as an investigation of the processes that led to the formation of Rapa Nui's monumental landscape--a spatial pattern which appears best explained by the location of freshwater sources." (p. 19)

30. "If Rapa Nui's monuments did indeed serve a territorial display function (in addition to their well-known ritual roles), then their patterns are best explained by the availability of the island's limited freshwater." (p. 19)

## V. Alternative Explanations for Ahu Distribution (Not Requiring Chiefly Territorial Control)

31. The paper frames the freshwater-ahu association in terms of "community access/control" and costly signaling theory, not in terms of chiefly territorial boundaries. (p. 18)

**Assessment**: The language throughout is "community" not "chief" or "elite." The signaling model presented is one where the group investing in monument construction signals collective capacity to competitors, not where a chief directs monument construction to mark personal territorial claims.

32. "Given this lack of evidence for warfare, it is possible that inter-community competition took the form of territorial displays, or costly signals, through the construction of monumental architecture directly adjacent to the island's limited freshwater locations. However, additional formal analyses to specifically test these ideas are needed to evaluate this scenario." (p. 18)

**Assessment**: The paper explicitly frames this as "inter-community competition" rather than inter-chief or inter-polity competition, and acknowledges this interpretation requires further testing.

## VI. Methodological Claims

### Previous models were untested

33. "Many have debated whether *ahu* served as territorial displays of control or hereditary ownership over the island's limited subsistence resources (e.g., [11,19,20,52,58,61-65]), and while there is general agreement that competition and territoriality were centered around limited and predictable resources (see [66]), there is disagreement over which resources were most critical." (p. 4)

34. "To date, however, rigorous tests of these hypotheses are lacking, as are any attempts to construct formal models of *ahu* spatial patterns (but see Beardsley [71] for exploratory analyses)." (p. 4)

**Assessment**: This is an important methodological claim. Prior to this study, all arguments about what ahu placement "means" in terms of political organization or resource control were informal and untested. The chiefdom-territorial interpretation of ahu was never subjected to spatial analysis.

35. "The expectation is that if Rapa Nui's monuments were built, in part, to signal territorial resource control, then there should be a spatial association between *ahu* and the resources they are signaling control over [17]." (p. 2)

### Access to raw materials was not controlled

36. "Access to raw materials for architectural construction and tools seems to be unrelated to image-*ahu* locations. Obsidian used for cutting and scraping tools known as *mata'a* [72-75] was derived from several discrete locations near their source volcanic vents away from *ahu* activity [76,77]. Stone for large *moai* and red scoria for *pukao* come primarily from single quarries at Rano Raraku and Puna Pau (respectively), and there do not appear to have been limits to the access of these materials by any particular group (e.g., [80-82])." (pp. 4-5)

**Assessment**: The absence of localized resource control over construction materials is evidence against the chiefdom model's expectation that elites controlled access to prestige goods and monument-building resources.

## VII. Limitations Acknowledged

37. "One limitation of our analysis is the lack of explicit spatio-*temporal* analyses of *ahu*. Unfortunately, such a comprehensive temporal analysis is not possible at this time given the overall lack of secure and high precision radiocarbon dates for Rapa Nui." (p. 18)

38. "An extension of our analysis to the western portion of Rapa Nui, once freshwater data from that region become available, will be necessary to more fully explore these spatial patterns of monument construction." (p. 19)

39. "While we have not yet resolved the question of potential temporal variability in discharge rates, given Rapa Nui's hydrogeology, the spatial locations of these water sources have likely remained stable over the time period of human occupation." (pp. 9-10)

## VIII. Claims About Competing Hypotheses in the Literature

### Kirch's marine resource control argument

40. Kirch [18] "has argued that image-*ahu*, 'are found at the best embayments around the island,' which served as a means of, 'visually "controlling" access to the limited marine resources.'" (p. 4)

**Assessment**: This paper's results directly undermine this claim. Marine resource locations add no explanatory power to the model once coastline and freshwater are accounted for.

### Stevenson's agricultural monitoring argument

41. "Stevenson and colleagues [67-70] have consistently argued for an association between *ahu* and rock mulch gardens, that the development of intensified agriculture closely tracks the tempo of monument construction, and that '[t]hese repeated associations indicate that ranked persons were most likely ritually involved with agricultural production and used the position to manage the field systems under their authority' [67]." (p. 4)

**Assessment**: This paper's results directly contradict this claim. Rock mulch gardens do not explain ahu distribution. The absence of this spatial association removes the empirical basis for inferring that elites used ahu to manage field systems.

### Van Tilburg's hereditary land use rights argument

42. Van Tilburg [11] "has argued that, 'the archaeological evidence illustrates clearly that the control of subsistence production in agriculture and marine resources was intimately and strongly linked to the typical Polynesian scheme of hereditary land use rights. . .The need constantly to restate that ownership, generation after generation and in the context of a growing population and a changing natural environment, seems to have been one of the driving forces of *ahu* construction, although other social and religious motivations obviously existed.'" (p. 4)

**Assessment**: This argument presupposes a "typical Polynesian scheme of hereditary land use rights" operating on Rapa Nui. The present study's results show that ahu placement is explained by freshwater proximity, which is consistent with the hereditary-rights model but equally consistent with community-level collective action. The spatial data cannot distinguish between these interpretations.

## Summary of Claims Relevant to Chiefdom Attribution

DiNapoli et al. 2019 provides the first formal, quantitative spatial analysis of ahu placement relative to subsistence resources. The central finding is that ahu locations are most parsimoniously explained by proximity to freshwater sources (especially coastal groundwater seeps) and the coastline.

Key implications for the chiefdom question:

1. **Ahu placement does not track agricultural field systems.** This directly contradicts the argument that ahu mark elite control over intensified agriculture (Stevenson et al.).

2. **Marine resources are a spurious correlate.** The apparent ahu-marine association disappears once coastline and freshwater are accounted for. This challenges Kirch's "visual control of marine resources" argument.

3. **No second-order clustering/repulsion.** Once inhomogeneity (freshwater + coastline) is accounted for, ahu exhibit no inter-ahu clustering or dispersion. This means no competitive spacing dynamics between polities need be invoked.

4. **The interpretation is framed in community-level terms.** The paper uses "community access/control" and costly signaling theory, not chiefly command. The territorial display model is compatible with collective action.

5. **Monument construction did not require large labor forces.** This removes the inferential basis for using monuments as evidence of chiefly labor mobilization.

6. **Raw material access was not controlled.** Obsidian, basalt, and tuff quarries show no evidence of restricted access.

7. **All prior hypotheses about ahu placement were informal and untested.** This study is the first to subject the territorial-signaling hypothesis to formal spatial analysis.

The paper does not claim to disprove the chiefdom model, but its findings remove or weaken several of the empirical claims that have been used to support it. The alternative it suggests, community-level competition over freshwater signaled through monument construction, does not require hierarchical political organization.
