# Claims Extraction: Métraux 1937

## Source Information

**Author**: Alfred Métraux, Bishop Museum Fellow, Ethnologist to Bishop Museum

**Fieldwork**: 1934, Franco-Belgian expedition to Easter Island

**Publication**: "The Kings of Easter Island," *Journal of the Polynesian Society*, Vol. 46, No. 2 (182), June 1937, pp. 41-62.

**PDF file**: `docs/references/pdfs/post_slave_raid/metraux_1937/Metraux_1937.pdf`

**Context**: Métraux visited Easter Island in 1934, 72 years after the 1862-1863 slave raids. The population was approximately 450-500. His principal informants included Juan Tepano (Tupahotu, ~60 years old, who had also served as Routledge's interpreter in 1914-1915) and Victoria Rapahango (an "intelligent woman belonging to the royal descent-group of the Miru," p. 44). Métraux was a trained Polynesian ethnographer with comparative knowledge of Mangareva and other island societies. This article is focused specifically on kingship; it predates and is distinct from his 1940 *Ethnology of Easter Island* monograph.

**[POST-RAID]** All claims in this source were recorded 72 years after the slave raids, from a population that had recovered from a nadir of ~111 (1877) to ~450-500. Tepano, the principal informant, was also Routledge's interpreter 20 years earlier; convergence between the two accounts may partly reflect Tepano's consistent narrative rather than fully independent confirmation. However, Métraux also worked with Victoria Rapahango and other informants, and independently analyzed published texts by Roussel, Thomson, and Geiseler.

## 1. Methodology and Source Assessment

### 1.1 Deficiency of evidence acknowledged

**Claim**: "As kingship disintegrated at a very early date, information about the powers of the *ariki*, his functions, and his authority is extremely deficient." (p. 43)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's own assessment (c).

**Note**: This is an important methodological caveat from the ethnographer himself, acknowledging that the evidence base for any claims about ariki-mau authority is thin.

### 1.2 Assessment of Roussel

**Claim**: "Father Roussel seems to have best understood the true nature of kingship." (p. 43)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's assessment (c).

**Note**: Métraux considers Roussel's definition of the king's status "in accord with the scattered information gathered from other sources" (p. 43). He quotes Roussel: "these kings who in the beginning were considered deities and had absolute power on the island, did not retain such authority for long, but only the prestige of a supernatural power with certain personal privileges" (Roussel, 15, p. 358, quoted on p. 43).

### 1.3 Dismissal of Roussel's claim about ancient kingly power

**Claim**: "Of course the opinion of Roussel as to the extension of kingly power in ancient days is an entirely gratuitous assumption, but there is good evidence that his statement for historical times is quite accurate." (p. 58)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's critical assessment (c).

**Note**: This is a crucial analytical move. Métraux accepts Roussel's description of limited power in "historical times" (i.e., the period Roussel observed) while explicitly rejecting Roussel's claim that kings once had "absolute power" as unsupported. This directly addresses the interpretive move by which later scholars have used Roussel's "absolute power" claim as evidence for a pre-contact chiefdom.

### 1.4 Dismissal of Geiseler

**Claim**: "Geiseler (7, p. 41), who visited the island in 1882 for a few days, is not a very reliable source, and he is also the only one who says that 'in former days the almost despotical power of the king contrasted with the condition of the people.'" (p. 58-59)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's assessment (c).

### 1.5 Dismissal of Gana

**Claim**: Gana's report "is only a badly misinterpreted compilation of the data collected by Roussel" and "it is best to discard his discussion." (p. 59)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's assessment (c).

### 1.6 Critical engagement with Thomson

**Claim**: Métraux quotes Thomson's full "arbitrary monarchy" passage (p. 473) at length (p. 59), then argues: "Although in some details Thompson's text seems to contradict my views on the lack of political authority of the king, it contains some other information which supports my conception of the king's power on Easter island. Here again is a precise statement about the sacredness of the king's person, and about the little deference the chiefs paid him when they wanted to fight. The statement that no great homage was paid to the king may be interpreted as disregard of his authority." (p. 59)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's analysis (c).

**Note**: Métraux reads Thomson's own qualifications ("no great homage," "no tax") as supporting the limited-power interpretation, rather than contradicting it. He also notes that Thomson "asserts" the king had real influence, "but there is no way to check his statement" (p. 59).

### 1.7 Métraux's own sources combined

**Claim**: "From my informants I was able to obtain some new details and to collect several legends expressing the sacredness of the king. By combining these several sources of information it is possible to reconstruct, at least partially, the main features of kingship on the island." (p. 43)

**Evidential type**: Methodological statement (c).

## 2. Ariki-Mau: Divine/Supernatural Nature

### 2.1 Divine descent

**Claim**: "The *ariki-mau*, or king of Easter island, was a divine chief descended from the gods." (p. 44)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's synthesis of multiple traditions (b/c).

### 2.2 Honga lineage within the Miru

**Claim**: "My informant, Victoria Rapahango, an intelligent woman belonging to the royal descent-group of the Miru, told me on several occasions that the kings always belonged to the Honga, a lineage or sub-group of the Miru." (p. 44)

**Source**: Victoria Rapahango, directly to Métraux.

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

### 2.3 Tangaroa and Rongo as Honga ancestors

**Claim**: "Thanks to Mrs. Routledge some genealogies of the founders of the lineages in the Miru descent-group have been preserved and published recently by Williamson (19, vol. 2, p. 57). The two first ancestors of Honga were Tangaroa and Rongo." Métraux considers it "highly probable" these were the Polynesian gods, "so frequently claimed as ancestors by chiefly families in the Society islands and the Tuamotus." (p. 44)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's inference from genealogical data (b/c).

### 2.4 Mana: Rokoroko-he-tau legend

**Claim**: Legend of King Nga-ara's son Rokoroko-he-tau, whose mana was so powerful it was destructive: sharks and seals attacked people, white fowls appeared. Though Nga-ara's first two sons "had no *mana*, only one had *mana* and this was Rokoroko-he-tau." People "recognized as *ariki* only Rokoroko-he-tau to whom they presented wreaths and standards, tributes of respect and honour." Nga-ara killed Rokoroko-he-tau to stop the destructive emanations. (pp. 44-46)

**Evidential type**: Legend/tradition collected from informants (b).

**Note**: Legend text given in both Rapanui and English translation. Emphasizes that mana, not birth order, determined legitimacy: "Such an essential attribute of a king was the possession of *mana* that, though the people suffered from the noxious overload of *mana*, they refused to pay any attention to the first-born sons of Nga-ara." (p. 45)

### 2.5 Mana: Tu-ki-haka-he-vari legend

**Claim**: Legend of a king born from a hen's egg after Tangaroa copulated with the hen. The child was found by an old woman, named Tu-ki-haka-he-vari ("curled-up-as-a-chicken-in-an-egg"), and raised at Hakarava. "The people who went with him died on the way because of the *mana* of the king." The king was carried on a litter, "probably to prevent any contact between the ground and the *mana* embodied in him." (pp. 46-48)

**Evidential type**: Legend/tradition collected from informants (b), with Métraux's interpretive commentary (c).

**Note**: The father in this legend is named Tangaroa, which Métraux connects to the god who was "the probable ancestor of the royal Honga family" (p. 46).

### 2.6 Tapu of the king's person

**Claim**: "The entire person of the king was *tapu*; no one could touch him without running the risk of falling dead or of suffering severe pain." (p. 48)

**Evidential type**: Synthesis of traditions (b/c).

### 2.7 Head tapu and hair-cutting prohibition

**Claim**: Quotes Roussel (15, p. 360): "Their heads were *tapu* (sacred). They were obliged to let their hair grow without ever allowing the *mata* (cutting stone) to pass through it." Roussel personally witnessed the incident where cutting Gregorio's hair nearly caused the hairdresser to be stoned. (pp. 48-49)

**Source**: Roussel, eyewitness account.

**Evidential type**: Roussel's direct observation (a) of Gregorio episode; reported tradition for the general rule (b).

### 2.8 Te Haha's hair incident

**Claim**: Te Haha (Nga-ara's attendant) told Routledge that he had "long hair reaching to his heels" and when someone cut it while he slept, "by means of a spell the king blasted the offender who died." (p. 49, citing Routledge 16, p. 242)

**Source**: Te Haha via Routledge.

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

**Note**: Métraux uses this to suggest the hair tapu may have extended beyond the king to "perhaps noblemen related to the king" (p. 48-49).

### 2.9 Hands tapu, belongings tapu

**Claim**: "The hands of kings were also *tapu*." All belongings of the ariki were tapu. Quotes Roussel (15, p. 360): "Their huts, their enclosures, their food, their entire persons and everything they used were *tapu* for other persons of both sexes." (p. 49)

**Source**: Roussel.

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

### 2.10 Food plants vanished with kings

**Claim**: "There is still a belief among the natives that a great many food plants vanished from the island with the kings who controlled them. The favourite variety of sweet-potato called *huatea* is no longer found on Easter island. It could not exist without the king who made it grow, explained my informant." Tortoises disappeared after the kidnapping of Maurata and his family. (p. 54)

**Evidential type**: Contemporary belief among 1934 informants (b).

### 2.11 Ariki-paka skulls and fertility

**Claim**: "The beneficial influence of the king's sacredness was shared by the *ariki-paka* or noblemen. Their skulls made the fowls lay eggs. The same power was probably ascribed to the head of the king, since otherwise the stealing of King Nga-ara's skull would remain unexplained (Routledge, 16, p. 246)." (p. 54)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b) with Métraux's inference (c).

### 2.12 Fertility chant

**Claim**: Métraux re-transcribed and retranslated the fertility chant first published by Thompson (17, pp. 523-524), whose "translation is so free that it is entirely unreliable." With Tepano's help, Métraux reconstructed the native text. The chant celebrates "the beneficent influence of the king upon nature, especially on the sources of staple foods. On him depend the growth of plants, the abundance of fish, favourable atmospheric conditions, in a word everything related to food and the maintenance of life." (pp. 52-54)

Full chant refrain: "Eaha to rau ariki ki te mahua i uta nei?" / "What does the king make fertile in the country?" Items enumerated: sweet potatoes, crayfish, fish, conger eels, nohu, mosses, ferns, kavakava-atua plants, tuna, atu, ature, yams, taro, sugar cane, turtles, stars, sky, heat, sun, moon, dew, chiefs, chiefesses, worms, earwigs, beetles. (pp. 52-54)

**Source**: Thompson's original transcription, retranslated with Tepano's help.

**Evidential type**: Textual analysis (b/c). The chant itself is a traditional text; the retranslation is Métraux and Tepano's work.

## 3. Ariki-Mau: Functions and Prerogatives

### 3.1 Restricted activities

**Claim**: "The only activity permitted to the *ariki* was the recreation of making fishing-lines and nets. They were also permitted to go fishing in canoes." (p. 49)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

### 3.2 Two classes of servants: tūra and haka-pāpa

**Claim**: Métraux describes two classes of servants defined in a legend about the first chiefs. "The *haka-pāpa* had to stay in the house of Tū-ko-ihu. The *tūra* went with nets to catch the *kotea* fish for him." The tūra's duties "were not restricted to fishing. They provided for the entire maintenance of the king; they tilled the soil, gathered seashells for him, and practised all kinds of fishing. The *haka-pāpa* attended the king in the capacity of valets. Among their functions was the serving of the king's meals." (pp. 49-50)

**Evidential type**: Legend text (b) with Métraux's commentary (c).

**Note**: The legend is given in Rapanui with English translation. Métraux notes his informant said "there were several of each class of servants" but "was not sure of the number" (p. 49).

### 3.3 Servants were noblemen (ariki)

**Claim**: "none but the servants, who were noblemen (*ariki*), were allowed to enter his house." (p. 49)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

**Note**: This is significant. The king's servants were themselves ariki (noblemen), not commoners. This describes an elite retinue, not a commoner labor force.

### 3.4 Food tapus on the king

**Claim**: "He could not eat rats under any condition." Te Haha told Routledge that Nga-ara was "extremely angry" when he found Te Haha watching rats being cooked, "for it transpired that, if Te Haha had eaten them, his power for producing chickens would have diminished; presumably because he would have imbibed ratty nature, which was disastrous to eggs and young chickens." (p. 50, citing Routledge 16, p. 242)

Certain fish were reserved for the king: "The *mahore*, *kotea*, *pahika*, and *teteme*, all small fish, were remembered as the king's fish." (p. 50)

**Source**: Te Haha via Routledge; informants for the fish list.

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

### 3.5 Tuna tapu and vaka-vaero fishing

**Claim**: "The *tapu* on tuna fish during the winter months did not apply to the king who with some noblemen could eat the fish without running the risk of being poisoned. The boat in which expert fishermen went to catch tuna during the *tapu* period belonged to the king. It was called *vaka-vaero* and was decorated with cock feathers. All fish caught from the *vaka-vaero* were presented to the king, who either kept them for his own use or, more often, distributed them among the *tangata-honui* (important old men)." (p. 50)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

**Note**: This is evidence for food flowing through the king, but the distribution pattern is king → tangata-honui (elite old men), not king → general population. This is elite-to-elite circulation, not downward redistribution.

### 3.6 First tuna after tapu-lifting

**Claim**: "The first tuna taken after the lifting of the *tapu* in summer were taken to the king, who tasted a bit of the fish and gave the rest to the *tangata-honui*." (p. 50)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

**Note**: Same pattern as 3.5: food flows fishermen → king → tangata-honui, not to the general population.

### 3.7 First-fruits ceremony (Roussel eyewitness)

**Claim**: "The first-fruits of all the products of the land were presented to the king." Métraux quotes Roussel's eyewitness account (15, p. 360): "Such offerings were brought to him with great ceremony. I was present when yams were presented to the little Gregorio who lived in our house. The bearers led the way, followed by a numerous suite and by two files of boys holding in their hands branches of *hau*, peeled and stained dark. The procession was more like torch-bearing at a funeral than anything else. From time to time the air sounded with cries and with chants I was unable to understand." (pp. 54-55)

**Source**: Roussel, eyewitness account of ceremony for child-king Gregorio.

**Evidential type**: Roussel's direct observation (a).

**Note**: This is the strongest evidence for chiefly economic prerogatives in any source. However, Gregorio was a child in a devastated society (~650 people), and the ceremony's persistence may represent residual practice rather than a functioning redistributive economy.

### 3.8 Harvest tapu

**Claim**: Roussel (quoted by Métraux): "Only the king could initiate the harvesting of the crops which were *tapu* as long as he ordered them to be. Alas for the man who dared to violate the *tapu*. Often he was deprived of his property and possibly of his life." (p. 54-55)

**Source**: Roussel (15, p. 360).

**Evidential type**: Roussel's report, partly direct observation, partly tradition (a/b).

**Note**: The harvest tapu represents economic control through supernatural sanction, not political taxation. The enforcement mechanism is the tapu itself ("Alas for the man who dared to violate the *tapu*"), not political coercion.

### 3.9 Tuna given to king (Roussel)

**Claim**: "The tuna fish (*kahi*) caught by the fishermen were given to the king (Roussel, 15, p. 428)." (p. 55)

**Source**: Roussel.

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition or observation (a/b).

### 3.10 Rain prayers delegated to ariki-paka

**Claim**: "In time of drought he sent his son or another *ariki-paka* to perform rites in which Hiro was beseeched to make the rain fall. Such is the tradition obtained by Routledge (16, p. 242) and by me. Very likely the *ariki-paka* in charge of this duty was a high priest related to the king." (p. 55)

**Source**: Routledge and Métraux, independently.

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b), confirmed by two researchers.

### 3.11 Not a priest

**Claim**: "The sacredness of the *ariki-mau* is beyond any doubt, but this does not imply that he was a priest (*iviatua*) inspired by the gods and with a complete knowledge of the ritual. In Polynesia the divine chiefs played only a passive part in the religious life and this was probably true of the Easter island king." (p. 55)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's Polynesian comparative analysis (c).

**Note**: Métraux explicitly distinguishes the ariki-mau's sacred status from priestly function. "The importance of the sacerdotal group which came right after the king and the lack of any reference to the king as a priest point out that his functions were not confused with the ritualistic and prophetic activities of the ordinary priests." (p. 55)

### 3.12 Religious duties enumerated

**Claim**: "Many of the king's duties were purely religious. He inaugurated the harvest, received the first fruits of the crops, consecrated new houses or canoes, placed or lifted *tapu* and presided over many ceremonies." (p. 55)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's synthesis (c).

### 3.13 Twins presented to king

**Claim**: "All twins were presented to him to be given a 'royal name.'" Métraux "was unable to get details about the special names or the mystical beliefs about twins which necessitate the intervention of the *ariki*." (p. 55)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

### 3.14 Supervisory control over rongorongo and tattooing

**Claim**: "the king had a supervisory authority over activities of a more or less religious nature. For instance, the learning of chants and the tattooing of young men, both of which had a great deal to do with religion, were under the king's control." (p. 55)

Young men, recently tattooed, "gathered in Anakena and exhibited their designs to the king who sat on the head of an old image. The king examined their tattoo and if he was satisfied with the work of the artist he said to the young man, '*Ka hoa ki Tuna-roa*' (Go to the ahu Tuna-roa), but if he judged the motifs poor he told the unfortunate bearer of them to '*Ka hoa ki ahu runga*' (Go to the upper *ahu*). He criticized the form and the size of the piercings of the ears and probably the ornaments which were worn in them." (pp. 55-56)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

### 3.15 Rongorongo assembly at Anakena

**Claim**: "Tradition recounts that each year the *rongorongo* men went to Anakena to read the tablets to the king." Te Haha told Routledge that Nga-ara and his son Kamakoi "sat on seats made of tablets, and each had a tablet in his hand, they wore feather hats, as did all the professors." (p. 56, citing Routledge 16, p. 245)

"The people of the neighbouring districts brought offerings of food to Nga-ara for distribution among the *rongorongo* men." The king "listened attentively to the chants and punished those who made mistakes, which caused them to be laughed at." (p. 56)

Routledge adds: "When the function was over, the Ariki stood on a platform borne by eight men and addressed the *rongorongo* men on their duties, and doing well, and gave them each a chicken." Also, "there were minor assemblies at new moon, or at the last quarter of the moon, when the *rongorongo* men came to Anakena." (p. 56, quoting Routledge 16, p. 246)

**Source**: Te Haha via Routledge, confirmed by Métraux.

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

**Note**: The food mobilization is specific to the rongorongo assembly context, not routine redistribution. Food flows from "neighbouring districts" → king → rongorongo men. This is ceremonial-context mobilization, not a systematic redistributive economy.

### 3.16 Pua and huhu/maru presentations

**Claim**: Nga-ara "was visited one month in the year by all people who brought him the plant known as *pua* on the end of sticks, put the *pua* into his house, and retired backwards." (p. 58, citing Routledge 16, p. 243)

Also, "once a month (Tepano insisted once a week) the people went to Anakena to present the *ariki-mau* with *huhu* and *maru*." The *huhu* was "a pole with three to five small red, white, or black sticks inserted into it" with ends decorated with cock-feathers and a long feather garland (*maro*) at top. "The *huhu* and the *maru* were planted in the ground in front of the king's house and left there until they rotted." (pp. 57-58)

**Source**: Routledge (pua); Tepano (huhu/maru descriptions).

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

**Note**: These are tokens of respect and deference, not material tribute.

### 3.17 Food-increase magic via ariki-paka

**Claim**: Te Haha told Routledge that "if the people wanted chickens, they applied to the Ariki-mau, who sent him with *maru*, and his visits were always attended with satisfactory results." (p. 58, citing Routledge 16, p. 242)

**Source**: Te Haha via Routledge.

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

## 4. Succession and Royal Residence

### 4.1 Succession by abdication when son marries

**Claim**: "A king resigned his power in favour of his first-born son when he married. My informant, Juan Tepano, was positive about this fact, which is confirmed by Roussel (15, p. 360). 'The king resigned on the occasion of the marriage of his son, but his wedding could be delayed for a long time, since the custom of the country allowed him to marry only at an advanced age.'" (pp. 51-52)

**Source**: Tepano and Roussel, independently.

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

### 4.2 Geiseler mistaken about brother succession

**Claim**: "Geiseler (7, p. 41) is certainly mistaken when he says that after the king's death the rule passed to his brother." (p. 52)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's assessment (c).

### 4.3 Royal residence at Anakena

**Claim**: "Nga-ara, the last king of the island to die with full rank had his headquarters at Anakena." Anakena "was in the territory of the Honga lineage of kings. The six *ahu* which stand along the beach received the bones of the members of the Honga group, and the kings were buried there." (pp. 50-51)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

### 4.4 Changes of residence pattern

**Claim**: "According to a tradition that I was unable to check, each new king went to live at Ahu-akapu, near Tahai on the west coast. Later, when he had yielded his authority to his son he went to Tahai and then to Anakena where he spent his last years." Nga-ara was "the only king who died in Tahai and his remains were buried in the big *ahu* there." (p. 51)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b), which Métraux explicitly says he "was unable to check."

### 4.5 Young princes raised at Papa-o-pea

**Claim**: "The young princes were raised in a village called Papa-o-pea, near Ohau, not far from Ahu-te-peu." (p. 51)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

### 4.6 War forced relocations

**Claim**: Roussel: "Through prudence or fear some of them abandoned their homes to retire as far as possible from the war theatre, hence their sojourn at Hanga-roa, Anakena and Hotu-iti" (Roussel, 15, p. 360, quoted on p. 51). The king's sacredness "put him above the vicissitudes of the war" but did not protect him from being displaced.

**Source**: Roussel.

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

## 5. Political Power Assessment

### 5.1 No great political power

**Claim**: "Nothing is known about the activities and duties of the king to suggest any great political power. He was not a ruler in the common sense of the word." (p. 58)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's assessment (c).

### 5.2 Routledge's characterization endorsed

**Claim**: "So little has survived in the memory of the natives about the king's authority that Routledge (16, p. 242) could assume that the *ariki-mau* was 'neither a leader in war nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest.'" (p. 58)

**Evidential type**: Métraux endorsing Routledge's conclusion (c).

### 5.3 Roussel on authority "nil and entirely disregarded"

**Claim**: Métraux quotes Roussel (15, p. 360): "In late times, although the kings were still surrounded with respect by the natives who considered their persons to be sacred, their authority was nil and entirely disregarded. It has passed entirely into the hands of the *mata-toa* [warriors] who decided everything and carried on the war without beforehand consulting his majesty." Métraux judges this "seems to be very near the truth." (p. 58)

**Source**: Roussel, quoted and endorsed by Métraux.

**Evidential type**: Roussel's assessment (b/c), endorsed by Métraux (c).

### 5.4 Roussel's "entirely gratuitous assumption"

**Claim**: "Of course the opinion of Roussel as to the extension of kingly power in ancient days is an entirely gratuitous assumption." (p. 58)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's critical assessment (c).

**Note**: This is the key sentence. Métraux accepts Roussel's observation that authority was "nil" in historical times, but rejects the claim that kings once had absolute power as unsubstantiated projection.

### 5.5 Thomson's "arbitrary monarchy" reinterpreted

**Claim**: Métraux quotes Thomson's "arbitrary monarchy" passage at length (p. 59), then argues: "it contains some other information which supports my conception of the king's power on Easter island. Here again is a precise statement about the sacredness of the king's person, and about the little deference the chiefs paid him when they wanted to fight. The statement that no great homage was paid to the king may be interpreted as disregard of his authority." (p. 59)

Thomson "asserts" the king had real influence, "but there is no way to check his statement. It is probable that owing to his prestige the king might have acted as a mediator in quarrels." (p. 59)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's critical analysis of Thomson (c).

### 5.6 Ngaara captivity as evidence of political insignificance

**Claim**: "An historical incident which occurred a little before 1862 illustrates strikingly the insignificance of the king's political position. The Ngaure descent-group succeeded in defeating the Miru whom they took as slaves (*mata-kio*). The king, his son Kamakoi, and his grandson Maurata were held in captivity in the territory of the Ngaure and were not rescued until a few years later by the Miru who had in the meantime united with the Tupahotu." (p. 60)

**Source**: Reported tradition (b).

### 5.7 Key conclusion: "certainly not a ruler or civilian chief"

**Claim**: "The *ariki-mau* was respected because of his pedigree which related him with the gods and because of the sacredness of his person, but he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief. He transmitted the *mana* of the god, and it was necessary for the welfare of the island to keep him, but beyond this usefulness he was not allowed to enjoy any real power other than this more or less strictly religious and aristocratic prestige." (p. 60)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's summary conclusion (c).

### 5.8 Final summary

**Claim**: "His function in society was to insure through his very being the abundance of crops and the fertility of the ground and to exercise his influence on animal life. From his sacredness derived certain religious activities, and he held supervisory control over various practices connected with religion. His political power was little." (p. 61)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's final assessment (c).

## 6. Tangata Manu

### 6.1 Rejection of Williamson's dual-king hypothesis

**Claim**: Williamson hypothesized "the kingship of Easter island was of a double nature," with the tangata-manu as a "secular king elected each year" alongside the ordinary king (Williamson, 19, vol. 1, p. 405). Métraux rejects this: "The more detailed accounts of Thompson (17, p. 483) and of Routledge (16, pp. 254-266) of the bird-cult, together with some scattered evidence gathered by me, do not speak in favour of Williamson's hypothesis." (p. 60)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's critical assessment (c).

### 6.2 Tangata-manu as provisional incarnation of Makemake

**Claim**: "The analysis of available documents had already induced me to consider the *tangata-manu* as a man incarnating provisionally the god Makemake and entitled to rights of an aggressive nature." (p. 60)

**Evidential type**: Métraux's interpretation (c).

### 6.3 Roussel's matatoa passage

**Claim**: Métraux quotes Roussel (15, p. 427): "the great chief was out of the ordinary affairs and that chance, the discovery of seabird eggs gave authority to those lucky enough to find them. They took the name of *matortoa* (warriors). But the investiture of such an authority began only after they had shaved their heads. Their activity consisted in marauding day and night and in trying to fool as many people as they could during the year they were in power, and in taking part in continuous feasts, the most childish and licentious." (p. 61)

**Source**: Roussel (15, p. 427).

**Evidential type**: Roussel's report (b).

## 7. Post-Kingship History

### 7.1 Gregorio as shadowy child king

**Claim**: After Maurata (the last king) died in the guano islands, "there still existed a shadowy kingship incarnated in the person of a 12-year old boy. He was Gregorio." Gregorio "died, a catechumen in the Mission, when he was twelve, 'equally regretted by the missionaries and his subjects' (Roussel, 15, p. 358)." A missionary states "the natives felt for him a certain respect; 'they would bring him the first yams, but he never interfered in affairs of state' (Ollivier, 12, p. 255)." (pp. 41-42)

**Source**: Roussel and Ollivier.

**Evidential type**: Missionary reports (a/b).

### 7.2 Atamu Te Kena and Riroroko as fictitious royalty

**Claim**: Post-Chilean annexation, Atamu Te Kena and Riroroko were called kings but "informants make it clear that they had very little in common with the *ariki* of olden days. Their power was of an indefinite, dubious nature, and they seem to have enjoyed none of the prerogatives of former *ariki*. Perhaps their only claim to the title lay in their descent-line; both belonged to the Miru group." Atamu Te Kena was made "king" by Bishop Jaussen of Tahiti (p. 42). This was "fictitious and ephemeral royalty" (p. 42).

**Evidential type**: Métraux's assessment based on informants (b/c).

### 7.3 Failed Chilean attempt to make Tepano king

**Claim**: "Twenty years ago the Chilean officers of the *Baquedano* tried to proclaim my informant, Juan Tepano, king. His new dignity was conferred upon him in a public ceremony, but no one took it very seriously, not even the new sovereign himself." (p. 43)

**Source**: Tepano, directly to Métraux.

**Evidential type**: Reported personal experience (a/b).

## 8. King's Dress and Physical Markers

### 8.1 Royal dress

**Claim**: "The dress of the king was not very different from that of any wealthy man. He wore a beautiful *tapa* cloak stained yellow (*mia*) with turmeric, and he covered his head with 'feather hats of various descriptions.' Six *tahonga* (wooden balls) and six small wooden crescents (*rei-miro*) called *rei-matapuku*, hung from his neck back and front." (p. 50)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).

### 8.2 Sons of kings had down under eyes

**Claim**: "The only physical characteristic of the sons of kings was an alleged growth of down under the eyes." (p. 50)

**Evidential type**: Reported tradition (b).
