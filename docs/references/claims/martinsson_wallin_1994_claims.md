## I. Intellectual Genealogy

### Who Martinsson-Wallin cites

1. **Sahlins 1958** (*Social Stratification in Polynesia*): Cited in the introduction for the general chiefdom model: "Studies of the Polynesian chiefdom society have for a long period of time interested scientists from all over the world and the Polynesian way of structuring the society has been used extensively as a general model in prehistoric explanation (Sahlins 1958)" (p. 16). MW accepts the chiefdom framing as background context but does not build her analysis on it.

2. **Sahlins 1988** (*Kapten Cooks dod*, Swedish translation of *Islands of History*): Cited extensively in Ch 10 for the structuralist framework of change, particularly the "non-differentiation" tendency. MW draws on Sahlins's analysis of how Polynesian ranking and *tabu* systems move toward "a probable condition of non differentiation" (Sahlins 1988 pp. 149-150, cited on p. 131). Also cited for the structural change framework where "every new situation must be incorporated into the culture through a relevant regulation of its structure" (p. 134).

3. **Kirch 1984** (*Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms*): Cited for Polynesian settlement models and colonization patterns (pp. 108-109), the comparative framework for *ahu/marae* development (p. 109), and the general observation that "Easter Island ahu complex represents a remarkably elaborate and sophisticated development of the East Polynesian marae concept" (Kirch 1984 p. 270, quoted on p. 110). MW does not cite Kirch for the chiefdom typology or for hierarchical territorial models specific to Rapa Nui.

4. **Service 1962** (*Primitive Social Organization*): Cited once, on p. 42, in the context of describing Stevenson 1984's approach as "ethnoarchaeological 'middle range theory' where a certain type of organisation of the material culture indicates the structure of society (Service 1962, 1975)." MW does not use Service's chiefdom typology for her own analysis.

5. **Metraux 1940** (*Ethnology of Easter Island*): Cited extensively throughout for ethnohistoric data on clan divisions, territorial boundaries, social classes, genealogies, and religious practices (pp. 33, 49-51, 75-76, 103-106, 129, 131). MW quotes Metraux on the paucity of the surviving record: "I know of few places in the Pacific where so little remains of the ancient culture" (Metraux 1940 p. 3, quoted p. 33). [POST-RAID]

6. **Routledge 1919** (*The Mystery of Easter Island*): Cited for territorial divisions, clan structure, and survey data. Figure 13 (p. 35) presents "Territorial division on Easter Island (based on Routledge 1919)." MW uses Routledge's divisions throughout as an analytical framework but treats them as a convenience, not as established fact: "it is difficult to have exact knowledge regarding the location of district borders and smaller land divisions" (p. 103). [POST-RAID]

7. **McCoy 1976** (settlement pattern dissertation): Cited for settlement pattern data, family unit structure (10-12 individuals), the *kainga* model, and the observation that "Ruins of ancient settlements are always thick around water holes" (Metraux 1940 p. 11, quoted via McCoy on p. 105). Also cited for the view that warfare was linked to unequal resource distribution and water access (p. 105).

8. **Stevenson 1984** (dissertation): Cited and critiqued extensively in Ch 3 (pp. 41-43). MW reports Stevenson's five-type cluster analysis and his chronological model, then delivers a pointed critique (see claims 28-31). Also cited in Ch 8 for settlement pattern data and the suggestion that late secondary elite centres were located inland (p. 104).

9. **Van Tilburg 1986** (doctoral thesis): Cited for statue ideology, the view that statues were "symbols of a dominant kinship based ideology" (p. 130, citing Van Tilburg), and for the interpretation that statues functioned as "a power symbol where power was considered to be a positive force intimately associated with the social order of production, reproduction, tion and transformation" (p. 44).

10. **Handy 1927** (*Polynesian Religion*): Cited for the connection between stones, *mana*, land, and *marae*: "this ceremonial use of stones to seal a land claim is closely related to the building of stone marae for the purpose, among other, of establishing land rights... In the marae, corner stones marked the limits of the sacred precincts" (Handy 1927 p. 180, quoted on p. 128). Also cited for the symbolic significance of *ao/po* dualism (p. 133).

11. **Mulloy 1961, 1978** (Mulloy and Figueroa): Cited extensively for ahu excavation data, particularly Vinapu and A Kivi-Vai Teka complex (pp. 36-39, 73-74). MW notes Mulloy's opinion that the three-period division framework reflected differences in construction quality attributable to "lack of motivation or carelessness" (Mulloy 1961a p. 160, quoted on p. 38).

12. **Heyerdahl 1961** (Norwegian expedition reports): Cited for excavation data and the South American contact hypothesis. MW entertains the possibility of South American influence on well-dressed stone masonry more than current scholarship supports (pp. 110-112, 127-128).

13. **Englert 1974** (*La Tierra de Hotu Matu'a*): Cited for ahu typology (six types) and survey data. "He was the first who undertook a thorough survey of the *ahu*. He described 245 structures, which he classified into six different types" (p. 18).

14. **Thompson 1891**: Cited for legends, genealogies of 57 kings (p. 50), and territorial information. [POST-RAID]

15. **Rounds-Beardsley 1990** (PhD dissertation): Cited for the spatial analysis of 42 image ahu on the south and west coasts. MW summarizes her nearest-neighbor and cluster analysis results: "four major territories were identified in the reference area" (p. 43). But MW notes the methods "by themselves were limited" (p. 43).

16. **Hodder 1986, 1989** and **Tilley 1989, 1991**: Cited for the structuralist/post-processual theoretical framework that informs the thesis. MW states: "In principle this implies a structuralistic way of thinking, but one which considers time and space" (p. 17, citing Hodder 1989, Sahlins 1988, Tilley 1989, 1990, Martinsson-Wallin 1993).

17. **Ayres 1973** (PhD thesis): Cited for the view that the three-period division "was no longer valid" and that development should instead be understood as "internal developmental phases within a culturally unified period" (p. 39, summarizing Ayres). Also cited for early radiocarbon dates from Tahai (pp. 39-40) and the opinion that structures with vertical non-worked or partly worked slabs may constitute an early type (p. 40).

18. **Goldman** (*Ancient Polynesian Society*): Not prominently cited. Not mentioned in MW's bibliography (confirmed by checking the bibliography on pp. 143-148). Goldman's "Open" classification of Easter Island is therefore absent from this study.

### What is NOT cited

19. **Goldman 1970 NOT cited.** The absence of Goldman is significant because Stevenson 2002 would later cite Goldman alongside Kirch and Sahlins as establishing the chiefdom. MW's analysis proceeds without the Goldman framework entirely.

20. **Sahlins 1963 NOT cited.** The paper that placed all Polynesian societies on the "chief" side of the stratification gradient is absent.

21. **Sahlins & Service 1960 NOT cited.** The general/specific evolution framework is absent.

22. **Feinman & Neitzel 1984 NOT cited.** The major critique of the chiefdom concept is absent, though MW's own findings effectively support their argument that variability within the chiefdom category is too great for the term to be analytically useful.

### Assessment: what the citation network reveals

23. **MW's theoretical framework is structuralist, not neoevolutionary.** Her approach is explicitly stated: "all material culture is meaningful within its context" and the individual parts of material culture "are to be understood in relation to one another" (p. 17). She cites Hodder, Tilley, and Sahlins 1988 (the structuralist Sahlins, not the neoevolutionary Sahlins). She explicitly critiques Stevenson's processual approach as "deterministic and general" (p. 42). This positions her analysis outside the neoevolutionary framework that produces chiefdom attributions.

24. **Service is cited but not used.** The single citation to Service 1962 occurs in MW's description of Stevenson's approach, not in her own analysis. She does not adopt Service's typology.

25. **The chiefdom concept is background, not framework.** MW mentions the "Polynesian chiefdom society" once in her introduction (p. 16) as context, then proceeds without using the concept analytically. Her conclusions about ahu distribution and social organization do not depend on the chiefdom model.

## II. Theoretical Framework (Ch 1, pp. 17-18)

26. **Structuralist approach stated**: "A theoretical supposition is that all material culture is meaningful within its context and thus, the appearance and usage of the material culture result from meaning productions... The material culture is seen as readable texts when explored within its context" (p. 17). MW explicitly distances herself from processual/functional approaches.

27. **Anti-reductionist position**: "The view of the prehistory is thereby not reduced to simple generalisations but appears as a diversified network where the individual, the society, and the natural conditions and phenomena constitute active parts" (p. 16). This is a direct contrast to the type of functional generalizations that produce chiefdom typologies.

## III. Critique of Stevenson 1984 Cluster Analysis (Ch 3, pp. 42-43)

28. **Missing attributes in Stevenson's typology**: "A closer study of the *ahu* that were classified into different types revealed that the attributes signifying the different types, to a large extent were not present. I have performed closer studies of the information found in the appendices of his study and in only 6 cases out of 33 were all type defining attributes present, and in as many as 12 *ahu* two or more of these attributes were missing" (p. 42). This is devastating: only 18% of Stevenson's classified ahu had all the attributes that defined their type.

29. **Critique of deterministic model**: "His ethnoarchaeological model concerning society's organisation has a deterministic and general structure, and in my opinion he does not give appropriate weight to the specific cultural historical context of Easter Island" (p. 42). MW charges Stevenson with imposing a general model rather than attending to the specific Rapa Nui evidence.

30. **Stevenson's chronological model as reported by MW** (p. 42-43, citing Stevenson 1984 pp. 162-164):
- AD 1100-1300: "there were no institutionalised forms of ranking... nuclear families or extended families"
- AD 1300-1500: "an organisation based on lineage groups was noted, which put a special emphasis on the corporate group... These lineage groups were however not ranked in relation to one another"
- AD 1500-1700: "regional centres emerged, and this may have indicated that special lineage groups co-operated. There was possibly a ranking between different lineage groups at that time"
- After AD 1700: "there is nothing that indicates the earlier type of ranking among the lineage groups and co-operation between the different lineage groups seems to have ended"

31. **The significance of Stevenson's 1984 model for the hardening trajectory**: In 1984, Stevenson found no ranking before AD 1300, possible ranking only from AD 1500-1700, and no ranking after AD 1700. He described "corporate groups" not "chiefdom districts." By 2002, the same data becomes a "well-developed chiefdom" with 11 territorial divisions and a possible paramount chief.

## V. Construction Analysis (Ch 6, pp. 55-70)

40. **Database scale**: 313 ahu total, of which 164 are image ahu analyzed with 112 different variables organized in 20 variable groups (pp. 18, 55-60, 136). Also registered: 57 semi-pyramidal ahu, 26 rectangular ahu, 8 ahu poepoe, 18 ahu avanga, and 40 not classified (p. 67).

41. **Missing data predominant**: "In most variable groups the variable called 'absence of information' is the predominant one. This is due to the fact that many structures are in a state of ruin or were secondarily re-modelled, and this makes it difficult to decide what variables originally belonged to the structure" (p. 58).

42. **Chi-square tests: H0 accepted in most cases**: "The analysis exhibits that the H0 hypothesis is accepted in most cases... The distribution among the variables is thereby the expected one, and variations from the expected are most likely dependent on chance" (p. 61). The variables within and between size groups are independent, meaning construction features are not systematically correlated with size or type in the way a territorial hierarchy model would predict.

43. **Diverging variable group: pukao**: "In a few cases H0 is dismissed and the alternative hypothesis H1 is indicated" (p. 61). The diverging group is "concerning the absence or occurrence of *pukao* (variables 11.2-11.3+11.4)" (p. 63). The absence of *pukao* is more common on constructions with unworked stones than on those with worked stones.

44. **Larger ahu have worked rear walls**: "The size group analysis has indicated that structures belonging to the larger size groups are built with more well dressed or worked stones in the rear wall" (p. 63). This is a size-technique correlation, not a territorial one.

45. **Correspondence analysis: homogeneity confirmed**: "Both test one and two resulted in scattergrams which showed relatively homogeneous patterns. One more or less dense cluster is exhibited and some scattered variables and *ahu* are situated around this cluster and some variables and *ahu* that radically diverge from the others occur as well" (p. 66). The dense central cluster represents the "original or classical type of *ahu*" while outliers "may represent temporal, spatial or social variations" (p. 66).

46. **Complex type division proved difficult**: "The results of the correspondence analysis indicated that the *image ahu* are rather homogenous and it is difficult to distinguish different complex types. It seems that the structures were built according to one specific concept, but some construction variables may have been added over time, or resulted due to spatial or social variations. A complex type division proved to be difficult at least when using these categories of variables" (p. 68).

## VI. Ahu Homogeneity Finding (Ch 6, Ch 11)

47. **Formal statement of homogeneity (Ch 11 summary)**: "The result of the correspondence analysis in this study revealed that the *image ahu* are rather homogenous and that it is difficult to distinguish different complex types. The descriptive statistical analysis indicated that additional weight should be given to the variables concerning the appearance of the rear wall of the central platform when performing a type division. This was supported by the correspondence analysis" (p. 138). THIS IS THE KEY FINDING that Stevenson 2002 would override.

48. **Correspondence analysis exhibited great conformity**: "A correspondence analysis exhibited that there is great conformity in the appearance between different *ahu*" (p. 140, summary chapter). MW's island-wide statistical analysis found the opposite of what a hierarchical territorial model would predict.

49. **Four rear wall types, not complex social types**: The type division MW considers viable is based solely on the rear wall of the central platform: "1) Well dressed rear wall (3.2). 2) Worked rear wall (3.3). 3) Partly worked rear wall (3.4) and 4) Non-worked rear wall (3.5, 3.6)" (p. 70). These are construction technique categories, not social hierarchy markers.

## VII. No Territorial Size Correlation (Ch 8, Ch 11) -- THE KEY FINDING

50. **Equal distribution of significant ahu (Ch 8.5, p. 95)**: "It is noticed that large *ahu* (size group 3) are distributed all around the island and may mainly be indicative of early structures and/or elite centres. Further it is noticed that these structures are distributed in all districts. The Miru district and the Hotu iti area show a majority of large *ahu*, but if comparing the size of the district and the number of large *ahu*, the distribution is rather even between all districts."

51. **Five to six significant ahu per district (Ch 8.9, p. 106)**: "if the number of significant *ahu* in each district is compared with other districts there are similarities. There are generally five to six significant *ahu* in each district, independent of the size of the district. This may indicate a social equality between different clans." THIS FINDING IS STATED THREE TIMES in the book (pp. 95, 106, 139).

52. **Third statement of equal distribution (Ch 11, p. 139)**: "if the number of significant *ahu* in each district is compared with other districts there are similarities. There are generally five to six significant *ahu* in each district, independent of the size of the district. This may indicate a differentiation between different lineage groups in each district, but if the number of significant *ahu* in each district is compared with other districts there are similarities."

53. **Large ahu evenly distributed (Ch 11, p. 139)**: "Large structures are more evenly distributed around the island than are small and middle sized ones, which are mainly found on the south and north coast."

54. **Significance for the chiefdom question**: The equal distribution of significant ahu across districts of different sizes is inconsistent with hierarchical territorial organization. If some districts commanded more labor or had greater political power, their ahu should reflect this through greater elaboration or number. Instead, MW finds uniformity, which she interprets as indicating "social equality between different clans."

## VIII. Territorial Boundaries -- Difficulty Establishing (Ch 8)

55. **Borders difficult to establish (p. 86/90)**: "it is difficult to establish any borders between different territories" (paraphrased from the discussion on pp. 86, 90). MW states explicitly: "In this pattern it is difficult to establish borders between different territories" (p. 90) and "it is difficult to have exact knowledge regarding the location of district borders and smaller land divisions" (p. 103).

56. **Agglomeration point ambiguity (p. 86/90)**: MW notes that agglomeration points of ahu "may be interpreted as the core of a territorial unit, but may as well indicate a border between two units" (p. 90). The same clustering pattern that Stevenson 2002 would interpret as district centers could equally indicate inter-group boundary zones.

57. **Lineage group division is hypothetical (p. 104)**: "The division of the lineage groups performed here is hypothetical and further analyses of the material remains are needed to affirm or dismiss such a division" (p. 104). MW explicitly marks her own territorial discussion as preliminary, in contrast to Stevenson 2002's confident delineation of 11 districts.

## IX. Water Resources at Ahu Sites (Ch 8.8, pp. 105-107)

58. **Water resources at all major sites**: "water resources and quarries are ample in the south western part of the island, but water resources exist at all major *ahu* and settlement sites" (p. 105). This finding anticipates DiNapoli et al. 2019's demonstration that freshwater proximity is a primary predictor of ahu placement.

59. **Metraux quote on water and settlement**: MW quotes Metraux via McCoy: "Ruins of ancient settlements are always thick around water holes" (Metraux 1940 p. 11, quoted on p. 105).

60. **Water resources important for localization (summary)**: "water resources were important factors for localisation of settlements" (p. 139, repeated from p. 107). MW integrates environmental factors with the social/symbolic analysis, concluding that resource access influenced ahu placement even though her primary interpretive framework is structuralist.

## X. Non-differentiation in Late Period (Ch 10, pp. 134-135)

61. **Society moved in non-differentiated direction**: "There are strong indications that the society moved in a non-differentiated direction at the end of the prehistoric phase. The ceremonial structures may be seen as symbols of power, and a means to maintain the power was through the use of certain *tabu* regulations. If a society moves towards non-differentiation there may be difficulties in maintaining *tabu* regulations and then it will be difficult to exhibit the power through the monuments" (p. 135).

62. **Chiefly authority reduced to symbolic linkage**: "The old authority, the leading chief and his lineage group were still important, but only as a means to assure the society of linkage to the ancestors and the gods, and they probably had very little to do with the political power" (p. 135). This is repeated verbatim in the summary (p. 142).

63. **Bird-man cult replaced chiefly authority**: "The new power was thereby legitimised by a concept that was probably already incorporated in the earlier system. This was the bird-man cult, the fertility cult. The one who had the power over fertility, in other words, power over life and death, also obtained the power over the society" (p. 135).

64. **Sahlins non-differentiation tendency**: MW draws on Sahlins 1988 for the theoretical framework: "the ranking and *tabu* system always move toward a probable condition of non differentiation" (Sahlins 1988 pp. 149-150, cited on p. 131). The non-differentiation finding is not peculiar to Rapa Nui in MW's analysis; it reflects a general Polynesian tendency.

## XI. Spatial Distribution Analysis (Ch 8, pp. 86-107)

65. **All ahu types along coast**: "All types of *ahu* have been included in this dispersal pattern and eight areas show a high degree of agglomeration, mainly on the north and the south coasts" (p. 87). The north coast shows the largest agglomeration at Anakena, Hanga Hoonu, and Hanga o Miti; the south coast at Akahanga and Hanga Poukura.

66. **South coast shows early and extensive use**: "early *ahu* are more frequent on the south coast and that *ahu* around Hanga Roa are late" (p. 87). "An extensive utilisation of the south coast is indicated from early times and all through the prehistoric times" (p. 107).

67. **Inland ahu are late**: "Inland *ahu* are suggested to be late, but some of them may be explained by the fact that they have had a specific function, as that of *ahu* Huri a Urenga" (p. 106, repeated p. 139).

68. **Settlement pattern**: "The settlement pattern furthermore indicates that each lineage occupied a narrow stretch of land that extended from the coast toward the interior" (p. 139). Each lineage group "populated a so called *kainga* area, built one *ahu*" (p. 103, summarizing ethnohistoric sources).

69. **Miru district largest**: "A hypothetical division of the lineage groups in the districts was presented, where one *image ahu* was considered to represent one lineage group. This division exhibited the largest number of lineage groups in the Miru districts (24) and the smallest number in the Haumoana district (3)" (p. 139). But this variation in number of lineage groups does not translate into variation in significant ahu count (see claim 51).

## XII. Mana, Power, and Worked Stones (Ch 10, pp. 128-131)

70. **Stones associated with mana**: "Stones, especially worked stones, have been associated with power (*mana*), and the divine" (p. 128). "Worked stones have mainly been used in constructions associated with *mana*. Particularly worked stones related to ceremonial structures, which exhibit power and sacredness, were important" (p. 128).

71. **Ahu as network of ancestors, chiefs, and land**: MW proposes that ahu formed "a complex network concerning ancestors/chief, land/ceremonial structure, which could be interrelated in different ways. Stones for ceremonial usage, often worked, and chiefs, served as the visible mediums in this network" (pp. 128-129).

72. **Chief's umbilical cord (iho) and ahu**: "The chief was named *iho* (umbilicus cord/pit/core), and he was considered to be the most sacred medium in relation to the ancestors. The ceremonial constructions were thought of as the umbilical cord of the land, as was evident by the fact that the chief's umbilical cord was placed there" (p. 141, summarizing material from pp. 128-129).

73. **Statues as symbols of dominant kinship ideology**: MW cites Van Tilburg for the view that statues were "symbols of a dominant kinship based ideology" (p. 130). She adds that the statues, "facing inland, turned their backs to the sea," may be interpreted as "a symbolic way of controlling their own people" (p. 141).

## XIII. Social Organization (Ch 5, pp. 49-52)

74. **Four social classes from Metraux**: "According to Metraux there were four different 'classes' within the population, *ariki* (chief), *ivi-atua* (priest), *mata-toa* (warrior) and *kio* (servant/slave). The rest of the population was called *hurumanu*" (p. 50).

75. **Ten clans divided into two confederations**: "The clans were divided into two groups, *Kotu'u* and *Hotu iti*. The clans on the west side of the island (Marama, Haumoana, Ngatimo and Miru) belonged to the former and the clans of the east side of the island (Tupahotu, Koro'orongo, Hitiuira and Ngaure) belonged to the latter" (p. 50). [POST-RAID]

76. **Ariki mau declining power**: "In the beginning the *ariki mau* had total power, but with time his political power decreased, even though he still had great prestige and *mana* and was very *tabu*. His religious power and some of his economic power was thereby intact. The warriors obtained more political power with time and became the leaders of the clan" (p. 51, summarizing Metraux 1940 pp. 129-138). [POST-RAID]

77. **Six levels of segmentation**: "According to him [McCall 1976] there were six levels of segmentation where the first segment constituted the whole island, which was divided into two confederations, *Tuu Aro* and *Hotu iti*. They were further divided in different lineage groups, so called *mata*" (p. 51).

## XIV. Ahu Chronology (Ch 7, Ch 11)

78. **82 radiocarbon dates**: "It was possible to obtain information regarding 82 14C datings on Easter Island. Thirty-five are associated with *ahu* and forty-seven with settlements and statues" (p. 138).

79. **More certain datings from AD 1000-1100**: "More certain datings of *ahu* are suggested from A.D. 1000-1100" (p. 139). The earliest dating may be questioned, but settlement activity in Anakena is dated to ca. AD 700-1200 (p. 47).

80. **Three-period division no longer valid**: MW agrees with Ayres that the three-period division "was no longer valid" and that development should be thought of as "internal developmental phases within a culturally unified period" (p. 39, citing Ayres 1973 p. 333; restated p. 136).

81. **Seriation indicates worked rear walls are earlier**: "This seriation indicates that well dressed stones in the rear wall of the platform seems to be an early trait and unworked stones or natural rocks in the rear wall are late" (p. 72). However, she cautions that "all the types may also have occurred simultaneously at one time" (p. 138, summarizing Ch 7 findings).

82. **Temporal tendency caution**: "Even so, one has to be careful not to create a too rigid chronological frame since the structures are complex, and different variables may have been added at different times. Social and spatial relationships might also be indicated by this pattern" (p. 73).

## XV. Rounds-Beardsley Assessment (Ch 3, p. 43)

83. **Summary of Rounds-Beardsley study**: MW describes the 1990 study of 42 image ahu on the west and south coasts using nearest-neighbor and cluster analysis. "It was indicated that the two methods by themselves were limited, but together they gave good results. By the use of the nearest neighbour combined with the cluster analysis four major territories were identified in the reference area" (p. 43).

84. **Social-political correlation claimed**: "It was thereby indicated that the social and political division in oral historical accounts correlated with the ones in prehistoric society" (p. 43).

85. **Methods acknowledged as limited**: MW states that the nearest-neighbor analysis operates on the assumption "that agglomerations are central points and that the *ahu* is situated in the centre of the territory" but notes that in Tahiti "ceremonial structures also are located on the borders of a territory to ascertain this border (Martinsson and Wallin 1986 p. 106). This suggests that the result of a nearest neighbour analysis also may be interpreted in other ways" (p. 43).

## XVI. Construction Details and Differentiation (Ch 8.6-8.7, pp. 98-104)

86. **Construction variables show no special spatial patterns**: "Other construction variables do not exhibit any special spatial patterns" (p. 106, summary of Ch 8.6).

87. **Well-dressed constructions in all districts**: "There is at least one or a couple of constructions associated with well dressed or worked rear wall in each district" (p. 98). The key architectural markers of prestige are not concentrated in supposedly dominant districts.

88. **Red lintel spatial pattern**: The variable "red lintel" (3.19) "occurs rather sparsely but when it occurs it is mainly found on the south coast" (p. 98). Similarly, *pukao* (11.3-11.4) and crematorium (12.4-12.5) are mainly associated with ahu on the south coast. These are regional tendencies, not territorial hierarchy markers.

89. **Variation within districts indicates lineage-level differentiation**: "Since *image ahu*, with the aid of ethnohistorical data, have been associated with different lineage groups within different districts, variations of the structures in the same district may indicate different power relations among lineage groups belonging to the same clan" (p. 131).

## XVII. Claims Contradicted by Current Evidence

90. **Population estimate context**: MW works with a present-day population figure of "approximately 2730 people" (p. 136) and does not specify a prehistoric maximum. Davis et al. 2024 has since estimated maximum carrying capacity at approximately 3,900 based on rock garden extent, substantially lower than the 10,000 figure commonly used in the literature.

91. **South American contact**: MW entertains Heyerdahl's South American influence hypothesis more than current scholarship supports, suggesting that "the techniques used in well dressed stones in religious monuments may have been transmitted from South America to Easter Island" (p. 141). While Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 confirmed pre-European Native American admixture, the mechanism and significance differ from what MW proposes.

92. **Ahu placement as primarily social/religious**: MW's interpretive framework emphasizes the social, religious, and symbolic significance of ahu placement. DiNapoli et al. 2019 found freshwater proximity as the primary predictive factor, though MW's own observation that "water resources exist at all major *ahu* and settlement sites" (p. 105) already anticipated this finding.

## XVIII. The Progressive Hardening: 1984 -> 1994 -> 2002

93. The following table documents how Martinsson-Wallin's 1994 findings are systematically overridden by Stevenson 2002:

| Feature | Stevenson 1984 | Martinsson-Wallin 1994 | Stevenson 2002 |
|---------|---------------|----------------------|---------------|
| Ahu variability | Five types from cluster analysis on south coast | "Rather homogenous"; "difficult to distinguish different complex types" (p. 138) | Seven clusters from re-analysis of MW's own data |
| Territorial boundaries | "It will not be possible to draw boundaries between political units" (1984:162) | "Difficult to establish any borders between different territories" (p. 90) | 11 territories mapped with geometric boundaries |
| Significant ahu distribution | Not analyzed island-wide | "Five to six significant ahu in each district, independent of the size of the district" (p. 106) = social equality | Cluster 4 ahu identified as "district centers" |
| Large ahu distribution | Not analyzed island-wide | "Distributed all around the island" and "more evenly distributed" than small/medium (p. 106, 139) | Large ahu interpreted as markers of hierarchical centers |
| Typological attributes | Five types from 13-14 attributes; results mixed | Only 6/33 ahu had all type-defining attributes (p. 42) | 15 of 37 attributes used after removing 44 sites with missing data |
| Social model | "Corporate descent groups"; no chiefdom terminology | Social equality between clans; non-differentiation in late period | "Well-developed chiefdom" with paramount chief |
| Political framework | British social anthropology (Fortes, Firth) | Structuralist (Hodder, Tilley, Sahlins 1988) | Hawaiian ahupua'a model (Kirch, Handy & Pukui) |
| Post-raid sources | Used "with caution" | Acknowledged limitations; Metraux quoted on cultural loss | Acknowledged then bypassed |
| Goldman 1970 | Cited correctly (Open classification) | Not cited | Cited as supporting "well-developed chiefdom" |
| Water and ahu | Not analyzed | "Water resources exist at all major ahu and settlement sites" (p. 105) | Ahu placement is "social/religious" not environmental |

94. **The 1994 findings directly undermine the 2002 paper.** Martinsson-Wallin's island-wide analysis produced three results that are incompatible with Stevenson's 2002 territorial model: (1) ahu are architecturally homogeneous, not hierarchically differentiated; (2) significant ahu are equally distributed across districts regardless of district size; and (3) territorial boundaries cannot be established from the archaeological data. Stevenson 2002 re-analyzes Martinsson-Wallin's own dataset to produce results consistent with the chiefdom model, without explaining why her original analysis should be disregarded.

95. **Stevenson 2002 acknowledges but overrides MW.** Stevenson reports that Martinsson-Wallin "was reluctant to use architectural data to define complex and socially correlated ahu structural types" because her analysis "indicated that ahu platforms were highly homogeneous" and she "found a uniform spatial dispersal with no strong correlation to territorial size" (Stevenson 2002 pp. 218-219). Having stated this, he proceeds to reprocess her data to reach the opposite conclusion.

### What the bibliography reveals

96. **Service is present but marginal.** Unlike all Stevenson papers, MW does cite Service 1962, but only once and only to characterize Stevenson's approach, not her own. This is notable because it shows MW was aware of the chiefdom typology and chose not to use it.

97. **The theoretical apparatus is deliberately anti-processual.** MW cites Hodder, Tilley, Shanks, and the structuralist Sahlins (1988, not the neoevolutionary Sahlins 1958 framework). Her bibliography reflects a conscious decision to analyze ahu through meaning and context rather than through functional typology.

98. **Goldman is absent.** The book that classified Easter Island as "Open" (lowest stratification) is not cited. This means MW's analysis proceeds without engagement with the main comparative framework that could have supported or challenged her equality finding.

99. **The dataset that Stevenson 2002 re-analyzes originates here.** The 164 image ahu with their attribute data (appendix tables pp. 149-155) constitute the empirical foundation for both MW's homogeneity finding and Stevenson's territorial model. The same data produces opposite conclusions depending on the analytical approach and theoretical framework applied.
