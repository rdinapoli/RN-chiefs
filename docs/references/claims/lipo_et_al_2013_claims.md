## I. The Problem: Assumptions About Moai Transport

### Prior assumptions about labor and resources

1. "Early European visitors and later researchers have assumed *moai* carving and transport required a large labor force, a correspondingly large population, and a substantial expenditure of natural resources such as agricultural surplus and trees for rollers or other transport devices." (p. 2859)

**Assessment**: This passage directly identifies the assumption our paper targets: that moai transport required large-scale coordinated labor implying centralized command. The walking hypothesis undermines this assumption.

2. "The notion of an irrational statue cult fueled by overpopulation and overexploitation of resources has formed foundations for arguments for 'ecocide' and seemed to provide a coherent model for Easter's prehistory (Diamond, 2005, 2007)." (p. 2859)

3. "A conclusion favoring horizontal movement of *moai* in prone or supine positions on wooden sledges slid over logs has seen some favor, appearing to fit the assumed impact the statue cult played in the island's deforestation (Diamond, 2005, 2007)." (p. 2860)

**Assessment**: The horizontal-transport hypothesis feeds into the ecocide/chiefly-command narrative by requiring timber resources that imply deforestation, connecting monument transport to environmental collapse and by extension to the kind of unsustainable elite competition attributed to chiefdoms.

## II. Archaeological Evidence: Roads and Road Moai

### Road features

4. "In 2004, we undertook a study of prehistoric roads constructed as paths for the transport of *moai* (Lipo and Hunt, 2005, Fig. 1). First noted in 1917 by Routledge (1919), these paths form linear features approximately 4.5 m wide extending out from the quarry at Rano Raraku. Over 25 km of these roads remain visible on the landscape and in satellite images (Lipo and Hunt, 2005)." (p. 2860)

5. "A long stretch of *moai* road along the south coast has uphill gradients as steep as 13 degrees with an average of 2.9 degrees, and downhill slopes of 16 degrees with an average of 2.8 degrees." (p. 2860)

6. "Excavations of segments of the *moai* roads by Love (2001) also show that the roads are concave in cross section, a configuration that largely rules out use of transverse rollers." (p. 2860)

**Assessment**: The concave road cross-section is direct archaeological evidence against the horizontal-transport/roller hypothesis. This is significant because the roller hypothesis is what connects moai transport to deforestation and the ecocide narrative.

### Road moai characteristics (n = 62)

7. "During field research on *moai* roads, we documented 62 statues located on and parallel to road features (Table S1). These *moai* can be explained as those in the process of being moved, but transport failed and they were abandoned." (p. 2860)

8. "First, the statues lay in a direction parallel to the road direction that runs away from the main quarry at Rano Raraku, an orientation consistent with transport." (p. 2860-2861)

9. "Second, these *moai* are not associated with platforms (*ahu*) where they would be erected at their destinations." (p. 2861)

10. "Third, like the statues remaining at the quarry, none had eye sockets carved in them, in contrast to those erected on platforms." (p. 2861)

### Systematic shape differences between road moai and ahu moai

11. "The road *moai* have statistically wider bases when measured relative to shoulder width than *ahu moai* (Fig. S2). Once statues arrived on platforms, prehistoric carvers modified the statues to decrease the width of the base relative to shoulder." (p. 2861)

12. "While *ahu moai* stand in an upright fashion with their mass located well over their base, road *moai* show a distinctive angled base that would cause the statue to lean significantly forward, often more than 10 degrees. Indeed such forward lean means that most could not stand upright on their own." (p. 2861)

**Assessment**: The systematic shape differences between road moai and ahu moai are the core archaeological evidence for the walking hypothesis. These differences are consistent with engineering for vertical transport and inconsistent with horizontal transport. Previous experiments used replicas based on ahu moai forms, which conflated the transport and emplacement stages.

### Evidence from fallen road moai

13. "Thirty-seven percent of road *moai* are broken into two or more fragments consistent with breakage that resulted from falling from a vertical position." (p. 2861)

14. "70% of road *moai* exhibit evidence of fracture caused by force being applied in inverse vertical fashion along the lateral edges of the base. These fractures form broad shallow concave scars from which a wide pressure flake was removed." (p. 2861)

15. "The positions of fallen statues are statistically non-random (p < 0.05) with respect to the slope on which they lay (Table S2). The majority of statues are found facedown when the road slopes downhill, and often on their backs when going uphill." (p. 2861)

**Assessment**: The fall patterns are consistent with vertical transport and inconsistent with horizontal transport. Facedown on downhill slopes and on-backs on uphill slopes correspond to the predicted failure modes of a forward-leaning statue being walked.

## III. 3D Modeling and Biomechanics

### Center of mass analysis

16. "The center of mass was located in the middle of the statue in terms of its width as the statues are generally left/right symmetric. The height of the center of mass is also approximately in the center of the statue, midway between the base and the top of the head. But the center of mass in the depth dimension is remarkably forward relative to the base of the statue." (p. 2863)

17. "This peculiar configuration cannot be explained by a hypothesis of horizontal transport. In horizontal transport, one would expect to find the center of mass toward the base to facilitate raising the statue back up to its vertical orientation when placed on the *ahu*." (p. 2863)

18. "For the *moai* replicated, like others that were in transit, the center of mass is positioned just over the point of the front edge and standing the statue on its base would cause it to tip forward. Modification of the base for standing on an *ahu*, therefore, would have been necessary had the statue reached its destination, explaining the difference in the base to shoulder ratios we document." (p. 2863)

### Walking mechanics

19. "The basal flaking and forward lean point to how the statue was 'walked' in an upright position without significant wear to the base caused by twisting on its basal surface as in Pavel's earlier attempts. Rather than twisting in place, *moai* were tilted to the side and allowed to fall forward in the direction of their lean. With a slight tilt to the side, the statue then falls forward toward its inherent front lean. As it falls, the *moai* rolls across its front edge and rotates slightly to the opposite side. In this way, friction between the base and the ground is minimized allowing for the conservation of energy, increasing overall efficiency and removing the potential for damage as the statue 'walks.'" (p. 2863)

## IV. The Walking Experiment

### Replica specifications

20. "We constructed a scaled five-ton road-statue replica and show experimentally how relatively few people achieve movement in a 'walking' motion." (p. 2859)

21. "Ours is the first use of a precise proportionally scaled replica of an actual road *moai* shaped appropriately for transport, rather than standing erect on an *ahu*." (p. 2863)

22. The replica was "3 m tall, 4.35 metric ton *moai*-replica" constructed "using a mold created from the 3D model, the replica is formed of a density-corrected concrete and retains the same mass configuration as the road *moai* on which it is based." (p. 2864)

### Minimum labor requirements

23. "In our experiments, remarkably small teams were easily able to initiate 'walking' the 4.35 metric ton road statue replica. Even with our limited practical experience moving the statue, a minimum of only 18 people could achieve 'walking.'" (p. 2865)

**[KEY CLAIM FOR CHIEFDOM QUESTION]**: Only 18 people were needed to walk a 4.35-ton replica. This directly undermines the claim that moai transport required large labor forces under chiefly command.

24. "The lower limit for the number of individuals derives from the number needed on the sides to initiate rocking. The greatest initial energy input is the initiation of rocking of the statue from a static upright position. We achieved this with four individuals on each lateral rope. Once the statue began rocking, however, relatively little input was required from each team of lateral rope handlers as the statue motion conserved energy in its repeated transitions from kinetic to potential to kinetic energy. Ten people were needed to maintain the rear rope allowing the statue to lean forward, but not fall to the ground." (p. 2865)

**Assessment**: The breakdown is 8 people on lateral ropes (4 per side) plus 10 on the rear rope = 18 minimum. The lateral teams require minimal sustained effort once rocking begins due to energy conservation in the pendulum motion.

### Rope requirements only

25. "Using the fewest inputs possible, we found that three ropes attached around the statue's head were sufficient to initiate forward motion. We attached one rope near the top of the head at the eyes of the *moai* and stretched it behind in the direction of travel. This rope kept the statue position leaning slightly forward on its front edge, preventing it from falling too far forward. Tied to the same location at the eyes, we stretched two additional ropes perpendicular to the statue's direction of travel." (p. 2864)

### Speed and efficiency

26. "The 'walking' covered ground rapidly. In one continuous effort we were able to move the statue about 100 m in just 40 min." (p. 2865)

27. "In contrast, Heyerdahl et al. (1989) estimated that a highly experienced crew might 'wiggle' a *moai* 100 m in a full day." (p. 2865)

28. "The relatively rapid rate we achieved in 'walking' suggests that statues could have been moved several kilometers across the island in only a matter of weeks or months. It also follows that investment in statues was likely a part-time effort requiring relatively small groups of people." (p. 2865)

**[KEY CLAIM FOR CHIEFDOM QUESTION]**: Transport was "a part-time effort requiring relatively small groups of people." This is incompatible with models that posit moai transport as evidence of chiefly command over large labor forces and agricultural surplus.

### Terrain handling

29. "We were also able to easily 'walk' the statue uphill and downhill slopes as great as 6 degrees, turn and change directions, as well as rotate the statue 180 degrees, requiring little, if any, more space than its own base." (p. 2865)

## V. Scaling: Larger Statues Were Easier, Not Harder

30. "While large, our experimental *moai* replica is slightly smaller than the mean size for statues across the island (ca. 4 m). Many examples of *moai* that were successfully moved are much larger, with the largest moved measuring about 10 m in height." (p. 2865)

31. "Unlike methods of movement using sleds, however, the 'walking' transport method scales well as statues get larger. Larger statues are also taller, thus providing proportionally greater leverage for the rope handlers on the sides." (p. 2865)

32. "At the same time, taller statues become narrower overall and have increasingly longer and thinner heads relative to their bodies (Figs. S12 and S13). This 'bowling pin' shape not only gives *moai* their characteristic look, but it also means their volume is decreased more than if they were scaled proportionally in all dimensions and had a lower center of mass. Each of these features reduces the effort that is required to tip the statue enough to initiate its rocking and thus 'walking' motion." (p. 2865)

**Assessment**: The scaling argument is important for the chiefdom question. If larger statues are proportionally easier to walk (not harder), then the construction of large moai does not require proportionally more labor and does not constitute evidence for the mobilization of large labor forces.

33. "Indeed, the larger the statues, the less likely they could have been moved in any way other than 'walking.' The fact that statues get thinner with longer heads means that they become more fragile and prone to breakage at the neck, as evident by frequent neck breaks among the fallen road *moai*. Transporting large statues in a horizontal fashion would put stress on the weakest point of the statue." (p. 2865)

34. "Even greater stresses would be placed on *moai* if they also had to be levered up to a vertical position after transport. In this way, the shapes of the road *moai* are clearly inappropriate for transport methods other than 'walking' upright." (p. 2865)

## VI. Implications: What Moai Transport Does NOT Require

### No large labor force

35. "In contrast to popular notions of sledges, rollers or sliders of trees, the evidence shows that *moai* were specifically engineered to 'walk' in an upright position achieved using only ropes, human labor, and simple cleared pathways. A relatively small number of people are capable of moving a statue; just 18 rope handlers could 'walk' a statue weighing more than 4.35 metric tons." (p. 2866)

**[KEY CLAIM FOR CHIEFDOM QUESTION]**: Explicit statement that moai transport does NOT require the large labor forces that have been assumed. Only 18 people, ropes, and cleared paths.

### No large population

36. "The statue evidence does not imply that a large population once existed on Easter, contrary to earlier and now popularized notions (e.g., Brown, 1924; Diamond, 2005, 2007)." (p. 2866)

### No trees or timber

37. "Apart from labor and engineering expertise, statue transport required only ropes; few if any trees were required in statue transport. In fact, the primary vegetation on the island was the now extinct palm, *Jubaea chilensis* or a close relative, and given palm structure would likely not have been suitable for use in building contraptions or making rollers that could support a great amount of weight." (p. 2866)

38. "Material for ropes, however, was abundant on the island as they were made from a woody shrub (*Triumfetta semitrioba* that grows in disturbed habitats, see Metraux, 1940)." (p. 2866)

### No deforestation link

39. "Consequently, statue making and transport cannot be linked to deforestation, nor can forest clearance for extensive cultivation of agricultural surplus to feed thousands of statue workers, as some have supposed (see Diamond, 2005, 2007; Van Tilburg and Ralston, 2005: 299)." (p. 2866)

**Assessment**: This claim severs a key link in the ecocide/chiefdom narrative: that moai transport drove deforestation, which in turn evidences an unsustainable chiefly elite competition.

### No centralized chiefdom

40. "The evidence for *moai* carving and transport points to activities by small-scale social groups rather than the product of laborers unified under a powerful centralized chiefdom. Here monumentality does not imply large-scale social organization as assumed for many cases worldwide." (p. 2866)

**[KEY CLAIM FOR CHIEFDOM QUESTION]**: The most explicit statement in the paper bearing on our argument. The authors directly state that moai evidence points to small-scale social groups, not centralized chiefdom organization. Monumentality does not imply large-scale social organization.

### Competitive signaling interpretation

41. "Instead, we see *moai* carving and 'walking' as vivid expressions of costly signaling and evolutionary bet hedging in a competitive environment. Multiple lines of evidence, including the ingenious engineering to 'walk' statues, point to Easter Island as a remarkable history of success in a most unlikely place." (p. 2866)

**Assessment**: The paper frames moai not as products of chiefly command but as expressions of competitive signaling, consistent with collective action and costly signaling theory rather than hierarchical redistribution.

## VII. Claims About Previous Experimental Failures

42. "Van Tilburg and Ralston (2005), for example, created a 'statistically average *moai*' thereby masking statue variability and modeling transport by confounding forms designed to 'walk' versus those modified to stand erect on *ahu* platforms. Significantly, a 'statistically average *moai*' replica would not be capable of transport by 'walking' since the vast majority of statues were prehistorically modified to stand stably on platforms." (p. 2863)

**Assessment**: This critique explains why previous experiments failed to demonstrate walking and why they appeared to support horizontal transport. The experimental failure was an artifact of using the wrong moai form, not evidence against vertical transport.

43. "While experimental attempts highlight the plausibility of methods that could have been used to move statues, they have overlooked systematic variation in the statues and their broader context in the archaeological record." (p. 2860)

---

## VIII. Road Moai as Abandoned Transport, Not Ritual Placement

44. "This observation rebuts early (Routledge, 1919) and recently elaborated (Richards et al., 2011) speculations that road *moai* are not failed attempts at moving a statue, but deliberate placements that mark a ritual path. The notion of a ritual path, however, bears no impact on the issue of how the statues were transported, since it has no direct empirical implications. Nor does such speculation explain why road statues have their characteristic shape and lack eye sockets as found in all *ahu moai*." (p. 2865)

## Summary of Claims Relevant to Chiefdom Attribution

Lipo et al. 2013 provides experimental and archaeological evidence that moai transport was achieved through vertical "walking" using an engineered forward lean, requiring only ropes and small teams of people. The paper's key implications for the chiefdom question are:

1. **Minimum labor**: Only 18 people needed to walk a 4.35-ton replica; the method was "a part-time effort requiring relatively small groups of people" (p. 2865, 2866).

2. **No scaling problem**: Larger statues were proportionally easier to walk due to increased leverage and lower relative mass, eliminating the need for proportionally larger labor forces.

3. **No timber required**: Transport required ropes only; no trees, rollers, or sleds. This severs the link between moai transport and deforestation.

4. **No surplus extraction**: With small teams and part-time effort, there is no need for the agricultural surplus and labor mobilization that chiefdom models require.

5. **Explicit rejection of chiefdom interpretation**: "The evidence for *moai* carving and transport points to activities by small-scale social groups rather than the product of laborers unified under a powerful centralized chiefdom" (p. 2866).

6. **Alternative framework**: Moai are interpreted as costly signaling in a competitive environment, consistent with collective action models rather than hierarchical command.

**Own-work note**: This is a co-authored publication by Lipo and Hunt. Its claims should be treated with the same rigor as any other source, per project protocols. The paper demonstrates what walking achieves with a scaled replica; it does not demonstrate that all moai were actually transported this way (though the archaeological evidence from road moai is consistent with this explanation). The scaling argument (larger = easier) has not been experimentally tested at full scale.
