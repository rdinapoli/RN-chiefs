## I. Intellectual Genealogy and Source Methodology (Introduction, pp. xvii-xxviii)

**1. [Three source categories]**: Valeri distinguishes three main groups of sources for Hawaiian religion: "the accounts of European voyagers who, from the time of Cook (1778), visited the Hawaiian archipelago; the writings of missionaries (from 1820); and the traditions or eyewitness accounts recorded by the Hawaiians in their own language" (p. xvii). The Hawaiian-language texts "began to be produced only in the 1830s" (p. xviii).

**2. [Source unevenness]**: "This material is extremely uneven in value, especially concerning ritual practices, and must be used with discernment" (p. xviii). Valeri systematically evaluates each source category and names the best and worst within each.

**3. [Temporal limitation]**: The year 1819 marks the abolition of the kapu system. "From this point on the literature left by sailors and other voyagers loses its interest for us. Of the rites and traditional practices constituting the object of this study, little remained" (p. xxii). Hawaiian religion was "modified and began to weaken even before its abolition in 1819" (p. xvii).

**4. [Malo as primary source]**: David Malo's work "is the most important source on ancient Hawaiian culture, written by a man of great intelligence, well versed in the traditions and events of the time of Kamehameha and Liholiho" (p. xxiv). Born 1795. However, Emerson's translation "often took liberties with a text that he sometimes poorly understood" (p. xxiv).

**5. [Kelou Kamakau as ritual authority]**: K. Kamakau "with Malo is the most important source on the traditional ritual system." Born about 1773, before Cook's arrival: "he had a much more direct and profound knowledge of the rites than Malo (who was born in 1795)" (p. xxvii).

**6. [Ethnologist sources]**: Fornander compiled "the largest and most important published collection of mythological, historical, and ritual texts in the Hawaiian language" but his first volume "is an inextricable mixture of facts and absurd interpretation" driven by Aryan-origin theories (p. xxvii).

**7. [Source criticism conclusion]**: "The documents at our disposal cannot be treated like the Ersatz of a nonexistent ethnography" (p. xxviii). Hawaiian authors "constructed their texts according to their own principles. By neglecting them one unconsciously attributes to these writings our own principles of organization and criteria of intelligibility" (p. xxviii). European accounts "come from people who changed, sometimes by their very presence, what they were describing" (p. xxviii).

**8. [Valeri's training]**: Acknowledgments confirm Valeri was trained by "Claude Levi-Strauss and Louis Dumont" in Paris and owed "an even greater debt to another former teacher and now colleague: Marshall Sahlins" at Chicago (p. xv). This intellectual genealogy is: Levi-Strauss/Dumont -> Valeri -> Sahlins/Kirch pipeline.

## II. Kapu/Noa System (Ch. 3, pp. 84-107)

**9. [Purity as integrity]**: "Basically, purity denotes a state of integrity, that is, of close realization of a normative type. Impurity denotes, of course, the opposite state" (p. 84). Purity and impurity are states of relative closeness to or distance from divine types.

**10. [Bodily-social correlation]**: "Given this correlation of bodily and social integrity, bodily states can function as signifiers of social states and relations" (p. 85). Rank is "signified by bodily states of purity or impurity" (p. 86).

**11. [Kapu definition]**: "Kapu may be translated as 'marked' and indexes the need to pay attention, while 'unmarked' may be translated noa, which indicates that there is no need to pay attention" (p. 90). Ali'i are kapu to commoners; commoners are noa to ali'i (p. 91).

**12. [Kapu as purely relational]**: "Kapu and noa are purely relative notions. They are therefore not substances or states but marks of relations between substances or states. A being cannot be absolutely kapu or absolutely noa, but is kapu relative to certain beings and noa relative to others" (p. 91). This contrasts with how kapu/tapu gets applied to Rapa Nui moai as an inherent property.

**13. [Mutual pollution]**: "Inferiors may pollute superiors, exactly as in India. Yet, contrary to the situation in India, superiors may also pollute inferiors" (p. 91). The purer a person, the greater his power of pollution on inferiors (p. 91). This bidirectional pollution system requires active management absent from Rapa Nui evidence.

**14. [Hawaiian vs. Indian ideology]**: Unlike India where purity/impurity pertains only to the human sphere, in Hawaii "the states of relative purity and efficacy of gods and humans are interconnected" (p. 105). Divine purity depends on human recognition. This is a specifically Hawaiian theological position.

## III. Mana (Ch. 3, pp. 95-107)

**15. [Mana definitions]**: "The meanings most often attributed to the word mana in the literature are 'efficacy,' 'to be efficacious,' 'potency,' 'to be potent,' 'success,' 'to be successful,' 'to be true'" (p. 101). All imply each other: "success is the necessary consequence of efficacy and efficacy implies potency" (p. 101).

**16. [Mana as substance in Hawaii]**: In Hawaii mana is "substantialized" (p. 100): "conceived as an invisible substance that manifests itself in a variety of visible signifiers" (p. 99). Transmitted through spitting in mouth or breathing on fontanel (p. 99). "The meaning 'luck' retreats from the mana notion in Hawaii. Mana is a much more stable and predictable quality than luck: it is acquired and transmitted in predictable ways in ritual" (p. 100).

**17. [Mana as authority]**: "Mana is clearly used as a substantive when it means 'authority': one speaks of 'the authority [mana] of the ali'i over the land'" (p. 100). Also means "a formally recognized and transmitted aptitude to perform efficaciously a certain task" (p. 100).

**18. [Ho'omana = worship = empowerment]**: The causative form ho'omana is defined as "'to ascribe divine honors; to worship; to cause one to have regal authority'" (Andrews, quoted p. 102). "An act of worship presupposes making mana an image of the god worshiped" through sacrifices and prayers (p. 102). This is a specifically Hawaiian ritual mechanism.

**19. [God's mana depends on human recognition]**: "The god's mana, and indeed his very existence, depends on man's ho'omana" (p. 104). "A king, as supreme sacrifier, is addressed in a chant by saying 'you give life to a multitude of gods'" (p. 104). "Gods who no longer receive human recognition in the form of sacrifices and prayers die" (p. 104). The reciprocal but hierarchical relationship between gods and humans is distinctive to Hawaiian theology.

**20. [Mana as fellowship]**: Following Johansen's Maori analysis, Valeri concludes mana is "the efficacy of a system of relations personified by an individual (for instance a chief): it is the notion that that system 'works'" (p. 97). Mana is "a kind of fellowship" (Johansen, quoted p. 96).

## IV. Kahuna and Priesthood (Ch. 5, pp. 130-139)

**21. [Sacrificer function]**: The function of the sacrificer in Hawaii "is carried out by two persons, the kahuna and the ali'i. The first is always an 'employee' of the sacrifier and does not have a higher rank. The second is the one upon whom the sacrifier depends and who relates him to the upper echelons of the hierarchy" (p. 134).

**22. [Kahuna pule]**: Temple priests "are ali'i of somewhat lower rank, delegated by higher-ranking ali'i to maintain a constant relationship with the gods" (p. 136). Two main orders: order of Ku (Kanalu) and order of Lono (Paliku) (p. 136). Membership "hereditary" but many "co-opted by hereditary priests or chosen by the king from among the children of secondary unions of high-ranking ali'i" (p. 136).

**23. [Kahuna nui]**: The high priest "is responsible for most of the king's religious duties as well as his temples and main gods" (p. 136). "The recognition of the king's legitimacy depends largely upon him" (p. 136). "Nevertheless, the balance of power between the king and the chaplain is such that a denial of the king's legitimacy on the part of the chaplain is rare" (p. 136).

**24. [Universal priesthood]**: "In the extreme, any artisan or possessor of a specialized knowledge is a kahuna" and "no productive activity exists that does not include ritual knowledge and a relationship with the deities" (p. 137). "Each man worshipped the akua that presided over the occupation or the profession he followed" (Malo, quoted p. 137).

**25. [Sorcerer priests]**: Kahuna 'ana'ana (sorcerers) "are in clear opposition to the priests of the 'central' cult" (p. 138). Their impurity makes them "marginal and residual" but they "spoke openly, without fear of a kapu chief, even if he was a ruler" (Kamakau, quoted p. 138).

**26. [Kaula prophets]**: The kaula "were independent people, and were inspired by the spirit of a god" (Kamakau, quoted p. 139). They "transcend society because he is free from all desire" (p. 139). A "difficult relationship" existed between king and kaula, "sometimes of complementarity, but more often of opposition" (p. 139). Each represents the totality in opposing form.

## V. Divine Kingship (Ch. 5, pp. 140-170)

**27. [King as supreme sacrifier]**: "The king is king, therefore, because he is the head of the cult, the supreme sacrifier, the man closest to the divine" (p. 140). When all society is threatened, "no delegation is possible; the king himself must approach the gods" (p. 140). His "privilege is in fact a fearsome duty" (p. 140).

**28. [Human sacrifice as royal prerogative]**: "It is this privilege/duty [to consecrate human sacrifice], rather than a special title, that sets him apart from the rest of the nobility" (p. 141). Story of Kamehameha ceding this right to Kalani'opu'u: "He is telling me to beat the drum and offer the human sacrifice" (Ii, quoted pp. 141-142). No parallel institution on Rapa Nui.

**29. [King as divine]**: "The idea that the king is the closest instantiation of the major gods implies that he is the most divine of men" (p. 142). Ali'i are explicitly called akua ("gods") in ancient chants. "The predicates of the king are identical to the predicates of the divine" (p. 153). The king "can do everything and be everything because each god constitutes the possibility of a different action" (p. 153).

**30. [Cosmic effects of kingship]**: The king's birth "has cosmic effects: 'a great storm swept over the land; the thunder roared, the earth was shaken'" (Fornander, quoted p. 146). "All of nature resonates from the ali'i's presence" (p. 146). Marriage of divine ali'i produces thunder, lightning, rainbows. No such claims in Rapa Nui record.

**31. [Immobility and invisibility]**: "The highest point of etiquette among illustrious Hawaiians was, not to move" (Thurston, quoted p. 147). "Such tabu chiefs were of old a mere legend; it was only in later times that anyone was allowed to see them" (Kamakau, quoted p. 147). Prostration taboo (kapu moe) shared by gods and highest ali'i (p. 147).

**32. [Royal incest]**: "Like the gods, the high-ranking ali'i manifest their autonomy, their capacity to be self-sufficient, by practicing incestuous marriage" (p. 147). This "re-creates" divinity; "forbidden to lower-ranking ali'i precisely because it has a creative and not solely a preservative value" (p. 147). Produces ranks of pi'o, ni'aupi'o. No comparable institution on Rapa Nui.

**33. [Purity regime]**: "Each man, being an imperfect and incomplete realization of the ideal human species, needs other men; he must associate with them in order to attain the integrity of the species at the level of exchange. In contrast, the king is ideally a perfect realization of the species in the individual" (p. 148). Taboos: cannot touch or soil the ali'i, approach in impurity, give impure food, use foul language in his presence (p. 148).

**34. [King as devourer]**: The king "is often represented as a devourer: the devourer of transgressors and the devourer of land" (p. 151). Equated with sharks: "a shark was sometimes called chief, and a chief called a shark" (p. 151). King kills those who don't recognize "the ideal human essence for which he stands" (p. 150).

## VI. Land Tenure and Political System (Ch. 5, pp. 153-170)

**35. [Hierarchical land rights]**: "Each right to a piece of land is contained in a higher, more encompassing right. The ultimate right is vested in the king, who is therefore the source of all land rights" (p. 154). At accession, king divides lands: keeps some, distributes rest to high-ranking noble supporters, who subdivide to their supporters, and so on "to the sixth or seventh degree" (Richards, quoted p. 154).

**36. [No permanent land rights]**: "No right to land is permanent or hereditary in principle. On a man's death his land goes back to his overlord" (p. 155). "On the overlord's death or disgrace, his dependents automatically lose their rights and must renegotiate them with his successor" (p. 155). This constant re-negotiation has no parallel on Rapa Nui.

**37. [Firstfruits chain]**: "Each holder of a land title gives the firstfruits of his land to the individual from whom he holds his title. These prestations follow the hierarchical route until they reach the king, who consecrates them to the major gods" (p. 156). The king's "high sacrifice makes their low sacrifices possible" (p. 156). Sahlins 1954 admitted "little direct evidence" of redistribution on Rapa Nui.

**38. [Ali'i/commoner distinction]**: "A noble is an individual who is able to trace a genealogical relationship to the reigning king" within "the tenth ascending generation. Beyond that it seems that the status of noble is lost" (p. 157). Commoners "do not preserve" genealogies and "are even forbidden to do so, at least officially" (p. 157).

**39. [Bilateral rank and political instability]**: "Rank is inherited bilaterally," allowing a "genealogically inferior 'strong man' to marry a very high ranking woman in order to produce a high-ranking son" (p. 157). "There are no clear or automatic rules of succession" (p. 159). "The kingdom is always conquered by its king, who moreover must constantly defend it from the 'rebels'" (p. 160).

**40. [Faction building]**: Support obtained "by constituting a large faction of people who believe that their interests coincide with those of their leader" (p. 160). Clients "always live and move" with their master "and share in the provisions of his house" (Stewart, quoted p. 160). "The search for a superior and the finding of a chief was a custom considered high and honorable" (Kamakau, quoted p. 160).

**41. [Fratricidal succession]**: "The king's human sacrifice is always a fratricide: either a literal one, because his most likely rivals are his brothers, or a metaphorical one" (p. 165). Detailed historical case: Kamehameha vs. Kiwala'o vs. Keoua, ending with Keoua sacrificed at Pu'ukohola temple in 1792 (pp. 161-163).

**42. [Royal sacrifice + royal incest]**: "What begins as fratricide must end as incest; the destruction of a brother must, through marriage with a sister, become the production of an heir" (p. 166). After sacrificing cousin Kiwala'o, Kamehameha "hastened to capture the dead man's mother (Kalola) and daughter (Keopiolani) in order to marry the latter" (p. 168).

## VII. Temple Hierarchy (Ch. 6, pp. 172-189)

**43. [Heiau defined by function]**: "The word heiau or haiau designates any place of worship and thus the places where sacrifices are offered" deriving from hai, "'a sacrifice,' 'to sacrifice'" (p. 173). "The temple is the place of the sacrifice, in short. It is therefore defined by its function, not its material aspect" (p. 173).

**44. [Hale mua as homologous hierarchy]**: The king's temple "has the same position and function that the hale mua has with respect to the household; it is the hale mua of all hale mua, the 'first house' of all 'first houses,' just as the king is the mua ('eldest') of all eldests" (p. 174). This underscores "the homogeneity of the hierarchy of sacrificial places and sacrifiers" (p. 174).

**45. [Luakini exclusively royal]**: "The main temple reserved for the king is the luakini, that is, the temple where human sacrifices, which only the king and his delegates may consecrate, are made. It is therefore in the luakini that the principal royal rituals take place" (p. 179). No comparable restricted-access monumental architecture on Rapa Nui.

**46. [Two luakini types]**: War luakini (ohi'a-lehua wood, associated with blood and destruction) vs. peace luakini (loulu palm, associated with "fertility of soil," purification) (pp. 180-181). The opposition is "only relative"; the same luakini can serve both functions depending on the occasion (p. 181).

**47. [Temple hierarchy model]**: "The hierarchy of temple types corresponds to that of the major gods": Ku (luakini, ko'a) > Lono (hale o Lono, agricultural temples) > Kane/Kanaloa (medical temples, sorcery temples) (pp. 183-185). "This model is valid mostly for the island of Hawai'i" (p. 185); different systems existed on Kaua'i where Kane/Kanaloa were dominant.

**48. [King controls hierarchy through temples]**: "By being at the head of the temple hierarchy, and therefore of the hierarchy of sacrifices, the king reproduces society as a consistent system, as a totality" (p. 187). The king "owns the most important temples of each type in each district" and "controls a district of an island by controlling its principal luakini" (pp. 185, 187).

**49. [Annual ritual cycle]**: "Each year, at the beginning of the eight-month period in which sacrifices are made and temples thus are open, the process begins at the main luakini temple" (p. 187). If consecration succeeds, king then consecrates agricultural temples, "following the royal reproduction of the hierarchy of divine types" (p. 187). "One after the other, according to rank, the king's subordinates build their temples" (p. 187).

**50. [816 temples documented]**: Archaeological and traditional evidence documents 816 heiau across the Hawaiian archipelago: 177 on Hawai'i, 221 on Maui, 165 on O'ahu, 122 on Kaua'i, etc. (Table 5, p. 188). "These numbers do not include ko'a and pohaku altars" (p. 188).

## VIII. Conclusion (pp. 340-347)

**51. [Ritual reproduces society]**: "The study of the kapu luakini has demonstrated that the reproduction of Hawaiian society requires the reproduction of the concept of man on which this society is based" (p. 340). The concept is embodied by "the so-called Haku 'ohi'a, which summarizes them all and is the most encompassing ritual symbol created by the Hawaiians" (p. 340).

**52. [Ritual as experiential]**: "Ritual does not simply communicate the concepts that must be understood; it also creates artificial situations in which those concepts are likely to emerge or reemerge by pre-predicative or even predicative inference" (p. 344). Ritual "creates situations in which the codes underlying those messages can be inferred and mastered in 'simulated' situations" (p. 344).

**53. [No comparative Polynesian claims]**: The Conclusion makes no claims about the applicability of this model beyond Hawaii. It is a theoretical discussion of ritual communication (drawing on Searle, Austin, Durkheim, Bourdieu, Lienhardt) focused entirely on Hawaiian material. The absence of any comparative Polynesian extension is notable.

## IX. Rapa Nui Analogy Assessment

The following features of Valeri's Hawaiian political-ritual system are **absent from, or contradicted by, the Rapa Nui archaeological and ethnohistoric record**:

**54. [Human sacrifice system]**: The entire luakini temple complex, human sacrifice as royal prerogative, and the elaborate treatment of victims (immobilization, eye-gouging, bloodless sacrifice, bone preservation as relics) have no parallel in Rapa Nui evidence. No ethnohistoric or archaeological evidence for human sacrifice on Rapa Nui in any form remotely comparable to Hawaii's institutionalized system.

**55. [Elaborate kapu system]**: The graduated kapu system (kapu moe requiring prostration, kapu noho requiring sitting, multiple ranked taboo categories) operated on a scale absent from Rapa Nui. Contact-era observers found no prostration behavior, no visible hierarchy of kapu observance. La Perouse (1786): "all conditions are equal."

**56. [Divine kingship]**: The king as living god with cosmic effects, sharing insignia with deities (feather cloaks, kahili, 'aha kapu), invisible to commoners, producing cosmic events at birth, was a level of political-religious centralization with no Rapa Nui parallel. The ariki mau of Rapa Nui was, per Routledge (1919:242), "neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest."

**57. [Royal incest producing ranked offspring]**: The ranked incest system (pi'o, ni'aupi'o, naha) producing hierarchically differentiated offspring has no Rapa Nui analogue. No contact-era or ethnohistoric source records a comparable institution.

**58. [Hierarchical priesthood]**: The differentiated priesthood (kahuna pule, kahuna nui, kaula, kahuna lapa'au, kahuna 'ana'ana) with hereditary orders, formal training, and institutional linkage to ali'i power has no Rapa Nui parallel. The ivi atua class described in post-raid sources is vaguely defined and post-dates the demographic collapse.

**59. [Land redistribution system]**: The king's prerogative to redistribute all land at accession, creating a chain of rights from king to commoner renegotiated with each succession, has no archaeological or ethnohistoric support on Rapa Nui. Rapa Nui territory was organized by kin groups with stable, descent-based land tenure (Stevenson 1984, Mulloy & Figueroa 1966).

**60. [Firstfruits chain]**: The hierarchical chain of firstfruits prestations flowing upward from commoner to king to gods is the defining feature of Polynesian "redistribution." Sahlins (1954:101) admitted "little direct evidence" of this on Rapa Nui. Goldman (1970:114-115) placed redistribution with commoner heads, not the ariki mau.

**61. [Annual ritual cycle]**: The eight-month open temple period, annual reconsecration of luakini, sequential temple building by rank, and Makahiki festival cycle constitute a ritual calendar with no Rapa Nui parallel. The tangata manu ceremony was annual and competitive but lacked the hierarchical nesting Valeri describes.

**62. [Genealogical verification ceremony]**: The hale naua ceremony, in which the king publicly verified genealogical claims within 10 generations to establish the nobility, has no Rapa Nui analogue. King lists on Rapa Nui are irreconcilable across sources (Metraux 1940, Table 2).

**63. [Faction-based political competition]**: The system of faction-building, client-patron relationships, shifting alliances, and perpetual warfare for succession that Valeri describes is a specifically Hawaiian mode of political competition. While Rapa Nui had inter-group conflict (mata'a, ana kionga), the evidence does not support a comparable system of centralized factional politics aimed at island-wide control.
