# Claims Extraction: Roggeveen 1722 Expedition

## Source Information

**Expedition**: Dutch West India Company (W.I.C.) fleet under Commander Jacob Roggeveen, April 5-12, 1722. Three ships: *Den Arend* (flagship, Capt. Jan Koster, 110 men, 32 cannon), *Thienhoven* (Capt. Cornelis Bouman, 80 men, 24 cannon), *De Afrikaansche Galey* (Capt. Roelof Rosendaal, 33 men, 14 cannon). Total crew approximately 223 men.

**Duration of contact**: The fleet was near Easter Island from April 5-12, 1722. Only one day was spent ashore (April 10), with a landing party of approximately 134 armed men. The ships anchored off the north coast (likely Anakena Bay per von Saher's analysis) on April 8.

**Primary sources consulted**:

1. **Roggeveen's official log** (Corney 1903 translation), in *Easter Island: The First Three Expeditions*, Part 1. PDF pages 17-41 correspond to the Corney translation of the *Dagverhaal* (first published 1838 in Middelburg). Referenced below as "Roggeveen/Corney" with PDF page numbers from the compilation.

2. **Bouman's journal** (von Saher 1994 translation), "The Complete Journal of Captain Cornelis Bouman, Master of the ship *Theinhoven*," *Rapa Nui Journal* 8(4):95-100. Referenced below as "Bouman/von Saher 1994" with journal page numbers.

3. **Von Saher 1990a**, "Some Details of the Journal of Jacob Roggeveen," *Rapa Nui Journal* 4(3):33-35. Referenced as "von Saher 1990a" with page numbers.

4. **Von Saher 1990b**, "Some Details From the Journal of Captain Bouman on the Discovery of Easter Island," *Rapa Nui Journal* 4(4):49-52. Referenced as "von Saher 1990b" with page numbers.

5. **Von Saher 1993**, "More Details From the Journals of Jacob Roggeveen and Cornelis Bouman, Nautical Department," *Rapa Nui Journal* 7(3):56-59. Referenced as "von Saher 1993" with page numbers.

6. **Von Saher 1993b**, "Roggeveen and Bouman: An Inventory of all the Narratives," *Rapa Nui Journal* 7(4):77-82. Referenced as "von Saher 1993b" with page numbers.

7. **Jakubowska 2012**, "Behrens' narrative of the discovery of Easter Island: Two editions, two personalities, two realities," *Rapa Nui Journal* 26(1):21-30. Referenced as "Jakubowska 2012" with page numbers.

**Note on Behrens**: Carl Friedrich Behrens was a German soldier serving on the expedition. His published narrative (first edition 1737) is the least reliable of the three primary accounts. Behrens had no knowledge of navigation, exaggerated his own role, and his account was further distorted by a later editor (Hans Plischke, 1923 edition). The 1737 edition and the 1923 edition differ substantially. Behrens' account is not available in direct translation in these PDFs; it is accessed through the Corney 1903 English translation (Appendix I of the Hakluyt Society edition) and through Jakubowska's 2012 critical comparison. Behrens claims he was "commander" of the military group and "the first to set foot on Easter Island," claims that are contradicted by both Roggeveen and Bouman (von Saher 1993b, p. 79).

## Claims Extraction

### 1. Direct Observations of Political Authority / Social Differentiation

**1.1. The "particular one" who appeared to be in authority (Roggeveen)**

After the shooting on April 10, when the islanders began to recover from their shock, Roggeveen records: "the inhabitants, who had been just in front of us all the time, approached our leaders again; in particular one who seemed to be in authority over the other headmen, for, giving a general direction that everything they had should be fetched and laid before us, including fruit, root crops, and poultry, the order was promptly obeyed with reverence and bowing by those round about, as the event proved; for in a little while they brought a great abundance of sugar-cane, fowls, yams, and bananas" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 27).

**Observation**: One individual appeared to direct others to bring provisions. Others complied with "reverence and bowing." This is the single strongest passage in Roggeveen regarding differentiated authority. Note that it occurs after the traumatic shooting incident, in the specific context of mediating relations with armed foreigners, and describes one individual giving directions in this crisis situation.

**1.2. Bouman's account of the same individual ("chief")**

Bouman describes the same encounter on April 10 in more detail: "When we were preparing to march back to the beach, one inhabitant approached us with soft steps. He wore a headdress of white feathers on his head, a white dress around his body and a white shell on his breast. Already from a distance he curtsied by bowing and other movements of his body; he carried a chicken and a bunch of bananas that he put down on the ground at about 30 paces from us, and then ran away like Pegasus. But he returned when he saw that this was agreeable to us, he returned together with other inhabitants, bringing more chickens, bananas and young plants and sugarcane. We only took the chickens and the bananas. This Indian with the feathers was one of their chiefs as we could judge from the one event. When he ordered others to do something which was not done, he took a stone and threatened to throw it at the unwilling person, whereupon that person immediately went on his knees with folded hands and laid down to bid his mercy, which was granted. Then the other swiftly rose and executed what he had been ordered to do." (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99; closely paralleled in von Saher 1990b, p. 51)

**Observation**: Bouman describes a specific behavioral sequence: (a) a distinctively dressed man approaches with gifts, (b) he gives orders to others, (c) when one person does not comply, he threatens with a stone, (d) the non-compliant person prostrates and then obeys. Bouman calls this man "one of their chiefs" (note the plural, implying multiple chiefs). The distinctive dress (white feathers, white garment, white shell) differentiates this individual. This is the most detailed behavioral observation of authority in any 1722 account.

**1.3. Bouman: "This chief ordered to bring us more chickens and bananas"**

Immediately following the above: "This chief ordered to bring us more chickens and bananas but they were not very well provided with either sort for we could clearly notice from their fear that they would have brought more, if they would have had more. As they were so poor, we honored them in recompense with half a piece of Haarlem cloth of 5 to 6 penny per ell, which they accepted with great gratitude." (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99; von Saher 1990b, p. 51)

**Observation**: The "chief" orders further provisioning, but the supplies are limited. Bouman infers that scarcity, not unwillingness, limited what was brought. This passage shows Bouman interpreting the individual as a chief who commands resources, but the actual observation is of someone directing others to bring food in the specific context of dealing with armed foreigners.

**1.4. Bouman: "we were accompanied by some of the chiefs where we stayed"**

"We were accompanied by a great crowd up to the beach where we stayed until everyone was embarked again. So we left like good friends." Also: "we were accompanied to the beach by some of the chiefs where we stayed until everyone was on board" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99; von Saher 1990b, p. 51).

**Observation**: Bouman uses the plural "chiefs," indicating multiple differentiated individuals accompanied the Dutch back to their boats. No specific behavioral description is given for these additional "chiefs" beyond accompanying the group.

**1.5. Roggeveen: differentiation by skin color/painting suggesting rank**

Roggeveen notes physical variation: "their natural hue is not black, but pale yellow or sallowish, as we saw in the case of many of the lads, either because they had not painted their bodies with dark blue, or because they were of superior rank and had consequently no need to labour in the field." (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 31)

**Observation**: Roggeveen speculates that lighter-skinned individuals may be of "superior rank" and exempt from field labor. This is explicitly presented as one of two possible explanations (the other being that they simply had not painted themselves). The inference about rank is Roggeveen's, not a direct observation of hierarchical behavior.

**1.6. Differentiated dress: clothing of "all kinds of colours"**

On April 8, before the landing, the shallops from the *Thienhoven* reported: "the inhabitants there were very finely clad in some stuffs of all kinds of colours, and that they made many signs that we should come on shore" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 24).

**Observation**: Observers noted variation in dress quality and color from the ships. No specification of whether this represented rank differentiation or individual variation.

**1.7. Bouman: natives wore "yellow and white dresses"**

From the shore observation before landing: "the natives wore yellow and white dresses as I had already noted from a distance while tacking in" (Bouman/von Saher 1990b, p. 50).

**Observation**: Bouman confirms colored clothing was visible from the ships. No rank differentiation is explicitly inferred from this observation.

**1.8. Ear ornaments, body painting, and feather decorations**

Roggeveen describes variation in personal adornment: "Furthermore, some thought they had seen the natives to have plates of silver in their ears, and mother-of-pearl shells about their necks" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 24). Later, on shore: "their cloth stuffs and the dyes of them, and also the supposed silver plates and mother-of-pearl, it was found that they were made up of pieces patched together" and "the wraps worn on their bodies were composed of some field-product, sewn three or four ply in thickness, yet neat and trim" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 27).

The "silver plates" were actually ear ornaments made from "the root of some vegetable, as one might say in Holland, of good stout parsnips or carrots" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 28). The ear-lobes were "stretched, from their youth up; and their centre is slit open" to accommodate plugs (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 28).

**Observation**: Personal adornment is described in detail. The ear ornaments appear universal rather than restricted to elites, as Roggeveen describes the practice generally. No explicit connection to rank is made for ear ornaments.

**1.9. Feather headdresses and body painting as possible rank markers**

Roggeveen notes: "Furthermore that they plait together as ornaments some feathers of the domestic fowl (of which last very few were seen, however) so as to form a circlet worn on the head, and the painting of their faces, and other parts of the body as well, with regular and well proportioned designs after such a manner that one side of the body is in conformity with the other" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF pp. 37-38).

Von Saher 1990a summarizes: "the visitors admired the Islanders' proportioned muscular bodies, the light colour of the skin (sometimes painted dark blue by members of the higher classes)" (von Saher 1990a, p. 35).

**Observation**: Feather circlets are mentioned but described as made from domestic fowl feathers and apparently widespread. Body painting is noted, with von Saher's summary (not Roggeveen's direct text) attributing dark blue painting specifically to "higher classes." Roggeveen's own text (PDF p. 31) gives two alternative explanations for lighter skin color: either unpainted or "of superior rank."

**1.10. The first islander to visit the ships: no authority signaled**

On April 7, before landing, Bouman brought a single islander to Roggeveen's ship: "an Easter Islander on board, together with his craft, in which he had come off close to the Ship from the land; he was quite nude, without the slightest covering for that which modesty shrinks from revealing. This hapless creature seemed to be very glad to behold us, and showed the greatest wonder at the build of our Ship. He took special notice of the tautness of our spars, the stoutness of our rigging and running gear, the sails, the guns, which he felt all over with minute attention, and with everything else that he saw" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 22).

Bouman's account of the same man: "He was a man, deep in his fifties, with dark skin, a goatee in the Turkish manner and of very strong build. He was greatly surprised by the construction of our ship and all that was on it, as we understood from his gestures. As we could not understand him at all, we just had to interpret his gestures. We gave him a small mirror and when he saw himself, it frightened the wits out of him, as did the sounding of our bell. We gave him a small glass of Dutch gin which he poured over his face and when he felt the force of it he started to rub his eyes clean; then we gave him a second glass of gin and a small bisquit but he did not eat any of it. He felt ashamed of his nakedness because he saw that we were all dressed. Therefore he put his arms and head on the table and it seemed he addressed his gods about this, as we could clearly see from his movements: he raised his head and hands many times toward heaven, and used many words in a loud voice. He was busy in this way for about a half an hour, then he started jumping and singing and appeared very happy and gay." (Bouman/von Saher 1990b, p. 50; Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 96)

**Observation**: This first individual to visit the ships shows no signs of being a political authority. He is naked, alone, apparently of humble status (nude, in a very small canoe). He appears to engage in religious behavior (raising hands to heaven, loud vocalizations). Neither Roggeveen nor Bouman identify him as anyone of importance. He is "a man," not "a chief" or "a king."

**1.11. Crowd behavior during the landing: mass welcome without evident hierarchy**

Roggeveen describes the April 10 landing: "we proceeded in open order, but keeping well together, and clambered over the rocks, which are very numerous on the sea margin, as far as the level land or flat, making signs with the hand that the natives, who pressed round us in great numbers, should stand out of our way and make room for us" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 25).

Bouman: "The inhabitants had absolutely no weapons at all, they approached us in multitudes with their bare hands in order to welcome us, hopping and jumping for joy" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99).

**Observation**: The crowd greeting is described as a mass of people, not organized or directed by visible leaders. No one individual mediates initial contact. The islanders "press round" the Dutch, requiring the Dutch to wave them back. No hierarchical reception protocol is described.

**1.12. "King" invitation to the other side of the island**

Roggeveen mentions: "It was now deemed advisable to go to the other side of the Island, whereto the King or Head Chief invited us, as being the principal place of their plantations and fruit-trees" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 35).

Von Saher 1990a summarizes: "Later in the day when the King of Rapa Nui invites the Dutch party to the other side of the island where they understood that agriculture and fruit trees were to be found" (von Saher 1990a, p. 58/RNJ 7(3)).

**Observation**: Roggeveen uses the terms "King or Head Chief" for someone who apparently invited or gestured the Dutch party to visit another part of the island. This is the only time Roggeveen uses "King" in reference to a specific individual. The term is entirely European framing; no behavior is described that would distinguish this person's authority from the "particular one" described earlier. The invitation to visit agricultural areas could reflect hospitality or an attempt to direct the visitors away from the landing area, not necessarily sovereign authority. The strong northerly wind that started up prevented the visit.

### 2. Interpretive Statements About Political Organization

**2.1. Roggeveen's inference about communal possessions**

"from the place where we landed, we did not see more than six or seven of them at the place where we landed, from which it may clearly be inferred that all the Indians make use of their possessions in common, for the large size and small number of their dwellings give one to know that many live together and sleep in a single building; but if one should therefore conclude that the women are held in common among them, one must naturally expect depravity and bickering to ensue." (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 35)

**Observation**: Roggeveen infers communal use of possessions from the small number of large dwellings. This is an inference about social organization, not a direct observation of political authority. The suggestion of communal living is potentially relevant to debates about household organization and resource sharing, but Roggeveen's moral judgment about women colors the passage.

**2.2. Bouman's inference about islanders' poverty**

"Seeing how poor they were, we honored them in recompense with half a piece of Haarlemmer cloth" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99).

**Observation**: Bouman interprets the limited quantities of food brought as evidence of poverty. This inference has implications for the redistribution question: if the population was resource-poor, claims about surplus-accumulating chiefs require scrutiny.

**2.3. Roggeveen's speculation about no inter-island contact**

Roggeveen reports the first islander visitor was astonished by the ship, and Bouman adds: "From all this I conclude that they have never seen other people than those who live on this island" (Bouman/von Saher 1990b, p. 52; Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 100).

**Observation**: The extreme reaction of the first visitor to the ships, and the islanders' unfamiliarity with metal, scissors, mirrors, and other trade goods, led Bouman to conclude complete isolation. This is an inference, but it bears on the question of whether Rapa Nui was integrated into any inter-island exchange network.

### 3. European Categories Imposed

**3.1. "King or Head Chief" (Roggeveen)**

Roggeveen uses the phrase "the King or Head Chief" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 35) exactly once, referring to the person who invited the Dutch to the other side of the island. This is a European political category imposed on observed behavior (someone gesturing/communicating an invitation). Roggeveen offers both terms as alternatives, suggesting uncertainty about the appropriate category.

**3.2. "Headmen" (Roggeveen)**

Roggeveen uses the term "headmen" in: "in particular one who seemed to be in authority over the other headmen" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 27). The Dutch term is not specified in the Corney translation; this implies Roggeveen perceived multiple levels of authority but the original Dutch wording would need verification.

**3.3. "Chiefs" (Bouman, plural)**

Bouman uses "chief" and "chiefs" in two contexts: "This Indian with the feathers was one of their chiefs" (singular event, plural category, Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99) and "we were accompanied to the beach by some of the chiefs" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99). These are European impositions. The behavior described for the feathered individual (ordering provisions, threatening non-compliance) could reflect a leadership role but does not demonstrate a hereditary chieftainship or a redistributive political system.

**3.4. "Leaders" of the Dutch party vs. "leaders" of the islanders**

Roggeveen's account of the aftermath of the shooting: "The leaders of the party, standing in front, prevented those in advance from firing on the fugitives; demanding, moreover, who had given the order to shoot, and what had induced him to do so" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 26). This refers to Dutch officers, but the framing is relevant because Roggeveen's narrative structure contrasts the clear Dutch command hierarchy with the much vaguer description of islander authority. He never describes the islander equivalent of a command chain.

**3.5. Behrens' embellishments (via Jakubowska and von Saher)**

According to von Saher 1993b (p. 79), Behrens claims he was the "commander" of the military group and "the first to set foot on Easter Island." These claims are contradicted by Roggeveen and Bouman. Behrens' German narrative (1737 edition) refers to the islanders as "giants" of 12 feet tall (von Saher 1993b, p. 78), an exaggeration that undermines his reliability on other descriptive details including any claims about political authority. Jakubowska (2012, p. 29) demonstrates that the 1923 Plischke edition substantially alters Behrens' text, transforming "an experienced, stubborn soldier that possesses a certain knowledge base and convictions" into "a pretentious man with a musket, but without character or charisma."

**3.6. Anonymous narratives and the "King" term**

Von Saher 1993b notes that the anonymous pamphlets of 1727 and 1728 (published before Roggeveen's journal was available) are unreliable secondhand accounts. These are the sources that introduced the most sensationalized descriptions, including the "giants" story. Their use of political terms for islander authority should be treated with extreme caution as they are not firsthand observations.

### 4. Significant Absences

**4.1. No one came forward to "treat with" the Dutch initially**

Roggeveen's explicit purpose in landing was diplomatic and commercial: "to see and inquire towards the inhabitants, endeavouring to see and inquire what they wear or make use of either as ornaments or for other purposes, also whether any refreshments in the way of green stuff, fruit, or beasts can be procured by barter" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 22). Despite this goal, no political authority presented themselves to negotiate or mediate the initial encounter. The first individual contact was a single naked man who paddled out alone in a tiny canoe; the group encounter on shore was an undifferentiated crowd.

**4.2. No organized reception or delegation**

Neither Roggeveen nor Bouman describes an organized reception party, a formal delegation, or a greeting protocol that would indicate a centralized political response to the arrival of foreign ships. The islanders came in masses, individually or in small groups, by canoe and by swimming. Compare this with accounts from Hawaii, Tonga, or Tahiti where chiefs organized formal receptions for European vessels.

**4.3. No evidence of tribute, redistribution, or resource mobilization**

The food brought to the Dutch (chickens, bananas, yams, sugar cane) was described by both Roggeveen and Bouman as brought by individuals or small groups. Bouman notes the quantities were small and the people were poor. No observer describes a centralized provisioning system, a storehouse, or resources being mobilized through a political hierarchy. The one instance of directed provisioning (the feathered chief ordering food brought) occurs after the shooting, in a crisis context, and Bouman notes the results were meager.

**4.4. No evidence of hereditary rank, succession, or lineage**

Neither Roggeveen nor Bouman provides any information about hereditary political succession, ruling lineages, genealogies, or institutionalized rank. The "King or Head Chief" and the feathered "chief" are identified solely by behavior in the moment of encounter, not by any description of their political position, genealogy, or institutional role.

**4.5. No evidence of territorial control or boundaries**

No observer describes territorial boundaries, districts, or areas controlled by specific leaders. The "King" invites the Dutch to "the other side of the Island" for plantations and fruit-trees, but this could indicate knowledge of resource locations rather than territorial jurisdiction.

**4.6. No weapons observed among the islanders**

Bouman explicitly notes: "The inhabitants had absolutely no weapons at all" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99). Bouman also reports: "the sailors who had been on the beach earlier also confirmed that they had seen no weapons at all" (Bouman/von Saher 1990b, p. 50). This is significant: a chiefly political system predicated on coercive authority typically requires some apparatus of force. The only aggression described is stone-throwing after the Dutch shooting, and the feathered chief threatening to throw a stone at a non-compliant individual.

**4.7. No description of differential access to resources**

No observer describes any individual or group as wealthier, better-fed, or having larger or better-appointed dwellings. Roggeveen's observation of communal living (large houses shared by many) and Bouman's observation of general poverty cut against the idea of elite resource accumulation.

**4.8. No formal religious authority described**

The first visitor's behavior (raising hands to heaven, loud vocalizations) is interpreted by Bouman as addressing "his gods" (Bouman/von Saher 1990b, p. 50; 1994, p. 96). Roggeveen describes fire-making and prostration before the statues (PDF p. 31). But no religious specialist, priest, or ceremonial authority is identified as a distinct role. Religious behavior appears to be performed by ordinary individuals.

**4.9. Canoes too small and flimsy for political or military projection**

Roggeveen describes the canoes as "of poor and flimsy construction; for their canoes are fitted together of a number of small boards and light frames, which they skilfully lace together with very fine laid twine" but "they consequently leak a great deal; on account of which they are necessitated to spend half their time in baling. Their canoes are about ten feet long" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 35). Bouman describes the first visitor's canoe as "made of small narrow pieces of wood that were glued together with some organic material and on the inside there were two wooden ribs. It was so light that one man alone could easily carry it" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 96).

**Observation**: The watercraft described are entirely inadequate for warfare, long-distance voyaging, or the kind of naval display associated with Polynesian chiefly power. This represents a significant absence of the material basis for political power projection.

### 5. Speculative / Uncertain Claims

**5.1. Roggeveen's uncertainty about religious practices**

"What the form of worship of these people comprises we were not able to gather any full knowledge of, owing to the shortness of our stay among them; we noticed only that they kindle fire in front of certain remarkably tall stone figures they set up; and, thereafter squatting on their heels with heads bowed down, they bring the palms of their hands together and alternately raise and lower them." (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 31)

**Observation**: Roggeveen explicitly acknowledges insufficient information about religious practice. The fire-making and prostration before statues is observed but not explained. No connection to political authority is made.

**5.2. Roggeveen's uncertainty about statue construction**

"At first, these stone figures caused us to be filled with wonder, for we could not understand how it was possible that people who are destitute of heavy or thick timber, and also of stout cordage, out of which to construct gear, had been able to erect them; nevertheless some of these statues were a good 30 feet in height and broad in proportion." (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 31-32)

He then discovers: "on removing a piece of the stone, that they were formed out of clay or some kind of rich earth, and that small smooth flints had been stuck over afterwards, which are fitted very closely and neatly to each other, so as to make up the semblance of a human figure" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 32). A footnote by Corney notes: "Roggeveen's description of the statues seems to show that he never got close to one of them, but saw them only from a distance of some hundreds of yards. His 'basket' was doubtless one of the corroded cylinders of tufaceous rock by which most of the figures were surmounted" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 32, footnote).

**Observation**: Roggeveen was mistaken about the composition of the statues, observing them only from a distance. This error is significant because it demonstrates the limits of what the 1722 observers actually ascertained. If Roggeveen could be wrong about what the statues were made of, claims about what they signified politically should be treated with proportional caution.

**5.3. Bouman's uncertainty about whether house orientation was religious**

Bouman notes that all house entrances face northeast and interprets: "so that, when the inhabitants leave their dwellings in the morning, they can honour and serve their god in the same direction, because all openings correspond with the statues." He then adds: "[It takes a sailor to notice this but whether this uniform direction has a religious background or that it is indicated to be away from the strong prevailing winds, is another matter]" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99; von Saher 1990b, p. 51).

**Observation**: Bouman explicitly flags his own uncertainty about whether the house orientation is religious or pragmatic. This is an admirably cautious hedging that modern interpreters should heed.

**5.4. Whether the feathered individual's authority extended beyond the immediate context**

The feathered individual observed by Bouman exercised directive authority in the specific context of the Dutch encounter. Whether this individual held authority at other times, in other domains, or over a larger territory is not addressed in any account. The observation is limited to: this person gave orders, people obeyed, one person was threatened when non-compliant. The scope and institutional basis of this authority is unobserved.

### 6. Exchange and Trade Behavior

**6.1. Islanders brought food without being asked and without demanding payment**

Bouman: "Those who came in canoes as well as those who came swimming on reeds brought in their canoes both living and roasted chickens and bananas for which they asked nothing" (Bouman/von Saher 1990b, p. 50; 1994, p. 97-98).

**6.2. Theft as primary mode of acquisition from the ships**

Roggeveen: "A great many canoes came off to the ships; these people showed us at that time their great cupidity for every thing they saw; and were so daring that they took the seamen's hats and caps from off their heads, and sprang overboard with the spoil" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 25). Bouman: "they were great thieves who desired everything they saw, because many of them took worn-out brooms, small pieces of wood, spokes, firewood and such stuff with them, jumped overboard and swam to the shore with the coast. I think that they did this more out of curiosity than to get an advantage from this, as these objects had absolutely no value at all" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 98). Also: "There was also an Easter Islander who climbed in through the cabin window of THE AFRICAN GALLEY, from his canoe, and seeing on the table, a cloth with which it was covered, and deeming it to be a good prize, he made his escape with it" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 25).

**Observation**: The taking of items is described as individual and opportunistic, not organized or directed by any authority. The Dutch interpret it variously as "cupidity," theft, and curiosity. No redistribution of acquired goods is described.

**6.3. Dutch payment for provisions**

Roggeveen: "we gave them to understand through signs that we desired nothing, excepting only the fowls, which were about sixty in number, and thirty bunches of bananas, for which we paid them ample value in striped linen" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 27). Bouman: "we honored them in recompense with half a piece of Haarlemmer cloth of 5 to 6 penny per ell, which they accepted with great gratitude" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99).

**Observation**: Exchange with the Dutch was apparently conducted on a direct basis. No intermediary collected and redistributed Dutch goods. The cloth was given and apparently distributed to those present, not channeled through a political authority.

### 7. Ceremonial / Religious Behavior Observed

**7.1. Fire and prostration before statues**

Roggeveen: "we noticed only that they kindle fire in front of certain remarkably tall stone figures they set up; and, thereafter squatting on their heels with heads bowed down, they bring the palms of their hands together and alternately raise and lower them" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 31).

**7.2. First visitor's apparent prayer**

Bouman: "he put his arms and head on the table, he seemed to be addressing his god, which we could clearly see from his movements when he raised his head and hands many times towards the sky, using many words in a loud voice. He was busy for half an hour and when he stopped, he started jumping and singing" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 96).

**7.3. God names: "Taurico" and "Dago"**

Von Saher's 1993 article on the Inventory of Narratives notes: "By what the Dutch could understand, one of these idols was called Taurico, and the other Dago, as by these words they address them. They paid great regard to these idols, the savages had deep respect and they venerated them with great esteem, principally by dancing, jumping, jubilation and the clapping of hands" (from the anonymous narrative, cited in von Saher 1993b, p. 78). "When the great guns were fired among them, they applied, as it should seem, to one of their gods for assistance, shouting and howling out Dago! Dago! whose succour they thought so necessary at that time of danger" (von Saher 1993b, p. 56/p. 78).

**Observation**: Religious veneration of the statues was observed. The cries of "Dago" during the shooting may indicate appeal to a deity in crisis. No priestly class or religious hierarchy is described.

### 8. Group Organization During the Encounter

**8.1. Mass approach on shore, no military organization**

Bouman: "The inhabitants had absolutely no weapons at all, they approached us in multitudes with their bare hands in order to welcome us, hopping and jumping for joy" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99).

**8.2. Islanders fled to houses after shooting, then returned**

Bouman: "After the shooting the inhabitants started fleeing inland in a great hurry, upon which we marched after them in formation up to their houses, in which we found nothing at all" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99).

**8.3. The shooting incident: no organized islander response**

Roggeveen: "The leaders of the party, standing in advance from firing on the fugitives; demanding, moreover, who had given the order to shoot, and what had induced him to do so" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 26). The assistant pilot of the *Thienhoven* told Roggeveen "that he, with six other men, was the hindmost of the party; that, on one of the natives laying hold of the muzzle of his piece to snatch it from him, he struck him a blow; and, further, that another Indian had attempted to strip the jacket off one of the seamen, and that some of the natives seeing our men resist, picked up stones, using threatening gestures as if to pelt us with them, whereby, from all appearance, the firing on my part of my small troop was brought about" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 26).

**Observation**: The shooting was triggered by individual interactions (grabbing a musket, pulling a jacket), not by organized resistance. The islander response to the shooting was flight, not organized counterattack. No military leader or war chief directed the islanders' behavior.

**8.4. Islanders swimming and in canoes: individual initiative**

The approaches to the ships came from individuals and small groups, not organized flotillas: "many came swimming from land to the ships" and others came in small canoes and on reed bundles. This pattern of individual initiative is consistent throughout both accounts.

### Second-Pass Additions (2026-03-13)

**SP-1. No individual initiates formal contact: a marked absence of expected chiefly behavior.**

The first individual to approach the Dutch was "quite nude, without the slightest covering" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 22), paddling alone in a tiny canoe. In Hawaiian, Tongan, and Samoan contact accounts, chiefs characteristically sent emissaries or delegations to negotiate with foreign ships. The absence of any formal diplomatic initiative at first contact is a marked absence of the kind of chiefly protocol that Polynesian chiefdom models would predict.

**Assessment**: This is an absence that gains significance through the comparative Polynesian frame. If Rapa Nui operated under a chiefdom comparable to those documented elsewhere in Polynesia, the initial contact protocol should have been mediated by political authorities, not conducted by a single naked man on a fragile canoe.

**SP-2. The feathered individual's POST-CRISIS timing: absent during peaceful first contact, present only during crisis.**

The feathered individual (Claim 1.2) appears only AFTER the Dutch had shot and killed 10-12 islanders. He was not present during the initial approach (April 7-8), not present when islanders swam and canoed to the ships to trade (April 8-9), and not present during the initial landing and crowd encounter (April 10 before the shooting). He appears exclusively in the aftermath of violence, when crisis mediation is needed. This timing pattern (absent during peaceful contact, present during crisis) fits crisis/emergency leadership rather than institutionalized authority. In a functioning chiefdom, the paramount authority would be visible at the outset, not invisible until violence erupts.

**Assessment**: The claims file (1.1, 1.2) notes the crisis context but does not emphasize the timing contrast. The timing itself is evidence: the "chief" is not a permanent fixture of political life observed by Europeans but an individual who emerges in response to a specific violent crisis.

**SP-3. Bouman's plural "chiefs" implies multiple individuals of similar standing, not a unified hierarchy.**

Bouman writes "This Indian with the feathers was one of their chiefs" (emphasis added) and later "we were accompanied to the beach by some of the chiefs" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99). The phrasing "one of their chiefs" implies multiple individuals of similar standing. If any individual with some directive capacity qualifies as "one of the chiefs," the term describes a distributed pattern of achieved or situational authority, not a paramount hierarchy. Bouman never identifies one chief as superior to the others or describes any ranking among them.

**Assessment**: While Claims 1.2 and 1.4 note the plural usage, the analytical implication deserves emphasis: the plural "chiefs" is more consistent with a system of multiple situational leaders than with a paramount chiefdom.

**SP-4. The "offering before taking" exchange pattern: gift-exchange at the individual level, not chiefly redistribution.**

Bouman reports that islanders "brought in their canoes both living and roasted chickens and bananas for which they asked nothing" (Bouman/von Saher 1990b, p. 50; 1994, pp. 97-98). Islanders brought food voluntarily, without being asked and without demanding payment. No intermediary collected or organized these goods. This pattern of unprompted individual gift-giving is consistent with reciprocity-based or obligation-based exchange at the individual or household level. It is not consistent with chiefly redistribution, in which a central authority collects surplus and distributes it to visitors as a political act. The provisioning is diffuse and spontaneous, not centralized and directed.

**Assessment**: Claim 6.1 records this observation but does not draw out the redistribution contrast. The absence of any collection point or intermediary is analytically significant.

**SP-5. The first visitor's solo approach as evidence of open access to outsiders.**

In stratified chiefdoms documented elsewhere in Polynesia, commoners typically required permission from political authorities to engage with outsiders, particularly foreign ships. Initial contact was mediated by chiefly delegates. On Rapa Nui, a single ordinary man paddled out alone to the flagship without any apparent constraint, mediation, or authorization (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 22; Bouman/von Saher 1990b, p. 50; 1994, p. 96). His behavior aboard (examining the ship with "minute attention," apparently praying, accepting gifts) shows individual agency without reference to any authority. No one subsequently arrived to check on him, redirect him, or supersede his contact.

**Assessment**: The unmediated nature of first contact is consistent with a society in which individuals had open access to outsiders without requiring chiefly permission. This contrasts with the controlled access patterns documented in Hawaiian and Tongan chiefdoms.

## Cross-Reference Notes

### Discrepancies Between Accounts

**A. The shooting: who fired and why**

Roggeveen states the firing happened from "our rear" and that the leaders in front tried to stop it and demanded who gave the order. The assistant pilot claimed he was in the rear, was attacked, and fired in self-defense. Bouman's account says it was his under-coxswain, Cornelis Mens, "who had fired without any order from the commander" and that Mens "tried to excuse himself by presenting some invalid arguments such as the inhabitants had tried to fetch the barrel of his gun and had also threatened to throw stones" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99). Bouman adds that "this was not believed by Mr. Roggeveen, Captains Coster and Roosendaal, the lieutenant, the ensign and me, neither by the other officers" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99). This is important because the shooting response reveals how the Dutch perceived the islander threat level: all senior officers rejected the justification for firing, suggesting they did not perceive organized hostility.

**B. Number of islanders killed**

Roggeveen says "more than thirty shots were fired" leaving "10 or 12 dead, besides the wounded" (Roggeveen/Corney, PDF p. 26). Bouman says "9 or 10 of them were shot dead and some wounded" (Bouman/von Saher 1994, p. 99). Von Saher 1990a says "ten to twelve Easter Islanders were dead and more wounded" (von Saher 1990a, p. 35).

**C. The feathered individual: "particular one" vs. "chief"**

Roggeveen describes a "particular one who seemed to be in authority over the other headmen" without detailing specific dress. Bouman describes a person wearing "a headdress of white feathers on his head, a white dress around his body and a white shell on his breast" whom he calls "one of their chiefs." These are very likely the same individual, but the descriptions differ in emphasis: Roggeveen focuses on the behavioral authority (giving orders that were obeyed), while Bouman focuses on the distinctive dress and the specific threat-and-submission sequence.

**D. Interpretation of the "chief's" behavior**

Roggeveen: the orders were "promptly obeyed with reverence and bowing." Bouman adds the detail of the stone-throwing threat and the prostration of the non-compliant individual. The prostration and mercy-granting sequence (as described by Bouman) is the single most elaborate description of hierarchical behavior in any 1722 account. It appears in Bouman alone.

**E. Behrens vs. Roggeveen and Bouman**

Behrens' account (as analyzed by Jakubowska 2012 and von Saher 1993b) contains substantial embellishments, errors, and distortions. Behrens claims to have been "commander" of the landing party and "the first to set foot on Easter Island," both contradicted by the other accounts. Behrens describes the islanders as "giants" of 12 feet tall. The 1923 Plischke edition introduces further distortions. Any claims about political authority derived solely from Behrens should be flagged as unreliable.

**F. Reliability hierarchy**

Von Saher 1993b (p. 81) establishes that the Roggeveen journal (published 1838) and the Bouman journal (found 1908, published 1911) are the only reliable primary sources. The anonymous narratives (1727, 1728) and Behrens (1737, 1739) are derivative, embellished, and unreliable. The Linschoten Vereeniging edition (Mulert 1911), which publishes both Roggeveen and Bouman in full, "must be considered as the standard work on this voyage" (von Saher 1993b, p. 81).

## Summary Assessment

### What the 1722 Accounts Can Tell Us

1. **One individual exercised directive authority in the specific context of mediating the European encounter.** Both Roggeveen and Bouman describe someone who gave orders about provisioning that were obeyed, and Bouman describes a threat-and-submission sequence. This person wore distinctive dress (white feathers, white garment, white shell on breast).

2. **Multiple "chiefs" or "headmen" are mentioned.** Both accounts use plural forms, suggesting multiple individuals of some standing, not a single paramount authority. Bouman refers to being accompanied to the beach by "some of the chiefs."

3. **The islanders had no weapons.** This is confirmed by both Roggeveen and Bouman and is significant for evaluating claims about coercive chiefly power.

4. **Material culture showed no evidence of differential wealth.** Dwellings were communal. Possessions were described as shared. Food resources were limited. No storehouses, no accumulated surplus, and no differentiated consumption are described.

5. **Religious behavior was observed but not connected to political authority.** Fire-making and prostration before statues were seen. Cries to "Dago" occurred during the shooting. No priest or religious specialist was identified.

6. **Exchange was individual, not centralized.** Food was brought by individuals and small groups, not mobilized through a political hierarchy. The one instance of directed provisioning occurred in crisis conditions after the shooting.

### What the 1722 Accounts Cannot Tell Us

1. **Whether the observed authority was hereditary or institutional.** The "chief" or "particular one" was observed in a single encounter lasting a few hours. Nothing is known about how this person attained their position, whether it was permanent, whether it was hereditary, or what institutional structures supported it.

2. **Whether the observed authority extended beyond the landing area.** The Dutch visited one location on one day. They have no information about political organization elsewhere on the island, about territorial divisions, or about relationships between groups.

3. **Whether the observed behavior was typical or situational.** The encounter with armed foreigners who had just killed 10-12 people was an extraordinary crisis. The "chief's" behavior may represent emergency leadership, diplomatic mediation, or a situational response, not necessarily the normal exercise of political authority.

4. **Anything about political organization before or after 1722.** The accounts describe a single day's encounter. They provide no information about long-term political processes, succession, alliance, warfare, or institutional change.

5. **Whether the chiefdom model applies.** Nothing in the 1722 accounts describes redistribution networks, hereditary rank, surplus mobilization, tribute systems, or the other institutional features that define the chiefdom concept in neoevolutionary theory. The maximum that the accounts support is: some individuals had directive authority over others in the context of a specific encounter, wore distinctive dress, and were referred to by European observers using European political categories.

### Key Finding for the Paper's Argument

The 1722 accounts contain exactly one substantive observation of political authority exercised among Rapa Nui islanders: the feathered individual directing provisioning after the shooting, with the threat-and-submission sequence described by Bouman. Everything else is either European categorization ("King," "Head Chief," "headmen," "chiefs") imposed on unspecified behavior, or inference from physical appearance (lighter skin implying rank). The gap between this limited observational basis and the chiefdom model subsequently attributed to pre-contact Rapa Nui is the epistemological problem the paper addresses.
