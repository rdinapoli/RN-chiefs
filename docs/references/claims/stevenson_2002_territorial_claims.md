## I. Intellectual Genealogy

### Who Stevenson cites in 2002

1. **Goldman 1970** (*Ancient Polynesian Society*): Cited in the opening sentence as evidence that Rapa Nui "evolved into a well-developed chiefdom" (p. 213). THIS IS A MISREPRESENTATION. Goldman 1970 classified Easter Island as "Open" (the lowest stratification level, lacking centralized authority). Goldman's "Open" classification directly contradicts "well-developed chiefdom." Stevenson previously used Goldman accurately in 1984 (for the dual status system). By 2002, Goldman is enlisted to support the opposite claim.

2. **Kirch 1984** (*Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms*): Cited alongside Goldman as establishing the chiefdom (p. 213). Also cited for the "idealized model of Polynesian settlement" and the Hawaiian ahupua'a framework applied to Rapa Nui (p. 217). Additionally cited as Kirch 1985 (*Feathered Gods and Fishhooks*) for the same settlement model. This is the first time Stevenson cites Kirch 1984 directly in a Rapa Nui paper; it was absent from 1984, 1986, and 2001.

3. **Sahlins 1958** (*Social Stratification in Polynesia*): Cited in the opening sentence as establishing the chiefdom (p. 213). The stratification gradient that ranked all Polynesian societies is now enlisted as evidence for a "well-developed chiefdom." In 1984, Sahlins was cited once in passing; by 2002, he is a foundational citation.

4. **Stevenson 1984** (own dissertation): Cited in the opening sentence alongside Goldman, Kirch, and Sahlins as establishing the chiefdom (p. 213). THIS IS SELF-MISREPRESENTATION. The 1984 dissertation explicitly avoided "chiefdom" terminology, used "corporate descent groups" as the analytical unit, stated "it will not be possible to draw boundaries between political units" (1984:162), and argued for a dual status system (ranked/non-ranked). None of these positions support "well-developed chiefdom."

5. **Routledge 1919** (*The Mystery of Easter Island*): Cited as a primary source for ethnohistoric territorial divisions (pp. 214-215). Figure 2 maps "Rapa Nui ethnohistoric territorial divisions (after Routledge 1919)." This is a post-slave-raid source, and Stevenson acknowledges the demographic context (see claim 15).

6. **Métraux 1940** (*Ethnology of Easter Island*): Cited for district information, including the five additional districts "formed after the initial division" (p. 215, citing Métraux 1940:122). Post-slave-raid source. [POST-RAID]

7. **Thompson 1889** (*Te Pito de Henua, or Easter Island*): Cited for reporting six territorial divisions (p. 214). Post-slave-raid source. [POST-RAID]

8. **Englert 1974** (*La Tierra de Hotu Matu'a*): Cited for noting "eight principal divisions and four minor ones" (p. 215). Post-slave-raid source. [POST-RAID]

9. **McCall 1981** (*Rapanui: Tradition and Survival on Easter Island*): Cited for European cultural disruption: "nearly 50 vessels in the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries introduced the culture to European traditions and merchandise" (p. 213). Also cited for population estimate: "reduced the population to about 1000 persons (McCall 1981:59)" (p. 214).

10. **Rounds-Beardsley 1990** (PhD dissertation, University of Oregon): Cited as having "summarized and reviewed all of these previous accounts" of territorial divisions (p. 214). Her nearest-neighbor analysis compared ahu clusters with ethnohistoric boundaries and was "only partially successful" (pp. 218-219). Key finding: "slight shifts in boundary location may account for the lack of complete correspondence" (p. 219).

11. **Martinsson-Wallin 1994** (*Ahu: The Ceremonial Stone Structures*): Cited for the 164-ahu dataset that Stevenson re-analyzes (pp. 219-220). Critically, Martinsson-Wallin "was reluctant to use architectural data to define complex and socially correlated ahu structural types" because her analysis "indicated that ahu platforms were highly homogeneous" and she "found a uniform spatial dispersal with no strong correlation to territorial size" (pp. 218-219). Stevenson re-analyzes her data to reach a different conclusion.

12. **Kirch & Green 1987**: Cited for the idealized Polynesian settlement model (p. 217). "History, Phylogeny and Evolution in Polynesia."

13. **Handy & Pukui 1972** (*The Polynesian Family System in Ka'u*): Cited as the Hawaiian source for the ahupua'a model that Stevenson applies to Rapa Nui (p. 217).

14. **Van Tilburg 1994** (*Easter Island: Archaeology, Ecology and Culture*): Cited alongside Lee 1992 and Martinsson-Wallin 1994 as studies that have used the Routledge divisions as a framework (p. 225). Van Tilburg used Routledge for moai distribution analysis.

38. **McCall 1979 NOT cited.** The primary kinship source from 1984 is replaced by McCall 1981.

39. **Fortes, Firth, Kirchhoff, Hayden & Cannon NOT cited.** The British social anthropology and descent group theory framework from 1984 is entirely absent. The "corporate descent groups" framework has been replaced by "chiefdom districts."

40. **Hunt & Lipo 2001 NOT cited.** Despite being cited in the 2001 paper for the uncertainty-complexity framework, Hunt & Lipo are absent here. The theoretical framework has shifted to settlement pattern analysis.

### Assessment: what the citation network reveals

43. **The opening citation chain is fabricated authority.** The sentence "Rapa Nui society evolved into a well-developed chiefdom involving numerous descent groups and several levels of political office (Goldman 1970; Kirch 1984; Sahlins 1958; Stevenson 1984)" enlists four sources, of which: Goldman classified Easter Island as "Open" (contradicts "well-developed chiefdom"); Sahlins 1958 is a comparative framework, not a Rapa Nui-specific claim; Kirch 1984 applied the model from Hawaii, not from Easter Island evidence; and Stevenson 1984 explicitly avoided chiefdom terminology. The citation chain creates apparent consensus where none exists.

44. **The post-raid sources are now visible but not treated as problematic.** Unlike the 2001 paper, which concealed the post-raid dependency, the 2002 paper cites Routledge 1919, Métraux 1940, Thompson 1889, and Englert 1974 directly. However, Stevenson acknowledges these sources "reveal little about the organization of the chiefdom prior to the impacts of western culture" (p. 214), then proceeds to use them as the basis for his territorial model. The acknowledgment does not lead to methodological caution.

45. **The Hawaiian model is now the explicit framework.** Where the 2001 paper applied the ahupua'a analogy implicitly through Ladefoged's co-authorship, the 2002 paper makes it explicit: "the idealized model of Polynesian settlement presented by Kirch (1984, 1985) and Kirch and Green (1987) that relies heavily on the Hawaiian ahupua'a model" (p. 217). The Polynesian analogy is stated rather than hidden.

46. **Martinsson-Wallin's negative finding is overridden.** Martinsson-Wallin (1994) found ahu "highly homogeneous" and found "no strong correlation" between large ahu distribution and territorial size (pp. 218-219). Stevenson re-analyzes her dataset to produce a territorial model. The methodological move is to reprocess data that did not support the territorial hypothesis until it produces a result consistent with it.

## II. The Chiefdom Claim (pp. 213-214)

47. **"Well-developed chiefdom" stated as fact**: "During this approximately 1000 year interval, Rapa Nui society evolved into a well-developed chiefdom involving numerous descent groups and several levels of political office" (p. 213). This is the paper's opening claim, presented as established knowledge, not hypothesis.

48. **Chiefdom characteristics listed**: "These developments were manifested in the complex manufacture, transport, and construction of religious and domestic architecture that consisted of large ceremonial platforms (ahu), anthropomorphic stone statues (moai), and grouped elite residences with dressed stone foundations (hare paenga)" (p. 213). Monumental architecture is treated as evidence of chiefdom organization.

49. **Paramount chief proposed**: "The pan-island distribution of highly stylised ahu architecture and the occurrence of one extraordinarily large structure (e.g., Tongariki) suggest that a paramount chief may have created an integrated island chiefdom" (p. 213). This escalates the claim from district chiefdoms to a possible paramount chiefdom. The word "may" provides qualification, but the suggestion is presented as reasonable inference from architectural distribution.

50. **Chiefdom collapse narrative**: "During the late seventeenth to early eighteenth century this organization broke apart into its constituent lineage segments. Semi-autonomous competing groups replaced what was formerly a cooperatively based socio-political system" (p. 213). This narrative assumes that a chiefdom existed and then collapsed. The pre-collapse state is described as "cooperatively based," and the post-collapse state as "competing groups," without acknowledging that the "competing groups" description might reflect the actual pre-contact situation rather than a degraded form.

51. **Bird Man ceremony as reformulation**: "After a period of reformulation, a new organization emerged and was reflected in a radical change in religious ideology and mortuary practice" (p. 213). The Bird Man ceremony is presented as a post-chiefdom development, though Stevenson notes "it lacks the monumental constructions characteristic of the hierarchical chiefdom organization" (p. 214).

## III. Post-Slave-Raid Acknowledgment (pp. 213-214)

52. **Explicit demographic data**: "Virtually all information on Rapa Nui political systems was obtained by brief European contact after AD 1722 with the majority of the data on social structure and land divisions collected early in the twentieth century (Routledge 1919; Laverchery 1936; Métraux 1940)" (p. 214). [POST-RAID]

53. **Population collapse documented**: "By 1866 introduced diseases from Peru such as small pox, measles and respiratory ailments had reduced the population to about 1000 persons (McCall 1981:59) from its historically high level estimated to be around 10,000. By 1877 only a relic population of 110 persons existed" (p. 214). [POST-RAID]

54. **Organizational loss acknowledged**: "I propose that such a drastic population decline also resulted in the loss of much oral history and detail on organizational structure" (p. 214). This is a significant methodological acknowledgment. [POST-RAID]

55. **Ethnographic data limitation stated**: "The surviving details and newly developed organizational forms were documented in the salvage ethnographies of anthropologists 50-80 years later. These data relate principally to the final century of Rapa Nui political history as an independent island, and reveal little about the organization of the chiefdom prior to the impacts of western culture" (p. 214). [POST-RAID]

56. **The contradiction**: Claims 54-55 acknowledge that post-raid sources cannot reveal pre-contact chiefdom organization, yet the paper proceeds to reconstruct "sixteenth century" territorial divisions using precisely these sources (Routledge 1919, Métraux 1940, Thompson 1889). The acknowledgment is stated but not acted upon methodologically.

## IV. Ethnohistoric Territorial Divisions (pp. 214-215)

57. **Three levels of territorial organization from ethnographic sources**: (1) Two principal clans: Ko Tu'u in the west and Hotu iti in the east (from Routledge's informants); (2) Districts corresponding to sons of founding ancestor Hotu Matua: Tuu maheke, Miru, Marama, Koro-orongo, Hotu iti; (3) Five additional districts: Haumoana, Ngatimo, Ngaure, Tupahotu, Hitiuira (p. 215).

58. **Informant variation noted**: "the number of territorial divisions varies with Thompson reporting six and Routledge and Métraux identifying ten. Englert notes eight principal divisions and four minor ones" (p. 215). The variation is attributed to "variation in the information content given by different informants" (Rounds-Beardsley 1990:14), not to the possibility that coherent territorial divisions may not have existed in their reported form. [POST-RAID]

59. **Figure 2 sourced to Routledge**: "Rapa Nui ethnohistoric territorial divisions (after Routledge 1919)" (p. 215). The map shows named districts as clearly bounded units. This is the source map for the "chiefdom districts" that appear in the 2001 paper.

60. **Ethnohistoric districts confined to coast**: "The distribution of ethnohistoric districts is confined to the island coastal plain and lower volcanic slopes. The area of Rano Kau crater and site of the ceremonial center of 'Orongo is unclaimed as is the Poike area" (p. 215). The central uplands are also "not assigned to any particular group" (p. 215). The districts cover only a fraction of the island.

## V. Ahu Typology and Chronology (pp. 216-217)

61. **Ahu as marae outgrowths**: "These structures are outgrowths of the marae temple complex common in other parts of Polynesia (Kirch 2000)" (p. 216). The Polynesian comparative framework is explicit.

62. **Construction chronology**: Ahu built between ca. AD 1000 and AD 1600 (p. 216, citing Ayres 1971, Mulloy & Figueroa 1978, Martinsson-Wallin 1994, Skjølsvold 1994). Internal development trend from simpler to more elaborate masonry.

63. **Ahu Nau Nau sequence**: Ahu Nau Nau I (AD 1051-1218): sloping ramp, flat field stone, undressed retaining wall. Ahu Nau Nau III (AD 1305-1412): smooth beach cobble ramp, well-dressed masonry, red scoria lintel (pp. 216-217). Demonstrates increasing architectural investment over time.

64. **Obsidian hydration chronology for south coast**: "No platforms from the period AD 1100-1200 have been defined although obsidian hydration dates from this time occur in the fill of later period structures suggesting that earlier ahu were once present but had been destroyed or recycled in later construction" (p. 217).

65. **Settlement pattern model**: "During the interval AD 1300-1400 the locations of what were to become the largest ahu, or district centers on the south coast, were established as simple structures that may have functioned at the lineage level of inclusiveness" (p. 217). "The period of AD 1400-1600 represents a population in-filling process in which additional small ahu and shrines were built in the intermediate areas" while the earliest structures "were re-built into increasingly large and elaborate architectural monuments" (p. 217).

## VI. Prior Ahu Distribution Studies (pp. 217-219)

66. **Hawaiian ahupua'a model explicitly imported**: "These studies have developed out of the idealized model of Polynesian settlement presented by Kirch (1984, 1985) and Kirch and Green (1987) that relies heavily on the Hawaiian ahupua'a model of settlement (Handy and Pukui 1972). This model states that self-sufficient lineage units occupied pie-shaped slices of the island terrain that included marine and inland resource locations" (p. 217). THIS IS THE EXPLICIT STATEMENT OF POLYNESIAN ANALOGY. The Hawaiian ahupua'a is the model; Rapa Nui is the application.

67. **Acknowledgment that model is untested**: "Whether this model actually holds for Rapa Nui has yet to be ascertained" (p. 217). Despite this qualification, the paper proceeds to apply the model.

68. **Stevenson 1984/1986 study**: Five architectural types identified on south coast; five territorial units proposed; "No attempt was made to identify divisions between any of the proposed territories" (p. 218). Compare with 2002: boundaries are drawn.

69. **Rounds-Beardsley 1990 study**: Nearest-neighbor analysis on same area plus west coast. Compared ahu clusters with ethnohistoric boundaries. Results: "moderately successful, with two of four boundary locations corresponding to areas without ahu" (p. 218). When extended to style groups: "only partially successful" (p. 219). The ethnohistoric model is not confirmed by the archaeological data.

70. **Martinsson-Wallin 1994 study**: 164 ahu island-wide. Found ahu "highly homogeneous with respect to their architectural elements" (pp. 218-219). Found "a uniform spatial dispersal with no strong correlation to territorial size. Had the territories been long-term stable entities a correlation of this type would have been expected" (p. 219). THIS IS A DIRECT NEGATIVE RESULT for the territorial model that Stevenson is about to propose. Martinsson-Wallin's own conclusion was that the data did not support stable territorial divisions.

## VII. New Data Analysis (pp. 219-221)

71. **Re-analysis of Martinsson-Wallin's data**: "In this paper the data set developed by Martinsson-Wallin is re-analyzed to determine if descent-social group districts can be identified on the Rapa Nui landscape" (p. 219). The analytical choice is to reprocess data that did not support the territorial hypothesis.

72. **Method**: Cluster analysis using SYSTAT, Euclidean distance algorithm, Ward's method. Martinsson-Wallin's 37 attribute states converted to binary (presence/absence). Continuous variables categorized into nominal ranges. Data simplified by collapsing distinctions (e.g., two masonry variants merged into one) (pp. 219-220).

73. **Missing data problem**: "In 44 of the 164 sites studied the missing data significantly impacted the ability of the analysis to provide an interpretable solution for certain clusters" (p. 220). 27% of sites had problematic missing data. Final analysis used only 15 of 37 attributes (Table 2).

74. **Seven-cluster solution** (pp. 220-221): Cluster 1 (n=21): poorly preserved/destroyed; Cluster 2 (n=31): simple platforms with statues; Cluster 3 (n=17): multi-tiered with statues and crematoria; Cluster 4 (n=27): most elaborate, multi-tiered, dressed masonry, statues, ramps, topknots, lintels, crematoria; Cluster 5 (n=45): undressed/partly dressed, variable features; Cluster 6 (n=10): undressed seawalls only, destroyed/altered; Cluster 7 (n=13): no attribute data.

75. **Three clusters are uninformative**: Clusters 1, 6, and 7 (total n=44, 27% of sample) consist of poorly preserved, destroyed, or undocumented platforms with no interpretable attribute data (p. 221). These are the same 44 sites with the missing data problem.

76. **Interpretation caveat acknowledged then bypassed**: "it would be premature to attempt a detailed behavioural interpretation of the architectural forms because of the weaknesses in the data for three of the groups (e.g., missing data). Prehistoric and historic period scavenging and recycling of materials have, in some cases, so heavily affected the final architectural form of an ahu that confident inferences about what was present cannot be made" (p. 221). Despite this caveat, the paper proceeds to make territorial inferences.

## VIII. Cluster 4 as District Centers (pp. 222-224)

77. **Cluster 4 identified as central places**: "These ahu structures tend to be the largest within a region, and are surrounded by platforms of a much lesser scale and complexity. The degree of labor investment in such structures is significant and reflects the cooperation of a larger social group" (p. 222).

78. **Elite coordination claimed**: "The architectural complexity and use of different stone types from different parts of the island also suggests coordination at the regional level that could only be conducted by elite members of larger lineage segments" (p. 222). THIS IS A KEY CLAIM: architectural complexity requires elite coordination. The alternative, that communities cooperated without elite direction, is excluded by assertion ("could only be").

79. **Figure 6**: Distribution of Cluster 4 ahu (rectangles) and elite villages (ovals). Shows ahu "dispersed at positions along the coast line at intervals ranging from approximately one to five kilometers" (p. 222).

80. **Ahu placement argument**: "I argue that social/religious considerations on Rapa Nui were an influential factor in ahu location, rather than strictly environmental or resource factors" (p. 222). This directly contradicts DiNapoli et al. 2019, which found ahu placement explained by freshwater proximity.

81. **Sacred coastal zone**: "In this sacred zone, permanent domestic settlement was forbidden and appears not to have occurred until the 1800s" (p. 222, citing Stevenson 1997). Coastal ahu zone characterized as ritually restricted space.

82. **Eleven territorial units proposed**: "If Ahu Mahatua and the five un-associated small ahu are eliminated, there are 11 elite dwelling cluster-district center pairs, or 11 territorial divisions for Rapa Nui. Following the traditional Hawaiian model, a set of proposed territorial subdivisions is shown in Figure 7" (p. 224).

83. **Boundary construction method**: "A boundary has been placed near the center of the island between the north, south and west coasts. Territorial limits along each coast were then estimated based on the assumption that the boundary between any two lineages was approximately midway between the district centers of the two adjacent territories" (p. 224). THE BOUNDARIES ARE GEOMETRIC CONSTRUCTIONS, not empirically derived. They assume the Hawaiian model and divide the coast by midpoint interpolation.

84. **Poike excluded**: "The Poike volcano at the east end of the island was not included in the territorial subdivisions since there is virtually no sign of settlement or agricultural activity in this area" (p. 224).

## IX. Discussion (pp. 224-226)

85. **Ethnohistoric-archaeological mismatch acknowledged**: "A comparison of the ethnohistoric and archaeological district maps (Figure 2 and Figure 7) shows how the newly developed model of territorial divisions for the AD 1500s differs from the subdivisions reported by Routledge (1919) for the historic period. It is easily seen that there is little correspondence between the ethnohistoric land divisions and the archaeologically defined district centers and territories" (p. 224).

86. **Mismatch interpreted as temporal change, not model failure**: "This does not mean that the divisions reported by Routledge (1919) are in any sense wrong. Rather they reflect a later socio-political situation at the time of her visit. It is not known when or by whom these island divisions were created although they might have emerged from a general political process as some families and leaders declined in power and others emerged to take their place" (pp. 224-225). The discrepancy between the archaeological and ethnohistoric models is explained away rather than treated as evidence that the territorial model may be inappropriate.

87. **Framework claimed as established**: "What is significant here is that we now have a framework within which interpretation of numerous aspects of the archaeological record is possible" (p. 225). The territorial model is presented as infrastructure for future research.

88. **Ecological variation between territories**: "the territories are of disproportionate size and are not comparable in terms of the ecological zones represented" (p. 225). Some territories (Tepeu, Vai Mata, Anakena, Vaihu, Akahanga) include upland sections; others (Heki'i, Mahatua, Tongariki, Poukura) "are smaller and do not include central areas which are at a higher elevation" (p. 225).

89. **Resource distribution question raised**: "Some territories have desirable lithic resources (e.g., obsidian, basalt, red scoria, tuff) while others do not" (p. 225). "Since not all of the territories possessed the materials fundamental to the subsistence and social spheres of life, it must be asked what were the mechanisms by which these resources were redistributed throughout the island's population?" (p. 226). The question is raised but not answered. The assumption of "redistribution" presupposes a chiefdom-level exchange system.

90. **Working model declared**: "there is now a working model for the chiefdom political organization on Rapa Nui prior to the transformation in the middle AD 1600s" (p. 226). The model is declared established.

91. **Hare paenga as elite markers**: "Hare paenga or 'boat-shaped houses' have been traditionally interpreted as being occupied by priests (Mulloy 1961) and are likely a marker of hereditary rank" (p. 223). "All of these house attributes are indicative of extensive labor for dwelling construction and could only have been achieved with resources available to elite members of the lineage" (p. 223).

92. **Elite villages**: "Hare paenga tend to occur in groups or clusters of structures (Mulloy 1961; Cristino et al. 1981) and are interpreted to represent elite 'villages'" (p. 223). "Eleven elite villages are associated with the elaborated coastal ahu centers" (p. 223).

93. **Note 1**: "The elite villages at Hanga Poukura and Vaihu were destroyed by the mechanical clearing of the surface for fill used in the airport runway construction. This was completed in the early 1960s. McCoy (1976) mapped the extent of this alteration of the landscape" (p. 226). An important caveat: some of the evidence base has been physically destroyed.

## X. Claims Contradicted by Current Evidence

94. **Ahu placement as social/religious rather than environmental**: DiNapoli et al. 2019 found ahu placement strongly predicted by proximity to freshwater sources (coastal seeps), directly contradicting Stevenson's claim that "social/religious considerations... were an influential factor in ahu location, rather than strictly environmental or resource factors" (p. 222).

95. **Elite coordination required for monumental architecture**: Lipo et al. 2025 found 30+ redundant quarry workshops with varied production methods, consistent with autonomous community-level production, not elite-coordinated regional management.

96. **"Well-developed chiefdom"**: Goldman 1970 classified Easter Island as "Open" (lowest stratification), which Stevenson himself cited correctly in 1984. The 2002 paper's citation of Goldman as supporting "well-developed chiefdom" misrepresents the source.

97. **Territorial boundaries from midpoint interpolation**: The boundary-drawing method (midpoint between centers) is a geometric assumption, not an empirical finding. No independent evidence confirms these boundaries.

98. **Ahu construction timeline**: DiNapoli et al. 2020 found steady construction tempo continuing through contact, not the "AD 1000-1600" window with implicit cessation that Stevenson's chronology suggests.

99. **Martinsson-Wallin's negative result**: Stevenson overrides Martinsson-Wallin's finding that ahu distribution showed "no strong correlation to territorial size" by re-analyzing the same data with a different method. The re-analysis does not address why the original finding should be disregarded.

100. **Population estimate**: "historically high level estimated to be around 10,000" (p. 214). Davis et al. 2024 estimated maximum carrying capacity of approximately 3,900 based on rock garden extent. The 10,000 figure is substantially higher than what the agricultural system could have supported.

## XI. The Progressive Hardening: 1984 -> 1986 -> 2001 -> 2002

101. | Feature | 1984 | 1986 | 2001 | 2002 |
|---------|------|------|------|------|
| "Chiefdom" usage | Absent | Absent from text | 8+ uses | "Well-developed chiefdom" in opening sentence |
| Political boundaries | "Cannot be drawn" (p. 162) | No boundary maps | 10 districts mapped | 11 territories mapped with geometric boundaries |
| Elite terminology | "Elite residential complex" | Same | "Elite managed plantations" | "Elite villages," "elite members" |
| Theoretical frame | British social anthropology | Method without theory | Evolutionary ecology | Hawaiian ahupua'a model |
| Goldman 1970 | Cited correctly (Open classification) | Cited | Not cited | Cited as supporting "well-developed chiefdom" |
| Post-raid sources | Cited; used "with caution" | Cited; caveat weakened | Not cited; hidden | Cited; limitation acknowledged then bypassed |
| Polynesian analogy | Rejected (Ibo chosen) | Retained Ibo | Hawaiian ahupua'a (implicit) | Hawaiian ahupua'a (explicit) |
| Boundaries method | Not attempted | Not attempted | Adopted from "Stevenson in press" | Midpoint interpolation between centers |
| Paramount chief | Not mentioned | Not mentioned | Not mentioned | "may have created an integrated island chiefdom" |

102. **The 2002 paper completes the progressive hardening trajectory.** Where 1984 concluded that political boundaries could not be drawn, 2002 draws them. Where 1984 used corporate descent groups as the analytical unit, 2002 uses chiefdom districts. Where 1984 cited Goldman accurately, 2002 misrepresents him. Where 1984 rejected Polynesian analogy (choosing the Ibo), 2002 explicitly adopts the Hawaiian ahupua'a. The transformation from cautious empiricism to chiefdom assertion spans 18 years of Stevenson's own work, making it a documented case of interpretive hardening within a single researcher's career.

### What the bibliography reveals

103. **The theoretical apparatus is narrow.** Only four sources provide the chiefdom framework: Goldman 1970, Kirch 1984/1985, Sahlins 1958. No chiefdom critics are cited (no Feinman & Neitzel, no Pauketat, no Yoffee). No collective action literature appears. The chiefdom model is treated as the only available framework.

104. **The Hawaiian model is the only comparative framework.** Three sources (Handy & Pukui, Kirch & Green, Kirch 1984/1985) provide the ahupua'a model. No other Polynesian societies are used for comparison, despite Goldman's framework covering multiple Polynesian cases. The comparison is Hawaii-to-Rapa Nui only.

105. **Self-citation density is high.** Eight of 41 sources (20%) are Stevenson self-citations. The settlement model, agricultural data, and chronological framework all derive from Stevenson's own prior work. The paper's empirical base is largely self-referential.

106. **Post-raid sources are cited but their limitations are stated, then ignored.** Stevenson explicitly acknowledges that post-raid ethnographic data "reveal little about the organization of the chiefdom prior to the impacts of western culture" (p. 214), then uses Routledge 1919 as the basis for Figure 2 and the ethnohistoric comparison in the Discussion.

107. **Martinsson-Wallin's contrary finding is addressed but not resolved.** Her conclusion that ahu show "no strong correlation to territorial size" directly undermines the paper's thesis. Stevenson re-analyzes her data but does not explain why her original analysis was wrong, only that his re-analysis produces different results.
