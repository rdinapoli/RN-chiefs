## I. Intellectual Genealogy

### Who Van Tilburg cites

1. **Kirch 1984**: PRIMARY theoretical framework, as in 1986 dissertation. Cited via endnotes for chiefdom typology (Ch. 5 n. 1, Ch. 6 n. 11), APS framework, and chronology. Kirch described as having "helpfully summarised" Goldman and Sahlins (p. 99). Remains the explicit scaffolding.

2. **Métraux 1940**: PRIMARY ethnographic source. Cited throughout for ariki mau, social classes, carver guild (p. 137 passage reused), east-west division, birdman cult. Every political organization claim derives from Métraux directly or indirectly. [POST-RAID]

3. **Routledge 1919**: Cited extensively for kin group territories, political organization, Kopeka ceremony, birdman cult. Described as indispensable: "virtually no contemporary research...can be undertaken without consulting the data collected by Routledge" (p. 17). [POST-RAID]

4. **Englert 1948/1970**: Cited for ariki henua term (p. 96), ranked lineage order, various ethnographic details. [POST-RAID]

5. **Thomson 1891**: Cited for early observations, king list, birdman cult timing. [POST-RAID]

6. **Goldman 1970**: CITED AND DISMISSED. Goldman's "Open" classification mentioned (p. 99) via Kirch's summary, then dismissed as "overly simplistic and not fully accurate" (p. 100). In 1986, Goldman was reframed to apply only to the late period; in 1994, he is explicitly rejected.

7. **Sahlins 1958**: Cited via Kirch's summary for Group IIA classification (p. 99). Cited for redistribution theory (pp. 103-104). Sahlins 1985 (*Islands of History*) NEW: cited for "heroic history" framework.

8. **Earle 1991**: NEW. *Chiefdoms: Power, Economy and Ideology*. Cited in Ch. 5 n. 1 for chiefdom typology (group-oriented vs. individualizing). Provides updated theoretical scaffolding absent from 1986 dissertation.

9. **Van Tilburg 1986** (her own dissertation): Explicitly cited as foundation. "A preliminary description of sites and an ordering of the statues into artefact type categories was the subject of my 1986 doctoral dissertation" (p. 22). Self-citation pattern: 13-15 entries in bibliography.

10. **McCoy 1976**: Cited for ecological crisis and cultural sequence. McCoy's anti-chiefdom conclusion ("retarded" chiefdom) NOT cited, same as 1986.

11. **Stevenson 1984/1986**: Cited for settlement data and population estimates. Stevenson's anti-chiefdom framework NOT cited.

12. **Ayres 1973**: Cited for ahu typology and cultural phases. Ayres's adoption of Goldman's "Open" framework NOT cited.

### What is NOT cited

13. **Service 1962 NOT cited.** The band/tribe/chiefdom/state typology is used but never attributed. Same absence as 1986.

14. **Sahlins 1955 NOT cited.** "Esoteric Efflorescence in Easter Island," the only Sahlins article entirely on Easter Island, remains ignored despite this being an entire book on Easter Island.

15. **Sahlins 1963 NOT cited.** "Poor Man, Rich Man, Big-Man, Chief" remains absent.

16. **Sahlins & Service 1960 NOT cited.** General/specific evolution framework absent.

17. **Feinman & Neitzel 1984 NOT cited.** By 1994, this major chiefdom critique had been in the literature for a decade. Still no engagement.

18. **Miller & Tilley 1984 NOT cited** as a standalone work. The 1986 dissertation's primary theoretical engine for ideology/power disappears from the 1994 bibliography. Hodder 1984 (from the Miller & Tilley volume) is cited in an endnote but Miller & Tilley as editors appear only parenthetically.

### Assessment: what the citation pattern means

19. **The chiefdom concept enters without genealogy.** Service 1962, Sahlins's typological works, and Sahlins & Service 1960 are all absent. The chiefdom label arrives through Kirch 1984 and Earle 1991 as if it were a natural category rather than a product of a specific intellectual tradition. The concept's origins are invisible.

20. **The concept's critics remain invisible.** Feinman & Neitzel 1984, Goldman's "Open" classification (mentioned only to be dismissed), McCoy's "retarded" chiefdom finding, Stevenson's corporate group model, and Ayres's Goldman-based framework are all absent or suppressed.

21. **Post-raid sources used without systematic qualification.** Despite extensive methodological caveats in Chapter 2, every political organization claim in the substantive chapters rests on Métraux 1940, Routledge 1919, and/or Englert 1948. The caveat-then-proceed pattern from 1986 is repeated.

## II. How the Chiefdom Label Is Introduced (Chapters 2-3, pp. 29-53)

22. **"Precontact Rapa Nui was a traditional, hierarchical society."** (p. 30). Stated as fact, not hypothesis. No citation. No hedging. Compare 1986, where the Polynesian cultural matrix was explicitly "assumed" (p. 299). In 1994, the assumption has become invisible presupposition.

23. **"The Rapa Nui was a tribal society organised as a Polynesian chiefdom."** (p. 94, opening sentence of Chapter 6). Definitional, not hypothetical.

24. **"Neolithic, agrarian chiefdom."** (p. 63, Chapter 5). Used without citation or hedging.

25. **APS as inherited template presented as fact.** "Ancestral Polynesian society was organised on the principle of the conical clan...chiefs were both sacred and secular rulers whose power (mana) emanated from the gods" (p. 42). No source cited. Kirch 1984's APS reconstruction compressed and presented as settled knowledge.

26. **The Polynesian analogy justified by broken traditions.** "On islands like Rapa Nui, where there was a serious break in traditional historic continuity due to outside influence, this Pacific context is an especially important research advantage" (p. 40). The absence of evidence becomes the reason to import the model.

27. **"Assuredly of chiefly rank."** Applied to the hypothetical leader of the settlement party (p. 45). Polynesian analogy presented as certainty.

28. **"Political integration increased."** (p. 52). No source or evidence cited.

## III. Post-Slave-Raid Source Handling (Chapter 2, pp. 29-39)

### Caveats stated

29. **Contact-era sources cautioned.** "This information is, by definition, anecdotal" (p. 29).

30. **Observer-effect acknowledged.** "Each subsequent ship and ship's crew became a part of each island's history" (pp. 29-30).

31. **"Broken traditions" acknowledged.** "Enduring traditions...were often disrupted, frequently altered and occasionally destroyed" (pp. 29-30).

32. **Demographic collapse acknowledged.** "Only 111 Rapa Nui remained on Easter Island in 1872" (p. 33).

33. **Missionary influence acknowledged.** Missionaries played "a major role in the conversion of the islanders to a more European mode of living and thinking" (p. 32).

34. **Mangarevan/Tuamotuan intermediaries acknowledged.** "In 1866 the island's priestly staff was augmented by native assistants from Mangareva and the Tuamotus" (p. 31).

35. **Informant contamination acknowledged.** Information was "synthesised from a variety of known and unknown familial and other sources, including impressions, opinions and values communicated to them, knowingly or not, by ethnographers, visitors, traders and various other outsiders" (p. 37).

36. **Oral traditions "altered and restructured to an unknown degree."** "A very late and imperfect remnant of a rich and varied oral literature" (p. 39).

37. **Informant chain bottleneck: Tepano.** Born c. 1876, fourteen years after slave raids. Key informant for Routledge, Métraux, Lavachery, and Englert. Much apparent independent corroboration traces to one individual (p. 33).

38. **All informant data is post-1860.** "The information secured from them, of course, was all post-1860" (p. 37).

### Caveats not applied

39. **Despite all the above, Van Tilburg uses the same ethnographic sources uncritically in Chapters 3, 5, 6, and 9.** The caution of Chapter 2 is not carried into the substantive chapters where political organization is described.

## IV. The Chiefdom Framework (Chapter 6, pp. 94-104)

### Political organization

40. **Simple vs. complex chiefdom classification.** "A simple chiefdom has a few thousand people, one level in the political hierarchy above the local community, and a system of graduated status ranking" (p. 94). No source cited for this definition.

41. **Population estimate 7,000-9,000.** (p. 94). Contradicted by Davis et al. 2024 (max ~3,900) and Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 (~3,000).

### Ariki mau

42. **Ariki mau as hereditary spiritual leader.** "The ranking elder of the Honga ure within the Miru mata was traditionally acknowledged to be the hereditary spiritual leader of the entire island family...designated paramount chief (ariki mau)" (p. 96). [POST-RAID: Englert]

43. **Ariki mau's sacredness and tapu.** "His behaviour, lifestyle, movements and even physical appearance were circumscribed by a series of rules and regulations which formed a complex and intricate tapu web" (p. 96). [POST-RAID]

44. **EUROPEANS NEVER SAW THE ARIKI MAU.** "There is no evidence, however, that Europeans ever actually saw the ariki mau and class distinctions may have been more marked during the Ahu Moai phase of prehistory than they were at the time of contact" (p. 96). Most epistemologically significant admission in the book.

45. **Ariki mau compared to Rotuman sau.** "The sau exercised no direct political power, although eligibility to the office was limited to those of chiefly rank" (pp. 96-97). NEW in 1994. Implications (no political power) not followed through.

46. **Ariki mau contrasted with Marquesan haka'iki.** "The genealogically senior haka'iki was only regarded as possessing limited sanctity" (p. 97). Acknowledges radical variation in Polynesian political structures but does not question the chiefdom label.

47. **First fruits claimed.** "Seasonally receiving 'first fruits'" (p. 96). No specific evidence. [POST-RAID]

48. **Fish distribution as only specific redistribution.** Ariki mau distributed fish from expert fishermen to tangata honui (p. 98). [POST-RAID]

### Social classes

49. **Ariki paka (aristocracy).** Title ariki "reserved only for the Miru" (p. 98). [POST-RAID]

50. **Priestly class (ivi atua).** Drawn "from the Miru ariki paka, although not exclusively" (p. 98). [POST-RAID]

51. **Carver guild.** "They were a privileged class, highly esteemed, and their profession was transmitted from father to son" (pp. 98-99, quoting Métraux 1940:137). [POST-RAID]

52. **Warriors (matatoa).** "The matatoa, as professional warrior, led the war forces in battle, not the chief" (p. 99). [POST-RAID]

53. **Kio reinterpreted.** "Really means an individual who was forced, by strife or economic circumstances, into being temporarily landless" (p. 99). [POST-RAID]

### Goldman's "Open" classification

54. **Goldman's "Open" mentioned via Kirch.** (p. 99).

55. **Goldman dismissed as "overly simplistic."** (p. 100). Hardening from 1986's reframing to outright rejection.

56. **Goldman AND Sahlins criticized for relying on "limited, late ethnographic data."** (pp. 99-100). Ironic: Van Tilburg's own political description relies on the same data.

### Redistribution

57. **Sahlins on redistribution cited.** (pp. 103-104).

58. **Rappaport's critique cited.** Redistribution's "true value" was demonstrating "dominant/subordinate nature of social relationships" (p. 103). Undercuts economic basis for chiefdom.

59. **Labour tribute claimed from statue data.** Large statues = chiefs "exacting tribute in the form of labour beyond that of familial obligations" (p. 104). Alternative: voluntary cooperative labor.

60. **Statue production as reinforcing subordination.** "Every statue reinforced the subordination of each individual lineage chief to the dominant paramount chief" (p. 103). Contradicts Kirch 1984:270.

### Self-critical admission

61. **Social complexity "not clearly articulated research question."** (pp. 103-104). Admits the chiefdom label is underexamined.

62. **Ahu within pan-Polynesian marae tradition.** Extensive comparative discussion importing chiefly investiture functions (pp. 71-73).

63. **Kirch 1984:270 limiting statement ABSENT.** In 1986, Van Tilburg quoted "do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority." By 1994, gone.

64. **Ahu construction locally variable.** "Each individual site possesses an individualised history" (p. 79). Compatible with decentralized production.

66. **Emergence driven by ecological and political factors.** Priest/chief distinction assumed from Polynesian analogy (p. 54).

## VII. Moai Interpretation (Chapter 9, pp. 125-147)

67. **Moai-as-paramount-chief hypothesis RETAINED AND STRENGTHENED.** From 1986's "two possible suggestions" to 1994's "I believe."

68. **"The Perfect Chief."** NEW concept: Tahaki archetype applied to moai.

69. **"Neutral vessel" concept.** NEW. Moai as flexible communal resource. More compatible with collective action.

70. **ALL iconographic interpretations derive from Polynesian analogy, not Rapa Nui-specific evidence.** Head, eyes, hands, hami, dorsal designs, pukao, red color all interpreted through imported meanings.

71. **Southeast peripheral lineages "more productive" than central ones.** Van Tilburg's own data undermines centralized command.

74. **"The Price They Paid."** Ecocide framing endorsed.

## XII. Progressive Hardening Assessment: 1986 → 1987 → 1994

| Chiefdom framework | "Assumed" (p. 299) | Implicit | Definitional: "a Polynesian chiefdom" (p. 94) |
| Goldman "Open" | Acknowledged, reframed | Absent | Dismissed as "overly simplistic" |
| Miller & Tilley | Primary engine | Absent | Absent |
| Moai-as-paramount-chief | "Two possible suggestions" | Absent | Retained, strengthened |
| Four-stage model | Formal (pp. 338-355) | Implicit | Absorbed into narrative |
| Post-raid caution | Stated, not applied | None | Extensive caveats, not applied |
| Service 1962 cited | No | No | No |
| Feinman & Neitzel cited | No | No | No |

87. **The hardening is rhetorical, not evidential.** No new archaeological evidence justifies the transition from "assumed" to "definitional." Same post-raid sources, same Polynesian analogies, same iconographic interpretations. The progressive hardening is a publication phenomenon.

## XIII. Citation Pattern Analysis

88. **Chiefdom label justified by three mechanisms, none involving new evidence.** (1) Kirch 1984's APS inheritance argument; (2) post-slave-raid ethnographic sources; (3) Polynesian analogies.

89. **Counter-evidence systematically absent.** Service 1962's criteria (which Easter Island fails), Sahlins 1954's redistribution gap, Goldman's full "Open" argument, Feinman & Neitzel 1984, McCoy 1976's "retarded" chiefdom, Stevenson 1984's corporate groups, Ayres 1973's non-chiefdom analysis, Kirch 1984:270 on descent-group structures.

## XIV. Post-Raid Source Dependency Register

All political-organization claims rest on post-slave-raid sources: ariki mau attributes (p. 96), first fruits (p. 96), fish distribution (p. 98), ariki paka (p. 98), ivi atua (p. 98), carver guild (pp. 98-99), matatoa (p. 99), hurumanu/kio (p. 99), Miru supremacy (pp. 52-53), east/west confederacy (pp. 53, 95), neru practices (p. 92), paina ceremony (pp. 92-93), marriage patterns (pp. 105-106). No political-organization claim is supported by contact-era evidence alone. Van Tilburg acknowledges "there is no evidence...that Europeans ever actually saw the ariki mau" (p. 96).

## XV. Summary Assessment

Van Tilburg 1994 is the pivotal consolidation of the chiefdom attribution. The book transforms a tentative 1986 framework into the established default without new archaeological evidence. The same post-slave-raid sources and Polynesian analogies are redeployed with elevated epistemic status: "assumed" becomes "was." Counter-evidence (Goldman's "Open") is dismissed rather than engaged. The theoretical apparatus (Miller & Tilley, Service) becomes invisible.

The book contains important internal tensions: extensive Chapter 2 methodological caveats; the admission that Europeans never saw the ariki mau; warfare skepticism; the "neutral vessel" and "collaborative religious coping" concepts compatible with collective action; the sau comparison implying no political power; and the "implied gain" over coercion mechanism. These tensions are unresolved because the chiefdom label operates as presupposition rather than hypothesis.

The progressive hardening follows the pattern documented across the attribution chain (Sahlins 1954 → 1955 → 1957, Stevenson 1984 → 1986): qualified findings are stripped of caveats through successive publications. The result is a chiefdom label that appears evidence-based but rests on inherited assumption, repeated citation, and rhetorical hardening.
