# Claims Extraction: Eyraud 1864

## Source Information

**Observer**: Brother Eugene Eyraud, lay member of the Congregation of the Sacred Hearts of Jesus and Mary (Picpus Fathers). First European to live on Rapa Nui. Arrived January 2, 1864; departed approximately nine months later (c. October 1864) aboard the *Teresa Ramos*.

**Duration of stay**: Approximately nine months. Eyraud lived among the islanders throughout this period, initially at Hanga Roa (Anarova), then at Anakena near Torometi, with visits to other parts of the island including Anapika (probably Hanga Piko) and Vaihu.

**Context**: Eyraud arrived approximately one year after the 1862-1863 Peruvian slave raids, which removed an estimated 1,000-1,500 islanders, reportedly including chiefs and priests. Of roughly 100 who were repatriated from Peru, only 15 survived the return voyage. Those survivors brought smallpox, which spread across the island. Eyraud was accompanied by six repatriated Rapanui (four men, a woman, and a child), including one named Pana. The island was, by the introduction to the Altman translation, in "a time of considerable anarchy" following the raids.

**Primary sources consulted**:

1. **Altman, Ann M. et al. 2003.** "The Sojourn of the First Missionary on Rapa Nui: Eugene Eyraud Among the Kanacs, 1864." *Rapa Nui Journal* 17(1):49-57. Translation of Eyraud's letter of December 1864 to the Superior of the Sacred Hearts in Paris, originally published in *Annales de la Propagation de la Foi* 38:52-71, 124-138 (1866). Referenced below as "Altman 2003" with RNJ page numbers. OCR text available at `docs/references/pdfs/post_slave_raid/altman_2004/altman_2003_eyraud_text.txt`.

2. **Altman, Ann M. 2004.** *Early Visitors to Easter Island, 1864-1877* (Part 1, pp. 1-33). Los Osos, CA: Easter Island Foundation. Contains the same Eyraud letter with minor translation variants and additional introductory material. Referenced below as "Altman 2004" with book page numbers where available.

**Note on [POST-RAID] designation**: All claims in this file carry the [POST-RAID] flag. Eyraud arrived one year after the slave raids. The population he encountered (estimated by him at approximately 1,200) had already lost a substantial portion of its members, reportedly including political and religious leaders. What Eyraud observed was a society in the immediate aftermath of catastrophic depopulation and the introduction of epidemic disease. His observations cannot be taken as evidence of pre-raid or pre-contact social organization without independent corroboration.

**Note on the observer**: Eyraud was a lay brother, not an ethnographer. His letter was written to his religious superior and is framed as a missionary report, emphasizing prospects for conversion and the conditions facing missionary work. His observations of political authority, religion, and social organization are incidental to his primary purpose and filtered through French Catholic assumptions about "savages" and their capacity for religion and civilization. His extended stay nonetheless provides sustained observation unavailable from any earlier European visitor.

## Claims Extraction

### 1. Political Authority

**1.1. Eyraud's characterization of chiefly authority as informal and influence-based** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** The single most important political passage in the account: "I noticed that he lorded it over his neighbors. It is hard for me to characterize the authority of chiefs on the island. I don't even know the basis for such authority. It seems that authority depended only on the influence that some natives were able to exert over their neighbors and that the people gradually grew accustomed to such displays of superiority. In any case, it was clear that Torometi was a chief: he was my chief and my neighbor." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 52)

**Observation**: Eyraud, who lived on the island for nine months and thus had far longer to observe political dynamics than any previous European visitor, found chiefly authority opaque and difficult to characterize. He describes it as based on personal influence rather than institutional position, hereditary right, or control of resources. The phrase "gradually grew accustomed to such displays of superiority" suggests habituated dominance rather than a formal office. Critically, this is observed one year after the slave raids, which reportedly removed chiefs and priests. Whether this informality reflects post-raid social disintegration or pre-raid conditions cannot be determined from this passage alone.

**1.2. Torometi as Eyraud's "chief"** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** Torometi, described as "a man of about thirty, tall and strong," took possession of Eyraud and his belongings upon arrival. "Torometi considered me and my belongings to be his personal property. Bearing this in mind, he gave me daily my ration of cooked sweet potato -- he took care to feed me." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 52) Torometi also appropriated Eyraud's axe, bell, and other goods serially throughout the nine-month stay, using threats and group pressure when Eyraud resisted.

**Observation**: Torometi's authority over Eyraud appears to derive from physical proximity and assertiveness rather than any formal assignment of the foreigner to a chief's jurisdiction. Eyraud was told Torometi "was not a native of the island" (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 52), which, if accurate, complicates any interpretation of his status as reflecting traditional hereditary authority. The relationship resembles personal domination of a vulnerable outsider more than the exercise of chiefly office.

**1.3. No organized political reception on arrival** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** When Eyraud landed at Hanga Roa, there was no formal greeting, no delegation, and no evident authority figure directing the reception. "I looked for the Kanacs who had come with me from Tahiti. I found them in the middle of the throng and they looked almost as uncomfortable as I was. The compatriots of Pana and the others hardly seemed to be celebrating their return; they seemed keener in getting their hands on their belongings." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 50)

**Observation**: This contrasts with the organized receptions described by earlier visitors. Roggeveen (1722) encountered an individual who directed others to bring provisions. Gonzalez (1770) received a coordinated reception. Cook (1774) met Ko Toheetai, identified as *aree*. La Perouse (1786) was greeted by organized delegations. The absence of any such reception for Eyraud may reflect post-raid social disorganization, the fact that Eyraud arrived as a lone civilian rather than with an armed fleet, or both.

**1.4. Anapika people equally exploitative** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** When Eyraud attempted to escape Torometi by fleeing to Anapika (probably Hanga Piko), the people there "were well disposed towards popa because, of course, they planned to rob him in their turn." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 56) Torometi subsequently retrieved Eyraud by force, and the Anapika people did indeed steal everything Eyraud had brought with him: "I was able to bring back nothing except damaged and empty trunks." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 56)

**Observation**: This episode suggests that no single chief exercised authority over Eyraud's movements across the island. Torometi's claim was contested, and the Anapika people operated independently. The absence of any appeal to a higher or island-wide authority is notable.

**1.5. Community punishment of Torometi** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** Tamateka informed Eyraud that "Torometi was hated by everyone and that his misdeeds would bring him an exemplary punishment." Subsequently, "I saw Tamateka arrive, followed by a crowd of people who gathered in front of Torometi's hut. Everyone was talking at once, the discussion became heated." The crowd then "tore at the straw that covered it, and tried to tip it over. Immediately, it burst into flames." Torometi "had remained impassive, sitting beside the fire. One of his friends had to take him by the hand to pull him away from the fire." Eyraud's own hut was spared: "a few Kanacs, armed with spears, set themselves up as guards all around it." (Altman 2003, RNJ pp. 56-57)

**Observation**: This episode describes collective community action against a "chief" whose behavior was deemed unacceptable, with no recourse to any higher authority and no formal judicial process. The punishment was mob destruction of property. Torometi was not deposed by a superior chief but disciplined by a crowd led by Tamateka. This is consistent with an acephalous or weakly hierarchical society in which authority is constrained by community tolerance, not with a formalized chiefdom in which succession, jurisdiction, and punishment follow institutional rules. However, since this occurred one year post-raid, it may also reflect the breakdown of whatever authority structures existed previously.

**1.6. No territorial chiefs or island-wide authority mentioned** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation / (c) Author's inference.** At no point in the nine-month account does Eyraud describe territorial divisions, named chiefs with jurisdiction over specific regions, tribute obligations, or any island-wide political authority. The only individual identified as exercising authority is Torometi, whose influence is described as personal and local.

**Observation**: This is a significant absence given the length of Eyraud's stay and his movement across the island (Hanga Roa, Anakena, Anapika, Vaihu, and the mataveri gathering). A system of territorial chiefs with recognized jurisdictions would likely have been encountered during these travels, even if Eyraud lacked the vocabulary to describe it precisely.

### 2. Social Organization

**2.1. Collective boat-building as "national cooperation"** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** When the islanders pressured Eyraud to build a boat, "they ran all over the island, gathering all the bits of planks, all the pieces of wood -- straight, bent or rotten -- that they could find. This boat was going to be the result of national cooperation. On other occasions, I had noted this custom of making everyone contribute to a project that was considered important. Nobody would think of not participating." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 55)

**Observation**: Eyraud explicitly identifies a pattern of universal participation in collective projects. He notes that this was not unique to the boat episode but was a recognized "custom" that he had observed on multiple occasions. The phrase "Nobody would think of not participating" describes social obligation enforced by norm rather than by chiefly command. This passage is directly relevant to collective action models of Rapanui monument production: it describes cooperative mobilization without reference to any directing authority.

**2.2. Universal theft and mutual suspicion** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "All the Kanacs accuse each other of theft... and all the accusations are true. If there are some who steal less than others, it's only because they lack the chance or the nerve." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 55)

**Observation**: Eyraud describes a society without effective property enforcement beyond individual vigilance. The absence of any authority capable of adjudicating theft or enforcing property rights is consistent with the picture of informal, influence-based authority described in 1.1. This characterization should be read with caution: Eyraud was a foreigner whose possessions were exotic luxuries, and the "theft" he experienced may not reflect norms governing property among islanders themselves. Additionally, post-raid social disruption may have weakened whatever property conventions existed previously.

**2.3. Group pressure as the enforcement mechanism** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** When Torometi wanted something from Eyraud, "he would go and sit some fifty paces from my hut, and soon his wife, his neighbors and the anyone in the neighborhood would sit down with him and they would begin to make a terrible row. They would throw stones and make me see quite clearly that it would be better for me to surrender the object that Torometi coveted than to let them demolish my home or to wait until they set it on fire." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 55)

**Observation**: Authority was enforced through collective pressure and the threat of property destruction, not through any formal prerogative. Torometi did not simply take what he wanted by right; he mobilized social support to coerce compliance.

**2.4. Violence: property destruction rather than physical combat** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "The natives don't often resort to violence. I have seen them have noisy arguments and burn down each other's huts but without, nevertheless, coming to blows." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 55) Separately, Eyraud notes the aversion to spilling blood (see 4.5 below). When violence occurred against persons, stones were the weapon: "if they want to send someone into the next world, they find it simpler to use stones." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 55)

**Observation**: The pattern of hut-burning as the primary form of inter-personal conflict, combined with the blood avoidance taboo, describes a distinctive conflict resolution pattern. The burning of Torometi's hut (1.5 above) fits this general pattern.

**2.5. Population estimate: approximately 1,200** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "The multitude of men, women and children, perhaps as many as twelve hundred, was hardly reassuring." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 50)

**Observation**: This is Eyraud's estimate upon landing at Hanga Roa in January 1864, approximately one year after the slave raids. The pre-raid population is generally estimated at approximately 3,000 (based on pre-contact estimates and raid documentation). Eyraud's figure of 1,200 suggests a loss of roughly 60% of the population in one year through raids, repatriation deaths, and the smallpox epidemic. This demographic catastrophe is the essential context for every other observation in this account.

### 3. Religion and Ritual

**3.1. Religion "occupies a very minor place in their lives"** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation / (c) Author's inference.** "As far as I could make out during my nine-month stay, religion seems to occupy a very minor place in their lives. My imperfect knowledge of their language, it's true, prevented me from asking many questions on this subject but, in spite of the fact that I lived with them in close proximity, I never witnessed any act that was really indicative of a religious cult." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 54)

**Observation**: Eyraud acknowledges the limitation of his language skills but insists that nine months of close observation revealed no organized religious practice. This is a striking claim given the island's monumental religious architecture. Whether it reflects post-raid collapse of organized religion (if religious specialists were among those taken) or Eyraud's inability to recognize non-Christian religious practice, or both, cannot be determined.

**3.2. Household statuettes: "without a doubt, idols"** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "In each of their homes there were many statuettes, of about thirty centimeters in height, that represented a man, a fish, or a bird, for example. These are, without a doubt, idols, but I never noticed anyone paying particular attention to them." He then adds: "Sometimes I saw a Kanac take one of these statuettes, raise it into the air, make a few gestures, to the accompaniment of a type of dance and some kind of vague chant." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 54)

**Observation**: Eyraud observes material objects of apparent religious significance but reports that they received minimal active veneration. The gestures he describes could represent residual ritual practice. His characterization of the statuettes as "idols" reflects his Catholic framework.

**3.3. Ritual as unreflective custom** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "They seemed just to be doing what their fathers had done, without giving it any more thought. If you ask them what it means, they answer, as they do about their games, that it is just a custom on the island." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 54)

**Observation**: This may reflect the loss of religious specialists who maintained esoteric knowledge, or it may reflect Eyraud's limited access to deeper explanations due to language barriers and his outsider status. The response "it is just a custom" could equally be a deflection of an outsider's intrusive questions rather than evidence of genuine loss of meaning.

**3.4. No religious rites observed after death** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "I also never saw any religious rites after someone had died." Bodies were wrapped in straw shrouds, tied with *piirau* (hibiscus bark rope), and "placed on a pile of stones or on a type of wooden trestle, with the head pointing towards the sea. Since the people are spread over the entire island, there are dried out bodies all along the coastline, and nobody pays much attention to them." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 54)

**Observation**: The absence of funerary ritual is notable but must be contextualized. Eyraud may not have been present at or invited to funerary rites. The body disposal practice he describes (exposure on stone platforms near the coast, oriented seaward) has material correlates that could be checked archaeologically. The smallpox epidemic, which was ongoing during his stay, may also have altered normal funerary practices.

**3.5. Superstition regarding death pronouncements** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** When Eyraud told Torometi "You will die" (*E pohe o'e*, which Altman notes is Tahitian for "dying of hunger"), "Torometi looked as if he had been struck by lightning. He started to tremble violently, expressing anger and terror. The bystanders did the same." The reaction lasted over two weeks and Eyraud "was treated as if I had committed a terrible crime." (Altman 2003, RNJ pp. 54)

**Observation**: This episode suggests active beliefs about the power of spoken words, curses, or prophecy, even if Eyraud witnessed no organized religious practice. It complicates his claim that religion occupied a "very minor place" in their lives and may indicate that the boundary between religion and daily practice was not one Eyraud could recognize.

**3.6. Seasonal festivals: paina, areauti, mataveri** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** Eyraud describes three seasonal gatherings that structured the annual cycle:

- **Paina** (summer): "Everyone brings enough food to last through the whole celebration, especially for the last day, the day of the banquet. All the food, placed in a long line and covered with branches, is the centerpiece." After feasting, the branches were gathered into "a kind of column or mast, which is called the paina." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 53)

- **Areauti** (autumn/winter): "They build large houses at the site of the festivities... the natives gather in groups, form two lines, and sing." Songs concerned recent events: "if a disease has been introduced, smallpox, for example, they sing about smallpox." The singing was highly repetitive: "sometimes just a single word... from the beginning to the end of the festivities." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 53; Fischer identifies *areauti* as probably a French transcription of *hare ha'uti*, "play/lovemaking house," a Tahitian borrowing)

- **Mataveri** (spring): "They gather on a kind of parade ground. The gathering lasts for two months and again they run and participate in all possible kinds of exercise." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 53)

**Observation**: These festivals involved communal feasting, performance, and physical competition. Notably, Eyraud describes no priestly or chiefly role in organizing or presiding over them. The paina involved collective food contribution (everyone brings their own provisions), not redistribution from a central authority. The gathering at mataveri drew "a large part of the population" from across the island, indicating supra-local social integration, but no directing authority is mentioned. The festivals continued despite the post-raid disruption, suggesting they were resilient social institutions.

### 4. Material Culture

**4.1. Rongorongo tablets** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "In all the homes there were wooden tablets covered with many types of hieroglyphic characters, which are symbols of animals that do not exist on the island and which the natives incise with a sharp stone. Each symbol has a name but the minimal fuss that the natives make of these tablets leads me to believe that these symbols, the remains of a primitive writing system, represent a custom that they continue to practice without trying to recall its meaning." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 54)

**Observation**: This is one of the earliest descriptions of rongorongo tablets in situ. The observation that tablets were found "in all the homes" suggests they were not restricted to an elite or priestly class at this date, though this may reflect post-raid redistribution of objects previously held by specialists. Eyraud's claim that the islanders made "minimal fuss" about them and practiced the custom "without trying to recall its meaning" parallels his observations about ritual statuettes (3.2-3.3): material culture persisting after the knowledge systems that gave it meaning had been disrupted. The loss of literate specialists in the slave raids is one explanation; the alternative is that Eyraud simply could not access the knowledge due to language barriers and cultural distance.

**4.2. Dwellings: hare paenga** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "Think of a mussel that is half open that is resting on its valve and you will have an idea of the shape of the house. A few sticks covered with straw make the framework and the roof. An opening like the opening to an oven allows the family and their visitors to enter -- not on their knees but on their stomachs. This entry marks the middle of the building." Inside, people slept "lying head to toe alternately, leaving between them enough space for others to come and go." (Altman 2003, RNJ pp. 51-52)

**Observation**: This description matches the archaeological hare paenga (boat-shaped house) known from stone foundation remains across the island. The mussel-shell comparison is apt for the elongated, low-profile form. The crawl-in entrance at the midpoint is consistent with archaeological evidence. The description of communal sleeping arrangements suggests these were multi-family or communal dwellings.

**4.3. Caves as hiding places** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "There are deep caves, some natural and some artificial, all over the island, and they have very narrow entrances. Only a few stones are needed to cover and hide each entrance. At any given moment, the entire population of the island could disappear, hiding in these caves." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 56)

**Observation**: Eyraud describes extensive use of caves for concealment and storage, consistent with the archaeological cave record. His claim that the entire population could disappear into caves may be an exaggeration but indicates widespread knowledge and use of cave systems. The "suspicious" hiding behavior he describes (people concealing possessions from each other) may reflect post-raid insecurity.

**4.4. Dress and body decoration** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** Basic dress consisted of "a band of material made of papyrus or some other plant, which they fastened with a cord of hair around their hips. A piece of the same material, but larger, was thrown over their shoulders and tied around their necks by the two ends." This was "the usual dress of both men and women." Women wore straw-based material; men's coverings were made of a different material. (Altman 2003, RNJ pp. 50-51) For festivals, "They put on everything that they can lay their hands on" including European clothing, with an emphasis on visual display and noise-making accessories. (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 53) Women's ear ornaments involved progressive piercing and stretching of earlobes, with bark rolls inserted to enlarge the holes until "the earlobe becomes a slender loop that falls onto the shoulder like a ribbon." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 53)

**Observation**: Eyraud describes no differentiation in dress that would mark political rank. Festival dress was competitive and display-oriented but based on individual acquisition rather than marks of office. The ear-stretching practice is well-attested in other sources and in moai iconography.

**4.5. Blood avoidance** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "I have noticed that the Kanacs are very careful not to spill blood. They strangle chickens." When Eyraud used a knife to kill a chicken, "a woman who saw me almost passed out." Dogs and goats were killed by burying the animal's head until suffocation. "Even though they have had knives since the arrival of the Peruvians, they never use them in their feuds. If they want to send someone into the next world, they find it simpler to use stones." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 55)

**Observation**: The blood taboo is described as applying to both animal slaughter and interpersonal violence. This represents a distinctive cultural pattern that Eyraud observed consistently over nine months.

**4.6. Craft skills** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "They braid straw adroitly and work adeptly with thread, with which they make belts, nets, etc. The thread is pulled from the fibrous tissues of the piirau. The bark of the maute, beaten and stretched, furnishes the material with which they cover their shoulders. Their tools are just their fingers and any stone that they pick up. They don't know how to use European tools." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 54)

**Observation**: Eyraud describes skilled craft production using traditional materials and stone tools, with no reference to craft specialization or specialists attached to chiefly households.

### 5. Population and Demography

**5.1. Population approximately 1,200 in January 1864** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** See 2.5 above. Eyraud estimated approximately 1,200 people upon arrival at Hanga Roa. This is the first population estimate after the slave raids.

**5.2. Smallpox epidemic ongoing** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation / (b) Reported tradition.** Daniel, the Mangarevan second-in-command on Eyraud's ship, reported that "there is smallpox all over the island. Some of the men who came back from Peru brought it and the epidemic has spread everywhere except to Anakena." Of approximately 100 repatriated from Peru, "only fifteen had escaped death and they had infected their compatriots." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 50)

**Observation**: The smallpox epidemic was active during Eyraud's stay and would have been contributing to ongoing population decline beyond the initial losses from the raids themselves. The population of 1,200 that Eyraud estimated was likely still declining.

**5.3. Population distributed across the island** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** Eyraud encountered people at Hanga Roa, Anakena, Anapika, Vaihu, and at seasonal festival gatherings. Bodies in shrouds were found "all along the coastline" because "the people are spread over the entire island." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 54)

**Observation**: Even at a population of approximately 1,200, people were dispersed across the island rather than concentrated in a few settlements. This is consistent with the dispersed settlement pattern known from the archaeological record.

### 6. Agriculture and Subsistence

**6.1. Sweet potatoes as the universal staple** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "That's what they eat every day, the invariable everyday meal of the Kanacs, both young and old." Sweet potatoes were "cooked in the Polynesian manner... by steaming in a hole in the ground, with heated stones." (Altman 2003, RNJ pp. 54-55)

**6.2. Minimal agricultural labor** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "One day's work assures them of an abundant harvest of sweet potatoes that will last an entire year. During the other 364 days; they take walks, they sleep, and they go visiting." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 53) Agricultural technique was simple: "they use a pointed stick to make little holes in the ground, they put in the potato eyes, and that's it." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 54)

**Observation**: Even accounting for rhetorical exaggeration ("one day's work" for a year's food), Eyraud describes a subsistence system requiring minimal coordinated labor. The absence of any mention of organized labor mobilization for agriculture is notable. No chief is described as directing agricultural production or collecting surplus.

**6.3. Chickens and fish as luxury foods** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** "There are indeed a few chickens and, very occasionally, someone catches a fish but these tasty delicacies are shared only by a few privileged people. The wife and children, when the husband has eaten, might perhaps suck on some bones, which have already been sucked free of flesh once and even twice." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 55)

**Observation**: This passage describes differential access to protein, with men eating first and women and children receiving leftovers. However, Eyraud does not describe this differential as a function of chiefly rank; it appears to operate at the household level. No redistribution of food through chiefly channels is described.

**6.4. Fertile soil, limited exploitation** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation / (c) Author's inference.** "The soil is fertile, if stony, and the regular rains and temperate climate seem to render this little island suitable for all kinds of plant." Eyraud's own vegetable garden succeeded before being destroyed by neighbors and children. He concluded "it should be possible to grow all crops found at the middle latitudes." (Altman 2003, RNJ p. 54)

### 7. Absences and Negative Evidence

**7.1. No organized political reception** [POST-RAID]

See 1.3 above. No delegation, no formal greeting, no authority figure directing the encounter with a newly arrived European.

**7.2. No tribute or redistribution described** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** In nine months of residence, Eyraud never describes tribute collection, food redistribution through political channels, or any economic transaction mediated by a chief. Torometi fed Eyraud sweet potatoes daily, but this appears to be an individual arrangement (Eyraud was "his personal property") rather than a redistributive system. The paina festival involved each participant bringing their own food, not centralized provisioning.

**7.3. No religious ceremonies observed** [POST-RAID]

See 3.1 above. Despite nine months of close observation, Eyraud "never witnessed any act that was really indicative of a religious cult."

**7.4. No formal justice system** [POST-RAID]

The only "justice" described is community mob action (burning Torometi's hut, 1.5 above) and individual coercion (stone-throwing, wife-beating). No judicial authority, adjudication process, or formal sanctions are described.

**7.5. No differentiated wealth** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** No individual or group is described as possessing markedly greater material wealth than others. Dwellings, dress, and subsistence are described as uniform. The only differentiation Eyraud notes is access to protein (6.3 above), which operates at the household level and is gendered rather than ranked.

**7.6. No mention of ahu or moai** [POST-RAID]

**(a) Direct observation.** Remarkably, Eyraud's nine-month account contains no discussion of the monumental ahu or moai that dominate the landscape. This may reflect the fact that by 1864 the moai had been toppled (the last were reportedly standing in the 1830s-1840s) and the ahu were no longer functioning as active ceremonial centers. Alternatively, Eyraud simply did not find them relevant to his missionary concerns. The absence is nonetheless striking and underscores the difficulty of reading social organization from a European observer's selective reporting.

### 8. Cross-Reference Notes

**8.1. Comparison with pre-raid contact accounts**

The contrast between Eyraud's description of political authority and those of earlier visitors is sharp. Roggeveen (1722) described one individual who appeared to direct others with "reverence and bowing." Gonzalez (1770) received a formal reception with differentiated participants. Cook (1774) met Ko Toheetai, identified as *aree* (king) of the whole island. La Perouse (1786) encountered organized delegations and individuals who directed food distribution. Eyraud, one year post-raid, found no organized reception, no identifiable political office, and authority based solely on personal influence. This contrast is consistent with two interpretations: (a) the slave raids destroyed an existing political hierarchy, or (b) earlier visitors, arriving with armed ships and conducting brief visits, were presented with coordinated responses to external threats that created an impression of centralized authority not characteristic of everyday governance. Both interpretations, and combinations of them, are consistent with the evidence.

**8.2. Relevance to chiefdom models**

Eyraud's account provides no support for a chiefdom model of Rapanui political organization, but this negative evidence is heavily compromised by the post-raid context. The account is most useful as evidence of what the society looked like one year after catastrophic depopulation: informal authority, collective action norms, universal participation in communal projects, absence of redistribution or tribute, and conflict resolution through property destruction rather than judicial process. Whether these features are consequences of societal collapse or continuations of pre-raid patterns cannot be determined from this source alone.

**8.3. The "national cooperation" passage and collective action theory**

Eyraud's description of the boat-building project (2.1 above) is the earliest explicit description of collective mobilization on Rapa Nui in which the observer specifically notes (a) universal participation, (b) the absence of any individual directing the project, and (c) social compulsion to participate enforced by norm rather than authority. This passage is directly relevant to evaluating whether monumental construction on Rapa Nui required chiefly command or could have been accomplished through collective action mechanisms.

**8.4. Loss of knowledge and the slave raids**

Eyraud's observations about rongorongo tablets (4.1), religious statuettes (3.2-3.3), and ritual practice (3.1) collectively suggest a society in which material culture persisted after the knowledge systems that animated it had been disrupted. If the slave raids removed the individuals who maintained esoteric knowledge (priests, literate specialists, senior chiefs), then the pattern Eyraud describes -- objects present but meaning lost, practices continued but unreflectively -- is exactly what one would predict. This has implications for all post-raid ethnographic sources (Thompson 1891, Routledge 1919, Metraux 1940): the informants available to those later researchers were the survivors of a knowledge catastrophe, not bearers of intact tradition.

**8.5. Eyraud's limitations as an observer**

Eyraud was a lay brother with a missionary agenda, limited language skills (he relied partly on Tahitian cognates), and no ethnographic training. His account is structured as a letter to his religious superior, not as a systematic description of Rapanui society. He spent most of his time teaching catechism and defending his possessions. His observations of political organization, religion, and social structure are incidental and filtered through French Catholic assumptions. The absence of information about topics he does not mention (ahu, moai, genealogies, land tenure, inter-group relations beyond theft and hut-burning) cannot be taken as evidence that these things did not exist or function; they may simply have fallen outside his frame of reference or interest.
