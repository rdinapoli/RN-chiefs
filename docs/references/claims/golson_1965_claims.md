# Claims Extraction: Golson 1965

**Source**: Golson, J. 1965. "Thor Heyerdahl and the Prehistory of Easter Island." *Oceania* 36(1):38-83.

**Type**: Critical review article; detailed archaeological reanalysis of the Norwegian Archaeological Expedition (Heyerdahl & Ferdon 1961) data and conclusions

**Date processed**: 2026-03-16

---

## I. Archaeological Continuity

### A. Polynesian Cultural Continuity

1. "It follows that Easter Island culture history, read in terms of the form and fortunes of its temples, is Polynesian throughout." (p. 80)

   **Note**: Golson's central conclusion. Ahu development can be explained as continuous Polynesian cultural evolution on Easter Island; no outside population or influence required. This is the continuity framework that later chiefdom attributions built upon: Easter Island is Polynesian, therefore Polynesian political models (chiefdoms) apply. The logical gap is that cultural continuity in material culture does not entail political continuity in social organization.

2. "The purpose of the introductory section of this paper was to show that the changes in ahu architecture and function which provide a useful if limited framework for the periodisation of Easter Island prehistory need not be ascribed to the arrival of different populations on the island. This thesis has been argued in terms of certain factors of continuity throughout ahu history." (p. 78)

3. "As between Early and Middle Period ahu, there is general continuity of ceremonial site and ground plan and some continuity in less general features like associated plazas and purely seaward orientation of the facade of the central platform." (p. 78)

4. "It is also evident that the skills exhibited by the builders of the Early Period in lifting, dressing and fitting large blocks of stone were in the possession of the builders of the Middle Period though directed to different ends, the erection of statues and preparation of base plates for them." (p. 78)

5. "Between the ahu of the Middle and Late Periods there is marked continuity of ceremonial site and less obvious continuity of burial practice." (p. 78)

### B. Artifacts as Polynesian

6. Duff's conclusion (1964, p. 80) cited: artifacts "represent the expected assemblage of categories, of stone and bone, all thoroughly familiar within the context of East Polynesian material culture." (p. 80)

7. "More specifically we have concluded that the excavated fishhooks are securely within the Polynesian tradition." (pp. 80-81)

## II. Chronological Critique

### A. Poike Ditch Dating

8. The "date of ad 386 ± 100 is an isolated one in the general context of Easter Island radiocarbon dates." (p. 77)

9. "The discovery of identical slingstones in the bank and the ditch fill at Poike's ditch leads us to question the validity of the interpretation of the two remarkably different radiocarbon dates obtained from that archaeological feature." (p. 81)

10. "It is as easy to believe that the dating is in error as that slingstones of exactly the same form, cylindrical specimens of vesicular lava with a shallow depression at one end, should have persisted over a period of about 1300 years." (p. 77)

### B. Period Boundaries Challenged

11. "What does more reliably emerge from this review of the radiocarbon dating evidence for Easter Island prehistory is the possibility that the dating of the three periods by the excavators is too old." (pp. 77-78)

12. Golson proposes later dates: "we might propose a later date than the excavators do for the beginning of the Middle Period - say ad 1250 instead of ad 1100: a pure guess in both cases." (p. 82)

13. For the Late Period: "A date c. 1750, between the Dutch discovery and the visit of Cook, might be more appropriate" than the c. AD 1680 suggested by the excavators. (p. 81)

   **Note**: Golson's proposed later chronology for all three periods has been partially vindicated. DiNapoli et al. 2020 shows ahu construction continuing through contact. Robinson & Stevenson 2017 place peak Orongo activity at c. AD 1795. Hunt & Lipo 2006 establish late colonization c. AD 1200. Golson's methodological skepticism about early dates was ahead of its time.

### C. Radiocarbon Date Quality

14. The Rano Raraku quarry date (AD 1206 ± 250) "was adjudged by the laboratory inadequate for accurate ageing" because "the charcoal had been crushed to powder under the great weight of debris above and the sample to be dated was thus impure." (p. 75)

15. Vinapu 2 plaza wall date (AD 857 ± 200): "This is admittedly only a single date and one that only very circumstantially dates the Early Period structure at Vinapu 2." (p. 77)

16. "These speculations are based upon the mere manipulation of a few radiocarbon dates of doubtful value." (p. 77)

## III. Late Period Warfare and Disruption

### A. Statue Toppling

17. "Most of the statues thus overturned could not be reused and Mulloy argues (pp. 111-112) that political conditions were unfavourable for them being replaced." (p. 74)

18. "Support is lent to the general picture of political unrest by the large numbers of mataa found in Late Period contexts." (p. 74)

19. "It may be significant that Roggeveen in 1722 and Gonzalez in 1770 make no reference to statues overturned." (p. 74)

20. "This circumstance is first recorded during Cook's visit of 1774." (p. 74)

   **Note**: Golson notes that the chronology of statue toppling may be later than assumed. If Roggeveen (1722) and González (1770) saw no overturned statues, the "Late Period" may not have begun until after 1770, consistent with Golson's proposed c. 1750 date and Pollard et al. 2010's suggestion that moai toppling was contact-driven.

### B. Heyerdahl's Multi-Population Thesis Critiqued

21. "The purpose of this article is to examine some of the conclusions reached on this score, through an analysis of the data provided by the excavation reports themselves." (p. 38)

22. Golson argues that changes in ahu architecture "need not be ascribed to the arrival of different populations on the island" (p. 78).

23. He challenges the claim that Vinapu 1 must predate Vinapu 2 and argues for the reverse order, undermining the South American origin theory for the precision masonry.

24. "If the proposed inversion of the sequence of Early Period ahu building at Vinapu is admitted, that is if we make Vinapu 2 earlier than Vinapu 1, earliest in the architectural sequence that type of Early Period ahu with resemblances in form and execution to Polynesian marae." (p. 80)

## IV. Methodological Observations

### A. Critique of Tradition-Based Chronology

25. "I am only concerned to emphasize how unsatisfactory the practice is of using traditions to make good the deficiencies of archaeological evidence in the strictly archaeological sphere." (p. 74)

   **Note**: This methodological critique of using oral traditions to fill archaeological gaps anticipates the epistemological argument of the current paper: ethnohistoric accounts cannot substitute for independent archaeological evidence.

26. "In the particular instance under discussion the traditional event that supplies the opening date for the Late Period is external to the events at the ahu in terms of which the Late Period is defined." (p. 75)

### B. Limited Evidence Base

27. "It is impossible to reconstruct ahu history in detail because we are very badly informed about its earliest stages." (p. 78)

28. "So few artifacts were found during the ahu investigations that other sites reported on can be assigned to period only by way of radiocarbon dates." (p. 78)

29. "Unfortunately the investigation of habitation sites did not advance far enough for sequences in terms of artifacts or domestic architecture to be possible." (p. 78)

### C. Orongo House Dating

30. "We have attempted to show that the claim for the antiquity of the Orongo house is invalid on the evidence used." (p. 81)

31. "If the Orongo house is late, and such a view it must be admitted is supported less by evidence than lack of it, it could conceivably have evolved on Easter Island from a timber prototype." (p. 81)

   **Note**: Golson challenges the claim that Orongo stone houses are ancient, consistent with Robinson & Stevenson 2017's finding that peak Orongo activity dates to c. AD 1795. If Orongo is late, the birdman ceremony formalization is a late development, not an ancient institution.

## V. Contact-Era Observations

32. "The records of the earliest European visitors to Easter Island should not, of course, be expected to give anything like a complete picture of contemporary conditions." (p. 74)

33. Roggeveen (1722) and González (1770) "make no reference to statues overturned" while Cook (1774) first records this. (p. 74)

## VI. Terminology Audit

**Terms present**: "political conditions" (once, p. 74, referring to Mulloy's argument), "political unrest" (once, p. 74)

**Terms absent**: "chiefdom," "chief," "hierarchy," "rank," "stratified," "redistribution," "Service," "Sahlins," "Goldman," "ramage," "big man," "paramount," "authority" (as political term)

**Significance**: Golson's article is an archaeological methodological critique focused on ahu architecture, chronology, and cultural affiliation. It contains no discussion of political organization. The two uses of "political" both refer to Late Period warfare/disruption in Mulloy's terms, not to any typological classification. Service 1962 was published three years before Golson 1965 but does not appear in the bibliography. This is consistent with the pre-chiefdom-model baseline: the chiefdom framework had not yet been applied to Easter Island.

## VII. Source Dependencies

Golson's analysis is based entirely on the published Norwegian Archaeological Expedition reports (Heyerdahl & Ferdon 1961). Additional sources cited:
- Duff 1964 (review article; Polynesian artifact tradition)
- Smith (multiple pages): ahu excavation reports
- Mulloy: Vinapu excavation reports
- Skjølsvold: Rano Raraku and habitation site reports
- Ferdon: habitation site reports, Orongo
- Green 1963: Pacific settlement chronology
- Emory 1933: Society Islands marae comparison
- Skinner 1942: fishhook classification

No ethnographic or ethnohistoric sources cited for political organization. No theoretical framework for social organization.
