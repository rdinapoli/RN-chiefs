# Claims Extraction: McCoy 1976

**Source**: McCoy, Patrick Carlton. 1976. *Easter Island Settlement Patterns in the Late Prehistoric and Protohistoric Periods*. Bulletin Five, Easter Island Committee, International Fund for Monuments Inc.

**Type**: Archaeological monograph (published dissertation research)
**Accessed**: PDF scan (`mccoy_1976.pdf`)
**Extracted**: 2026-03-16

---

## I. Methodological Framework

### How McCoy uses ethnohistoric data

1. "Ethnographic data is the basis for much of the temporal-functional interpretation and societal reconstruction in this study. It is justified on the basis of demonstrable genetic affinity between archaeological remains and the known historic Easter Island culture." (p. 2)

2. "The traditional way around these limiting factors in Polynesian settlement pattern studies has been the judicious use of ethnohistoric and ethnographic records, the relevant aspects of which are projected with care back in time (cf. Groube 1965; Green 1967, 1970; Green, *et al.* 1967). This line of evidence is good enough to document in fair depth the protohistoric period settlement. The archaeological data are checked against this baseline and the appropriateness of the pattern evaluated in a late prehistoric context." (p. 70)

**Assessment**: This passage is a direct, explicit description of the methodology that creates the five-step circularity our paper identifies. McCoy starts with ethnohistoric descriptions, projects them back in time, then "checks" archaeological data against them. The archaeological data cannot disconfirm the ethnohistoric model because the model defines the interpretive framework.

3. "The emphasis in this study is given to descriptive, static models rather than dynamic, predictive models (Haggett and Chorley 1968: 25) in view of the type and quantity of archaeological data presently available for Easter Island." (p. 2)

4. "Clearly, the greatest weakness in most prehistoric settlement pattern models, including the present one, is the lack of chronological control in sorting out contemporaneous sites constituting a settlement (Gathercole 1972: 55)." (p. 70)

## II. Settlement Pattern Claims

### Village structure

5. "Examination of the site survey maps (Figs. 46-50) reveals that the villages were partially dispersed settlements (Murdock and Wilson 1972: 257) composed of a central core of communal houses, communal structures, and an *ahu*, with more scattered family residences further inland." (p. 109)

6. "The description of house types in Chapter III indicates that the seaward residential complex associated with the *ahu* and other communal structures was that of high-ranking persons, while the more interior, scattered habitations belonged to commoners. The spatial relationship of the two residential segments is constant, and indicative of a pervading pattern determined by the underlying principles of social organization." (p. 109)

7. "The communal center can be viewed as the fundamental unit of economics, since it functioned as a base of supply and redistribution." (p. 152)

**Assessment**: McCoy infers the "redistribution" function of the communal center entirely from ethnohistoric/ethnographic description, not from archaeological evidence. No archaeological evidence for redistribution is presented.

### Settlement location and density

8. "The relationship between density and distance can be stated as follows: site density decreases with increasing distance from the shoreline." (p. 135)

9. "The conclusion is that density varies directly with accessibility to drinking water, marine resources, and the community center. Settlement was a continuum with the most desirable areas along the coast and least desirable further inland." (p. 135)

10. "Village location was not determined by proximity to one single activity, but in relation to a fresh-water supply and accessibility to marine resources. In short, location was a function of basic biological needs. They are of first order importance and underlie all other determinants." (pp. 123-124)

## III. Social Organization Claims

### Status differentiation in housing

11. "A status hierarchy, if it existed in the family, is not manifested archaeologically. Nuclear units were never physically separated by partitioned rooms, contrary to Ferdon's identification of contiguous-walled, stone enclosures as houses." (p. 149)

12. "Marked social stratification is evident in domestic architecture. A dual status system was manifested in the differential size, quality and relative position of elliptical houses, the dominant house type occupied permanently in the late prehistoric and protohistoric periods." (p. 149)

13. "The most common house was, by identification from early navigators' descriptions, a low thatch hut with the rafters set directly into the ground. This was the abode of commoners (*hurumanu*). Larger, more elaborate houses with dressed curbstone foundations and larger pavements on the front, seaward side were occupied by the chiefly and priestly (*ariki*) class." (p. 149)

**Assessment**: McCoy infers social meaning of house types from ethnohistoric descriptions. The correlation of house size/elaboration with status is derived from contact-era accounts, not from independent archaeological evidence (e.g., differential artifact assemblages, mortuary treatment).

14. "In short, there was a positive correlation between discreteness of social status and discreteness in residence (Groube 1965: 4-5). House size and elaboration of foundations and pavements, furthermore, is the only visible surviving insignia of wealth (synonymous with high rank)." (pp. 149-150)

15. "The general absence of gardens and fowl houses on chiefly sites is one measure of the degree of stratification in the society. It indicates that upper status members were exempt from production." (p. 150)

**Assessment**: McCoy first identifies certain houses as "chiefly" based on ethnohistoric descriptions, then uses the absence of production features on those sites as evidence of stratification. This is circular: the houses are labeled "chiefly" before the evidence is assessed.

### Household production

16. "Production of individual households was supervised by household heads rather than directly through chiefs (Sahlins 1958: 55). There is no evidence to indicate that households were economically self-sufficient, and a good deal to argue against it." (p. 148)

17. "The appearance of the chicken house and its proximity to the house clearly demonstrates the value placed on fowl for food and as the principal item of exchange in life-crises rites, and is an indirect expression of the economics of scarcity in a period of political stress brought on by internal warfare between kin groups." (p. 148)

### Community organization

18. "Sociologically, Easter Island communities were composed of a number of related households, tracing descent to a common ancestor. Collectively, households formed a lineage, or sub-tribe, headed by the direct descendant of this ancestor. Like households, lineages originated by a continuous process of segmentation (Metraux 1940: 126; Sahlins 1958: 168)." (p. 74)

19. "To each lineage was appropriated a fairly large piece of land, called *henua poreko ranga* (the land where the ancestors were born), and divided among households." (p. 74)

## IV. Tribal Territories

20. "According to accepted tradition, the island was divided into six large land divisions shortly after settlement. Hotu Matua, the founding king, is said to have portioned out lands, just prior to his death, to each of his six sons." (p. 126)

21. McCoy maps tribal territories based on Routledge (1919) and Métraux (1940), Fig. 54 (p. 127). The territories are: Miru, Haumoana (part), Ngatimo, Tuu, Hotu Iti, and others.

22. "Superimposing the roughly approximate tribal boundaries originally established by Routledge (1919; Fig. 91) on the site density map reveals some interesting correlations between tribal location and density." (p. 139)

**Assessment**: McCoy superimposes ethnohistoric tribal boundaries on archaeological density data. The tribal boundaries are derived from post-raid ethnographic accounts, not from independent archaeological evidence. The "correlations" he finds are between ethnographic claims and archaeological data interpreted through those same claims.

## V. Ahu and Monuments

23. "An integral part of the settlement and the focal point of religious activities was the *ahu*, a stone platform with its direct structural-functional equivalent in the Eastern and Central Polynesian *marae*." (p. 91)

24. "In all probability the statues represent deified ancestors. Cook and his botanist, George Forster, heard the term *ariki* (chief) applied to some of them, from which they concluded that the statues 'were monuments erected to the memory of some areekees, or kings' (Forster 1777, Vol. 1: 375)." (p. 91)

25. "Evidence for continuity in location of *ahu* through time is based on architectural differences in wall construction and differences in statue sizes present on or beside *ahu*." (p. 95)

26. "*Ahu* with vertical and well-fitted masonry are regarded as the oldest. *Ahu* with vertical masonry and small statues are assumed to be later and date to the early Middle Period—ca. A.D. 1200-1400." (p. 101)

27. "Following the toppling of statues and general destruction of image *ahu*, in the late prehistoric-protohistoric wars, many of them were converted into burial cairns that assumed a semi-pyramidal form." (p. 93)

28. 43 *ahu* were recorded in the 1968 survey area, distributed across seven typological categories: image (18), semi-pyramidal (9), *ahu poepoe* (3), rectangular (1), upright slabs (3), enclosed court (1), unclassified (10). (Table 15, p. 95)

## VI. The Chiefdom Question — Key Claims

### McCoy's explicit statement on chiefdom development

29. "If there existed any tendencies toward the development of a chiefdom, the political flux caused by the ascendancy of the warrior class to positions of authority clearly retarded it." (p. 145)

**Assessment**: THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT CLAIM IN THE MONOGRAPH FOR OUR PAPER. McCoy, who is often cited as one of the first to apply the chiefdom framework to Rapa Nui, actually concluded that Rapa Nui did NOT develop a chiefdom because the warrior class (*matatoa*) disrupted any such tendencies. This is the opposite of what subsequent scholars have attributed to the archaeological record.

### Warriors and political organization

30. "The second [type of authority], held by the warrior class (*matatoa*), was secular, although there is evidence that the *matatoa* assumed the prerogatives of *mana* and *tapu* (Goldman 1971: 96). If there existed any tendencies toward the development of a chiefdom, the political flux caused by the ascendancy of the warrior class to positions of authority clearly retarded it." (p. 145)

31. "The ascendancy of a warrior class to powers of authority probably functioned to promote more warfare. The net effect was decreasing social integration and increasing dispersion—shifting—of people." (p. 154)

32. "The effects of an increasingly secular political organization in the late prehistoric period must have had a profound impact on the settlement pattern." (p. 154)

### Environmental pressure model

33. "Population pressure on land and resources is isolated as the antecedent causal condition to warfare, the adaptive and maladaptive effects of which are then discussed in terms of observable and probable changes in settlement patterns." (p. 141)

34. "Almost constant conflict resulted from large population size and concomitant scarcity of vital resources." (p. 141)

35. "A maximum population figure of 7,000 or more is conjecture based on the evaluation of eighteenth and nineteenth century population estimates." (p. 141)

36. "There were sharp limitations to population growth on Easter Island set by a finite land area of approximately 60 square miles with no nearby islands to settle, and a physiographically restricted and biotically impoverished environment." (p. 142)

## VII. On Evidence and Interpretation

### Explicit acknowledgment of circularity risk

37. "It is important to note that the site cluster analysis was undertaken prior to review of the contact period literature, and that the findings were in no way biased by the protohistoric model previously summarized. This statement is interjected here because there is such a close correlation between the present analysis and ethnohistoric description of site grouping." (p. 117)

**Assessment**: McCoy is aware of the risk of circularity and claims independence. But his framework was established through ethnohistoric training and he uses ethnohistoric categories (commoner/chief house types, tribal territories) throughout. The claim of independence is undermined by the interpretive framework.

### On settlement models and limitations

38. "The individual settlement pattern constructs developed in this study are strictly applicable to only the area covered by the 1968 survey. They constitute predictive models to be tested by fieldwork on other parts of the island." (p. 155)

39. "Implicit in archaeological homogeneity and regularity in observable ordering of social relations, is the notion of minimal culture change beyond initial adaptation in the settlement period." (p. 153)

## VIII. Other Notable Claims

### On household self-sufficiency

40. "Gardens and chicken houses, rarely located far from a dwelling site, are clear-cut evidence that the household was the smallest unit of production and consumption, and deployment of labor in the society." (p. 148)

### On Sahlins and Easter Island

41. "Easter Island is an exception to Sahlins' (1958) theory linking dispersed settlements *vis-a-vis* ramage social organization to dispersed zones of highly productive resources. Sahlins conjectured that the carry-over of the ranked lineage system on Easter Island was instead a matter of continuity in a traditional system. The tendency toward dispersion and loose clustering in Easter Island settlements is also hypothesized to be in part a function of environmental constraints." (p. 152)

**Assessment**: McCoy notes that Sahlins himself recognized Easter Island as an exception to his model, not a confirmation of it. This is significant for our paper.

### On water and settlement

42. "Water, rather than soil fertility, then, seems to have been the major limiting factor in the exploitation of uninhabited lands." (p. 142)

**Assessment**: This claim, from 1976, anticipates the DiNapoli et al. 2019 finding that ahu placement correlates with freshwater access.

### On cultural continuity

43. "Little diversity in the content and layout of the household residence and settlement, are suggestive clues of a dominant, if not single, important cultural tradition which, without question, is Polynesian." (p. 153)

---

## Summary of Claims Relevant to Chiefdom Attribution

McCoy 1976 is often cited as the first archaeological application of the chiefdom framework to Rapa Nui, but this characterization is misleading. McCoy's actual conclusions:

1. He describes a **ranked society** with a dual status system (chiefs/commoners) visible in house types, but the evidence for this ranking comes from ethnohistoric descriptions, not independent archaeological analysis.

2. He explicitly states that **chiefdom development was "retarded"** by the warrior class (p. 145). Far from confirming a chiefdom, McCoy concludes the opposite.

3. He documents **environmental pressure and warfare** as dominant forces in the late prehistoric period, leading to social fragmentation, not consolidation.

4. His methodology **explicitly projects ethnohistoric data back in time** and uses it as the interpretive baseline against which archaeological evidence is evaluated (p. 70). This is the circularity our paper identifies.

5. He notes that **Sahlins himself treated Easter Island as an exception** to his Polynesian chiefdom model, not a confirmation (p. 152).

6. His evidence for the "communal center" as a "base of supply and redistribution" (p. 152) is derived entirely from ethnographic description, not from archaeological indicators of redistribution.

The gap between what McCoy actually wrote and how subsequent scholars have used his work is itself evidence of the circularity problem. McCoy's careful settlement data was later reinterpreted through the Kirch framework as confirming the chiefdom model that McCoy himself said did not develop.
