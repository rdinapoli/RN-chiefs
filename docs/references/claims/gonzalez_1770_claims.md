# Claims Extraction: Gonzalez 1770

## Source Information

**Primary source:** The Spanish expedition to Easter Island (Isla de David / San Carlos) under Commodore Don Felipe Gonzalez de Haedo, November 15-21, 1770. Two ships: the San Lorenzo and the frigate Santa Rosalia.

**Texts consulted (all via Corney 1903 translation unless otherwise noted):**
1. "Journal of the Principal Occurrences during the Voyage of the Frigate Santa Rosalia" (probably by Don Francisco Antonio de Aguera y Infanzon, Chief Pilot of the Santa Rosalia). Corney 1903, pp. 47-76 in the Hakluyt Society volume. [PDF: Easter Island Expeditions_Part 2 (Spain).pdf, pp. 47-76]
2. "Narrative of the Expedition undertaken by order of His Excellency Don Manuel de Amat, Viceroy of Peru" (probably by Sub-Lieut. Don Juan Herve, First Pilot / Senior Navigating Officer of the San Lorenzo). Corney 1903, pp. 79-94. [PDF: Easter Island Expeditions_Part 3 (Peru-Cook).pdf, pp. 79-94]
3. Jakubowska, Zuzanna (2014). "The Spanish expedition to Easter Island, 1770: Original documents and their rendition by Bolton Glanvill Corney." In Handzlik & Sorokowski (eds.), *Found in Multiculturalism*, pp. 107-119. [PDF: Z.Jakubowska2014]

**Context:** This was a formal annexation expedition. The Spanish claimed the island for Spain under Carlos III. The expedition stayed approximately 6 days (November 15-21, 1770). Multiple officers participated in shore excursions. The annexation ceremony took place on November 20, 1770.

**Note on authorship:** Corney attributes the "Journal" to an officer of the Santa Rosalia, probably Aguera. He attributes the "Narrative" to Herve of the San Lorenzo. Throughout this document, I use "Aguera" and "Herve" as shorthand for the probable authors of these two accounts.

## Claims Extraction

### Direct Observations of Political Authority / Social Differentiation

1. **First sighting of islanders: group on ridge, clothing differences noted.** "we observed a troop of people composed of eighteen persons who were walking briskly along the summit of a high ridge, where they all collected together and sat down, remaining in this wise while we passed in view about a gunshot off. We noticed some of them clothed in garments like a *poncho* or cloak, coloured: at the first sight we thought them European soldiers, but having approached within a mile of them we became satisfied that they were natives, all of them unarmed, and some nude, wearing plumes on their heads." (Aguera, p. 55)
    - **Assessment:** Describes differentiated dress (some cloaked, some nude, some plumed) but does NOT describe anyone giving orders or exercising authority over the group. The group's behavior (collecting together, sitting down, watching) is collective, not directed.

2. **Body paint differentiation by status.** "The principal men, or those in authority, paint the whole of their bodies with some herb, or liquor, having a bright red hue, drawing great numbers of lines, pyramids, cocks, and most hideous masks [*rostros feisimos*], but all disposed in such order and symmetry that it would require the most dexterous pencil to imitate." (Aguera, p. 62)
    - **Assessment:** Aguera distinguishes "principal men, or those in authority" from others based on extent of body paint. This is a direct observation of visual differentiation. However, the phrase "those in authority" is Aguera's interpretive gloss on the visual difference; the text does not describe what authority these men exercised. Also see Jakubowska's note that Corney mistranslates "hombres mayores" (elderly men) as "principal men" (see Cross-Reference Notes below).

3. **Women's deference to older/important men.** "the older and more important men retain some preference in the matter, as these are always the ones who accompany and make offer of them, and to whom the women render obedience, and not to the younger men, with whom we have never seen them in company. So that one notices a more modest behaviour among the youths and young women than among the elders." (Aguera, p. 61)
    - **Assessment:** Describes an age-graded pattern of social deference related to sexual/marital arrangements. The "obedience" described is specifically in the context of women's relationships to older men versus younger men. This is a direct observation of age-based social hierarchy but is NOT specifically about political authority or command over resources.

4. **Cave-dwelling patterns and status differentiation.** "Most of the natives of the island dwell in underground caves, or in the hollow of some rock, the entrances to which are so narrow and inconvenient that I have seen some of them introduce themselves in the opposite manner to what is natural, beginning by projecting their feet and the head last. The more polished or powerful persons, whether in virtue of their age or of authority, are held in esteem. These inhabit small huts covered with reeds [*totora*], and constructed in the form of a large tunnel, in whose bilge or bellying portion [*vientre o barriga*] is the entrance, after the manner of a trap-door for cats' egress, so narrow that only one man can pass in or out at a time, and with effort. Others (whom I believe to be their ministers) occupy dwellings close to the statues; these are built of earth below, but with an entrance way or porch of very roughly hewn and clumsily set up stones, after the fashion of a wall, with a certain number of steps for passing from one platform or surface of ground to another on different levels." (Aguera, pp. 65-66)
    - **Assessment:** This is one of the most significant passages. Aguera describes three tiers of dwelling: (a) caves for most people, (b) reed huts for "the more polished or powerful persons," and (c) stone-built dwellings near statues for those he "believes to be their ministers." The third category is explicitly his inference ("I believe"). The differentiation in housing quality is a direct observation; the attribution of the differentiation to authority/status is interpretive.

5. **"Ministers" or "priests" near statues, religious procession behavior.** "They seem to me to have ministers or priests for their idols; because I observed that on the day which we erected the crosses, when our chaplains went accompanying the holy images, clothed in their cassocks and *pellis*, chanting the litanies, numbers of natives stepped forward on to the path and offered the women presented hens and pullets, and all cried *Maca Maca*, treating them with much veneration until they had passed beyond the rocks by which the track they were following was encumbered." (Aguera, p. 64)
    - **Assessment:** Aguera observes people who step forward during a religious-like procession and who live near statues. He infers they are "ministers or priests." The observed behavior is: stepping forward during the chaplains' procession, offering fowls, crying "Maca Maca," treating the chaplains with "veneration." This could indicate religious specialists, or it could indicate people who happened to live in the area or who were responding to the novel European religious display.

6. **Annexation ceremony: natives carry crosses, accompany march.** "On the party forming up, together with those bearing arms, we set out on the march, accompanied by the natives, who lent a willing hand in carrying the crosses, singing and dancing in their fashion as they went. We made the whole circuit of the bay with some pains, for the ground was rough and rugged, although level, a great retinue of natives collecting round us all the while as far as the foot of the rise, where the most part of them quitted us on account of the troublesome and protracted nature of the ascent." (Aguera, pp. 67-68)
    - **Assessment:** Describes enthusiastic native participation in the procession but without any indication of who organized or directed the native participation. The natives "lent a willing hand" and accompanied the Spanish voluntarily. Many dropped off at the steep ascent. No individual is described as directing or organizing the native contingent.

7. **Annexation ceremony: "signing" the document.** "for the greater confirmation of so serious an act some of the natives present signed or attested the official document by marking upon it certain characters in their own form of script. Then we cheered the king seven times, next to which followed a triple volley of musketry from the whole party, and, lastly, our ships saluted with 21 guns." (Aguera, p. 68)
    - **Assessment:** This is the critical passage. Some natives "signed or attested" the document. The text says they marked it with "certain characters in their own form of script." Aguera does NOT describe how these particular signatories were selected, whether they were understood to represent any political authority, or what the Spanish told them or they understood about the act. The plate (p. 75) reproduces what are labeled "SIGNATURES OF CHIEFS OF EASTER ISLAND, TO THE SPANISH TREATY, 1770." The label "Chiefs" appears on the plate caption, imposed by Corney or the Hakluyt Society editors; the Aguera text itself does not use the word "chiefs" for the signatories.

8. **Natives received Spanish with "great number" at disembarkation on Nov 20.** "A great number of the native inhabitants received them on landing, and offered to assist our officers in the disembarkation, which, in fact, they did; and took charge of the three crosses, which they carried up to the said hills: the chaplains chanting Litanies, and the islanders joining with our people in the responses, *ora pro nobis*." (Herve, p. 91)
    - **Assessment:** Herve describes collective native assistance, not directed by any identified leader. The natives collectively carried crosses and joined in the litanies. No individual is identified as organizing or directing this activity.

9. **Herve's observation of islanders on first approach.** "when we drew close in to the shore taking soundings, we saw several natives of the country on the beach shouting to us in their language, of which we understood nothing. These were naked, and painted, body and face." (Herve, p. 85)
    - **Assessment:** No differentiation or leadership described among the shore party.

10. **Islanders conduct Spanish to a dwelling-house.** "putting aside their shyness, came close up to our people and conducted us to see a long dwelling-house which was about a quarter of a league off. This house was 27 paces in length, 2 1/2 *varas* high at the centre, and 1 1/2 *varas* at the ends, more or less; and at the middle part was a doorway 1 *vara* in height. It was framed on some six poles of 4 *varas* long, and a span in thickness. After having shown us this sumptuous edifice, they began to sing and to dance by way of paying us a compliment and being very happy at seeing us." (Herve, pp. 88-89)
    - **Assessment:** Islanders collectively show the Spanish a large house. No single individual is identified as host or owner. The house is described as relatively substantial (27 paces long). The collective singing and dancing is presented as a group response. Herve's ironic use of "sumptuous edifice" reflects European condescension.

11. **Dining observation: group withdrawal, one remains as "watch."** "When we sat down to eat we noticed that they all withdrew, and that only one remained, as if to watch; I ordered my servant to give this one a little cooked rice and salt pork, all of which he ate and found much to his taste." (Herve, p. 89)
    - **Assessment:** An individual remains while others withdraw during the Spanish meal, described as being "as if to watch." This could suggest a designated observer or guard, but could also be individual curiosity. Herve does not describe this person as a leader.

12. **Exchange behavior on the water: small canoes with trade goods.** "two little canoes were coming out from among them with two men in each, making for the *Santa Rosalia*'s launch; so we waited for them in order that they might join our party. They gave the people of the said launch plantains, Chili peppers, sweet potatoes and fowls; and in return our men gave them hats, *chamorretas*, &c., and they went off contentedly with these to the shore." (Herve, p. 87)
    - **Assessment:** Exchange is described without any indication of centralized control or redistribution. Small groups (two men per canoe) engage in direct exchange with the Spanish. No intermediary or authority figure oversees the transactions.

13. **Hundred natives come to look on during a meal ashore.** "some hundred or so natives came to look on, offering us fruits and hens. The officer, Don Cayetano de Langara, issued orders to our people that no one, under pain of a severe flogging, should accept any article from the islanders without giving some equivalent in return, or something of greater value than that which they received, since it was known there was a disposition to exchange articles; and such in fact was put into practice." (Herve, p. 88)
    - **Assessment:** About 100 natives offer food to the Spanish. The exchange is described as a general "disposition" among the islanders, not organized by any particular leader. The regulation came from the Spanish officer, not from any islander authority.

14. **Natives' reaction to gunfire and musketry during annexation.** "It need not be said that the islanders were terrified at the noise of the gunfire and musketry: that must happen to people who have not used or seen such inventions." (Herve, p. 92)
    - **Assessment:** Collective reaction, no differentiated leadership response described.

15. **Vocabulary list includes term for "High chief or lord": *Tequeteque*.** The vocabulary collected by Aguera (p. 73) includes the entry: "High chief or lord ... Tequeteque" and "Priest of the idol ... Maca Maca."
    - **Assessment:** This is significant. The Spanish collected a specific term for what they understood as "High chief or lord." This indicates that either (a) the islanders had a term for a political leader and communicated it, or (b) the Spanish asked "what do you call your chief?" and the islanders provided a word that may or may not mean what the Spanish understood. The word appears to be a reduplicated form. The vocabulary was collected "making use of signs, demonstrations, and figures shown by drawings" (p. 73), which is a very imprecise method for abstract political concepts. Note also "Priest of the idol ... Maca Maca" confirms the "Maca Maca" exclamation during the cross-planting procession may be a term for a religious specialist rather than a general exclamation.

16. **Age-based distinction in body paint.** "The young people do not paint themselves in this fashion, only a few of them have a collar of the same colour traced round the neck, and depending from it the figure of a small animal resembling a toad, or frog, which they call *coge*." (Aguera, p. 62)
    - **Assessment:** Direct observation of age-graded body paint practices. Only "principal men" / "those in authority" (or more accurately, per Jakubowska, "elderly men") have full-body paint. Young people are largely unpainted. This is consistent with age-grading rather than hereditary political rank.

17. **"Maca Maca" exclamation repeated during religious-seeming events.** During the chaplains' procession with crosses, "all cried Maca Maca, treating them with much veneration" (Aguera, p. 64). The vocabulary list gives "Priest of the idol ... Maca Maca" (Aguera, p. 73).
    - **Assessment:** The exclamation may indicate recognition of a religious category. The islanders may have been identifying the Spanish chaplains with their own religious specialists. This is potentially relevant to whether there was a recognized priest class, though the nature and political authority of such specialists cannot be determined from this passage alone.

18. **No weapons observed; stones as only defense.** "We never saw their bravery put to the test, but I suspect they are faint-hearted; they possess no arms, and although in some we observed sundry wounds on the body, which we thought to have been inflicted by cutting instruments of iron or steel, we found that they proceeded from stones, which are their only [weapons of] defence and offence, and as most of these are sharp edged they produce the injury referred to." (Aguera, p. 63)
    - **Assessment:** No organized military capacity observed. Weapons limited to stones. Wounds observed on some bodies suggest interpersonal or intergroup conflict, but no organized military force or warrior class is described.

19. **No ornaments of gold, silver, jewellery, or metal among islanders.** "No ornaments of gold, silver, jewellery, or any other metal, nor any kind of clothes or hardware, were seen among them; from which it may be inferred that they have at present no interchange of goods with any European, Asiatic, or American nation." (Aguera, pp. 59-60)
    - **Assessment:** Direct observation of absence of prestige goods or trade materials that might indicate external trade networks or wealth accumulation.

20. **Property behavior: communal or individual?** "It appears as if among themselves their goods are held in common, and I believe they conceal as much as they can get possession of below the ground, for we never saw afterwards any of the things we gave them." (Aguera, p. 63)
    - **Assessment:** Aguera makes two contradictory observations: (a) goods appear "held in common," but (b) people conceal items underground, suggesting individual possession. The fact that gifted items disappeared suggests private caching rather than communal redistribution.

21. **Theft behavior described as universal, not status-differentiated.** "they are so fond of taking other people's property that what one man obtains another will take from him, and he yields it without feeling aggrieved: the most he will do is to resist a little, then he loosens his hold of it and they remain friends." (Aguera, p. 62)
    - **Assessment:** Describes a pattern of easy property transfer without resentment. No authority figure is described as mediating disputes or controlling redistribution. The behavior described is egalitarian in character.

22. **Population estimate.** "The number of the inhabitants, including both sexes, will be from about nine hundred to a thousand souls: and of these very few indeed are women" (Aguera, p. 60). Herve similarly estimates "The number of the inhabitants, including both sexes, will be from about nine hundred to a thousand souls" (Herve, p. 93).
    - **Assessment:** Both accounts give the same population estimate (~900-1,000). Both note the apparent scarcity of women. [Note: Jakubowska points out that Corney mistranslates "sesenta" (sixty) as "seventy" in the same passage regarding the number of women; the original says women "amount to sixty" not "seventy."]

23. **Islanders board ships freely in large numbers.** "The natives continued to gather on board in greater number than on the preceding days, so that on this day there have been more than 400 in the frigate. What with men and women they collected in such crowds that it became necessary to send away some in order to make room for others, as we could not contain them on board." (Aguera, p. 64)
    - **Assessment:** Over 400 islanders board the frigate in a single day. No individual is described as mediating access or organizing the boarding. The Spanish had to manage the crowds themselves. This suggests no chiefly control over access to the Europeans.

### Interpretive Statements About Political Organization

1. **Aguera's inference about "ministers" near statues.** "Others (whom I believe to be their ministers) occupy dwellings close to the statues" (Aguera, p. 66).
    - **Assessment:** Explicitly acknowledged as Aguera's belief/inference, not a direct observation. The association of certain dwellings near statues with a "minister" role is Aguera's interpretation of spatial proximity.

2. **Aguera's inference about idol-related religious practices.** "They seem to me to have ministers or priests for their idols" (Aguera, p. 64).
    - **Assessment:** Explicitly hedged ("seem to me"). Based on behavior during the cross-planting procession and dwelling patterns near statues.

3. **Aguera's inference about "propagation" / marital affairs.** "As we had no opportunity of enquiring into the methods they observe in regard to marital affairs [*propagacion*] it can only be inferred that the women whom we saw are held in common among them, although we noticed that the older and more important men retain some preference in the matter" (Aguera, p. 61).
    - **Assessment:** Aguera explicitly frames this as inference ("it can only be inferred"). He reasons from the apparent scarcity of women to communal marriage, then qualifies with an observation about older men's precedence. The term "more important men" is his interpretation of observed deference patterns.

4. **Herve's inference about European-like potential.** "they in no way resemble the Indians of the South American continent; and if they wore clothing like ourselves they might very well pass for Europeans. They eat very little, and have few needs: they do altogether without liquor of any kind." (Herve, p. 93)
    - **Assessment:** Interpretive comparison framing islanders favorably relative to South American indigenous peoples, reflecting European racial categories rather than any observation about political organization.

5. **Aguera's inference about stone-working near statues.** "It is known that they work the stone, on which may be seen several different figures, squares, oblongs, arcs [*rumbos*], triangles, and trapezia, by means of another stone of harder substance than the mass, and the same method is followed, I believe, in fashioning the statues." (Aguera, p. 66)
    - **Assessment:** Observes stone-working capability but does not attribute it to organized labor directed by chiefs. The inference about statue-making method is hedged ("I believe").

### European Categories Imposed

1. **"SIGNATURES OF CHIEFS OF EASTER ISLAND, TO THE SPANISH TREATY, 1770."** This caption appears on the plate (p. 75) reproducing the marks made on the annexation document. The word "Chiefs" is editorial, appearing in the plate caption but not in the body text of Aguera's account, which says only "some of the natives present signed or attested the official document" (p. 68).
    - **Assessment:** CRITICAL. The most consequential imposition of European political categories in the entire expedition record. The Spanish needed identifiable political authorities to make the annexation legally valid under Spanish colonial law. The plate labels the signatories "Chiefs" without any evidence in the accompanying text that these individuals were identified as chiefs by the islanders or by the Spanish at the time. The text says only that "some of the natives present" signed. The selection criteria for these signatories are never described.

2. **"High chief or lord ... Tequeteque" in the vocabulary list.** (Aguera, p. 73)
    - **Assessment:** The Spanish asked for or elicited a term they mapped onto "High chief or lord." The method of elicitation was "signs, demonstrations, and figures shown by drawings" (p. 73). Whether the Rapanui term *tequeteque* actually denotes a political office equivalent to "chief or lord" cannot be determined from this exchange. The term could denote an elder, a priest, a respected person, or indeed a political leader; the signs-and-drawings method cannot convey the structural distinctions between these roles.

3. **"The principal men, or those in authority" (Corney's translation of *hombres mayores*).** (Aguera, p. 62)
    - **Assessment:** See Jakubowska's correction below. The original Spanish *hombres mayores* means "elderly men" or "older men," not "principal men" or "those in authority." Corney's translation imposes a political-authority reading on what may be a simple age description. This is one of the most consequential translation errors for interpreting political organization.

4. **"The principal men, as well as the women, are extremely addicted to beg, and take with gladness whatever comes to their hands, without making any return."** (Aguera/Corney, p. 62; corresponding to p. 117 in Jakubowska)
    - **Assessment:** Jakubowska demonstrates that "principal men" is Corney's rendering of *hombres mayores* ("elderly men"), and "without making any return" mistranslates *sin reparo alguno* ("without any scruple" or "without hesitation"). The original says elderly men and women eagerly accept whatever comes to hand without scruple; Corney transforms this into a statement about "principal men" who take without reciprocating, which has very different implications for understanding exchange and political hierarchy.

5. **Corney's rendering of group behavior on shore.** Jakubowska (p. 115) notes that Aguera's description of islanders observing ships from the shore in "separate groups" (*en Ranchos separados*) is changed by Corney to a scene of natives "in loose cloaks," distorting a description of social organization (separate groups) into a description of dress.
    - **Assessment:** This is significant because the original Spanish describes islanders organized in "separate groups" (*ranchos*), which could indicate territorial or kin-based groupings. Corney's mistranslation eliminates this observation of social organization from the English-language record.

6. **"Crown" for *canasto* (basket).** Corney translates the cylindrical stone piece (*pukao*) atop moai as a "crown" (Aguera, p. 58), where the original Spanish uses *canasto* (basket). Jakubowska (p. 116) notes this imposes a royal/political symbol on a cultural artifact.
    - **Assessment:** The translation of *canasto* (basket) as "crown" subtly imports European political symbolism into the description of moai. While this does not directly assert political hierarchy, it contributes to a frame in which monumental architecture is read through European categories of royalty and political authority.

7. **Formal annexation procedure imposes sovereignty framework.** The entire annexation ceremony (Aguera, pp. 66-68; Herve, pp. 90-91) is structured by Spanish colonial legal requirements that presuppose the existence of identifiable political authorities who can cede sovereignty. The Spanish needed to identify "chiefs" to legitimate the act. The ceremony was conducted with military precision: 250 armed troops, crosses, litanies, musketry volleys, 21-gun salute from ships.
    - **Assessment:** The formal legal framework required the Spanish to find or create political interlocutors. The "natives present" who "signed" the document were treated as political representatives, but the text provides no evidence that the islanders understood this, that the signatories had any political authority, or that they were recognized by other islanders as representatives. The Spanish legal and political needs, not the observed reality, structured the identification of "chiefs."

### Significant Absences

1. **No individual described as giving orders to other islanders.** Throughout both the Aguera and Herve accounts, no single islander is described directing, commanding, or organizing other islanders. All group behavior is described as collective: the islanders collectively carry crosses, collectively board ships, collectively offer food, collectively sing and dance.

2. **No individual identified as mediating contact.** Unlike some other Pacific contact narratives, no individual islander steps forward as a mediator, spokesperson, or point of contact for the group. The Spanish interact with the islanders as a mass. When the Spanish need to manage crowds, they do so through their own officers' orders.

3. **No tribute, redistribution, or centralized exchange described.** No passage in either account describes goods being collected by or distributed through a central authority figure. Exchange is described as direct, individual, and reciprocal (or unreciprocal in both directions: islanders take things without return, and Spanish give things without expecting return).

4. **No description of anyone directing labor.** Despite observations of monumental architecture (moai, ahu, stone platforms), neither account describes any individual organizing or directing construction or maintenance work. Aguera wonders at how the statues were erected (pp. 57-58) but does not describe or speculate about the labor organization that built them.

5. **No hereditary succession, lineage, or dynastic information.** Neither account contains any information about how leadership is transmitted, whether any positions are inherited, or any genealogical information about the islanders.

6. **No differentiated residence pattern suggesting a chiefly compound or palace.** Aguera describes three types of dwelling (caves, reed huts, stone dwellings near statues; p. 65-66), but none is described as a chiefly residence. The largest house described by Herve (27 paces long; p. 89) is not attributed to any particular individual or authority figure.

7. **No feasting or large-scale food preparation/distribution described.** Despite the several-day stay, no feast or organized food distribution is described. Food exchange is always small-scale and direct.

8. **No war leaders, warriors, or military organization described.** "We never saw their bravery put to the test, but I suspect they are faint-hearted; they possess no arms" (Aguera, p. 63). No warrior class, military force, or organized defense is observed.

9. **No description of who selected the annexation treaty signatories.** The most glaring absence. The document was signed, but neither Aguera nor Herve describes how the native signatories were chosen: whether the Spanish selected them, whether they stepped forward, whether other islanders deferred to them, or whether they had any recognized authority.

10. **No sacred or tapu restrictions observed being enforced.** While the islanders "appear to hold [the statues] in great veneration, and are displeased when we approach to examine them closely" (Aguera, p. 59), no enforcement of sacred restrictions by any authority figure is described. The displeasure is collective.

11. **No council or deliberative body observed.** No gathering of leaders, council meeting, or formal deliberation among islanders is described at any point during the 6-day stay.

### Speculative / Uncertain Claims

1. **Population scarcity of women.** "of these very few indeed are women, -- I do not believe they amount to seventy" (Aguera, p. 60). Herve similarly notes few women. Both acknowledge this as remarkable. Aguera speculates about communal marriage as a result.
    - **Assessment:** The observed sex imbalance is presented as genuine observation, but both authors acknowledge uncertainty about the explanation. The women may have been deliberately kept away from the Spanish, as Aguera partially recognizes ("on the afternoon when we came ashore, when passing near to a small hut, we saw some eight women or so all youthful and not bad looking, accompanied by an old man who only allowed them to expose their heads to look at us," p. 61).

2. **Aguera's speculation about ease of conversion.** "I believe, from their docility and intelligence, that it would be easy to domesticate them and to convert them to any religion which might be put before them." (Aguera, p. 60)
    - **Assessment:** Interpretive projection reflecting colonial assumptions about indigenous "docility." This characterization of the islanders as docile and easily converted contradicts any image of a powerful, organized chiefdom.

3. **Islanders repeat Spanish words.** "The islanders responded with our own people; they pronounce with such ease that they repeat whatever is said to them just like ourselves." (Herve, p. 91)
    - **Assessment:** Herve observes linguistic mimicry during the annexation ceremony (*Viva el Rey*, *ora pro nobis*). He attributes this to "pronouncing with ease." This is significant because the islanders' repetition of Spanish formulas during the annexation was presumably taken as assent or participation, when it may have been imitation without comprehension.

4. **Herve's inference about cloth material.** "I fancy that the cloaks or wraps of the said islanders are made from the fibres of stems of the banana plant, which, when dry, they put together as may suit their purpose: it is not woven, but is joined together by strands of the same material which they thread on bone needles the size of a cloak-maker's needle." (Herve, p. 92)
    - **Assessment:** Corney's footnote corrects this: the material was really bark cloth (*Broussonetia papyrifera*), not banana fiber. Herve's identification is wrong on the material, though his observation of the sewing technique may be accurate.

### Second-Pass Additions (2026-03-13)

**SP-1. "Ranchos separados" (separate groups): recoverable social-organizational data lost in translation.**

Aguera's original Spanish describes islanders on the shore organized "in separate groups" (*en Ranchos separados*). Corney mistranslated this as "in loose cloaks," eliminating a direct observation of social grouping patterns from the English-language record (Jakubowska 2014, p. 115). The original indicates that islanders were organized into distinct groups on the shore, possibly territorial, kin-based, or age-based. This observation, recoverable only through Jakubowska's correction, provides rare direct evidence of social organization at the group level. It deserves emphasis as one of the few observations in any contact account that describes how islanders organized themselves when not interacting with Europeans.

**Assessment**: While Claim "European Categories Imposed #5" notes the mistranslation, the analytical significance of the *original* observation has not been drawn out. The "separate groups" pattern could indicate territorial/kin-based groupings consistent with segmentary organization.

**SP-2. Aguera's dwelling hierarchy is tentative in his own text: speculation, not observation.**

Aguera describes stone-dwellers near statues as those he "believes to be their ministers" ("whom I believe to be their ministers," Aguera, pp. 65-66). The top tier of his three-tier dwelling hierarchy is explicitly acknowledged as his inference, not an observation. He hedges with "I believe" (*creo*). Stone dwellings near statues could indicate a caretaker function (maintaining the ahu/moai complex), a lineage association (descent groups associated with particular platforms), or ritual responsibility, rather than political rank. The spatial association of dwellings with monuments does not by itself demonstrate political hierarchy.

**Assessment**: Claim "Direct Observations #4" records Aguera's hedging but does not emphasize how heavily the three-tier interpretation depends on Aguera's inference about the top tier. The bottom two tiers (caves for most, reed huts for older/more powerful persons) describe age-grading; only the third tier introduces a potentially political category, and that tier rests on Aguera's explicit speculation.

**SP-3. Women sequestration as kinship protection, not chiefly control.**

Aguera notes: "on the afternoon when we came ashore, when passing near to a small hut, we saw some eight women or so all youthful and not bad looking, accompanied by an old man who only allowed them to expose their heads to look at us" (Aguera, p. 61). This passage is often read as evidence of chiefly control over women. However, the specific behavioral pattern described (an old man protecting young women from foreign men) is more consistent with elder kinship protection than with chiefly command. The man exercises authority specifically in the context of foreign men approaching young women, a protective function that exists in egalitarian kinship systems. No broader political authority is attributed to this individual; his "control" is exercised in a single, specific protective context.

**Assessment**: This observation should be distinguished from evidence of political hierarchy. The protective behavior of an older male kinsman toward young women in the presence of foreign sailors is culturally expected across a wide range of social organization types and does not require chiefly authority to explain.

**SP-4. No islander is named in either Dutch or Spanish accounts across 48 years of contact.**

Across the Roggeveen (1722) and González (1770) expeditions, spanning 48 years and involving hundreds of islanders interacting with Europeans, not a single islander is identified by name. In Hawaiian, Tongan, and Samoan contact accounts, chiefs are typically named and sometimes genealogies are recorded. The complete anonymity of Rapa Nui islanders in these accounts could indicate: (a) language barriers prevented name exchange, (b) islanders did not present individuals as persons of political importance whose names should be recorded, or (c) Europeans did not ask or did not record names. Explanation (b) is consistent with the absence of a political system that made particular individuals noteworthy to outsiders. The first named individual in the contact record is Ko Toheetai, identified during the Cook 1774 expedition through Mahine's partial linguistic competence.

**Assessment**: The absence of named individuals across two major expeditions is a negative finding that gains significance when contrasted with the Polynesian chiefdom pattern. It was Cook's partial translator, not improved European methods, that finally produced a name.

**SP-5. The vocabulary list method is unreliable for political concepts.**

The term "tequeteque" mapped to "High chief or lord" was elicited through "signs, demonstrations, and figures shown by drawings" (Aguera, p. 73). This method is adequate for concrete nouns (body parts, food items, animals) but methodologically incapable of conveying abstract political concepts. You cannot reliably communicate "chief" through drawings to speakers of an unknown language. The Rapanui word could denote an elder, a ritual specialist, a respected person, a lineage head, or indeed a political leader. The Spanish mapping tells us what the Spanish were looking for (a "High chief or lord"), not what the Rapanui were describing. This vocabulary entry has been treated in the literature as evidence that Rapanui had a political office of "high chief," but the elicitation method cannot support that inference.

**Assessment**: Claim "Direct Observations #15" notes the methodological weakness but does not fully draw out the epistemological problem. The entry tells us about Spanish expectations, not about Rapanui political structure.

**SP-6. Annexation signatories are never described: the most consequential evidentiary gap.**

Neither Aguera nor Herve identifies who signed the annexation document, how they were selected, what was explained to them, or whether other islanders recognized them as representatives. Aguera says only that "some of the natives present signed or attested the official document by marking upon it certain characters in their own form of script" (p. 68). The label "SIGNATURES OF CHIEFS OF EASTER ISLAND, TO THE SPANISH TREATY, 1770" appears on the plate (p. 75) and is a Corney/Hakluyt Society editorial imposition; it does not appear in the Spanish body text. The signatories could have been individuals who happened to be standing nearby, elders who were curious about the ceremony, people who volunteered, or indeed political leaders. The accounts provide no basis for determining which. The legal fiction that these were "chiefs" signing a treaty of cession was a requirement of Spanish colonial law, not a description of observed indigenous authority.

**Assessment**: Claim "Direct Observations #7" and "European Categories Imposed #1" cover this ground, but the analytical point deserves sharpening: the absence of any description of the signatories' identity or selection is the single most consequential evidentiary gap in the González accounts, because it is this gap that the editorial label "SIGNATURES OF CHIEFS" fills with an unsupported political claim.

## Cross-Reference Notes

### Discrepancies Between the Aguera and Herve Accounts

1. **Both accounts give very similar population estimates** (~900-1,000) and note the apparent scarcity of women, suggesting these may derive from a shared expedition estimate rather than independent observations.

2. **Herve's account of the annexation (p. 91) is briefer than Aguera's (pp. 66-68)** and provides fewer details about who signed the document. Herve simply states the crosses were planted and "the party fired three volleys of musketry, and the ships replied with twenty-one guns each." He does not mention the native signatures at all.

3. **Herve was on the circumnavigation party** (the launch that sailed around the island) and therefore had more extensive contact with islanders along the coast. Aguera was on the frigate and participated in the annexation day. Their observations come from different vantage points.

### Jakubowska's Findings on Corney's Translations (2014)

Jakubowska's article systematically demonstrates that Corney's English translations contain numerous errors, mistranslations, and editorial insertions that distort the original Spanish texts. Those most relevant to political authority and social organization include:

1. **"Hombres mayores" mistranslated as "principal men."** (Jakubowska, p. 117) The Spanish *hombres mayores* simply means "elderly men" or "older men." *Mayor* is a polysemous word, but in this context means "elder." Corney translates it as "principal men," importing political authority into an age description. This is one of the most consequential errors for the chiefdom question because it transforms an observation about age-differentiated behavior into evidence for political hierarchy.

2. **"Sin reparo alguno" mistranslated as "without making any return."** (Jakubowska, p. 117) The original Spanish says the elderly men and women accept things "without any scruple" or "without hesitation" (*sin reparo alguno*). Corney translates this as "without making any return," which changes the meaning from a comment about uninhibited acquisition to a statement about non-reciprocal exchange. The original says nothing about return or reciprocity.

3. **"En Ranchos separados" mistranslated as "in loose cloaks."** (Jakubowska, p. 115) Aguera describes islanders on the shore organized "in separate groups" (*en Ranchos separados*), with the remainder staying on the shore "shouting with delight" (*gritando con mucha alegria*). Corney renders this as natives "remained on the sea beach, in loose cloaks, shouting with delight." The social-organizational observation (separate groups) is entirely lost.

4. **"Canasto" (basket) translated as "crown."** (Jakubowska, p. 116) The red stone cylinder atop some moai (*pukao*) is described in the original as a *canasto* (basket), which Corney changes to "crown," importing royal symbolism.

5. **"Cuatro pequenas piedras" (four small stones) inflated to "so many small stones."** (Jakubowska, p. 116) The original describes the moai base as resting on "four small stones"; Corney changes this to "so many small stones," altering the engineering observation.

6. **"Loxuria" (lust) softened to "enjoyment."** (Jakubowska, p. 116) The portable idol associated with *Loxuria* (lust/sexuality) is described by Corney as dedicated to "enjoyment," sanitizing the original.

7. **"Picaza" (pick-axe or stone tool) translated as "hoe."** (Jakubowska, p. 116) Corney translates *picaza* as "hoe," which Jakubowska argues should be "pick-axe" or a similar stone tool, not an agricultural implement.

8. **"Chacara" (cultivated field) translated as "gravel."** (Jakubowska, pp. 111-112) The original Spanish *chacara* (from Quechua, meaning a small farm or cultivated field) is translated by Corney as "gravel." The original passage about the reconnaissance group at the cove of San Juan says "we did not disclose our presence there" (*no nos detubimos en ella*), but Corney transforms this into a statement about finding gravel.

9. **General pattern of translation errors inflating political/cultural significance.** Jakubowska's overall finding is that Corney "had no understanding of some of the described phenomena" (p. 110), frequently mistranslated Spanish terms, and imposed interpretations that range from slightly inaccurate to fundamentally misleading. For the chiefdom question, the consistent pattern is that age-related and descriptive terms are translated into political-authority terms, and descriptive observations are given interpretive glosses that import European political categories.

10. **"Platanos guineos" (a variety of small bananas) mistranslated as "Chili peppers."** (Jakubowska, p. 112) This error had broader historiographic consequences, as the supposed presence of chili peppers on Easter Island was used by Heyerdahl followers as evidence for South American contact (Langdon 1988: 325-326).

## Summary Assessment

### What the Gonzalez 1770 Sources Can Tell Us

1. **Basic demographic facts:** Population roughly 900-1,000, apparently few women visible (possibly because women were kept out of contact with Europeans).

2. **Material culture observations:** Dwelling types (caves, reed huts, stone structures near statues), body paint practices differentiated by age, stone tools, bark cloth garments, small outrigger canoes, cultivation of sweet potatoes, yams, plantains, and sugar cane.

3. **Age-graded social differentiation:** Older men had more elaborate body paint, appeared to have precedence in sexual/marital matters, and may have inhabited better-quality dwellings. This is consistent with age-grading and achieved status but does not specifically indicate hereditary chiefdom.

4. **Exchange was direct and decentralized:** No evidence of redistribution, tribute, or centralized control over exchange. Islanders exchanged food for European goods in small groups without apparent mediation.

5. **Collective behavior during novel events:** Islanders responded to the Spanish collectively. They carried crosses, boarded ships, offered food, and "signed" the annexation document without any individual being described as organizing or directing these activities.

6. **Possible religious specialists:** The "Maca Maca" exclamation and dwellings near statues may indicate religious specialists, though this is Aguera's inference.

7. **A Rapanui term mapped to "High chief or lord":** The vocabulary list includes *tequeteque* for "High chief or lord," but the elicitation method (signs and drawings) makes the mapping unreliable for the specific political content of the term.

### What the Gonzalez 1770 Sources Cannot Tell Us

1. **Whether any political hierarchy existed beyond age-grading.** The accounts describe age-based social differentiation but no political command structure, no tribute, no redistribution, no directed labor.

2. **Who the annexation signatories were or what authority they held.** The most politically significant act of the entire visit, the "signing" of the annexation document, is described without any explanation of who these individuals were, how they were selected, or what authority they represented. The label "Chiefs" is imposed by the English-language plate caption, not by the original accounts.

3. **Whether the observed social organization was "traditional" or a contact-period artifact.** The Spanish arrived 48 years after Roggeveen. The islanders' behavior toward Europeans may have been shaped by knowledge of the earlier visit.

4. **The nature of the social groups observed.** Aguera's original description of islanders in "separate groups" (*en Ranchos separados*), obscured by Corney's mistranslation, might have indicated territorial, kin-based, or other social groupings, but insufficient detail survives.

### Key Finding for the Chiefdom Question

The Gonzalez 1770 expedition provides **no direct evidence of chiefly authority** in the sense required by chiefdom models: no individual is described commanding others, directing labor, controlling exchange, redistributing goods, enforcing tapu, leading warriors, or exercising any form of coercive or institutionalized authority. The evidence for social differentiation is limited to age-grading (older men had more elaborate body paint and housing, and precedence in marital matters) and possible religious specialization.

The single most consequential feature of this source for the chiefdom narrative is the annexation ceremony, which required the Spanish to identify "chiefs" for legal purposes. The label "SIGNATURES OF CHIEFS" on the treaty plate has entered the literature as evidence for chieftainship, but the primary accounts themselves do not describe the signatories as chiefs or explain how they were selected. The Spanish legal framework, not observed indigenous political reality, generated the identification of "chiefs."

Jakubowska's analysis reveals that Corney's translation systematically inflates political-authority language, most critically translating *hombres mayores* ("elderly men") as "principal men" or "those in authority." Scholars relying on Corney's English translation rather than the Spanish originals have been working with a text that already imposes European political categories more strongly than the original accounts warrant.
