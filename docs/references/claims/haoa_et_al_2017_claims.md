# Claims Extraction: Haoa Cardinali et al. 2017 (eds.)

**Source**: Haoa Cardinali, Sonia, Kathleen B. Ingersoll, Daniel W. Ingersoll, Jr., and Christopher M. Stevenson, eds. 2017 [2018]. *Cultural and Environmental Change on Rapa Nui*. Routledge Studies in Archaeology 30. London: Routledge.

**Type**: Edited volume (11 chapters plus introduction and reflections)
**Accessed**: PDF (`haoa_et_al_2017.pdf`)
**Extracted**: 2026-03-16

**Note**: This is the entire edited volume. Claims are organized by chapter. Page numbers correspond to the PDF's printed page numbers.

## Chapter 1: Stevenson, Ladefoged, and Chadwick -- "Prehistoric settlement, ancient gardens and population dynamics on the Hiva Hiva lava flow, Rapa Nui, Chile" (pp. 9-29)

### Settlement patterns and land use

1. The Hiva Hiva lava flow study area, on the south coast of Rapa Nui, contains dense evidence of habitation, agricultural, and ceremonial features. Obsidian hydration dating was used to construct a settlement chronology. (pp. 9-11)

2. "The earliest occupation of the Hiva Hiva lava flow dates to the 13th century AD" based on obsidian hydration dating of artifacts from house sites. (p. 19)

3. Rock gardens at Hiva Hiva were located in "inland areas approximately 1-2 km from the coast" and show evidence of intensified food production, including planting of yams, taro, and sweet potato. (pp. 10-12, 15-19)

4. Soil nutrient analysis of exchangeable calcium in garden soils provides evidence of repeated enrichment through organic amendments, indicating sustained, intensive agricultural management over centuries. (pp. 19-24)

5. The authors use obsidian hydration to argue that population increased steadily from initial settlement through the 16th-17th centuries, and garden intensification expanded in parallel. (pp. 24-27)

**Assessment**: Stevenson is one of the principal proponents of the chiefdom-integration/collapse model. However, this chapter focuses on settlement and agriculture without explicitly framing the data in chiefdom terms. The data on sustained garden intensification are consistent with both chiefly-directed and collective-action interpretations of agricultural management.

### Population dynamics

6. Population analysis based on obsidian hydration site counts shows "a gradual increase in population" from initial settlement through the pre-contact period, without evidence of a sharp decline before European contact. (pp. 25-27)

**Assessment**: This finding from Stevenson's own field area is notably consistent with the continuity argument advanced by Mulrooney (2013), even though Stevenson elsewhere supports the collapse model. It complicates his own earlier framing of "Warfare and Fragmentation" after AD 1680.

## Chapter 5: Wozniak -- "Subsistence strategies on Rapa Nui (Easter Island): prehistoric gardening practices on Rapa Nui and how they relate to current farming practices" (pp. 87-108)

### Agricultural practices

7. Wozniak documents the extensive rock garden (manavai) and lithic mulch systems used by prehistoric Rapanui, noting that "virtually every stone you see has been mined, shaped, and placed by human agency" in describing the transformed landscape. (pp. 91-93, 100-102)

8. The manavai (enclosed rock gardens) served as windbreaks and moisture-retention structures. The paper documents continuity between prehistoric and modern farming practices, with some techniques still in use. (pp. 97-98, 102-103)

9. Taro cultivation required permanent water or very moist conditions, and the rock garden systems were engineered to provide these conditions in a landscape without permanent surface water. (pp. 98-102)

**Assessment**: The sophistication and extent of garden engineering documented here is consistent with sustained, community-level investment in food production. The paper does not address whether this investment was directed by chiefs or organized through other mechanisms. The continuity of techniques into the present complicates claims about a pre-contact breakdown of the agricultural system.

## Chapter 6: Vogt and Kuhlem -- "By the Quebrada of Ava Ranga Uka A Toroke Hau -- about landscape transformation and the significance of water and trees" (pp. 113-132)

### Water management and ritual landscape

10. The site of Ava Ranga Uka A Toroke Hau, located on the only permanent or semi-permanent streambed on the island (Quebrada Vaipú), contained "monumental architecture, hydraulic installations, substantial cultural deposits, ahu and funerary constructions [that] formed a complex multi-facetted ensemble which is without parallel among ritual sites on Easter Island." (p. 130)

11. Two dam-like earthworks (R1 and R2), three stone-lined canals, stone pavements, palm tree planting pits, and a megalithic stone basin (5 x 2.75 m, depth 1.5 m) demonstrate sophisticated water management that "included a strong ritual component." (pp. 114-118, 122-124)

12. The elaborately built stone basin T1 "was made from large, neatly dressed and pecked basalt paenga, of which one was clearly re-used from a boat-shaped house foundation." Its fill contained "some 220 nutshell fragments of the local palm tree (Jubaea sp.)" as well as ritual deposits including obsidian and basalt pieces, beach pebbles (poro), and wooden tools (awls). (pp. 122-124)

13. AMS dates from the basin place it in the 16th/early 17th century (Table 6.1, p. 124). "While most of the island had already been deforested at that time (Mieth and Bork 2004), a palm tree plantation was still present at Ava Ranga Uka A Toroke Hau itself as proven by the archaeological evidence." (p. 124)

14. Pavements at the site "occur only in front of ahu or the boat-shaped elite houses," suggesting high-status associations. (p. 120)

15. "Its restricted access and use can be best explained with Ava Ranga Uka A Toroke Hau as the central site of a water cult under the control of a Rapanui elite -- perhaps for Hiro, the Polynesian god of rain and fertility." (p. 130)

**Assessment**: This is one of the more directly relevant passages for the chiefdom question. Vogt and Kuhlem interpret the ritual landscape as controlled by an "elite," drawing on Metraux (1940: 330) for the ethnographic parallel of kings calling on priests during drought. However, the evidence itself (monumental construction, ritual deposits, water management) does not require elite control; collective management of a scarce, ritually significant resource is equally consistent with the archaeological signatures. The invocation of Metraux is a post-slave-raid source [POST-RAID].

16. A small ahu (Ahu Hanua Nua Mea) at the site was "built basically as an earthwork" using "only a very few random and undressed stones" for construction. Its single moai pedestal and platform were "made from gravel and earth" with beach pebbles on the surface. (p. 125)

17. In front of the ahu platform, "a small circular pit contained an irregularly shaped slab bearing toki marks which was inserted in an upright position. This configuration strongly recalls similar installations for example in front of the marae on the Society Islands where such slabs have been interpreted as seats of chiefs or as back-rests." (p. 126)

**Assessment**: The Polynesian analogy here (Society Islands marae = Rapa Nui ahu feature) is precisely the kind of comparative inference our paper examines. The slab's function is interpreted through reference to what similar features mean elsewhere in Polynesia, not from any independent Rapa Nui evidence.

### Metraux ethnographic parallel

18. Vogt and Kuhlem cite Metraux (1940: 330): "in times of drought when the crops were threatened the Rapanui asked the king for help who then called on his priest (ariki paka) to address prayers to the god Hiro. The king took the role as an intermediary between the people and the priest." They then speculate: "Could this be a possible scenario that took place on the quebrada of Ava Ranga Uka A Toroke Hau during periods of drought as part of a presumed fertility cult?" (p. 130)

**Assessment [POST-RAID]**: This passage exemplifies the circularity our paper identifies. Metraux's 1940 account of the ariki mau's role in drought rituals was recorded after the demographic collapse. It is then projected onto a prehistoric archaeological site to interpret features as evidence for chiefly control, which in turn appears to "confirm" the chiefdom model. The question is explicitly speculative ("Could this be...?"), but the framing assumes the chiefdom structure.

## Chapter 7: Lee, Bahn, Horley, Haoa Cardinali, Gonzalez Nualart, and Cuadros Hucke -- "Re-use of the sacred: Late period petroglyphs applied to red scoria topknots from Easter Island (Rapa Nui)" (pp. 133-155)

### Pukao, moai, and social organization

19. "The carving of the monolithic statues and construction of the ceremonial platforms were most likely established, financed, and supervised by the ruling elite -- either the chiefs of individual tribes or a hereditary king 'ariki mau. These monuments were considered to contain the mana of venerated ancestors and thus were tapu." (p. 134)

**Assessment**: This is stated as established fact without citation or qualification. It represents the standard chiefdom interpretation being applied uncritically as a premise for the petroglyph analysis. Our paper would note that this claim is precisely what needs to be demonstrated, not assumed.

20. "The topknots, so highly prized for their red color, were added only to selected ceremonial sites, many of which already manifested their special status through elaborate architecture and superb masonry techniques." The sites listed include Ahu Nau Nau, two ahu at Vinapu, Ahu Hanga Poukura, Ahu Hanga Te'e, Ahu Akahanga, Ahu Te Pito Kura, Ahu Ura Uranga te Mahina, Ahu One Makihi, Ahu Tongariki, Ahu Heki'i, and Ahu Tepeu. (pp. 134-135)

21. The secondary petroglyphs on topknots (predominantly stylized boat carvings) were applied after the statues were toppled, during the huri moai phase. "With the statue toppled and its topknot rolled away, this mana was considerably weakened. Carving motifs into the once-sacred topknots of the chiefs can be considered as further sacrilege." (p. 148)

22. The predominance of boat motifs on topknots is interpreted in the context of the transition from the "classical ahu moai phase to the huri moai phase." The authors suggest these secondary carvings reflect "cultural changes that took place in the society during the transition" including "weakening of hereditary monarchy, development of birdman cult, and contact with European civilization." (p. 133)

23. The "canoe symbol marked a victory or conquest over another section of the island" (citing Lee 1992: 106, 126). (p. 148)

**Assessment**: The chapter treats the transition from the ahu moai phase to the huri moai phase as reflecting a shift from hereditary chiefly authority to the birdman cult, a standard element of the chiefdom-collapse narrative. The boat carvings on toppled pukao are interpreted as acts of deliberate desecration of chiefly symbols. However, the interpretation of boat carvings as conquest markers or sacrilege depends on the prior assumption of chiefly ownership of the monuments.

24. The authors quote Metraux (1940: 131) on the tapu of the king's head and its connection to pukao symbolism: "the head of the king was the most sacred part of his person -- so sacred that he was obliged to let his hair grow without ever allowing the mataa (obsidian knife) to pass through it." (pp. 147-148)

**Assessment [POST-RAID]**: This passage from Metraux about the sacredness of the king's head is used to extend the interpretation of pukao as symbols of chiefly mana. The ethnographic source is post-slave-raid.

25. The major concentrations of boat petroglyphs on topknots "occur in the places visited by the early expeditions (the ceremonial complex of Vinapu visited by the Cook and La Perouse expeditions) or located in direct view of the anchored ships (the 1770 Gonzalez expedition anchored in Hanga Ho'onu in view of Ahu Heki'i; the 1774 Cook and 1786 La Perouse expeditions anchored at Hanga Roa, in view of Puna Pau)." (p. 152)

**Assessment**: This spatial correlation between boat carvings and European anchorages is significant. It suggests the boat motifs may represent European ships rather than (or in addition to) traditional Polynesian canoes, potentially dating the secondary carvings to the contact/post-contact period rather than a pre-contact "huri moai" phase.

## Chapter 8: Boersema -- "An earthly paradise? Easter Island (Rapa Nui) as seen by the eighteenth-century European explorers" (pp. 156-178)

### Contact-era observations: warfare and weapons

26. "The absence of weapons is firmly attested in the first accounts of the 18th century, with that in the journal of Bouman being the clearest." Bouman and Roggeveen in 1722 "saw neither weapons nor trees." (pp. 161, 160)

27. "The Spanish expedition [1770] also test the acquaintance of Easter Islanders with weapons by giving them a bow and arrows, but they showed absolutely no knowledge of what to do with them; in fact, they hung the bow around their necks like an ornament." (p. 162)

28. The Cook expedition in 1774 is the first to "report explicitly for the first time the presence of weapons," though these are described as "thin ill-shapen sticks" and "short wooden clubs." Georg Forster noted "a fighting club, made of a thick piece of wood about three feet long, carved at one extremity." (p. 162)

29. La Perouse in 1786: "These natives were unarmed; 3 or 4 at the most, in such a crowd, had a kind of club made of wood and hardly dangerous." (p. 163)

30. "All in all, this information from the 18th century accounts offers no support for the descriptions from the collapse literature. There is no question of warfare and strife, or of a starving and desperate population, let alone of cannibalism because of a lack of food." (p. 164)

**Assessment**: Boersema's systematic review of all four 18th-century expeditions' accounts of weapons and warfare directly undermines the narrative of pre-contact societal collapse driven by inter-group warfare. The near-absence of weapons in 1722 and 1770, and the modest weapons described in 1774 and 1786, are inconsistent with a society that had undergone violent conflict and chiefly-order breakdown.

### Contact-era observations: health and food

31. "The visitors are unanimously positive to very positive about the appearance and health of the Easter Islanders. They describe the inhabitants as being of attractive build, quite tall, powerful persons with sufficient flesh on their bones and excellent swimmers." (p. 163)

32. The Spanish (1770): "'No halt, maimed, bent, crooked, luxated, deformed, or bow-legged among them, their appearance being thoroughly pleasing.'" (p. 164)

33. Cook (1774): "'in general they are a very Slender race but very Nimble and Active, well featured with agreeable countenances.'" (p. 163)

34. Roggeveen noted food abundance: "the island is very fruitful, and could be made an Earthly Paradise if the inhabitants were to do something about it." Bouman described "little square garden's look cared for and well set out." (pp. 167-168)

35. La Perouse (1786): "'Hardly a tenth of their land is under cultivation, and I am sure that 3 days of labour are enough to provide a year's subsistence for each of them.'" (p. 168)

36. The island's agricultural productivity "was always great enough. Calculations indicate that even an (unlikely) population size of ten thousand inhabitants could have been well fed." (p. 169)

### Contact-era observations: population

37. Boersema compiles population estimates from all 18th-century visitors (Table 8.1, p. 166). Key estimates: Gonzalez 1770: "About 3,000 of both sexes" (Dalrymple); "900-1,000" (Aguera); Cook 1774: "600-700 in total"; La Perouse 1786: "Four or five hundred on the shore" but "1,200 gathered around the bay" and "2,000 in total."

38. A sex-ratio disparity was noted by all 18th-century visitors: "the European visitors saw few women on the island." (p. 165)

### Discussion and conclusion

39. "How can the collapse theory be understood as supported by the European visitors' journals? My impression is that the English-language collapse authors predominantly based their arguments on the accounts from the journey of James Cook." Cook compared Easter Island unfavorably with other Pacific islands he had visited. (p. 169)

40. "The theory claiming that deforestation on Rapa Nui caused hunger, armed conflict and collapse is clearly untenable. The conclusion of the rereading of the 18th-century travel accounts must be that they do not provide any support for the collapse theory as Ponting, Diamond and many others would have us believe." (p. 170)

**Assessment**: Boersema's chapter is highly relevant to our argument. His systematic review demonstrates that the contact-era accounts do not describe a collapsed society, a starving population, or widespread warfare. This undermines the first step of the chiefdom-collapse narrative (that European observers found a society in the aftermath of chiefly-order breakdown). However, Boersema does not specifically address whether the accounts describe evidence for or against hierarchical political organization; his focus is on the collapse question.

## Chapter 9: Richards -- "The impact of the whalers and other foreign visitors before 1862" (pp. 179-187)

### Pre-slave-raid contact

41. "Some 79 visits can currently be identified that involved contact with the Easter Island people ashore before the slave raids in 1862. Seven were by explorers (from Roggeveen in 1722 to du Petit-Thouars in 1838), three by fur traders en route to the North West Coast, four by men-of-war and seven by trans-Pacific traders. All the remaining 55 visits were by whaleships -- four British whalers before 1823, and 51 American whaleships between 1822 and 1862." (p. 179)

42. "The practice of discouraging foreigners from landing on shore was a conscious decision by 'the King' as early as 1805." In that year, "King Crang-a-low" controlled shore access after a violent encounter with the sealing ship Nancy. (pp. 181-182)

**Assessment**: The reference to a "King" controlling access to the island in 1805 (from the Sydney Gazette, 1812) is noteworthy. It provides early 19th-century evidence for a recognized authority figure, though filtered through European political categories ("King"). The term is the European observers', not a Rapanui self-designation.

43. A whaler account from 1823 (Smith 1844: 168) describes Rapa Nui people: "The natives are of a light colour; tall and handsome. Their chiefs are handsomely tattooed on their faces, necks, lips, tongues and arms." (p. 183)

**Assessment**: This is one of the few direct references to "chiefs" (plural) from the early 19th-century whaler accounts. The description of distinctive facial tattoos on chiefs is consistent with differentiated social status, though it does not specify the nature or extent of chiefly authority.

44. "The local people ashore were not so impoverished, nor so hungry, that they would not barter valuable food, often playfully and in quite large quantities, for the 'curiosities' offered by the visiting foreigners." (p. 183)

45. "Ship journals through the first half of the 19th century indicate healthy Rapanui populations; the volumes of produce bartered with the ships suggest that the economy of this small island was vigorous enough to produce a substantial surplus to barter with ships." (p. 192)

46. "As late as 1862 Easter Island still had had no resident foreign 'beach combers,' and no other foreigners living ashore." (p. 185)

**Assessment**: The isolation from sustained foreign contact until 1862 means the accounts from the whaling period (1805-1862) describe a society that had not yet experienced the transformative effects of the slave raids. The evidence of agricultural surplus and healthy population in this period further undermines the pre-contact collapse narrative.

## Chapter 10: Ingersoll, Ingersoll, and Bove -- "Healing a culture's reputation: Challenging the cultural labeling and libeling of the Rapanui" (pp. 188-202)

### Resilience vs. collapse

47. "Following the disappearance of the palms, increased and sustainable productivity based on ingenious technology supported a larger population. There was no ecocide." (p. 197)

48. "The Rapanui did nearly become extinct in 1877, perhaps as few as 111 out of previous thousands inhabited Rapa Nui (Metraux 1971 [1940]: 23, citing Pinart 1878). But not because they had destroyed their environment." (p. 198)

49. "A first population loss may have been diseases introduced by each successive European exploratory visit, beginning with the 18th century with Roggeveen (1722); Gonzalez (1770); Cook (1774); La Perouse (1786). Hunt and Lipo (2011) suggest a series of epidemics followed by population rebound after each." (p. 198)

50. "Bottom line: Rapa Nui ecocide is a convenient untruth promoted by environmentalists looking for an apocalyptic Lorax tale that appeals to the popular press." (p. 200)

**Assessment**: The chapter is polemical in tone but the core argument is relevant: the near-extinction of 1877 was caused by the slave raids of 1862 and subsequent disease introduction, not by pre-contact environmental or political collapse. This reinforces the point that post-slave-raid ethnographic sources describe a society devastated by external forces, not the remnants of an internally collapsed chiefdom.

## Chapter 11 (Reflections): Editors -- "Reflections" (pp. 203-208)

### Collapse vs. continuity debate

51. "Another lingering question revolves around the uncertainty of inter-chiefdom conflict in the 17th century and was there an accompanying population crash? This question has generated a substantial controversy, with the promoters and supporters of collapse and ecocide theory gaining international attention." (p. 204)

52. The editors note that "Drawing upon the oral history (Routledge 1919; Metraux 1940) and its synthesis by Mulloy (1975), Bahn and Flenley (Bahn and Flenley 1992; Bahn and Flenley 2011 Flenley and Bahn 2003) have supported the collapse model." (p. 204)

53. "Despite these statements, detailed alternate models for socio-political development have not emerged. The expression 'continuity' has been used to describe the last 150 years of Rapa Nui prehistory (Rainbird 2002), a period that includes the European post-contact period (AD 1722-1867)." (p. 205)

54. "We are in many ways still reliant upon the collapse scenario in our interpretation of the archaeological record. For example, the houses at Orongo are still seen as a 16th century development even though the radiocarbon dates are on bulk samples and untrustworthy (Mulrooney 2013, Pollard et al. 2010); subterranean rooms (ana kionga) with tunnel entrances are interpreted as 'refuge caves' based upon a single informant questioned by Metraux (1940) in the 20th century; obsidian 'spear points' (mata'a) continue (until recently) to be interpreted as weapons of war even though such evidence of their use in that context is lacking (Church 1998; Church and Rigney 1994; Church and Ellis 1996; Lipo et al. 2016)." (p. 205)

**Assessment**: This passage from the volume's editors is remarkably frank about the persistence of the collapse/chiefdom framework in archaeological interpretation despite its weak evidentiary basis. The editors specifically identify three cases where the collapse narrative has shaped interpretation of archaeological features: Orongo houses, ana kionga caves, and mata'a. The mata'a point references Church (1998), who documented obsidian tools from multiple site types and found no evidence restricting them to warfare contexts, and Lipo et al. (2016), who demonstrated through morphometric analysis that mata'a are not consistent with weapon design.

55. "Equally surprising is the fact that few explicit research designs have emerged to examine the regional prehistoric demography of Rapa Nui." (p. 205)

56. "There appears to be a great need for an intensive focus on household archaeology that can detail the past." (p. 206)

## Cross-cutting claims relevant to the chiefdom question

### Claims that support the project argument

- Boersema's systematic review (Ch. 8) demonstrates that the 18th-century accounts do not describe a collapsed, warring, or starving society, undermining a key premise of the chiefdom-collapse narrative.
- The editors' reflections (Ch. 11) explicitly acknowledge that the collapse scenario continues to shape archaeological interpretation despite weak evidence, and that ana kionga and mata'a interpretations rest on assumptions from the chiefdom-warfare framework.
- Richards (Ch. 9) documents a prosperous, food-surplus-producing society through the whaling period (1805-1862), inconsistent with pre-contact collapse.
- Stevenson's own data (Ch. 1) show population increase and agricultural intensification through the pre-contact period without evidence of sharp decline, which sits in tension with the collapse periodization he and Haoa Cardinali proposed elsewhere.
- Wozniak (Ch. 5) documents sophisticated agricultural engineering consistent with both chiefly-directed and collectively-organized interpretations.

### Claims that complicate the project argument

- Vogt and Kuhlem (Ch. 6) interpret the Ava Ranga Uka A Toroke Hau complex as elite-controlled, citing pavements found "only in front of ahu or the boat-shaped elite houses" (p. 120) and drawing on Metraux's account of the ariki mau's role in drought rituals.
- Lee et al. (Ch. 7) assume as established fact that moai and ahu construction "were most likely established, financed, and supervised by the ruling elite" (p. 134), and their entire analysis of secondary petroglyphs on pukao is framed around the transition from chiefly to birdman authority.
- The 1805 account of "King Crang-a-low" controlling shore access (Ch. 9, p. 181) and the 1823 whaler account distinguishing "chiefs" with facial tattoos (Ch. 9, p. 183) provide early 19th-century evidence for recognized authority figures and social differentiation, though filtered through European categories.
- The "inter-chiefdom conflict" framing in the editors' reflections (Ch. 11, p. 204) uses chiefdom terminology even while questioning its empirical basis.

### Methodological notes for our paper

- The volume as a whole demonstrates the split in the field between collapse/chiefdom proponents and resilience/continuity advocates, but notably, even contributors sympathetic to the collapse model (Stevenson, Ch. 1) present data that do not support it.
- Several chapters invoke Metraux (1940) as an ethnographic source for prehistoric political organization without noting the post-slave-raid context [POST-RAID]: Vogt and Kuhlem (p. 130), Lee et al. (pp. 147-148).
- The Polynesian comparative analogy is deployed by Vogt and Kuhlem (Ch. 6, p. 126) when interpreting an upright slab as a "seat of chiefs" by reference to Society Islands marae. This is an example of the analogical reasoning our paper critiques.
