# Claims Extraction: Sahlins 1957

**Source**: Marshall D. Sahlins, "Differentiation by Adaptation in Polynesian Societies." *Journal of the Polynesian Society*, Vol. 66, No. 3 (September 1957), pp. 291-300.

**PDF location**: `docs/references/pdfs/sahlins_1957/Sahlins_1957.pdf` (11 pages including JSTOR cover)

**Relation to 1954 dissertation**: Sahlins states in fn. 1 (p. 291) that this article "modifies, in places extends, and generally develops materials embodied in the latter half of my monograph, *Social Stratification in Polynesia* (1957, in press), which was originally submitted as a Ph.D. dissertation at Columbia University in 1954." The article covers only Part Two of the dissertation (form of social organization: ramage vs. truncated descent line). Part One (amount of stratification, the four-group classification, productivity measurement) is not addressed.

---

## Article Overview

The article argues that two characteristic forms of descent group, "ramage" and "truncated descent line," have differentiated by adaptation from a single proto-Polynesian source. The two types consistently associate with distinct ecological conditions: ramages with intensively exploited scattered resources, descent lines with nucleated coastal resources. Atoll societies represent further divergence from high-island prototypes, driven by low productivity, small population, and catastrophic events.

This is a condensed, revised version of the dissertation's Part Two. The article does not cover stratification ranking, productivity measurement, redistribution analysis, or the detailed treatment of individual islands that constitutes Part One. For Easter Island, this means the article contains none of the detailed political organization analysis, data gap admissions, or source-based claims found in the dissertation (pp. 99-106).

---

## Easter Island Claims

Only three mentions of Easter Island appear in the article:

### 1. Ramage classification (p. 291)

"Ramages occur in Hawaii, Tonga, Tahiti, Mangaia, Mangareva, Marquesas, New Zealand, Tikopia, Easter Island, and in other less well known islands."

Easter Island is listed as a ramage society. No qualification or discussion accompanies this listing. The ecological anomaly noted in the dissertation (pp. 355-356, 361-362) is addressed later (see claim 3 below) but not at the point of classification.

### 2. Exchange practices (p. 294)

"Evidence of comparable exchange practices may be had for Easter Island, Mangareva, Tahiti, the Marquesas and Tonga." (fn. 9 cites: Routledge 1919:218; Buck 1938:36, 166, 199; Handy 1932:69; Ellis 1853 vol. 1:138; Smith 1813:32; Handy 1923:168; Gifford 929:146, 177.)

The context is a discussion of how ramage societies facilitate distribution of regionally diversified products through gift exchange between coastal and inland members of the same ramage. The specific evidence cited for Easter Island is Routledge 1919:218. This is the only Easter Island source cited in the entire article besides Sahlins 1955.

**Note on Routledge 1919:218**: This page describes the annual first-fruits ceremony at Orongo and food distributions associated with the birdman cult, not chiefly redistribution through a ramage hierarchy. Routledge's own account of Easter Island political organization (pp. 224, 242, 264) describes "no chiefs nor any form of government" except Miru, and an ariki-mau who was "neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest."

### 3. Ecological anomaly (p. 295)

"There are three possible exceptions to the hypothesis that ramage and truncated descent line structures have differentiated by adaptation to dispersed and concentrated resource exploitation, respectively. These are: Tikopia, Uvea and Easter Island. In these three the ecological situation is not clearly one of concentrated nor scattered production, but appears intermediate. The selective pressures are minimal or indifferent. Under indifferent selective pressures, either truncated descent line or ramage organization can be expected, depending on the cultural tradition previous to occupation. This explanation would account for Easter Island ramages, the culture having been derived from ramage-organized central Polynesia." (fn. 12 cites Sahlins 1955.)

This passage retains the dissertation's acknowledgment that Easter Island is ecologically anomalous for the ramage category. The explanation (cultural heritage from central Polynesia under weak selective pressure) is identical to the dissertation (pp. 361-362). The citation to "Sahlins 1955" refers to "Esoteric Efflorescence in Easter Island," *American Anthropologist* 57:1045-1052, which is a separate Sahlins article on Easter Island not cited in the 1954 dissertation.

## What the Article Does NOT Include (Comparison with 1954 Dissertation)

The following features of the dissertation's Easter Island treatment are entirely absent from the 1957 article:

1. **Stratification groupings**: No Group I/IIa/IIb/III classification. The four-group system does not appear.
2. **Productivity measurement**: No productivity scores, no Table II, no redistributive network size calculation.
3. **Redistribution analysis**: No discussion of whether Easter Island had redistributive networks. The critical admission that there is "little direct evidence" of redistribution (dissertation p. 101) is absent.
4. **Political offices**: No ariki mau, birdman, matatoa, kio, or tribal chief discussion.
5. **Status levels**: No classification of Easter Island's number of status levels.
6. **Moai**: No mention of stone statues, communal labor, or monument transport.
7. **Data gaps**: None of the eleven explicit data gaps Sahlins documented in the dissertation appear.
8. **Detailed sources**: No Metraux 1940, Thomson 1889, or Williamson 1924 citations for Easter Island. The only Easter Island sources cited are Routledge 1919 (fn. 9) and Sahlins 1955 (fn. 12).
9. **Source critique**: No discussion of post-slave-raid source limitations.
10. **Matatoa challenge**: No discussion of the matatoa's status or Sahlins's own skepticism about the professional warrior claim.

## General Claims (Not Easter Island-Specific)

### Ramage characterization (pp. 291-292, 294)

4. A ramage is "a common descent group internally ranked by a principle of genealogical seniority" with succession by primogeniture (p. 291).

5. Ramages are "ideally suited to act as political units" because of their expandability and internal ranking (p. 292).

6. Redistribution in ramage societies is "achieved by periodic accumulation and redistribution of surpluses by a central agency, namely the head of the ramage concerned. Redistribution is organized through ramage genealogical hierarchies. Lesser chiefs collect goods from lower order ramages and pass them on to the higher chiefs of major and maximal segments" (p. 294).

7. "The net effect of the process is equalization of consumption among kinsmen" (p. 294).

### Truncated descent line characterization (pp. 292-293, 294-295)

8. A truncated descent line "differs from a ramage by absence of internal ranking according to genealogical principles" (p. 292). Succession is by leadership qualifications, not primogeniture.

9. Truncated descent lines are associated with "an ecology wherein resource zones are not widely separated, but are clustered such that all domestic groups can engage in the entire range of production activities" (p. 294).

10. Redistribution occurs in descent line societies but "differs in character and organization from ramage redistribution. Often the accumulation is undertaken mainly by a single descent line" (p. 295). This is competitive, not hierarchical.

### Ecological adaptation argument (pp. 293-294)

11. "Ramages and truncated descent lines are consistently associated with distinct ecologies and hence have probably differentiated by adaptation" (p. 293).

12. Ramages are "usually found with intensive exploitation of dispersed resources" (p. 293). The diversity of ecological zones requires household specialization and exchange.

13. Truncated descent lines "typically occur on islands where narrow concentric zones of exploitation concentrate near the coast" (p. 294). All resources are accessible to all groups, reducing incentives for hierarchical redistribution.

### Atoll societies (pp. 295-297)

14. Atoll organizations "have been differentiated by adaptation to atoll environments" from high-island prototypes (p. 295).

15. Catastrophic events (cyclones, tidal waves) can produce "rapid culture changes" analogous to genetic drift in small populations (p. 296). The 1903 Raroia hurricane example: "Coupled with the loss of old time political and religious leaders in the cyclone, profound cultural changes ensued" (p. 296).

16. "Decimation of the atoll, including perhaps death of persons of authority in the old structure, permits complete rearrangement of the organization" (p. 296). [This observation is relevant to our argument about the 1862-1863 slave raids and subsequent demographic collapse on Easter Island.]

### Terminological refinement (fn. 5, p. 293)

17. Sahlins departs from Firth's 1957 redefinition of "ramage" as a non-unilineal kin group, preferring the original Firth 1936 usage of "ramage" for a strictly unilineal group. "I am thus departing from Firth's recent redefinition of 'ramage' as a non-unilineal kin group, in contrast to 'lineage,' a strictly unilineal group (Firth 1957). My use of 'ramage' corresponds closely to Firth's original usage (Firth 1936:367-372)."

## Bibliography Assessment

The bibliography (pp. 299-300) contains 24 entries. The Easter Island-relevant sources are:
- Routledge 1919 (cited for exchange practices, fn. 9)
- Sahlins 1955 ("Esoteric Efflorescence in Easter Island")
- Sahlins 1957 (in press), *Social Stratification in Polynesia* (the monograph)

**Absent from bibliography**: Metraux (1937, 1940), Thomson 1889/1891, Williamson 1924, any contact-era account. These absences reflect the article's focus on Part Two (form of organization) rather than a revision of the Easter Island source base.

**New source**: Sahlins 1955, "Esoteric Efflorescence in Easter Island," *American Anthropologist* 57:1045-1052. This article, published between the dissertation (1954) and the monograph (1957/1958), is a separate Sahlins piece specifically about Easter Island. It is not cited in the 1954 dissertation. Its content bears on Easter Island's cultural derivation from central Polynesia but has not been processed through our pipeline.

## Significance for the Chiefdom Attribution Chain

The 1957 article is significant for what it omits rather than what it adds. By condensing the dissertation to Part Two only:

1. **The Easter Island data gaps disappear.** Sahlins's eleven explicit admissions of evidential uncertainty (including the critical "little direct evidence" of redistribution) are absent from the published journal version.

2. **The redistribution gap is invisible.** The article's general discussion of ramage redistribution (p. 294) treats it as a standard Polynesian feature without noting that Easter Island lacks evidence for it.

3. **The source basis is obscured.** Anyone reading only the 1957 article would not know that Easter Island's ramage classification rests entirely on post-slave-raid ethnographic sources.

4. **The ecological anomaly is retained** but buried in a paragraph about exceptions (p. 295). A reader who cites "Sahlins 1957" for Easter Island as a ramage society would likely miss this qualification.

5. **The stratification rating is not carried forward** in this article. The Group IIa placement exists only in the dissertation and (presumably) the 1958 monograph. The journal article provides no basis for any claim about Easter Island's level of stratification.

The practical consequence: scholars citing "Sahlins 1958" (the monograph, which this article previews) for Easter Island's chiefdom status are inheriting a framework whose qualifications and data gaps are documented in the 1954 dissertation but progressively stripped from published versions. The 1957 article, as the most concise published statement, is also the most stripped-down: Easter Island appears only as a name in three lists, with no analysis and no caveats beyond the ecological anomaly note.
