# Claims Extraction: Roussel 1869

## Source Information

**Author**: Reverend Father Hippolyte Roussel, Congregation of the Sacred Hearts of Picpus

**Period on Easter Island**: March 23, 1866 to 1873 (approximately seven years). Notes written in 1869, three years after arrival.

**Publication history**: Notes prepared in 1869 and sent to Valparaiso, where they remained until 1914. Published posthumously in the *Annals of the Sacred Heart* (February-April-June 1926), Office of Annals of the Sacred Heart, Braine-le-Comte (Belgium) and 35 Rue de Picpus, Paris XIIth.

**Translation**: Translated from French by Ann M. Altman, published in Altman 2004, *Early Visitors to Easter Island 1864-1877*, Parts 2-3 (pp. 34-60 in the book).

**PDF file(s)**: `docs/references/pdfs/post_slave_raid/altman_2004/Altman-2004-EarlyVisitorsToEasterIsland-1864-1877-Part2.pdf` (pp. 34-45) and `Altman-2004-EarlyVisitorsToEasterIsland-1864-1877-Part3.pdf` (pp. 46-60).

**Context**: Roussel was a Catholic missionary whose primary purpose was conversion. He arrived on the island with Brother Eugene Eyraud (who had first landed on January 2, 1864, and returned with Roussel on March 23, 1866 after a visit to Valparaiso). The 1862-1863 Peruvian slave raids had occurred just two to four years before Roussel's arrival, reducing the population catastrophically. By the time Roussel wrote these notes in 1869, only approximately 650 people survived according to his census figures. His informants were traumatized survivors of a demographic catastrophe that had destroyed much of the island's institutional and cultural infrastructure.

**Preceded by**: Brother Eugene Eyraud, who landed January 2, 1864, and whose own account (see Altman 2003 translation) describes the island in the immediate aftermath of the slave raids.

**[POST-RAID]**: ALL claims in this file derive from a source written 3-6 years after the 1862-1863 slave raids. Roussel's informants were survivors of an event that reduced the population from roughly 3,000 to fewer than 200 at its nadir (with partial recovery to approximately 650 by the time of his census). The slave raids specifically targeted elites, leaders, and knowledge-holders. What Roussel describes is not straightforwardly "traditional" pre-contact society but a post-catastrophe reconstruction as reported by survivors.

---

## Analytical Significance

Roussel's account is the single most detailed "ethnographic" description of Rapa Nui political hierarchy produced during the missionary period. It provides the most elaborate king list, the most specific claims about royal prerogatives, and the most detailed account of the social class system. For precisely these reasons, it is the most analytically important source for evaluating the chiefdom attribution: nearly every later reconstruction of pre-contact political organization on Rapa Nui draws directly or indirectly on Roussel's claims.

The account is also, however, internally contradictory on the central question of political authority. Roussel simultaneously reports that kings "exercised absolute power over the island" (p. 40) and that royal authority "had passed completely into the hands of the matatoa" (p. 42). Whether the transfer of power to the matatoa was recent (as Roussel implies with "In recent times") or reflects a deeper structural pattern in which ariki were always primarily ritual figures with nominal political authority is the interpretive crux that the chiefdom debate turns on.

## Claims Extraction

### 1. Political Authority and Hierarchy

**1.1. Claimed absolute power of kings** [POST-RAID]

"From Hotu to the last king, who had been taken away with two of his sons and his two daughters on Peruvian ships, there had been an uninterrupted succession of great chiefs or kings. These kings, who were regarded as gods, exercised absolute power over the island and used their authority to retain the prestige associated with the gift of apparently superhuman powers, as well as certain personal privileges." (p. 40)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Roussel is relaying what survivors told him about the nature of royal authority. He did not observe "absolute power" being exercised. The claim that kings "exercised absolute power" is contradicted by Roussel's own account of the matatoa (see 4.1 below).

**1.2. First fruits and harvest taboo** [POST-RAID]

"To the kings alone belonged the first fruits of the land. These offerings were brought to them with great ceremony." (p. 40)

"Only the kings had the right to begin the harvest, which remained taboo until they wanted to begin. Woe unto anyone who dared to violate the taboo. Often, such an act would cost him the destruction of his property and sometimes, even, the loss of his life." (p. 41)

**(b) Reported tradition**, with one element of **(a) direct observation** (see 1.3). The first-fruits system and harvest taboo are described as general customs, not as things Roussel personally witnessed in operation across the population. The enforcement mechanism (destruction of property, death) is reported tradition about a system Roussel could not have observed functioning at pre-raid scale.

**1.3. Direct observation of the first-fruits ceremony for Gregorio** [POST-RAID]

"I was there when the natives brought the first vines [yams] of the year to Gregorio at his home. A procession was headed by people carrying the vines, followed by numerous natives and boys in two lines, each holding a peeled branch of hau (*Triumfetta semitriloba*, used for making rope) that had been painted black. The procession resembled nothing more than a funeral procession with flaming torches. From time to time, the air resounded with howling and incomprehensible singing." (pp. 40-41)

**(a) Direct observation.** This is one of the few passages where Roussel explicitly states he personally witnessed the event ("I was there"). However, the ceremony occurred after the slave raids, when Gregorio was the last surviving member of the royal line, a child who would die young. The ceremony may have been performative or residual rather than indicative of a functioning pre-raid political system. The question is whether this ceremony was a living institution or a post-catastrophe performance of remembered ritual for the benefit of the last royal heir.

**1.4. King's head taboo and the hair-cutting incident** [POST-RAID]

"The king's head was taboo. The king had to let his hair grow, without ever touching it with a stone blade. I recall quite clearly how, when I arrived on Easter Island, Gregorio was presented to me as the one and only true chief and how he was the only one to have long hair. When, for reasons of hygiene, I asked one of the Mangarevans who had accompanied me to cut his hair, the child put up considerable resistance and gave in only as a result of force or fear. The general feeling of discontent was so apparent that it would not have taken much for them to start hurling stones at the barber once his work was complete." (p. 41)

**Mixed: (a) direct observation and (b) reported tradition.** Roussel directly observed: (i) Gregorio being presented as "the one and only true chief," (ii) his distinctive long hair, (iii) the child's resistance to having his hair cut, and (iv) the community's angry reaction. The underlying concept that the king's head was taboo is reported tradition explaining the observed behavior.

This passage is significant because it demonstrates that, even after the slave raids, the surviving population still recognized Gregorio as holding a distinct status and reacted strongly to violations of his person. This is evidence that the ariki concept retained cultural salience in the immediate post-raid period. It is NOT evidence that the ariki exercised political power; the passage describes ritual prohibition and communal indignation, not political authority.

**1.5. King's hands taboo and restrictions** [POST-RAID]

"The kings' hands were also taboo - they were allowed only, under the guise of recreation, to make fishing lines and nets. They were allowed to fish from a canoe but their homes, their lands, their food and their entire persons and everything that they used were taboo for people of both sexes." (p. 41)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Roussel describes taboo restrictions on the king's person and activities. These restrictions are consistent with Polynesian concepts of tapu associated with high-ranking individuals, but tapu restrictions do not by themselves demonstrate political authority. In many Polynesian systems, tapu on the chief's person reflects a ritual/sacred status that may or may not correlate with effective political power.

**1.6. King safe during warfare** [POST-RAID]

"In these conflicts, the king or chief had nothing to fear in terms of insult from either party; they could stay at home without anyone even thinking of bothering them. Nonetheless, either from fear or out of prudence, some of them temporarily abandoned their domestic hearth to remove themselves from the theater of war: from there they might leave to spend some time in Hanga Roa, Anakena or Atuiti." (p. 42)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The king's immunity from warfare is reported as a general pattern. This is analytically significant: the king was not a war leader. His safety during conflict was assured not by military power but by his non-involvement. This is consistent with the ariki being a ritual figure rather than a political-military leader.

### 2. King Lists and Succession

**2.1. Settlement origin tradition** [POST-RAID]

"According to the natives, the island was deserted when two great ships, without masts, with the prow raised like a duck's neck and the poop raised similarly but split into two halves, sailed along the Southwest coast until they came to a bay, in the North, called Anakena. Each of these boats held about one hundred people. This poor small bay with its beach of white sand seemed a good place to land according to Hotu, the captain of this small fleet, and his wife, Avareipua. He ordered the two boats to be pulled up onto the beach and then men, women and children disembarked with all their belongings, which included pigs, chickens, taro, sweet potato, a tropical vine *igname* (yams), bananas, *toromiro*, *gaoho*, *maute*, sugar cane, etc." (pp. 38-39)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This is an origin narrative told to Roussel by survivors. Altman's endnote 1 notes: "There is no evidence that pigs arrived with the settlers. If they started out with them, these animals did not survive the sea voyage." The narrative presents Hotu as "captain" and later "king," establishing the royal lineage from the founding moment.

**2.2. Abdication and succession rule** [POST-RAID]

"Hotu, who was the king and who settled at Anakena, wasted no time before abdicating in favor of his son Taumeeke. The king used to abdicate when his son got married but this event did not occur for a long time since the customs of the country did not allow the son to get married until he had reached quite an advanced age." (pp. 39-40)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The abdication rule (king steps down when son marries) is a reported institutional norm. Whether this rule was consistently observed over twenty-two generations cannot be verified.

**2.3. The king list** [POST-RAID]

"Taumeeke was succeeded by Vakai, who was succeeded by Marama, who was succeeded, in turn, by Raa, Mitiake, Otuiti, Tunkura, Miru, Ataraga, Tuu, Ihu, Haumoana, Tupaariki, Mataivi, Terahai, Kaimokohi, Gahara, Tepito, and his son Gregorio, the last offspring of the royal family, who was an intelligent child and died when he was about twelve years old, mourned as much by the missionaries as by his subjects. The entire line spanned twenty-two generations." (p. 40)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This is the most detailed king list from the missionary period.

**Critical note on the king list**: Altman's endnote 7 states: "A comparative list of king's names is given by Metraux (1940:91). This list compares the Roussel, Jaussen, Thomson and Metraux' sequence of names. Twelve of the 23 names on Roussel's list are included in Thomson's list; Roussel and Jaussen have nine names in common. Few are given in the same order."

The disagreement between king lists is significant. If a stable hereditary dynasty had governed the island for twenty-two generations, one would expect greater consistency among informants regarding the succession. The variability suggests either: (a) multiple competing genealogical traditions existed, (b) the lists were reconstructed or elaborated under post-raid conditions when most knowledge-holders were dead, or (c) the "dynasty" was less institutionally stable than the lists imply. All three possibilities undermine the use of the king list as straightforward evidence for centralized hereditary authority.

**2.4. Gregorio, the last king** [POST-RAID]

Gregorio is described as "the last offspring of the royal family" who "died when he was about twelve years old" (p. 40). The previous king "had been taken away with two of his sons and his two daughters on Peruvian ships" (p. 40).

**(a) Direct observation** (regarding Gregorio's existence and death) combined with **(b) reported tradition** (regarding the previous king's abduction). The slave raids specifically removed the king and his older children, leaving only Gregorio, a young child, as the last royal heir. This is direct evidence that the slave raids decapitated the political-ritual system.

### 3. Social Classes

**3.1. The four-class system** [POST-RAID]

"I did not see any evidence of classes on Rapa Nui other than the kings (*ariki*), the priests (*ivi atua*), the warriors (*matatoa*) and the farmers (*kio*)." (p. 42)

**Mixed: (a) direct observation and (b) reported tradition.** The phrasing "I did not see any evidence of classes ... other than" suggests Roussel is combining what he observed with what he was told. The four categories (ariki, ivi atua, matatoa, kio) are Roussel's schematization of social differentiation. Whether these were rigid hereditary classes, contextual roles, or a post-raid simplification of a more complex system cannot be determined from this passage alone. The term *kio* is elsewhere translated as "servants" or "slaves" (Roussel uses "farmers" here and "domestic servants" on p. 48), suggesting the category was less clear-cut than "class" implies.

**3.2. Kio as domestic servants** [POST-RAID]

"Once the festivities have finished, the leftovers are eaten by the *kio* (domestic servants) and the plantation can once again, by right, serve the needs of the family that owns it." (p. 48)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) direct observation.** The passage describes kio eating leftovers after festivities. Whether Roussel witnessed this directly or was told about it is ambiguous. The description is consistent with either a subordinate class or a situational dependency relationship.

### 4. Government and the Matatoa

**4.1. Transfer of authority from ariki to matatoa** [POST-RAID]

"In recent times, even though the kings were still viewed with respect by the common people, who considered them to be something sacred, their authority was, nonetheless, either absent or totally unrecognized: it had passed completely into the hands of the *matatoa*, who decided everything and fought among themselves without prior consultations with His Majesty." (p. 42)

**Mixed: (b) reported tradition (about the transition) and (a) direct observation (of matatoa power).** THIS IS THE MOST ANALYTICALLY IMPORTANT PASSAGE IN ROUSSEL'S ACCOUNT. It directly contradicts claim 1.1 ("absolute power"). Roussel reports that royal authority was "either absent or totally unrecognized" and had "passed completely into the hands of the matatoa." The qualifier "In recent times" implies Roussel understood this as a recent development, but he provides no evidence for when the transition occurred. It could have been recent (post-slave-raid), or it could reflect a structural pattern in which ariki were always primarily ritual figures while matatoa held effective power, a pattern consistent with some Polynesian systems where paramount chiefs hold sacred status but lack coercive political authority.

The phrase "without prior consultations with His Majesty" is particularly revealing: the matatoa did not merely supplement royal authority but operated independently of it.

**4.2. Government as "perpetual thievery"** [POST-RAID]

"It would be a contradiction in terms to call the perpetual thievery a form of government." (p. 47)

**(c) Author's inference / (a) direct observation.** Roussel is characterizing what he observed of the post-raid political situation. His contempt for the system reflects his missionary perspective, but the observation that he could not identify anything resembling "government" is analytically significant.

**4.3. The Great Chief's non-involvement** [POST-RAID]

"the Great Chief stayed out of the affairs of the islanders" (p. 47)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This reiterates 1.6: the ariki was not politically active.

**4.4. Tangata manu and transfer of authority** [POST-RAID]

"luck alone, the discovery of a few eggs of a seabird, was all that was needed to transfer the chief's authority to the happy nest-robbers who then took the name of *matatoa* (warrior). In any case, the investiture of chiefly authority only began when the candidate's head had been shaved." (p. 47)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This is Roussel's description of the tangata manu (birdman) system, though he does not use that term. The passage describes effective political authority being transferred annually through a competitive ritual (egg-finding at Orongo) rather than through hereditary succession. This is fundamentally inconsistent with a hereditary chiefdom model: political power was obtained through ritual competition, not genealogical rank.

**4.5. The matatoa chief's behavior** [POST-RAID]

"The Chief's role was limited to marauding day and night and to spending his year in power tricking as many people as possible and having continuous parties of the most immature and licentious kind." (p. 47)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) author's inference.** Roussel's description of matatoa behavior is colored by missionary disapproval, but the underlying observation is of a rotating annual leadership based on competitive achievement, not hereditary right.

### 5. War and Conflict

**5.1. War preparation** [POST-RAID]

"Once war had been declared, all those who were going to take part smeared themselves with black dirt and spent the night without sleeping. They busied themselves with preparing their weapons and hiding their valuables." (p. 42)

**(b) Reported tradition.** War preparations are described as customary practice. Note the absence of any role for the ariki in organizing or directing warfare.

**5.2. No human sacrifices or religious sanction for war** [POST-RAID]

"There were certainly no human sacrifices or other offerings in honor of the divinity to ensure his protection. It seems that the idea of a religious cult disappeared with the last priests and priestesses, who died a long time ago." (p. 42)

**Mixed: (b) reported tradition and (c) author's inference.** The claim about the absence of war-related religious ritual is significant. In chiefdom models, warfare typically has religious sanction and is directed by or on behalf of chiefly authority. Roussel describes warfare as entirely secular and motivated by "personal vengeance, jealousy, covetousness and hunger" (p. 42), not by political or religious objectives.

The phrase "died a long time ago" regarding the priests and priestesses is important: Roussel's informants attributed the loss of religious specialists to events predating the slave raids.

**5.3. Motives for war** [POST-RAID]

"There was absolutely no thought of just cause in their declarations of war. Personal vengeance, jealousy, covetousness and hunger were the usual motives." (p. 42)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) author's inference.** The stated motives are individual and material, not political or territorial. This is inconsistent with chiefdom warfare, which typically involves territorial expansion, tribute extraction, or succession disputes.

**5.4. Defeated as slaves** [POST-RAID]

"After a day of vengeance, the defeated islanders were kept, under guard, in caves and they were only allowed out to work - all were now slaves and all had to plant a certain piece of land for their respective masters. When these little masters no longer needed their services, they sent them back to their homes under some pretext or other but, in reality, so that there would be fewer mouths to feed and, especially, to force them to plant all their own crops once again." (p. 43)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This describes a pattern of temporary enslavement following warfare. The victors are described as "little masters" (a diminutive that may be Roussel's editorialization or may reflect the Rapanui term). The system described is one of opportunistic raiding and temporary servitude, not the incorporation of defeated populations into a chiefdom's administrative structure.

**5.5. Defeated preferred starvation to planting** [POST-RAID]

"So what did the defeated islanders do? They contented themselves with just planting one twentieth of what they would have needed to live in relative ease, preferring to die of hunger than to plant for someone else." (p. 44)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This describes a form of resistance to exploitation that is consistent with an egalitarian ethos: the defeated would rather starve than produce surplus for others. This is inconsistent with an established tribute system in which surplus production for elites was a normalized part of social life.

**5.6. Weapons** [POST-RAID]

"Initially, the natives used the following weapons: the *hau*, a type of long club; a short heavy club; and a piece of a dried gourd attached to the end of a long *hau* branch. Later on, a man called Eta, returning from his field of sweet potatoes, banged his foot hard against a *mata* (a piece of very sharp stone). He hurt himself so badly that he conceived of the 'wonderful' idea of replacing the piece of calabash by a *mata*." (p. 44)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The innovation narrative (Eta inventing the obsidian-tipped spear) describes weapon development as an individual accident, not as a product of chiefly-directed military organization.

### 6. Property and Land

**6.1. Land ownership recognized** [POST-RAID]

"There is no doubt that the concept of ownership of property was accepted on Rapa Nui: the boundary markers and the *ahu* that are scattered all over the place are a testament to this fact." (p. 50)

**(a) Direct observation / (c) author's inference.** Roussel observed boundary markers and ahu and inferred that these attested to property ownership. This is consistent with a system of land tenure but does not specify whether ownership was individual, familial, or communal, nor whether it was mediated by chiefly authority.

**6.2. Post-raid disruption of land tenure** [POST-RAID]

"Most recently, however, this concept has become less established and the first person to occupy a piece of land claims it for himself. Each family used to remain simply the master of land that heredity or an enforced or voluntary stay had put into their hands. Nobody paid too much attention, given the vast areas of land that were not cultivated and the absence of anyone who wanted to buy any of the land." (p. 50)

**(a) Direct observation.** Roussel directly observed the breakdown of land tenure following the slave raids. His description of the pre-raid system ("each family used to remain simply the master of land") describes family-based tenure, not chiefly allocation or control of land.

**6.3. Land ownership as "inextricable tangle"** [POST-RAID]

"The bad behavior of the natives and their displacement, an obvious consequence of their continuous wars, have left questions of ownership of property in an almost inextricable tangle." (p. 50)

**(a) Direct observation / (c) author's inference.** Roussel attributes the confusion of property rights to both warfare and the slave raids. Altman's endnote 18 adds: "Also, land that belonged to those who were kidnapped by slavers and taken to Peru was up for grabs and ownership was disputed by the survivors."

**6.4. Marine tenure** [POST-RAID]

"The sea adjacent to a piece of land belonged to the owner of the land. Only he, if he wished to, had the right to fish there, to look for octopus and edible mollusks. To claim this right, all he had to do was make his *rahui*, which consisted of three stones, one on top of the other, aligned along the stretch of land beside the sea that he wished to reserve for himself." (pp. 48-49)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) direct observation.** The *rahui* system of marking exclusive fishing rights is described. This is a form of individual or household resource control, consistent with either chiefly or non-chiefly systems.

**6.5. Communal family plantations with first fruits to chief** [POST-RAID]

"plantations, held in common by the members of a single family or located on the same piece of land and defined only by a few stones placed at regular intervals, should be cultivated synchronously and then the first fruits should be taken to the Chief." (p. 48)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) direct observation.** Plantations were family-based and communally worked within families. The requirement to give first fruits to the chief is reported as a customary obligation. The system described is consistent with either a tribute system or a ritual obligation to a sacred (but not necessarily politically powerful) figure.

### 7. Customs and Festivities

**7.1. Tuna fish given to chief or matatoa** [POST-RAID]

"One custom that has been handed down to the present day is that any tuna fish caught by a fisherman should be given to the chief or, if not to him, to the matatoa." (p. 47)

**Mixed: (b) reported tradition and (a) direct observation.** The phrase "handed down to the present day" suggests Roussel observed this practice continuing. The phrasing "to the chief or, if not to him, to the matatoa" reveals the ambiguity of the system: the obligation could be discharged to either the ritual leader (ariki) or the effective power-holder (matatoa). This dual option is consistent with a system in which the ariki's role was ritual rather than political.

**7.2. Harvest festivities and matatoa predation** [POST-RAID]

"When the crop is perfectly ripe, no matter what it is, the plantation in which it is growing becomes the focus of festivities for the village and, sometimes, for several villages, depending on the size of the plantation and the importance of the person who is considered to be its owner. For these festivities, the natives gather as much food as they can, with chickens making the greatest contribution. That is when the *matatoa*, leaving their caves on the Mataveri mountain (Rano Kau), would fight like vultures for everything that took their fancy, without anyone else daring to ask too loudly for his share." (p. 48)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) direct observation.** The description of matatoa behavior at harvest festivities is striking: they "fight like vultures" and take what they want. This is predatory extraction, not organized redistribution. The matatoa lived in caves on Rano Kau (associated with the Orongo birdman site) and descended to take from community festivities. No role for the ariki is mentioned in organizing or mediating these events.

**7.3. Hero of festivities hides food** [POST-RAID]

"It was the Rapanui custom for the hero of the festivities to remain to one side and not to touch any of the food that was to be eaten. Fortunately for him, when night came and everyone had disappeared, he could bring out of hiding everything that he had taken care to put aside." (p. 48)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The host of the feast could not openly eat but hid food in advance. This describes a competitive feast system in which the host's visible generosity was performative, and the only way to retain resources was subterfuge. This is inconsistent with a redistributive chiefdom in which the chief openly retains and allocates surplus.

**7.4. Gender division of labor** [POST-RAID]

"The exclusive occupation of the men, both old and young, was the planting and tending of their plantations until the crops reached full maturity; to fish (in the case of men of property), to make fishing nets, and to build huts. The young men rented themselves out, with an average salary of one or two rats and a few sweet potatoes." (p. 49)

"Beyond that, everything was women's work. Each woman had to make sure she had sweet potatoes and the wood and grasses needed to cook them." (p. 49)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) direct observation.** The gender division of labor is described without reference to chiefly organization of production. Young men hired themselves out for subsistence wages (rats and sweet potatoes), suggesting an absence of chiefly labor mobilization.

### 8. Population

**8.1. Pre-raid population estimate** [POST-RAID]

"I have trouble believing that the population was as high as five or six thousand, as some of the natives insist. The interior of the island has never been settled as can be recognized from the absence of paved areas and of any signs of *maute* and *toromiro*, which provide clear evidence of ancient habitation. Only the shore was inhabited and the clusters of *maute*, *toromiro* and *hau* that are scattered about suggest a population of no more than five thousand souls with an average of five or six people per hut." (p. 46)

**(c) Author's inference** based on **(a) direct observation** of settlement patterns. Roussel estimates a maximum pre-raid population of approximately 5,000 based on the distribution of habitation indicators. Altman's endnote 13 notes: "Roussel is in error; the interior was settled, but sparsely."

**8.2. Post-raid population** [POST-RAID]

"According to the recent censuses, the population would have been reduced to 650 people." (p. 46)

**(a) Direct observation.** Roussel reports a census figure. The reduction from thousands to 650 represents the cumulative impact of the slave raids, smallpox, and other post-contact depopulation factors.

**8.3. Causes of population decline** [POST-RAID]

"What was the cause of this great diminution in numbers, I cannot think of any apart from, as I noted above, their general state of privation, the dampness of their huts, the unhealthy winter air, their immorality and the gross imprudence of cohabiting with those who are sick." (p. 46)

**(c) Author's inference.** Roussel attributes population decline to living conditions and behavioral factors rather than to the slave raids themselves, which is a significant understatement of the primary cause.

**8.4. Disproportionate female mortality** [POST-RAID]

"they all agree that long ago, before smallpox and before they had been taken away on Peruvian ships, there were very many of them and that the number of women and girls exceeded the number of men and boys, while quite the opposite is true today. And one should not be in too much of a hurry to accuse them of lies and exaggeration in the latter case because I notice regularly that of any three deaths, two are always of females." (p. 46)

**Mixed: (a) direct observation (of mortality patterns) and (b) reported tradition (about pre-raid demographics).** The disproportionate female mortality is an important observation about post-raid conditions.

### 9. Religion

**9.1. Principal gods** [POST-RAID]

"The Rapanui have a fair number of gods; the most powerful ones, those who are the source of all the others, are Hiva, Makemake and Tive. The worship of these gods ceased with the disappearance of the priests and priestesses. All that remained were certain taboos, to which nobody seemed to pay much attention." (p. 53)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) direct observation.** Roussel was told about the gods but directly observed the collapse of organized worship. The statement that worship "ceased with the disappearance of the priests and priestesses" indicates that religious specialists had been lost before or during the slave raids, and that organized religion had already collapsed by the time Roussel arrived.

**9.2. Belief in afterlife** [POST-RAID]

"The natives believed in the immortality of the soul. The soul, after death, went to a foreign land where it was happy or unhappy depending on whether its owner had been more or less faithful during his lifetime in the observance of taboos." (p. 53)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Standard Polynesian afterlife beliefs.

**9.3. Fear of spirits** [POST-RAID]

"They seemed to be very afraid of these ghostly souls; they came on many occasions to ask me to chase away, as they said, the bad spirits of a father or a mother, who was tormenting them and tearing at their entrails." (p. 53)

**(a) Direct observation.** Roussel was personally asked to perform exorcisms, indicating that the surviving population transferred some spiritual authority functions to the missionary.

**9.4. Priests' functions** [POST-RAID]

"There used to be both priests and priestesses here. Their functions were rather limited. They presided over the celebrations for a newborn and they chased away the evil spirits from the bodies of the sick. It was also their job to compose and sing songs in honor of the dead." (p. 54)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The description of priestly functions as "rather limited" is notable: birth rituals, healing, and funerary songs. No political role for priests is mentioned. The priests are described as a distinct category from the ariki.

**9.5. Uncertain about human sacrifices** [POST-RAID]

"I have no idea whether they ever participated in human sacrifices. I have only heard the natives speak of offerings on the *ahu*, offerings of chickens, fish, etc...." (p. 54)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) author's inference.** Roussel could not determine whether human sacrifice had been practiced. The offerings described (chickens, fish) are modest.

---

### 10. Statues and Monuments

**10.1. The walking legend and chiefly command** [POST-RAID]

"It is not known how these statues were transported and erected - only a legend remains. The Great Chief of the time, who was all-powerful like his predecessors, ordered the statues to walk and then all of them, on hearing his words, started move and chose destinations around the island that appealed to them." (p. 55)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The walking legend attributes moai movement to chiefly command. The Great Chief "ordered the statues to walk" by the power of his words. This tradition attributes supernatural power to the ariki, consistent with a concept of mana-based authority. It does not describe an organizational mechanism for statue transport.

**10.2. Roussel's inference about dragging** [POST-RAID]

"I am led to believe that the stone statues were dragged and not rolled because one never finds a statue that has been broken or abandoned during transit: all arrived safely at their destinations and have smooth backs." (p. 56)

**(c) Author's inference.** Roussel's reasoning about transport method based on the condition of the statues. Altman's endnote 25 notes that the italicized words may have been inadvertently transposed; Roussel may have intended to say "rolled rather than dragged."

**10.3. Clan-based collective transport** [POST-RAID]

"It is likely that, on festive occasions, all the clans gathered together, each one bringing its household gods, and dragged the statues with ropes made of *hau* to their respective locations." (p. 56)

**(c) Author's inference.** Roussel speculates that statue transport was a collective enterprise involving all clans. This inference is consistent with collective action models: the clans "gathered together" for the task, with each bringing their own items. Roussel does not attribute the organization to chiefly command (despite the walking legend); his own inference describes cooperative clan-level action.

**10.4. Pukao placement by ramp** [POST-RAID]

"Tradition has it that the enormous millstone that rested on each head was put on top of each statue by piling up stones. These hat-like objects, which rest cone-like on the heads of the statues, are about three and one half meters in diameter and they are made of a red stone that comes from a quarry at the other end of the island, at Puna Pau." (p. 56)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The tradition of raising pukao by piling stones as a ramp.

**10.5. Size gradient observation** [POST-RAID]

"It is also noteworthy that, the further the statues are from the crater, the smaller they are, and that the biggest ones that are standing today are those that remain at the Otuiti Crater." (p. 56)

**(a) Direct observation.** Roussel observed a size gradient: smaller moai farther from the quarry, larger ones remaining at Rano Raraku. This is consistent with transport constraints limiting the size of moai that could be moved long distances.

**10.6. Ahu as funerary and sacred site** [POST-RAID]

"The *ahu*, or sacred place, was where the natives placed their dead; its taboo was not inviolable as it is everywhere else." (p. 54)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) direct observation.** Roussel describes ahu as places for the dead. The comment about the taboo being "not inviolable" suggests that by the post-raid period, ahu taboos were being violated.

---

### 11. Rongorongo

**11.1. Writing system observed but not understood** [POST-RAID]

"They claim to have had a kind of written language, which allowed them to hand down the important facts about their country to posterity. I have seen this writing carved on a rather long piece of polished wood and it resembles, to a great extent, the hieroglyphics of the ancient Egyptians. In my opinion, I do not think that the natives were able to make much sense out of these characters. When asked to read them, the few natives who claimed to understand these symbols were reduced to telling ridiculous and unintelligible tales." (p. 52)

**(a) Direct observation.** Roussel personally saw rongorongo tablets and witnessed attempts to read them. His judgment that the readers produced "ridiculous and unintelligible tales" may reflect his inability to understand what was being recited, or it may accurately describe a situation in which the knowledge-holders who could read the tablets had been killed or taken in the slave raids, leaving only partial or confused knowledge. Altman's endnote 20 confirms: "The wise men who were able to read and write *rongorongo* had disappeared by the time the missionaries arrived, having been carried off by slave traders."

This passage is significant for the chiefdom question because rongorongo tablets have sometimes been cited as evidence of administrative complexity. Roussel's observation that no one could produce coherent readings by 1869 indicates that whatever knowledge system the tablets represented had been effectively destroyed.

---

### 12. Marriage and Family

**12.1. Marriage by mutual choice** [POST-RAID]

"Couples entered into marriage without consulting their parents. Their predilection for each other was the only factor. Dissolution of a marriage was a matter of minor importance. The slightest argument or the end of their mutual attraction was enough to break the ties that bound them." (p. 52)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Marriage was based on individual choice, without parental or chiefly involvement. Easy dissolution suggests an absence of marriage alliances serving political purposes, which are a hallmark of chiefdom systems where marriages cement inter-lineage political relationships.

**12.2. Polygamy and bigamy** [POST-RAID]

"not only were bigamy and polygamy considered acceptable, both were also held in high regard." (p. 52)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Polygamy "held in high regard" could indicate either a general cultural norm or a status marker (high-status individuals having multiple spouses). Without specification of whether polygamy was restricted by rank, this passage is ambiguous regarding the chiefdom question.

**12.3. Marriage between relatives abhorred** [POST-RAID]

"Marriage between relatives was abhorred." (p. 52)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Exogamy rules. This is potentially relevant to chiefdom models in which endogamy among elites is used to concentrate mana or status.

### 13. Absences and Negative Evidence

**13.1. No description of redistribution**

Across Roussel's entire account, there is no description of a functioning redistributive system. The first-fruits obligation is described (1.2, 6.5), but Roussel never describes the ariki redistributing resources to the population. The matatoa take by force at festivities (7.2); the ariki receives first fruits but is not described as returning anything. In chiefdom models (Sahlins 1958, Kirch 1984), redistribution is the economic mechanism that legitimates chiefly authority. Roussel describes extraction (first fruits to the chief, tuna to the chief/matatoa, matatoa predation at festivities) without any corresponding redistribution.

**13.2. No description of centralized labor mobilization (except in legend)**

The only reference to chiefly-directed labor is the walking-moai legend (10.1), which attributes statue movement to supernatural command. Roussel's own inference about transport (10.3) describes voluntary clan-level cooperation, not directed labor. No passage describes the ariki or matatoa organizing collective work projects, directing agricultural production, or mobilizing labor for any purpose.

**13.3. No description of a chiefly court, retinue, or administrative apparatus**

The ariki is described as a solitary figure who "stayed out of the affairs of the islanders" (4.3) and could "stay at home without anyone even thinking of bothering them" during warfare (1.6). No advisors, administrators, stewards, or other officials are mentioned in connection with the ariki. The only "government" described is the matatoa's annual predatory leadership (4.4, 4.5).

**13.4. No description of inter-group political integration**

Roussel describes "clans" (10.3) and settlements on different promontories (2.1) but never describes a mechanism by which these units were politically integrated under chiefly authority. Warfare between groups is described (5.1-5.5) without any reference to overarching political authority mediating or directing conflict.

**13.5. No description of tribute networks beyond first fruits**

The first-fruits obligation and the tuna-fish custom (7.1) are the only extractive mechanisms described. Both are single-item, single-occasion obligations. No ongoing tribute in goods, labor, or service is described. This is a thin economic basis for a chiefdom.

**13.6. No ceremonial or diplomatic role for the ariki in external relations**

Roussel describes his own arrival (p. 38) without any ariki mediating or organizing the reception. He was met by a crowd and guided by an individual self-appointed guardian, not by a chief. Compare this with Polynesian chiefdoms documented elsewhere, where paramount chiefs organized formal receptions for important visitors.

### 14. Cross-Reference Notes

**14.1. Roussel vs. contact-era accounts on political authority**

The contact-era accounts (Roggeveen 1722, Gonzalez 1770, Cook 1774, La Perouse 1786) describe varying degrees of apparent authority, from Roggeveen's "particular one who seemed to be in authority" to the Blossom's (1825) explicit finding of no observable command authority. Roussel's account describes a more elaborate system (king lists, first-fruits, taboos) but simultaneously acknowledges that effective authority had passed to the matatoa. The discrepancy may reflect: (a) the difference between brief ship visits and seven years of residence, (b) the difference between observing behavior and being told about institutions, or (c) elaboration by post-raid informants constructing an idealized past.

**14.2. Roussel vs. Thomson (1891) on the king list**

As noted in endnote 7, only twelve of Roussel's twenty-three kings appear in Thomson's list, and few are in the same order. Thomson collected his information in 1886, approximately seventeen years after Roussel's notes. The divergence suggests either deterioration of genealogical knowledge over those seventeen years, different informants with different traditions, or the inherent instability of orally transmitted king lists in a post-catastrophe society.

**14.3. Roussel vs. Eyraud (1864) on conditions**

Brother Eyraud, who arrived two years before Roussel, provides the earliest post-raid account. Eyraud's descriptions of chaos, thievery, and the absence of apparent authority are consistent with Roussel's description of the matatoa system and the political dysfunction he observed. The continuity suggests that what Roussel describes was not a temporary post-raid disruption but a stable (or at least persistent) pattern.

**14.4. The internal contradiction: absolute power vs. absent authority**

The central interpretive problem with Roussel's account is that it contains two incompatible descriptions of the same political system. Claim 1.1 reports "absolute power" for kings. Claim 4.1 reports that royal authority was "either absent or totally unrecognized." Both claims come from the same set of informants speaking to the same observer within the same three-year period. Possible explanations include:

(a) **Temporal layering**: informants described an idealized past ("absolute power") alongside their experience of the present ("absent authority"). The "absolute power" claim refers to an earlier era, and the transfer to matatoa represents genuine political change.

(b) **Dual registers**: informants described the ariki's ritual/sacred status ("regarded as gods," "absolute power" in a cosmological sense) alongside the practical political reality (matatoa held effective power). "Absolute power" referred to mana or tapu, not to political governance.

(c) **Post-raid idealization**: survivors, having lost their king in the slave raids, idealized the institution in retrospect. The claim of "absolute power" is nostalgic reconstruction rather than accurate description of pre-raid reality.

(d) **Roussel's own conflation**: Roussel may have combined different informants' accounts without recognizing that they described different aspects of authority (sacred vs. political).

For the chiefdom question, this contradiction means that Roussel's account cannot be cited straightforwardly as evidence for either a functioning chiefdom or its absence. It simultaneously provides the strongest evidence for hereditary sacred authority AND the strongest evidence that this authority lacked political substance.

**14.5. Pierre Loti (1872) as independent check**

Pierre Loti visited Easter Island in January 1872, during Roussel's period of residence (though the missionaries had apparently departed or died by that time, as the Dane tells Loti the missionaries "went away or died"). Loti's account (Altman 2004, Part 3, pp. 62-103) describes an "old chief" with tattoos and black feather headdress who receives Loti in his hut/cave and is described as "probably some kind of chief" (p. 69). The chief has a "chiefess" wife and daughter. Loti visits "all the important people on the island" (p. 70). This suggests that some form of differentiated status persisted in 1872, but Loti does not describe the chief exercising authority over anyone. The chief "spends his life kneeling, with his hands clasped on his blue-tattooed knees" in a cave (p. 82), which is consistent with Roussel's description of the ariki as a non-active figure.

## Summary Assessment

### What Roussel's Account Can Tell Us

Roussel provides the most detailed description of Rapa Nui's social categories, ritual practices, and reported traditions about political organization from the immediate post-raid period. His direct observations establish that:

1. A recognized ariki (Gregorio) existed as a distinct status category, marked by distinctive appearance (long hair) and ritual treatment (first-fruits ceremony, taboo on his person).
2. Effective political power was held not by the ariki but by the matatoa, who obtained their position through the birdman competition.
3. The four-category social system (ariki, ivi atua, matatoa, kio) was recognized by post-raid survivors.
4. Warfare was organized without chiefly direction and motivated by individual grievances.
5. Property and marine resources were held at the family/individual level.
6. The slave raids had destroyed the religious specialist class and much of the knowledge system (rongorongo).

### What Roussel's Account Cannot Tell Us

1. Whether the "absolute power" attributed to pre-raid kings reflects historical reality or post-raid idealization.
2. Whether the transfer of authority to the matatoa was a recent development or a longstanding structural pattern.
3. Whether the four-class system was a stable pre-contact institution or a post-raid simplification.
4. Whether the king list represents a genuine dynastic succession or a constructed genealogy.
5. What pre-raid political organization actually looked like in practice, as opposed to how survivors described it to a missionary three to six years after a demographic catastrophe.

### Implications for the Chiefdom Attribution

Roussel's account is routinely cited as evidence for a hierarchical chiefdom on Rapa Nui, but a close reading reveals that the evidence is far more ambiguous than typically acknowledged. The passages most supportive of a chiefdom interpretation (absolute power, uninterrupted succession, first-fruits obligations) are all reported tradition from post-raid survivors, and they are directly contradicted by Roussel's own observations and by other reported traditions in the same account (absent authority, matatoa dominance, warfare without chiefly involvement, no redistribution). The account describes a system in which sacred status (the ariki) and political power (the matatoa) were separated, with effective authority rotating annually through competitive ritual rather than being transmitted through hereditary succession. This is not a chiefdom in the Sahlins-Kirch sense of the term.
