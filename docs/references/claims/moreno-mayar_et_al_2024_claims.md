## I. Sample Description and Dating

1. "We reconstructed the genomic history of the Rapanui on the basis of 15 ancient Rapanui individuals that we radiocarbon dated (1670-1950 CE) and whole-genome sequenced (0.4-25.6x)." (p. 389, abstract)

2. "We sequenced the whole genomes from the 15 ancient individuals to an average depth of coverage between 0.4 and 25.6x (Supplementary Information sections 2 and 3 and Supplementary Table 1)." (p. 390)

3. "We obtained direct radiocarbon dates from 11 individuals at the Oxford Radiocarbon Accelerator Unit and Bristol Radiocarbon Accelerator Mass Spectrometer. Results are reported in radiocarbon years before present, corrected for isotopic fractionation using the 13C/12C ratio. The results were calibrated to take into account marine reservoir effects derived from the consumption of marine proteins, and broadly range between 1670 and 1950 CE." (p. 390)

4. "Although most of this range postdates European contact in 1722, these skeletal remains must pre-date the date in which they were collected according to the museum archives (that is, 1877 (11 individuals) and 1934-1935 CE (4 individuals))." (p. 390)

5. "Thus, it is unlikely that these individuals were born after the 1860s Peruvian slave raids and subsequent epidemics that decimated the island population to an estimated 110 individuals." (p. 390)

**Assessment**: The dating establishes that these individuals represent the pre-slave-raid population. This is significant because it means their genomes provide a window into Rapanui population history uncontaminated by the post-1860s demographic catastrophe that makes ethnohistoric sources unreliable.

## II. Genetic Ancestry and Origin

6. "We find that these individuals are Polynesian in origin and most closely related to present-day Rapanui, a finding that will contribute to repatriation efforts." (p. 389, abstract)

7. "We find that the ancient individuals shared the most drift with present-day Rapanui, followed by other Polynesian islanders." (p. 390)

8. "Concordantly, the ancient individuals remained most closely related to present-day Rapanui even when we considered a broader set of Oceanian reference individuals." (p. 390)

9. "Overall, our results demonstrate that the 15 ancient individuals sequenced in this study are Rapanui ancestors, most closely related to present-day Rapanui, also suggesting that the museum records are correct in this case." (p. 391)

## III. Biological Kinship and Relatedness

10. "Using READ and NGSrelate2, we did not find any first- or second-degree relatives among the Ancient Rapanui." (p. 391)

11. "We detected a single pair of third- to fourth-degree relatives on the basis of IBD sharing (highest estimated IBD sharing >15 cM between any two individuals is <1,000 cM; Supplementary Information section 8 and Supplementary Table 15)." (p. 391)

12. "Moreover, estimated inbreeding coefficients for all pairs of Ancient Rapanui were <0.01 (when using genotype likelihoods; <0.02 when estimating from imputed genotypes), showing that their immediate ancestors were not closely related to each other." (p. 391)

**Assessment**: The low inbreeding coefficients and absence of close relatives in a sample of 15 individuals is informative about population structure. It is consistent with a population large enough to avoid close-kin mating, or with social norms that prevented it. This bears on population size estimates, though the sample is small relative to the total population.

## IV. Runs of Homozygosity (ROH) and Historical Population Size

13. "Compared to other worldwide populations, Ancient Rapanui carry a large proportion of their genomes in ROH (for example, average length for Rapanui = 198 cM versus average length for Eurasia = 25 cM for hapROH; Fig. 2a and Supplementary Information section 10 and Supplementary Table 18)." (p. 391)

14. "However, most of these ROH are relatively short; on average, 80% of the ROH are <12 cM and none of the 15 individuals carries a long proportion of their genome in long ROH." (pp. 391-392)

15. "By contrast, in individuals with high consanguinity (for example, the Paiter Surui from southern Amazon and the Punjabi from Pakistan), a large proportion of ROH are long: the two Paiter Surui individuals carry more than 76% ROH >= 12 cM (>335 cM) and Punjabi-2 bears 18 cM out of 30 cM in ROH >= 12 cM." (p. 392)

16. "We observed that the Ancient Rapanui ROH distribution is similar to that of a present-day Rapanui genome (P2077). Nevertheless, the latter carries longer ROH than the ancient individuals (38 cM of the genome in >=20 cM runs)." (p. 392)

17. "The estimated inbreeding coefficients (Supplementary Information section 9.1.2) and the relative differences between the ROH length distributions in the Ancient Rapanui and populations with a history of consanguinity support the hypothesis that the Rapanui have a low historical effective population size. Yet unions between kins were seemingly infrequent pre-Peruvian slave raids and could have become more frequent in recent times." (p. 392)

**Assessment**: This is a key finding. The ROH pattern, dominated by short segments rather than long ones, is the genetic signature of a chronically small but not recently bottlenecked population. A catastrophic collapse in the 1600s would be expected to produce long ROH segments in individuals born after the bottleneck. The absence of long ROH in the ancient individuals is consistent with a population that was always small rather than one that crashed from a larger size. This directly bears on the collapse narrative: it supports a scenario of sustained small population size, not a dramatic decline from 15,000 to a few thousand.

## V. No Evidence for 1600s Population Collapse

18. "Through effective population size reconstructions and extensive population genetics simulations, we reject a scenario involving a severe population bottleneck during the 1600s, as proposed by the ecocide theory." (p. 389, abstract)

19. "We use the 15 Ancient Rapanui genomes to test the ecocide hypothesis using biological data as they postdate the proposed collapse and yet they are unlikely to be affected by the demographic events that followed the 1860s Peruvian slave raids." (p. 392)

20. "Our inferred HapNe-LD curve is pictured in Fig. 2b and Supplementary Fig. 28. It shows a decreasing population size that reaches its minimum about 28 generations (about 800 years) before the Ancient Rapanui time of birth, followed by a steady increase. These results do not suggest a population collapse in the 1600s, as argued for in the ecocide theory." (p. 392)

21. "We find that the HapNe-LD curve distance metric is minimized when we consider models with bottleneck 2 = 0% (no size reduction) and with a growth rate alpha < 0.3% per year. The observed data are not consistent with models in which bottleneck 2 after initial peopling of the island was strong or intermediate (when only 10-50% of the population remains) or when the growth rate after the first bottleneck (alpha) was high (>0.3% per year; Fig. 2c)." (p. 393)

22. "Furthermore, we obtain a P value < 10^-5 using a permutation test in which we split the simulations with Sb2 <= 50% (strong or intermediate bottleneck 2) from the ones with Sb2 > 50% (weak bottleneck 2)." (p. 393)

23. "We also used the total ROH in an individual (SROH) distribution as an alternative summary statistic to the HapNe-LD curves (Supplementary Information section 11.3). We found that the Ancient Rapanui SROH distribution did not match scenarios involving a very strong bottleneck (Sb2 <= 0.2; Supplementary Figs. 34 and 35 and Supplementary Table 24)." (p. 393)

24. "These results do not support a major population collapse on Rapa Nui after its initial peopling and before the 1800s. Rather, they suggest that the island was home to a small population whose effective size steadily increased after initial peopling of the island until the 1860s." (p. 393)

**Assessment**: This is the paper's central finding regarding the collapse narrative. Multiple independent analytical approaches (HapNe-LD curves, coalescent simulations across a parameter grid, SROH distributions) consistently reject the ecocide scenario of a catastrophic 1600s population decline. The genomic data instead support a population that was always small but gradually growing. This is directly relevant to the chiefdom question because: (1) it undermines the narrative arc of chiefdom rise followed by resource-driven collapse that structures much of the literature (Diamond 2005, Kirch 2017); (2) a population that was always small (Ne estimates suggest low thousands at most) raises questions about whether the demographic base existed to support the redistributive chiefdom organization attributed to Rapa Nui; (3) the steady growth pattern is more consistent with a resilient, adaptively managed population than with one that overexploited resources under chiefly command.

## VI. Population Size Trajectory

25. "European visitors to the island in the 1700s estimated that the Rapanui population size varied between 1,500 and 3,000 individuals, which corresponds to between 10% and 20% of the population surviving the population collapse proposed by the ecocide theory." (p. 389)

26. "According to some authors, the Rapanui census population size would have reached as many as 15,000 individuals. However, European records from the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries and Metraux's estimates suggest that the Rapanui population was as small as about 3,000 individuals." (p. 395)

27. "Although such a small population could have been the result of the proposed population collapse, this estimate is also compatible with a population that would have steadily grown after the initial peopling when assuming pre-industrial growth rates." (p. 395)

28. "These accounts are consistent with our inference that the effective population size monotonously increased after the initial peopling of the island but remained low during the past 1,000 years." (p. 395)

**Assessment**: The paper carefully distinguishes between census population size (what observers counted) and effective population size (what genetics estimates). The key point is that the contact-era estimates of 1,500-3,000 are consistent with the genetic data showing steady growth from a small founding population, without any need to invoke a prior larger population that collapsed. This removes the necessity of explaining a demographic catastrophe that may never have occurred.

---

## VII. Effective Population Size Estimates and Methods

29. "We reconstructed the Rapanui effective population size over the last 100 generations with HapNe-LD, which relies on linkage disequilibrium information and does not require high-confidence IBD calls." (p. 392)

30. "In population genetics, directly translating effective population sizes into census sizes is challenging owing to, for example, census inclusion criteria or variance in reproductive rates. Therefore, we follow common practice and interpret our subsequent simulation results (see below) qualitatively and in relative terms (that is, by focusing on the estimated effective population size trajectories and the timing of their shifts)." (p. 392)

31. "We highlight that the inferred HapNe-LD curves are not intended for estimating absolute effective population sizes. Rather, we use them to discriminate population histories using simulations." (p. 393)

**Assessment**: The authors are appropriately cautious about translating Ne to census size. This means the paper's results cannot directly determine whether the population was 3,000 or 15,000 in census terms. What they can determine is the trajectory: steady growth, not collapse-then-recovery.

## VIII. Native American Admixture

32. "The ancient and present-day Rapanui carry similar proportions of Native American admixture (about 10%)." (p. 389, abstract)

33. "Using a Bayesian approach integrating genetic and radiocarbon dates, we estimate that this admixture event occurred about 1250-1430 CE." (p. 389, abstract)

34. "All ancient and present-day Rapanui carried on average about 10% (6.0-11.4%) and about 8% (6.4-10.3%) of a Native American-like ancestry component, respectively." (p. 394)

35. "In contrast, European-like ancestry was only detected in present-day Rapanui, not in Ancient Rapanui." (p. 394)

36. "Using an f4-ratio of the form f4(Tonga, Yoruba; Ancient Rapanui, Native American)/f4(Tonga, Yoruba; Ancient Polynesians, Native American), we estimated 6.5-12.4% Native American admixture in the Ancient Rapanui." (p. 394)

37. "We infer the Native American component in Ancient Rapanui to be most closely related to Pacific Coast South Americans and not North Americans or populations east of the Andes." (pp. 395-396)

38. "Assuming 29 years per generation our favoured model gave a range of 1336-1402 CE (at 68.3% probability) and 1246-1425 CE (95.4%; Fig. 4d and Supplementary Information section 15)." (p. 394)

39. "These estimates overlap significantly with the most recent estimates for the peopling of Rapa Nui (1150-1280 CE) and strongly suggest that the admixture event did not pre-date the peopling." (p. 394)

40. "They show that the Polynesian ancestors of Rapanui were in contact with Native Americans significantly before the first appearance of Europeans on the island (364 +/- 41 years before 1722)." (p. 394)

**Assessment**: The Native American admixture finding, while primarily relevant to trans-Pacific contact questions, has secondary relevance to the chiefdom question. The ~10% Native American ancestry present in both ancient and modern Rapanui implies a single early admixture event, not ongoing contact. This suggests that by the time of European contact, the Rapanui population had been genetically isolated (from both American and Polynesian sources) for several centuries. This isolation is consistent with the archaeological evidence for cultural distinctiveness but raises questions about the applicability of Polynesian analogies to Rapa Nui's later prehistory.

## IX. European Admixture

41. "In contrast, European-like ancestry was only detected in present-day Rapanui, not in Ancient Rapanui." (p. 394)

42. "Although these individuals, radiocarbon dated to the 1800s, postdate the first arrival of Europeans in Rapa Nui, they do not bear European admixture. More importantly, they pre-date the Peruvian slave raids and the devastating smallpox outbreak of the 1860s." (p. 394)

**Assessment**: The absence of European admixture in the ancient individuals confirms that despite post-1722 European visits, the ancient sample represents an uncontaminated pre-contact gene pool. This reinforces the validity of using these genomes to make inferences about pre-contact population dynamics.

## X. Discussion: Resilience vs. Collapse

43. "Using biological (genomic) data, we found no evidence that the Rapanui underwent a population collapse in the 1600s, originally proposed to be a consequence of deforestation, resource overexploitation and warfare. Instead, our results support that the Rapanui population was resilient despite a changing environment." (pp. 394-395)

44. "Although trees once covered Rapa Nui, it has been proposed that their decline is likely to be a compound consequence of direct human action and the proliferation of rats brought by Polynesian settlers, as observed in other Polynesian islands." (p. 395)

45. "We highlight that our results (and the genomic data they are based on) shed light on the Rapanui demographic history exclusively. Thus, they cannot be used to directly evaluate the ecological impact of human activity on the island. Whereas anthropogenic impact is widespread in Polynesia, we specifically reject the hypothesis that such changes in Rapa Nui resulted in population collapse in the 1600s, before European contact." (p. 395)

**Assessment**: The authors carefully distinguish between environmental change (which they do not dispute) and the claim that environmental change caused a demographic catastrophe (which their data reject). This distinction is important for the chiefdom question: one can acknowledge deforestation and landscape transformation without accepting that these changes produced the societal collapse narrative that the chiefdom model's proponents have used to explain the archaeological record (moai toppling, Huri Moai phase, rise of the tangata manu cult, etc.).

## XI. Discussion: Contact-Era Population Estimates

46. "European visitors to the island in the 1700s estimated that the Rapanui population size varied between 1,500 and 3,000 individuals." (p. 389)

47. "According to some authors, the Rapanui census population size would have reached as many as 15,000 individuals." (p. 395)

48. "However, European records from the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries and Metraux's estimates suggest that the Rapanui population was as small as about 3,000 individuals. Although such a small population could have been the result of the proposed population collapse, this estimate is also compatible with a population that would have steadily grown after the initial peopling when assuming pre-industrial growth rates." (p. 395)

**Assessment**: The paper explicitly presents 1,500-3,000 as consistent with gradual growth, not as requiring a prior collapse. This reframes the contact-era population estimates in a way that undermines a key premise of the ecocide narrative: that a small contact-era population must have been the remnant of a larger pre-contact one. If the population was always in the low thousands, the entire narrative of resource overexploitation driven by population pressure requires reconsideration. This also bears on the chiefdom question: the demographic base for a complex redistributive chiefdom is harder to argue for a population of 1,500-3,000 on a 164 km2 island than for the 15,000 sometimes claimed.

---

## XII. Colonization Timing

49. "Archaeological, logical and genetic evidence shows that Polynesian peoples from the west had already reached the island about 1250 CE." (p. 389)

50. "These estimates overlap significantly with the most recent estimates for the peopling of Rapa Nui (1150-1280 CE)." (p. 394)

**Assessment**: The genetic evidence for colonization timing is consistent with the late colonization model (Hunt and Lipo 2006; Wilmshurst et al. 2011; DiNapoli et al. 2021). A late colonization date (ca. 1200 CE rather than 400-800 CE) compresses the time available for the development of the elaborate political hierarchy attributed to Rapa Nui by chiefdom models, making it more parsimonious to argue for simpler organizational forms.

## XIII. Founding Population and Bottleneck

51. "In our model, bottleneck 1 (Tb1 and Sb1) represents a bottleneck presumably associated with the initial peopling of the island, whereas bottleneck 2 (Tb2 and Sb2) represents the bottleneck associated with a potential population collapse as proposed in refs. 2, 3." (p. 393)

52. The HapNe-LD curve "shows a decreasing population size that reaches its minimum about 28 generations (about 800 years) before the Ancient Rapanui time of birth, followed by a steady increase." (p. 392)

**Assessment**: The initial colonization bottleneck (bottleneck 1) is detected in the genomic data and corresponds to the founding of the Rapa Nui population by a small group of Polynesian settlers. This is the expected genetic signature of island colonization. The key result is that a second bottleneck (corresponding to the proposed 1600s collapse) is not detected.

## XIV. Methodological Claims

53. "Several lines of bioanthropological, archaeological and historical evidence have been used to challenge the ecocide scenario. Yet the collapse hypothesis has remained very popular." (pp. 389-390)

54. "The only two ancient DNA studies of ancient Rapanui so far did not find evidence for Native American admixture. The first study focused on mitochondrial DNA from 12 individuals, whereas the second analysed low-depth (0.0004-0.0041x) whole-genome data from 5 individuals dating before and after European contact." (p. 390)

55. "However, even though the analysed human remains were post-dating the inferred Native American admixture time, no Native American ancestry was reported in these ancient genomes, casting doubt on the findings based on data from present-day populations." (p. 390)

56. "Through a downsampling experiment, we show that low depth can explain the absence of statistically significant D-statistics (Supplementary Fig. 15 and Supplementary Information section 7)." (p. 394)

**Assessment**: The resolution of the discrepancy between previous ancient DNA studies and present-day genetic data (regarding Native American admixture) through a downsampling experiment demonstrates the importance of sequencing depth. Previous studies lacked the statistical power to detect the admixture signal. This has implications for all ancient DNA studies of Pacific populations conducted at low coverage.

## XV. Community Engagement

57. "Throughout the course of the study, we met with representatives of the Rapanui community on the island, the Comision de Desarrollo Rapa Nui and the Comision Asesora de Monumentos Nacionales, where we presented our research goals and ongoing results. Both commissions voted in favour of us continuing with the research." (p. 390)

58. "The current manuscript we researched some of the topics suggested by the community including: kinship relationships between the 15 Ancient Rapanui individuals and how these relate to ancestral social structure; the genetic relationship between Ancient Rapanui, present-day Rapanui and other Polynesian islanders; the potential to explore the population size at the time of the peopling of the island; the difference between the time of the initial peopling of the island and the contact with Native Americans; the relationship between a potential 'cultural' collapse in the 1600s and the Rapanui population size." (pp. 399-400)

**Assessment**: The community engagement process is noteworthy because it demonstrates a model for conducting genetic research on Indigenous populations with meaningful community involvement. The Rapanui community's interest in questions about ancestral social structure is relevant to our project's concerns about how political organization is characterized.

## Summary of Claims Relevant to Chiefdom Attribution and Collapse Narrative

Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 provides the first high-quality ancient genomic data from Rapa Nui, with 15 individuals sequenced at sufficient depth to address population history questions. The paper's findings bear on the chiefdom and collapse questions in several ways:

### Claims that support the project argument

1. **No evidence for 1600s population collapse.** Multiple analytical approaches (HapNe-LD, coalescent simulations, ROH distributions) consistently reject the ecocide scenario. The population grew steadily from the founding bottleneck through European contact. This undermines the chiefdom-rise-then-collapse narrative that structures much Rapa Nui scholarship.

2. **Small but stable population.** The genomic data are consistent with a population that was always in the low thousands. If the population was never 15,000 (as some have claimed), the demographic basis for attributing a complex redistributive chiefdom to Rapa Nui is weaker.

3. **Contact-era estimates reframed.** The 1,500-3,000 contact-era population estimates are shown to be consistent with steady growth from a small founding population, not necessarily the remnant of a larger collapsed one.

4. **ROH pattern inconsistent with recent bottleneck.** Short ROH segments dominate, consistent with chronically small population size rather than a recent dramatic decline. Long ROH, the expected signature of a recent severe bottleneck, are absent.

5. **Late colonization confirmed.** The genetic evidence supports colonization ca. 1200 CE, consistent with the archaeological evidence (Hunt and Lipo 2006; DiNapoli et al. 2021). This compresses the timeline for developing complex hierarchical political organization.

6. **Pre-contact genetic isolation.** The single early Native American admixture event (ca. 1250-1430 CE) with no subsequent gene flow from any external source implies centuries of genetic isolation, which complicates the use of Polynesian analogies from more connected archipelagos.

### Claims that do not directly bear on (or complicate) the project argument

7. The paper does not address political organization, social hierarchy, or the chiefdom concept directly. Its findings concern demographic history, not governance.

8. The paper cannot distinguish between a population of 1,500 and 3,000 in census terms (the Ne-to-census-size translation is acknowledged as uncertain). Either figure might or might not be sufficient to support chiefdom-level organization.

9. The environmental changes on Rapa Nui (deforestation, rat proliferation) are not disputed by this paper. The paper rejects the demographic consequence (collapse) but not the ecological transformation. Chiefdom proponents could in principle argue that chiefly organization existed within a small, ecologically transformed population.

10. Low inbreeding coefficients suggest kin avoidance in mating, which could be interpreted as consistent with social rules about kinship and marriage, as would be expected in any Polynesian society regardless of whether it had a chiefdom-level political organization.
