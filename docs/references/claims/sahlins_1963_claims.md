## Overview

This is the article that crystallizes the big-man vs. chief distinction, placing Melanesia on the "big-man" side and Polynesia on the "chief" side as contrasting political types. Easter Island is mentioned once, as a geographic boundary marker (p. 285). The article contains no Easter Island-specific claims whatsoever. The "Polynesian chief" type is built overwhelmingly from Hawaiian data (Malo, Fornander), with secondary support from Tahitian (Handy 1930), Tongan (Gifford 1929, Mariner 1827), and Mangarevan (Buck 1938) evidence.

The article's significance for the chiefdom genealogy is not in what it says about Easter Island but in what it does to Easter Island by implication: by defining Polynesia as the domain of "chiefs properly so-called" (p. 289), Sahlins places every Polynesian island on the "chief" side of the binary without examining whether the evidence supports this for each island individually.

## I. The Big-Man / Chief Distinction

### The contrast

1. **Two political types** (p. 289): "An historically particular type of leader-figure, the 'big-man' as he is often locally styled, appears in the underdeveloped settings of Melanesia. Another type, a chief properly so-called, is associated with the Polynesian advance."

2. **Big-man = personal power** (p. 289): "Big-men do not come to office; they do not succeed to, nor are they installed in, existing positions of leadership over political groups. The attainment of big-man status is rather the outcome of a series of acts which elevate a person above the common herd."

3. **Chief = office** (pp. 294-295): "The pivotal paramount chief as well as the chieftains controlling parts of a chiefdom were true office holders and title holders. They were not, like Melanesian big-men, fishers of men: they held positions of authority over permanent groups."

4. **Power assigned to office, not person** (p. 295): "Power resided in the office; it was not made by the demonstration of personal superiority."

5. **Structural vs. personal** (p. 295): "the qualities of command that had to reside in men in Melanesia, that had to be personally demonstrated in order to attract loyal followers, were in Polynesia socially assigned to office and rank."

### Scale

6. **Melanesian polities small** (pp. 286-287): "ordered, independent political bodies in the region typically include seventy to three hundred persons."

7. **Polynesian polities large** (p. 287): "in Polynesia sovereignties of two thousand or three thousand are run-of-the-mill, and the most advanced chiefdoms, as in Tonga or Hawaii, might claim ten thousand, even tens of thousands."

### Structure

8. **Melanesian = segmental** (p. 287): "The tribal plan is one of politically unintegrated segments -- segmental."

9. **Polynesian = pyramidal** (p. 287): "the Polynesian polity is an extensive pyramid of groups capped by the family and following of a paramount chief."

10. **Conical clan as structural basis** (pp. 287-288): "This Polynesian political upshot is often, although not always, facilitated by the development of ranked lineages. Called conical clan by Kirchhoff, at one time ramage by Firth and status lineage by Goldman."

## II. The Polynesian Chief: Sources and Characterization

### Chiefly economic power

11. **KEY: Chiefs as "owners" of group resources** (p. 296): "Masters of their people and 'owners' in a titular sense of group resources, Polynesian chiefs had rights of call upon the labor and agricultural produce of households within their domains."

12. **Tabu as economic tool** (p. 296): "the widespread chiefly privilege, related to titular 'ownership' of land, of placing an interdiction, a tabu, on the harvest of some crop by way of reserving its use for a collective project."

13. **Chiefs generate surplus** (p. 296): "By means of the tabu the chief directs the course of production in a general way... Most significantly, he has generated a politically utilizable agricultural surplus."

14. **Hawaiian storehouses** (pp. 296-297): Malo quoted: kings built storehouses "as a means of keeping the people contented, so they would not desert the king."

### Redistribution as "supreme art"

15. **KEY: "Redistribution of the fund of power was the supreme art of Polynesian politics"** (p. 297): "By well-planned noblesse oblige the large domain of a paramount chief was held together, organized at times for massive projects, protected against other chiefdoms, even further enriched."

16. **Uses of the fund** (p. 297): "lavish hospitality and entertainments for outside chiefs and for the chief's own people, and succor of individuals or the underlying population at large in times of scarcities -- bread and circuses."

17. **Chiefs subsidized construction and war** (p. 297): "They initiated large-scale religious construction too, subsidized the great ceremonies, and organized logistic support for extensive military campaigns."

### The Polynesian contradiction

18. **Advanced chiefdoms siphoned funds for administration** (p. 297): "In the most advanced Polynesian chiefdoms, as in Hawaii and Tahiti, a significant part of the chiefly fund was deflected away from general redistribution towards the upkeep of the institution of chieftainship."

19. **Armed enforcement** (p. 298): "A Tahitian or Hawaiian high chief had more compelling sanctions than the harangue. He controlled a ready physical force, an armed body of executioners, which gave him mastery particularly over the lesser people of the community."

20. **Goldman cited for status rivalry** (p. 298): "the recent work of Irving Goldman on the intensity of 'status rivalry' in Polynesia, especially when it is considered that much of the status rivalry in developed chiefdoms, as the Hawaiian, amounted to popular rebellion against chiefly despotism."

21. **Cycles of centralization and decentralization** (p. 299): "In Hawaii and other islands cycles of political centralization and decentralization may be abstracted from traditional histories. That is, larger chiefdoms periodically fragmented into smaller and then were later reconstituted."

22. **"Eat the power of the government too much"** (p. 298): Tahitian chiefs were "inclined to 'eat the power of the government too much,' as the Tahitians put it, to divert an undue proportion of the general wealth toward the chiefly establishment."

### The evolutionary ceiling

23. **Polynesian tragedy = extraction from populace** (p. 300): "In Polynesia, the evolutionary ceiling was set by extraction from the population at large in favor of the chiefly faction, in Melanesia by extraction from the big-man's faction in favor of distribution to the population at large."

24. **Polynesian ceiling higher** (p. 300): "Most importantly, the Polynesian ceiling was higher."

25. **Chiefs' leverage on household output** (p. 300): "the more decisive impact of Polynesian chiefs on the economy, the chiefs' greater leverage on the output of the several households of society."

## III. Easter Island in This Article

26. **Easter Island mentioned once** (p. 285): "Polynesia, consisting in its main portion of the triangular constellation of lands between New Zealand, Easter Island, and the Hawaiian Islands." This is a geographic definition. Easter Island is not discussed, analyzed, or cited anywhere else in the article.

27. **No Easter Island sources cited**: The references include no Easter Island-specific works (no Metraux, no Routledge, no Thomson, no Heyerdahl).

28. **The "Polynesian chief" is built from other islands**: The Polynesian chief characterization relies on: Hawaii (Malo 1903, Fornander 1880), Tahiti (Handy 1930, Henry 1928), Tonga (Gifford 1929, Mariner 1827), Mangareva (Buck 1938), Marquesas (Handy 1923, Linton 1939), and Tikopia (Firth 1950, 1957). Sahlins's own 1958 monograph (*Social Stratification in Polynesia*) is cited once (p. 287) for population figures.

## IV. Methodological Notes

29. **Self-described method** (p. 285, fn.): "The comparative method so far followed in this research has involved reading the monographs and taking notes. I don't think I originated the method, but I would like to christen it -- The Method of Uncontrolled Comparison."

30. **Sociological types, not empirical descriptions** (p. 285, fn.): "The two forms are abstracted sociological types. Anyone conversant with the anthropological literature of the South Pacific knows there are important variants of the types, as well as exceptional political forms not fully treated here."

31. **Preliminary, not comprehensive** (p. 285, fn.): "The present paper is preliminary to a wider and more detailed comparison of Melanesian and Polynesian polities and economies. I have merely abstracted here some of the more striking political differences in the two areas."

32. **Gradient, not binary** (p. 286): "There is more of an upward west to east slope in political development in the southern Pacific than a step-like, quantum progression." Yet the article proceeds to compare the "extremes of this continuum."

33. **Tikopia acknowledged as not typical** (p. 289, fn. 7): "Tikopia, however, is not typical of the more advanced Polynesian chiefdoms with which we are principally concerned here."

34. **Service 1962 listed as "in press"** (p. 303): "Service, Elman R., Primitive Social Organization: An Evolutionary Perspective (New York: Random House, in press)." Confirms this article was written contemporaneously with Service's formalization of the chiefdom stage.

## VI. Significance for Our Argument

35. **The binary places all Polynesia on the "chief" side by default.** The article's structure -- Melanesia as big-man, Polynesia as chief -- means Easter Island is implicitly classified as a "chief" society simply by being geographically Polynesian. No island-specific examination is offered or required.

36. **The "Polynesian chief" is built from Hawaii, Tahiti, and Tonga.** The ethnographic evidence for chiefly power, redistribution, tabu-based surplus extraction, armed enforcement, and administrative establishments comes from the most complex Polynesian societies. The characterization is then attributed to "Polynesia" as a whole.

37. **Sahlins acknowledges this is a typological abstraction, not an empirical generalization.** He calls them "abstracted sociological types" and notes "variants" and "exceptional political forms." But the article's rhetorical force works against these qualifications: the big-man/chief binary becomes the standard framework for Pacific political typology.

38. **The article's contribution to the circularity chain:**

The inferential sequence is:
- Sahlins 1954/1958: classifies Easter Island on a Polynesian stratification gradient, acknowledging data gaps
- Sahlins 1963: creates a binary that places all Polynesia on the "chief" side, built from the most complex cases, without island-specific examination
- Service 1962 (contemporaneous): formalizes "chiefdom" as a universal evolutionary stage, drawing partly on Sahlins's Polynesian work
- Kirch 1984: applies the formalized chiefdom concept back to Easter Island archaeologically, inheriting the assumption that Easter Island, being Polynesian, is a chiefdom

Sahlins 1963 contributes to this chain by making the big-man/chief distinction so influential that the question shifts from "was Easter Island a chiefdom?" to "what kind of chiefdom was Easter Island?" The category is assumed; only the variation within it is examined.

39. **Goldman's counter-evidence is already available but not engaged.** Sahlins cites Goldman 1955, 1957, and 1960 for "status rivalry" (p. 298) but does not note that Goldman's framework classifies some Polynesian societies (Mangaia, Easter Island, Marquesas, Samoa, Niue) as "Open," where military/political power has displaced hereditary chiefly authority. The Goldman citations are used to support the observation that even developed chiefdoms generate "countervailing anti-authority pressures" (p. 298), but the deeper implication, that some Polynesian societies may not fit the "chief" type at all, is not drawn.

40. **What Easter Island actually looks like in this framework.** If we take Sahlins's own descriptions seriously, Easter Island does not match the "Polynesian chief" type:
- **Chiefs as office holders over permanent groups** (p. 295): Goldman (1970) shows the matatoa held achieved, not ascribed, positions
- **Chiefly rights over labor and agricultural produce** (p. 296): Goldman (1970) shows redistribution lay with commoner heads, not the ariki mau
- **Tabu as economic tool** (p. 296): Sahlins 1954 (p. 105) noted the ariki mau's only sanctions were supernatural, not secular
- **Administrative establishment, storehouses, armed corps** (pp. 297-298): None documented for Easter Island by any source
- **Large-scale construction under chiefly direction** (p. 297): Kirch 1984 (p. 270) and Goldman (1970, pp. 106-107) both deny centralized monument construction

Easter Island fits the "big-man" pattern in some respects (achieved leadership by matatoa, personal followings, factional warfare, competitive status rivalry) better than the "chief" pattern Sahlins describes. This is precisely what Goldman's "Open" classification captures.
