# Claims Extraction: Mulloy 1979

**Source**: Mulloy, William T. 1979. "A Preliminary Culture-Historical Research Model for Easter Island." In *Las Islas Oceánicas de Chile*, Vol. 1, pp. 105-151. Santiago: Instituto de Estudios Internacionales, Universidad de Chile. Reprinted (excerpted) in *Rapa Nui Notes* 7, Spring 1988.

**Type**: Culture-historical synthesis; the last paper Mulloy wrote before his death in 1978. Excerpt only (3 pages of a 47-page paper).

**Caveat**: The PDF is a 3-page excerpt from *Rapa Nui Notes* 7 (Spring 1988), not the full 47-page paper published in 1979. Claims extraction is limited to what appears in the excerpt. The full paper may contain additional relevant material.

**Date processed**: 2026-03-16

## I. Political Organization and Labor

### A. "Class Organized Society"

1. "A class organized society with enough coercive power to bring large work crews together and maintain them in systematic operation long enough to produce the remarkable amount of religious architecture and sculpture present on the island certainly possessed a considerable degree of political organization." (p. 2 of excerpt; corresponds to original pp. 105-106)

   **Note**: This is Mulloy's only statement on political organization in the excerpt. He assumes "class organized society" and "coercive power" but does NOT cite Service, Sahlins, Goldman, or any theoretical framework. He does not use the term "chiefdom," "chief," "hierarchy," or "redistribution." The assumption of coercive power for labor mobilization is stated without evidence and contrasts with the collective action interpretation (Lipo et al. 2013, 2025) and with Mulloy & Figueroa 1966's own attribution of ahu variation to kin group "numbers and capacity."

## II. Evidential Limitations

### A. Post-Slave-Raid Source Unreliability

2. "It appears that on Easter Island the events which terminated the period of climax were recent enough nearly to erase from the minds of the relatively few survivors much of the detail of the classic form of the culture and prevent its survival in legendary form." (p. 3 of excerpt)

3. "Drastic reductions of population in the 19th Century due to devastating slave raids and a smallpox epidemic further reduced population and memories of the past before many systematic observers were present." (p. 3 of excerpt)

4. "In 1877 the total population of the island is said to be only 111 people. Such a small number could have retained only a fragment of the former cultural inventory and apparently most of the collective culture-historical memory was lost." (p. 3 of excerpt)

5. "Thus only a demoralized and to a considerable extent deculturated remnant population has been available as culture-historical informants and a proportionately much greater reliance has had to be placed on archaeological evidence for an understanding of what went on here than has been true on many other Pacific islands." (p. 3 of excerpt)

   **Note**: Claims 2-5 constitute an explicit acknowledgment that post-slave-raid ethnographic sources are unreliable for reconstructing pre-contact political organization. Mulloy recognizes that the demographic collapse destroyed cultural memory and that archaeological evidence must carry greater weight than on other Pacific islands. This anticipates the epistemological argument of the current paper.

### B. Archaeological Evidence Insufficiency

6. "The presently available body of archaeological evidence is far from adequate to permit a reliable and detailed general reconstruction of the local prehistory." (p. 3 of excerpt)

7. "Information about a 'Settlement Period,' occupying the earliest phase of the local development, is as yet entirely lacking." (p. 3 of excerpt)

8. "Though we have some information about domestic architecture, a coherent knowledge of it in a time perspective is lacking." (p. 3 of excerpt)

9. "Land-use patterns have been studied, though we have little understanding of the course of their development." (p. 3 of excerpt)

10. "Information about modifications of the environment due to human exploitation is sketchy though of central importance to an understanding of developments." (p. 3 of excerpt)

11. "Studies of artifacts through time and suitable for comparison with those of other islands are little developed." (p. 3 of excerpt)

   **Note**: Claims 6-11 document Mulloy's own assessment of the state of archaeological knowledge as of the late 1970s. The record was "far from adequate" for reliable reconstruction. Yet subsequent chiefdom attributions (Kirch 1984) were applied to this same evidence base.

## III. Methodological Approach

### A. Tentative Model

12. "It is, however, possible to outline a sort of tentative model, not in the sense of a foreign historical sequence which local developments might be expected to parallel, but in the sense of a scenario which fits the scanty and incomplete local collection of information presently commonly taken to be fact." (p. 3 of excerpt)

13. "Such a model can be no more than one possible historical appreciation of the facts and would be expected continuously to be modified as new information became available." (p. 3 of excerpt)

14. "Its principle value, beyond being a preliminary ordering of the materials, is as a research tool." (p. 3 of excerpt)

15. "Written in the present state of knowledge, the above essay makes no pretense of conveying any precise or ultimate historical appreciation. It represents one reasonable interpretation of the scanty and incomplete collection of facts now available." (p. 3 of excerpt)

16. "Studies aimed at testing this framework will surely modify the model and may ultimately demonstrate that it should be discarded." (p. 4 of excerpt)

   **Note**: Claims 12-16 show Mulloy explicitly framing his model as tentative, preliminary, and subject to revision or discard. He rejects the imposition of "a foreign historical sequence" on local developments (Claim 12), which is precisely what Kirch 1984 subsequently did with the Polynesian chiefdom model. The contrast between Mulloy's epistemic caution and the subsequent treatment of the chiefdom attribution as established fact is stark.

## IV. Cultural Chronology and Ahu

### A. Phase Sequence

17. Mulloy describes a sequence from earliest settlement through "greater complexity" and "a climax which was followed by a period of population decrease, disorganization and decadence. All of this happened before the time of the first European contact." (p. 3 of excerpt)

   **Note**: The ecocide/collapse narrative appears already in Mulloy's framework (pre-contact climax followed by decline), but he attributes it to "over-population" and internal dynamics, not to chiefdom collapse. The narrative was later absorbed into Kirch's three-phase scheme.

### B. Ahu Destruction Hypothesis

18. "More studies aimed at determining the exact pattern of destruction of earlier image ahu are needed to test the hypothesis that these were destroyed by elements of the societies of their owners rather than by enemy groups from other localities." (p. 4 of excerpt)

   **Note**: Mulloy proposes that ahu may have been destroyed by their own communities (internal), not by external enemies. If correct, this challenges the warfare-driven collapse narrative and suggests that ahu destruction may reflect internal social processes within kin groups rather than inter-polity conflict.

### C. Ahu Architectural Sequence

19. "A recent comparative study of eleven dated ahu chronologically spread widely over the sequence has supported the hypothesis (Mulloy and Figueroa)." [Referring to the hypothesis of continuous local development without sharp outside influence.] (p. 4 of excerpt)

### D. Research Priorities

20. "The analysis of a part of the survey evidence by Patrick McCoy represents a most useful approach and must be extended to other parts of the island." (p. 3 of excerpt)

21. "The fact that the lowest levels of these caves, which might reasonably have been expected to have attracted early immigrants, consistently produced dates significantly later than some of the developed ceremonial architecture is a significant though somewhat surprising datum suggesting that 'Settlement Period' evidence is not to be sought in these contexts." (p. 3-4 of excerpt) [Referring to "a study recently completed by William Ayers"]

22. "The hypothesis that there was a change in burial customs beginning with this period from cremation to inhumation requires further testing." (p. 4 of excerpt)

23. "The late period of internal violence remains relatively unstudied in detail." (p. 4 of excerpt)

24. "Much greater attention needs to be given soon to the development of a collection of dated and context-established skeletal material for physical anthropological studies." (p. 4 of excerpt)

## V. Terminology Audit

**Terms present**: "class organized society," "coercive power," "political organization," "kin groups" (used in the editorial preamble discussing Mulloy's career but not in the excerpt text proper)

**Terms absent**: "chiefdom," "chief," "hierarchy," "redistribution," "stratification," "ranked," "Service," "Sahlins," "Goldman," "ramage," "big man," "paramount"

**Significance**: Mulloy's last published work uses neither chiefdom terminology nor the Service/Sahlins typological framework. His assumption of "class organized society" and "coercive power" is stated without theoretical justification and is not developed into a chiefdom argument. This confirms that Mulloy himself, who spent more time on Easter Island than any other archaeologist of his generation, did not frame Easter Island's political organization in chiefdom terms.
