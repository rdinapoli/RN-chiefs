# Claims Extraction: Sahlins 1955

**Source**: Marshall D. Sahlins, "Esoteric Efflorescence in Easter Island." *American Anthropologist*, New Series, Vol. 57, No. 5 (October 1955), pp. 1045-1052.

**PDF location**: `docs/references/pdfs/sahlins_1955/Sahlins_1955.pdf`

**Context**: Published while Sahlins was at Columbia, between the 1954 PhD dissertation and the 1957 JPS article. This is the only Sahlins article focused entirely on Easter Island. It was cited in the 1957 article (fn. 12, p. 295) for Easter Island's cultural derivation from central Polynesia. The article appeared in AA's "Brief Communications" section (8 pages).

---

## I. Article Structure and Hypothesis

1. **Central hypothesis**: "It is submitted that, due to unique environmental features of Easter Island, this communal and specialist productive capacity was largely channeled into an esoteric domain rather than into subsistence activities." (p. 1047)

2. **Hypothesis scope**: "The aims of the hypothesis are moderate. It seeks only to establish the cultural and environmental factors which make the great expenditure of labor on this esoteric endeavor intelligible." (p. 1047)

3. **What the hypothesis does not explain**: "Without more specific information about the role of the statues in the entire culture than is available, such a question as why heads instead of bodies, or why human images rather than animal, cannot be definitively answered." (p. 1047)

4. **Argument structure**: Three premises lead to the conclusion:
   - (a) Easter Island social organization was of the ramage type, suited to organizing communal and specialist production (from central-eastern Polynesian heritage)
   - (b) Environmental conditions precluded most forms of communal and specialist labor in subsistence production
   - (c) Therefore, productive capacity was "channeled into an esoteric domain"

## II. Easter Island's Polynesian Heritage (pp. 1045-1046)

5. **Statue origin**: "Metraux finds no support for any hypothesis other than that the statues were erected by the Easter Islanders themselves." (p. 1045, citing Metraux 1940:412-420)

6. **Post-contact knowledge loss acknowledged**: "The fact that since missionary times the natives have not been able to explain the significance of the statues is not perplexing in view of the early disastrous effects of contacts with European slave raiders and diseases, contacts which decimated the population before missionaries arrived (pp. 36ff.)." (p. 1045) [NOTE: This acknowledges knowledge loss from European contact and slave raids]

7. **Knowledge loss parallel**: "The ignorance of modern Easter Islanders with respect to the meaning of the statues is no more mystifying than their present ignorance of the use of the stone adzes which were the major cutting tools on the island three or four generations ago (p. 306)." (pp. 1045-1046) [Source: Metraux 1940:306]

8. **Cultural derivation**: "There exists conclusive evidence that the images are a product of the local culture, a culture which stems from a central-eastern Polynesian source. Archeological remains document the historic unity of Easter Island culture (p. 415)." (p. 1045) [Source: Metraux 1940:415]

9. **Racial continuity**: "The Easter Islanders do not show admixture with other groups. In physical traits the Easter Islanders are allied to Polynesian peoples; the few morphological extremes can be attributed to genetic developments in isolation (Shapiro 1940:24-27)." (p. 1046) [Source: Shapiro 1940]

10. **Linguistic evidence**: "No loanwords have been found in the Easter Island language; all place names are Polynesian (Metraux 1940:415). Recent historical linguistic research shows that the language is genetically related to the eastern variety of Polynesian, and the date of the differentiation of Easter Island language from proto-eastern Polynesian has been set (by glottochronology) at between A.D. 300 and A.D. 530 (Elbert 1953)." (p. 1046) [Source: Elbert 1953; Metraux 1940:415]

11. **Image carving as Polynesian trait**: "The carving of images is itself a central-eastern Polynesian trait, part of the historical tradition of the area (pp. 40-45). Most image carving in east Polynesia is in wood, it is true; but on the other hand, wood suitable for carving was relatively rare in Easter Island (see below)." (p. 1046) [Source: Metraux 1940:40-45]

12. **Scale problem stated**: "Although there can be little doubt that the stone images were created by Easter Islanders and that a tradition of image carving was part of their historical background, this local and unique development is not thereby explained. A tremendous amount of human energy was expended in carving the statues and in transporting and erecting them without the wheel or other advanced mechanical aids." (p. 1046)

13. **Metraux's weight estimate**: "Metraux conservatively estimates that some of the statues weigh up to thirty tons (1940:304)." (p. 1046)

## III. Critique of Metraux's Explanation (p. 1046)

14. **Metraux's hypothesis quoted in full**: "The desire for display was certainly a predominant motive for the carving of these giant statues, but it is questionable whether this was sufficient to provoke as much energy as that required for the transportation of enormous statues. On the other hand, to assume that the motive for making the images was entirely religious is to underestimate the tribal pride and competitive instinct of Polynesians [p. 307]." (p. 1046, quoting Metraux 1940:307) [NOTE: Metraux's "competitive instinct" framing is actually closer to what the ethnographic evidence supports than Sahlins's chiefly-command interpretation]

15. **Sahlins's objection**: "The explanation proposed by Metraux is inadequate. As all Polynesian groups had ornaments and most if not all had ornamental carvings of some sort, we can assume the 'desire to display' to be a constant in Polynesia. The same can be said, as Metraux indicates, for 'tribal pride and competitive instinct.' Differences between cultures, particularly unique differences, cannot be explained by constants." (p. 1046) [NOTE: This is methodologically sound as a general point but dismisses the competitive-instinct factor that our pipeline shows is better supported by the evidence than chiefly command]

## IV. Central-Eastern Polynesian Social Organization (pp. 1047-1049)

16. **Ramage definition**: "A ramage is a nonexogamous, internally stratified group of people tracing descent, usually patrilineally, to a common ancestor." (p. 1047) [Source: Firth 1936:367-372]

17. **Single ramified systems**: "The central-eastern Polynesian societies considered here were often single ramified social systems. The sociopolitical unit, which might be an entire island, can usually be diagrammed on one genealogical tree." (p. 1047)

18. **Redistributive economy**: "In central-eastern Polynesia, the ramified social system was the framework of economic processes, especially those of distribution and production. Extrahousehold distributions took two forms: reciprocal exchanges between households, a relatively minor form; and the more spectacular, major form, redistribution." (p. 1048)

19. **Redistribution mechanism described**: "In redistributions, surplus production, i.e., food, mats, bark-cloth and other stuffs not immediately consumed by the producers, was periodically collected and passed up the stratified hierarchy of household heads and heads of larger ramages, often up to the paramount chief of the social unit." (p. 1048) [NOTE: This describes the model for central-eastern Polynesia generally; the question is whether it applied to Easter Island specifically]

20. **Chiefs' control over communal labor**: "The controls exerted by chiefs over communal and specialist production were far-reaching. By virtue of their roles in the redistributive system, the chiefs initiated and supported the great bulk of these types of production." (p. 1048) [NOTE: Stated as a general Polynesian pattern]

21. **Types of communal labor**: "Mass labor was used in several or all of the following types of work in each locale: house building, large canoe building, irrigation works construction, fishing, bush clearing, fortress construction and temple construction." (p. 1048)

22. **Scale examples**: "In societies wherein large amounts of surplus could be produced, as, for example, Hawaii, thousands of men might be involved simultaneously in a single task (Ellis 1825:215)." (p. 1048) [Source: Ellis 1825:215]

23. **Specialist production examples**: "There were several types of specialists: canoe builders, house builders, master fishers, tattooers, priests, etc." (p. 1048)

24. **Mutually reinforcing controls**: "These forms of economic, social, and political controls were mutually reinforcing." (p. 1049)

25. **Reinforcement mechanism**: "The reinforcing aspect of these economic and social differentials is most marked when communal or specialized labor is expended in productivity-increasing, subsistence activities. A large surplus permits a large mobilization of manpower. When this manpower is applied to activities which directly affect the subsistence level, another large surplus, capable of sustaining more labor, is produced." (pp. 1048-1049) [NOTE: This reinforcement loop requires subsistence-increasing communal labor. Sahlins will argue Easter Island lacked this, which means the reinforcement loop is broken.]

## V. Easter Island Section (pp. 1049-1051)

### Social Organization

26. **CRITICAL CLAIM: Ramage classification**: "The social system of Easter Island was clearly of the ramage type." (p. 1049) [NOTE: No qualification; stated as established fact. Compare dissertation where this was acknowledged as ecologically anomalous]

27. **Cultural derivation assumption**: "As the culture of Easter Island was derived from central-eastern Polynesia, it may be assumed that major aspects of social organization were likewise so derived." (p. 1049) [NOTE: This assumption is the Polynesian analogy at work]

28. **Population estimate**: "The population of Easter Island at contact was between three and four thousand (Metraux 1940:20-22)." (p. 1049) [Source: Metraux 1940:20-22]

29. **Segmentary structure**: "The society was segmented into ten high-order ramages, mata (translated as 'tribe' by Metraux). Each mata traced patrilineal descent from a common ancestor, often a son or a grandson of the traditional original settler of the island." (p. 1049) [Source: Metraux 1940]

30. **Miru and ariki-mau**: "One mata, the Miru, claimed direct descent in the senior line from the original settler, and supplied the paramount sacred chief of the island, the ariki-mau. Members of the Miru were called 'chiefly men' (ariki-papa) and were accorded special esteem." (p. 1049) [Source: Metraux 1940]

31. **Lower-order segmentation**: "Mata were segmented into ramages of lower order ('lineages'), composed of a number of households tracing common descent from a near relative of the common mata ancestor." (p. 1049) [Source: Metraux 1940]

32. **Seniority and succession**: "The principle of seniority pervaded the entire society. Barring personality defects, primogeniture was the usual rule of succession to positions of authority." (p. 1049)

### Distribution and Production

33. **CRITICAL CLAIM: Redistribution as predominant method**: "The predominant method of extrahousehold distribution was redistribution." (p. 1049) [NOTE: Stated flatly here; contrast with dissertation where Sahlins admitted "little direct evidence" of redistribution for Easter Island specifically (1954, p. 101)]

34. **DATA GAP ADMISSION #1**: "Details are often lacking because of the badly disrupted nature of the culture at the time of scientific study." (p. 1049)

35. **Feast redistribution**: "Several types of feasts are described which involved food redistribution by high ranking men (pp. 333ff.)." (p. 1049) [Source: Metraux 1940:333ff.]

36. **Annual feast example**: "An annual feast was attended by people 'from all over the island' who had come to hear priest-chanters 'read' the famous Easter Island script (pp. 390-91). The food for this feast was supplied by the ariki-mau, 'who was aided by the people of neighboring districts' (p. 390)." (p. 1049) [Source: Metraux 1940:390-391] [POST-RAID]

37. **DATA GAP ADMISSION #2**: "Although information is not conclusive, it is apparent that ramage heads, through control of surplus produce, controlled also communal and specialist production." (pp. 1049-1050) [NOTE: "information is not conclusive" is a significant qualification, but the sentence then proceeds as if the evidence supports the conclusion anyway]

38. **CRITICAL PASSAGE: Stone carver evidence**: "Metraux reports, for example, 'Tepano told me that expert stone carvers ... received orders from people who wanted a monument for their ahu. They worked under the leadership of a master ... and were paid in fish, lobsters and eels' (p. 137)." (p. 1050) [Source: Metraux 1940:137, reporting informant Tepano] [POST-RAID]

39. **Sahlins's inference from ahu as lineage terraces**: "As ahu were lineage burial terraces, it may be inferred that the lineage head gave the 'order.'" (p. 1050) [NOTE: This inference is critical. Sahlins reads "people who wanted a monument for their ahu" through the chiefly-command lens, inferring that the lineage head must have given the order. But the Metraux passage says "people" (plural), not "the chief." The evidence describes lineage-level commissioning by group decision, paid through direct food exchange, not centralized chiefly command through redistribution.]

### The Environmental Channeling Argument (pp. 1050-1051)

40. **Socioeconomic system identified with central-eastern Polynesian pattern**: "The socioeconomic system in Easter Island is identical in principle to that system which predominated in central-eastern Polynesia, whence Easter Island culture derived." (p. 1050)

41. **CORE ARGUMENT: Environmental channeling**: "The first migrants to Easter Island arrived with an organization, political, social and economic, of which communal labor and specialist production were integral aspects. But the original immigrants encountered an environment in Easter Island that was peculiar in many ways when compared to central-eastern Polynesian islands. With the technology at hand, the environmental conditions largely precluded the use of communal or specialist labor in production which directly affected subsistence, and fostered the use of communal and specialist labor in such esoteric pursuits as erecting stone images." (p. 1050)

42. **Environmental constraints catalogued**: "Outstanding among the peculiarities of the local environment are: porous soil, lack of streams or rivers, very limited wood resources, and the absence of reefs, especially of a barrier reef and its accompanying lagoon (pp. 7-19, passim; Skottsberg 1920)." (p. 1050) [Source: Metraux 1940:7-19; Skottsberg 1920]

43. **Constraint #1: No irrigation**: "Without rivers or streams, irrigation is impossible; hence, the utilization of manpower on a large scale for waterworks construction, such as occurred in Hawaii, Rarotonga, and probably Tahiti, was ruled out in Easter Island." (p. 1050) [Comparative data: Perry 1913:94 for 700-man Hawaiian irrigation ditch]

44. **Constraint #2: No bush clearing**: "The absence of forests obviated problems of bush clearing in Easter Island." (p. 1050) [Comparative data: Best 1924, II:377, 401-402 for New Zealand village-scale forest clearing]

45. **Constraint #3: No canoe building**: "The shortage of wood placed strict limits on the amount of canoe building in Easter Island and on the size of canoes. None of the large Polynesian double canoes were built." (p. 1050) [Comparative data: Linton 1923:302 for 400-man Marquesan canoe construction]

46. **Constraint #4: No fortress building**: "Fortress building, usually involving wood constructions in Polynesia, was also restricted by the paucity of the wood supply in Easter Island." (p. 1050)

47. **Constraint #5: No large-scale fishing**: "This same lack of wood and the absence of a barrier reef and lagoon in Easter Island combined to restrict the size and frequency of communal fishing expeditions. Deep-sea fishing in large organized canoe fleets, a common Polynesian phenomenon, was precluded. Without a lagoon, many types of large-scale communal netting operations were also impossible." (pp. 1050-1051) [Comparative data: Buck 1938:297 for Mangareva 500-foot nets; Aitken 1930:59 for Tubuai mile-long nets]

48. **Easter Island fishing characterized**: "Some communal shore fishing did occur on Easter Island, but it was comparatively infrequent and small in scope. A net of 75 feet in length used for fishing of this type has been preserved in the U. S. National Museum." (p. 1051) [Source: Geiseler, as reported in Metraux 1940:187]

49. **Geiseler net size dispute**: "(Geisler reported seeing a net 200 feet long, but Metraux considers the size exaggerated [1940:187].)" (p. 1051) [NOTE: This is the sole reference to Geiseler in the article]

### What Communal Labor Existed on Easter Island (p. 1051)

50. **Domain of communal production restricted**: "The environmental conditions of Easter Island have thus severely restricted the domain of communal production." (p. 1051)

51. **Stone images as greatest communal investment**: "Outside of the construction and transportation of the stone images, there were few communal undertakings." (p. 1051)

52. **Other communal construction**: "Some large guest houses were built, undoubtedly by mass labor (p. 201). Wells, reservoirs, burial terraces and sea walls, all built on or near the coast, may also have been communally constructed." (p. 1051) [Source: Metraux 1940:201]

53. **Scale of statue enterprise**: "But it is the statues, some weighing up to thirty tons and some transported many miles across the island, that represent the greatest investment of mass energy in Easter Island culture." (p. 1051)

54. **Transport method unknown**: "The methods of transport and erection of the images are not known." (p. 1051)

55. **Metraux's transport speculation**: "Metraux opines that the statues were, 'probably hauled a bit at a time, possibly by a whole tribe working together as in the Marquesas' (p. 305)." (p. 1051) [Source: Metraux 1940:305] [NOTE: "a whole tribe" is Metraux's guess, not evidence. Lipo et al. 2013 demonstrated 18-person walking transport.]

### Specialist Production (p. 1051)

56. **Specializations restricted to esoteric domain**: "Specializations were also largely restricted to esoteric endeavors." (p. 1051)

57. **Rongorongo specialists**: "Outstanding were priests of various types, including the famous rongorongo men who read the Easter Island script and chanted genealogies. According to Metraux, special schools were set up for teaching these skills (p. 137)." (p. 1051) [Source: Metraux 1940:137]

58. **Stone carvers as specialists**: "Other craftsmen were the wood carvers and the stone carvers. The latter made the stone images. According to Metraux, they were 'organized into a sort of guild' (p. 137)." (p. 1051) [Source: Metraux 1940:137] [POST-RAID]

59. **Environmental inhibition conclusion**: "Geographical factors can be definitely seen to have inhibited the development of many sorts of communal and specialist production on Easter Island despite the fact that the social organization was adapted to these types of production. It is, therefore, not surprising that a great amount of manpower was used in stone image construction." (p. 1051)

## VI. Summary and Conclusions (p. 1051)

60. **Final statement of hypothesis**: "The evidence from historical and ethnographic sources suggests the following interpretation of the development of Easter Island stone images. The earliest Easter Islanders arrived from the central Polynesian hearth with a ramage organization and a tradition of image carving. The organization was suited to and reinforced by communal labor and specialized production. Environmental features of the new home largely precluded the use of communal and specialist labor in subsistence production. As a result, these efforts were channeled into an esoteric domain of culture. Perhaps facilitated by a tradition of carving, a limited amount of wood and the availability of easily worked tuff, the canalization toward esoteric production took the particular direction that resulted in the renowned stone heads of Easter Island." (p. 1051)

## VIII. Data Gap Admissions

Two explicit acknowledgments of evidential uncertainty:

61. "Details are often lacking because of the badly disrupted nature of the culture at the time of scientific study." (p. 1049)

62. "Although information is not conclusive, it is apparent that ramage heads, through control of surplus produce, controlled also communal and specialist production." (pp. 1049-1050)

These parallel admissions in the 1954 dissertation (eleven data gaps documented there). The first is a general acknowledgment that post-contact disruption has degraded the ethnographic record. The second specifically qualifies the claim that ramage heads controlled communal and specialist production.

## X. Significance for Our Argument

### 1. The environmental channeling hypothesis is more compatible with collective action than chiefly command

The core argument is that environmental constraints redirected labor capacity from subsistence into monument building. This capacity exists regardless of political organization: any group of people with the Polynesian tradition of communal labor and image carving, encountering Easter Island's constraints, could have redirected effort into stone carving without centralized chiefly command. The channeling hypothesis explains the existence of monument building; it does not require and does not demonstrate that chiefs controlled it.

Sahlins himself notes that the reinforcement loop between chiefly power and communal labor is "most marked when communal or specialized labor is expended in productivity-increasing, subsistence activities" (pp. 1048-1049). When labor is channeled into non-subsistence (esoteric) production, this reinforcement loop is broken. The chiefs cannot increase their surplus by directing statue construction; therefore, the mechanism linking redistribution to chiefly power over labor does not operate.

### 2. The stone carver passage describes lineage-level commissioning, not centralized command

Metraux 1940:137, as quoted by Sahlins, says carvers "received orders from people who wanted a monument for their ahu." They were "paid in fish, lobsters and eels." Sahlins infers the lineage head gave the order because ahu were lineage burial terraces. But the evidence describes:
- "People" (plural) commissioning work, not a single chief
- Payment in food directly to carvers, not through a redistributive system
- Work organized under a "master" craftsman, not under a chief
- The motivation is having "a monument for their ahu," which is lineage pride, not chiefly command

This is consistent with lineage-level decisions to commission ahu monuments, organized through direct exchange (food for labor), under craft-guild leadership. It is inconsistent with the model of centralized chiefly redistribution directing monument production. Metraux's own "tribal pride and competitive instinct" explanation, which Sahlins dismisses, is actually what this evidence describes.

### 3. Sahlins dismisses the explanation his own evidence supports

Sahlins criticizes Metraux's "tribal pride and competitive instinct" as constants that cannot explain Easter Island's uniqueness (p. 1046). Methodologically, the point is fair: constants cannot explain variation. But Sahlins's alternative (environmental channeling) explains only why Easter Island's communal labor went into stone rather than canoes or irrigation. It does not explain who organized that labor or how, and the stone carver passage he cites actually describes competitive lineage-level commissioning, not chiefly-command redistribution.

### 4. Two data gap admissions

The article acknowledges (a) that details are "often lacking because of the badly disrupted nature of the culture" (p. 1049) and (b) that "information is not conclusive" regarding ramage heads' control of communal production (pp. 1049-1050). These admissions parallel the dissertation's more extensive qualifications but are embedded in assertive sentences that proceed as if the evidence supports the claim regardless.

### 5. This article is the source for the moai-as-environmental-channeling argument

The 1954 dissertation has only a brief Appendix treatment of moai as "non-strategic" communal labor (pp. 433-434). This 1955 article provides the full ecological argument that becomes the standard interpretation: Easter Island's environment precluded normal Polynesian communal labor, so that capacity was redirected into monument building. This is the article that later scholars cite (including Sahlins himself in 1957, fn. 12) for the moai explanation. The argument is subsequently absorbed into the chiefdom framework when Kirch and others treat monument scale as evidence of chiefly power, reversing Sahlins's own logic (which treats moai as a consequence of environmental constraint, not political power).

### 6. Progressive hardening of claims between 1954 and 1955

The dissertation's "little direct evidence" of redistribution (p. 101) becomes the article's "The predominant method of extrahousehold distribution was redistribution" (p. 1049). The dissertation's ecologically anomalous ramage classification becomes "clearly of the ramage type." The dissertation's eleven data gaps become two. This pattern of progressive qualification-stripping continues into the 1957 article, where only the ecological anomaly survives.
