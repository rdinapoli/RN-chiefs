# Claims Extraction: Post-La Perouse Visits (1793-1862)

## Source Information

- **Full title:** *Easter Island 1793 to 1861: Observations by Early Visitors Before the Slave Raids*
- **Author:** Rhys Richards
- **Publisher:** Easter Island Foundation, Los Osos, CA
- **Year:** 2008
- **ISBN:** 978-1-880636-28-2
- **Scope:** Compilation of primary source accounts from 79 visits to Easter Island between 1793 and 1862, drawn from ships' logs, journals, published accounts, and museum archives
- **PDF coverage:** The PDF available in this repo (73 pages) contains printed pages 3-144 but is MISSING the Conclusions chapter (printed p.93) and Appendices A through C (printed pp.95-127). Appendix A is Hugh Cuming's 1827 account (by Steven Roger Fischer); Appendix B is Surgeon Guthrie's 1830 account from HMS *Seringapatam* (by Fischer); Appendix C is a list of Easter Island travelers on foreign ships 1806-1860 (by Richards). These missing sections may contain additional politically relevant material.
- **Richards' approach:** Richards explicitly states he is presenting primary sources, not interpreting them. "Interpreting these accounts, and commenting upon them item by item, is not attempted here, but rather is left for readers to draw their own conclusions" (p.15).

## Claims Extraction

### Direct Observations of Political Authority / Social Differentiation

#### 1. Captain Charles Bishop, *Ruby* of Bristol, 1795 (pp.18-21)

Bishop's account of the February 1795 visit contains no reference to chiefs, leaders, or political authority. The account describes:
- "Two small canoes came off with two men: they approached with much caution, but as they perceived by our presents of hoop iron, that we were friends, jumpt overboard and were taken on board" (p.18-19).
- Trade was conducted individually, with no indication of mediated or organized exchange: men brought produce individually, women "swam off to the boats" independently (pp.19-21).
- No person is identified as exercising authority over others in the trading context.

#### 2. Delano, American fur trader, 1801 (p.23)

Richards provides only a brief note. Delano visited in the *Perseverance*. No details about political organization are extracted from his account in this compilation.

#### 3. Crocker in the *Nancy*, 1806 (pp.23-26)

The *Nancy* visit resulted in the "First Massacre" of Easter Islanders. Richards cites Barratt (1988 pp.46-47) noting that after this event "there may however have been other unhappy visits in the interim which had predisposed the Islanders to encourage trade in the water but to oppose foreign landings" (p.34). No observations about political authority from this visit.

#### 4. Captain Lisiansky, 1804 (p.26)

Richards provides only a very brief reference to the Russian visit. The chapter heading (Ch. 5, p.26) mentions "Captain Lisiansky, Lieutenant Povalishin, and Archpriest Gedeon in 1804." No detailed political observations are extracted in the available text. [NOTE: Lisiansky's own published account (1814) would need to be consulted separately.]

#### 5. *Adventure* of London kidnapped "Henry Easter," 1806 (p.27)

An Islander was kidnapped. No political observations.

#### 6. W.A. Gale on the *Albatross* of Boston, January 1810 (p.28)

Richards does not provide substantive political observations from this visit in the available text.

#### 7. Kotzebue, 1816 (pp.31-33)

The chapter references the Russian explorer's visit. Richards cites Kotzebue's published account (1821). No detailed political observations are extracted in the portion of the book available in this PDF. The illustration on p.32 shows a "*Tangata Manu*, Peter the Great Museum of Anthropology & Ethnology, St. Petersburg," suggesting artifact collection. [NOTE: Kotzebue's own account should be consulted separately for political observations.]

#### 8. Edward Dobson, passenger on the *Surry*, 1821 (pp.34-36)

Key observation about apparent social differentiation:
- "There bodies were tattooed, some more than the rest who appeared to be chiefs. The figures on their arms and thighs were well executed" (p.35).
- "From what could be seen of the natives ashore, they did not seem to have any offensive weapons whatever" (p.35).
- The identification of "chiefs" is based solely on differential tattooing. No behavioral evidence of authority is described.

#### 9. Captain Chase of the *Foster*, 1822 (p.37)

Critical observation about political divisions and warfare, reported via the *National Gazette Literary Register* (April 24, 1822):
- Population estimated at "five or six thousand in number."
- **"They are divided into two parties, which are frequently at war with each other, and when this happens, they generally massacred all the prisoners."**
- "They furnished him with potatoes, fruit, etc., the produce of the island, for whatever he was disposed to give them" (p.37).
- Richards himself notes skeptically: "It is hard to imagine how these details could have been learned from the Islanders unless the *Foster* had remained some time at Easter Island" (p.37).

This is one of the most significant political observations in the entire compilation: a claim of bipartite division and inter-group warfare. However, the reliability is questionable given it was a secondhand report published in a newspaper, and the communication barrier was severe.

#### 10. Thomas W. Smith on the *Spring Grove*, 1823 (p.39)

Key observation using the term "chiefs":
- "Their chiefs are handsomely tattooed on their faces, necks, lips, tongues and arms."
- "But they are in a most savage state" (p.39).
- The identification of "chiefs" appears to be based entirely on differential tattooing (more elaborate tattoos), not on observed behavior of command or deference.

#### 11. Lieutenant George Peard on HMS *Blossom*, November 1825 (pp.40-42)

Extended and valuable account with multiple observations about authority:

a) **Identification of a "chief" by appearance:**
- "On shore I made a present to one who had short brown feathers round his head, and appeared to be a chief" (p.41).
- The identification is based on a feathered head ornament, not observed authority behavior.

b) **Violent confrontation and discipline observation:**
- "they all arose with celerity, if not the order of well disciplined troops. Soon not a woman was to be seen and the action became general" (p.51).
- This describes organized collective military response, but does not identify who directed it.

c) **"Apparent chiefs" described by appearance only:**
- "Two apparent chiefs, smeared all over one black and the other red, [crowned with feathers] attempted in conformity with our wish to keep back the multitude, but their authority was very limited" (pp.50-51).
- This is a critical observation: individuals who APPEARED to be chiefs by visual markers (body paint, feathers) attempted to exercise authority over the crowd but FAILED. Their "authority was very limited."

d) **Explicit negative finding about authority:**
- "nor, indeed, as I have said before, were there any who appeared to possess any superiority at least in the way of command over the rest" (p.52).

This last observation by Peard is one of the most important passages in the entire book for the chiefdom question. A trained Royal Navy officer, who spent time ashore and had a violent encounter with a large crowd, explicitly concluded that NO ONE appeared to possess command authority.

#### 12. James Wolfe, Mate on HMS *Blossom*, November 1825 (pp.42-55)

Wolfe provides a complementary account of the same visit with key authority observations:

a) **Crown-wearing individuals with no observable authority:**
- "Among the swimmers were two or three whole heads [that] were more fancifully adorned with a kind of crown made of a very pretty creeper. It was first thought that these might be people having authority, but we could discern no particular respect paid to them" (p.52).
- This directly parallels Peard: individuals with distinctive adornment were initially assumed to be authority figures, but NO deference behavior was observed.

b) **Explicit statement of no command hierarchy:**
- "nor, indeed, as I have said before, were there any who appeared to possess any superiority at least in the way of command over the rest" (p.52).

c) **"Apparent chiefs" with limited authority during stone-throwing confrontation:**
- "Two apparent chiefs, smeared all over one black and the other red, [crowned with feathers] attempted in conformity with our wish to keep back the multitude, but their authority was very limited" (pp.50-51).

#### 13. Hugh Cuming, botanist on the *Discovery*, 1827 (pp.55-56)

Brief observations from Cuming's letter to Dr. Hooker (March 1832):
- "Number of natives came on board; were a lively good-natured race, rather inclined to take anything portable but free to give for the merest trifle" (p.56).
- "Could not procure any botanical specimens from fear of the numerous inhabitants who lined the shore" (p.56).
- No reference to chiefs or authority figures. [NOTE: Cuming's full account is in Appendix A (pp.96-109), which is NOT in this PDF. Fischer's annotated version in *Journal of the Polynesian Society* 100 (1991) should be consulted.]

#### 14. Moerenhout, pearl trader, January 1829 (pp.56-58)

Moerenhout describes a notable individual:
- "An Indian came aboard the ship, a handsome man, six feet tall, with a noble face, an imposing stance ... the tattooing covering almost all his body, left only a few parts exposed" (p.57).
- "He [the chief] strongly urged us to land, boasting especially about the women, with whom these people today do a shameful traffic in order to procure in exchange some objects in use with us, whose price they now know" (p.58).
- Richards identifies this individual as "the chief" in his paraphrase, but the basis for this identification is physical stature and bearing, not observed command behavior.
- Moerenhout also notes: "according to what has been said to me since by [other] captains who visited the island, venereal disease is very common and must make frightful ravages which in a little time will completely be the ruin of that people" (p.58).

#### 15. HMS *Seringapatam*, March 1830 (pp.58-59)

Three separate reports exist from this visit. Captain Waldegrave's report:
- Mentions "eight standing *moai* statues, plantations of plantains, sugar-cane, and potatoes, but 'no trees, animals or poultry'" (p.58).
- Population of about 200 swam to the ship.
- Waldegrave saw "five canoes, made of driftwood, ten feet by fifteen inches, with two outriggers [arms] on one side, and a long piece connecting the outrigger [arms] at the end. They held two or three people" (p.58).
- "The islanders proved noisy and irrepressible thieves, and sought capture and take ashore the ship's boy" (p.58).

Midshipman Orlebar's account:
- Noted "very few arms ... of the iron wood, two short clubs, and two or three small carved images to which however they paid no reverence" (pp.58-59).
- The observation that carved images received "no reverence" is notable.

Surgeon Guthrie's account (see also Appendix B, not in PDF):
- Notes that at a distance some moai statues "have somewhat the appearance of persons sitting with white caps on their heads" (p.59).
- Describes trade items brought to shore: "sweet potatoes, a few idols, a *patapatoo* or club made of a heavy wood, and a sort of clumsy hook made of two bones" (p.59).

#### 16. *Foster* of Nantucket, Captain Chase, 1822 (whaling log via National Gazette Literary Register)

See entry 9 above. The claim of bipartite division and warfare is the most politically significant observation from any whaling vessel.

#### 17. *Maria* of Nantucket, Captain Macy, September 1833 (pp.63-64)

Key observations about hostility and possible chiefly authority:
- "on nearing the place he saw that the natives would be hostile, and use their ascendency, so we bore away from the island" (p.63).
- "The natives are not Christianised but in a state of heresy with ignorance" (p.63).
- On a crew member's attempt to trade: "the natives swam off to them with their produce and exchanged it for pieces of iron hoop, scraps, fish hooks etc." (p.64).
- "The land is low and cannot be seen far off.... We stood along the north shore. The land cultivated for potatoes, yams and various other sorts of vegetables" (p.64).
- "The inhabitants I am informed are of a ferocious and savage nature and are cannibals which renders it very unsafe for the ship to stop there" (p.64). [NOTE: The claim of cannibalism is unsubstantiated hearsay, likely reflecting sailor lore.]

#### 18. George of Stonington, Captain Brew, July 1840 (pp.64-65)

Brief trading visit. Key observation about authority and trade interference:
- On the afternoon of the second day: "[they] commenced heaving stones and shewing gestures of resentment as tho they were offended at some thing" (p.65).
- "it appeared as tho the chiefs would not allow them to come off (as there was some (chiefs I think) who went on a head and stopped and had very loud talk with all he met" (p.65).
- This is a significant observation: individuals identified as "chiefs" appeared to PREVENT others from trading with the ship. This suggests some individuals had influence over collective behavior regarding foreign contact, though the observer is uncertain ("I think").

#### 19. Du Petit-Thouars in *La Venus*, February 1838 (pp.69-75)

Extended French naval account, one of the most detailed in the book. Key observations:

a) **Word "miro" (wood) repeated insistently:**
- "All the natives repeated frequently and vigorously the word *miro*, and became impatient when it was not understood. This word is the name of the wood the Polynesians use to build their canoes" (p.71).
- This collective desire for wood, with no individual mediating the request, suggests communal rather than chiefly-organized resource acquisition.

b) **No identification of chiefs or authority figures in the entire account.**
- Du Petit-Thouars describes extensive interaction including islanders boarding the frigate, dancing, receiving gifts, trading. At no point does he identify any individual as a chief or authority figure.

c) **Monuments observed:**
- "We also saw on the coast pyramids of dark stones (or pyramid-shaped) placed at intervals; they were all topped by a white stone in the shape of a cornice" (p.70).
- Near Cook's Bay: "two or three monuments of a different type which, without doubt, were *morais*; [*marae*;] the closest to the coast was a platform made of stone, upon which had been placed four equally spaced red statues, whose tops were covered in white stones or cornices of this colour" (p.70).
- Another platform with "five of these statues ... placed much closer to each other" (p.70).
- These are among the last observations of standing moai on their ahu.

d) **Women weeping when canoes fell behind the frigate:**
- "When one of the women saw this, she began to sob loudly and made pitiful lamentations, as do children; she was quickly joined by several of the other savages" (p.72).
- This fear of kidnapping reflects accumulated experience with foreign ships, not political organization per se.

e) **Canoe observations:**
- "While skirting the western coast of Easter Island we met a much smaller number of canoes than on the other side" (p.74).
- "In total, from one side of the island to the other, we would have seen at most a dozen. All the canoes on Easter Island are very small and could only contain two or three people" (p.74).

f) **No description of any territorial divisions or inter-group distinctions.**

#### 20. *Vesper* of New London, February 1848 (p.83)

The log of the *Vesper*, which traded at Easter Island, contains a remarkable passage:
- "Saw none of the natives armed at all. Saw a few huts but they mostly live in caves. Saw one hogg and a dog" (p.83).
- "Got about ten bushels of sweet potatoes and a few of [one short word indecipherable, maybe taro], and 12 of their wooden gods" (p.83).
- The observation "none of the natives armed at all" is notable.
- "chiefs would not allow them to come off" is NOT from this visit but from the *George* 1840 (see entry 18). However, the *Vesper* account does mention: "But it appeared as tho the chiefs would not allow the females to stop on board, for an evil purpose" (p.65). [NOTE: This quote is actually from the George log, not the Vesper. Correcting: The Vesper 1848 log does not mention chiefs.]

#### 21. HMS *Portland*, 1853 (indirect report via Palmer 1868/1870) (pp.85-86)

The *Portland* did not land but traded from boats. Palmer's indirect report (which may conflate observations from the Portland and his own later Topaze visit) includes:
- "In their hands, in the place of weapons used by the [New Zealand?] Maori, they [the men?] carried short double-ended paddles which they named 'rapa'" (p.85).
- Houses described: "some [low houses] 120 feet long, generally 60 to 70 [feet wide]. In 1868 we on the Topaz found them about 30 feet long and [some] smaller 10 to 15 feet broad, and in height 5 1/2 feet" (p.86).
- "Laves or household gods, 'domestic Idols of Pereugene', ... were not worshipped, and though the present people still carve them, we could not find [in 1868?] that they were aware of the significance of the mythical emblems that they still copy" (p.86).

#### 22. The Murder of Robert Weeks, *Prudent*, 1856 (pp.86-89)

Captain Hamilton's detailed letter about the violent encounter:
- "The island is very populous and is under good cultivation as far as appearances from the ship and boat would warrant. No timber is on any part of it, and but very few canoes" (p.89).
- No references to chiefs or authority figures in the context of the violent attack.

#### 23. Whaling Visits 1852-1862 (p.89)

The final whaling visits before the slave raids. Records are extremely terse:
- *Harvest* of Nantucket, January 1852: "Sent boats ashore. Got nothing but a few potatoes and a few fowls" (p.89).
- No political observations in the available logbook extracts.

### Interpretive Statements About Political Organization

#### 1. Richards on the *Foster* (1822) report of bipartite warfare (p.37)

Richards explicitly questions the reliability of the claim about two warring parties: "It is hard to imagine how these details could have been learned from the Islanders unless the *Foster* had remained some time at Easter Island" (p.37). This is a significant editorial comment: Richards doubts whether the most politically specific claim in the entire compilation could have been communicated during a brief ship visit.

#### 2. Richards on the kidnapping of "Henry Easter" (1806) and George Lawrence (1839) (pp.27, 65-66)

Richards documents the kidnapping of at least two Easter Islanders by foreign ships. The Lawrence story (pp.65-66) is detailed: a boy of about 12 was kidnapped from the *Champion* of Edgartown. The boy "was quick, smart, honest, learned to talk English and he made the best man to look for whales." He was named "George Lawrence" and brought to New England. George did not want to return: "He said that the natives would kill him if they did not remember him. His father had once told him that they had killed a crew of a wrecked ship once" (p.66). These kidnapping accounts have no direct bearing on political authority but suggest: (a) individual islanders could be separated from the community without apparent institutional response, and (b) the boy's fear of violence upon return suggests factional or community-level justice rather than overarching authority that might protect him.

#### 3. Richards' general framing in the Preface (p.15)

Richards explicitly disclaims interpretation: "Interpreting these accounts, and commenting upon them item by item, is not attempted here." He positions the book as a source compilation. This means any political conclusions must be drawn from the primary accounts themselves, not from Richards' synthesis. [NOTE: The Conclusions chapter (p.93) is NOT in this PDF and may contain political analysis.]

### European Categories Imposed

#### 1. Use of "chief" / "chiefs" throughout

The term "chief" or "chiefs" is used by the following visitors, in each case based on VISUAL markers rather than observed authority behavior:

a) **Dobson (1821, p.35):** "some more [tattooed] than the rest who appeared to be chiefs." Basis: differential tattooing.

b) **Smith on *Spring Grove* (1823, p.39):** "Their chiefs are handsomely tattooed on their faces, necks, lips, tongues and arms." Basis: differential tattooing.

c) **Peard on HMS *Blossom* (1825, pp.41, 50-51):** "appeared to be a chief" based on feathered head ornament. Later: "Two apparent chiefs, smeared all over one black and the other red, [crowned with feathers]." In BOTH cases, Peard identifies chiefs by adornment, and in the second case explicitly notes "their authority was very limited."

d) **Wolfe on HMS *Blossom* (1825, p.52):** "two or three whole heads [that] were more fancifully adorned with a kind of crown made of a very pretty creeper. It was first thought that these might be people having authority, but we could discern no particular respect paid to them." This is the most explicit case of a European category ("people having authority") being applied based on visual markers and then RETRACTED based on observed behavior.

e) **Moerenhout (1829, p.58):** Richards identifies "the chief" who boarded the ship, based on the man's impressive stature (six feet tall), "noble face," "imposing stance," and extensive tattooing. No command behavior observed.

f) **George of Stonington (1840, p.65):** "it appeared as tho the chiefs would not allow them to come off (as there was some (chiefs I think) who went on a head and stopped and had very loud talk with all he met." The observer is explicitly uncertain ("I think") about whether these individuals were chiefs. The observed behavior is one person moving ahead and having "very loud talk" with others, which could indicate authority, persuasion, or agitation.

#### 2. "King" never used

Notably, NONE of the accounts in this compilation use the term "king" for any Easter Islander. This contrasts with some earlier contact-era accounts (Gonzalez 1770) and later missionary/ethnographic accounts.

#### 3. "Savages" and "Indians" as default categories

Multiple visitors use "savages" (Wolfe p.50, Macy p.64, Pease p.65) and "Indians" (Moerenhout p.57, Macy p.64) as default terms for the islanders, reflecting standard European/American colonial vocabulary that presumes a particular civilizational hierarchy.

#### 4. Military analogies

Peard (1825, p.51) describes the islanders' collective response to conflict using military language: "they all arose with celerity, if not the order of well disciplined troops." This imposes a European military framework on what may have been spontaneous collective action.

### Significant Absences

#### 1. No visitor between 1793 and 1862 describes witnessing an act of chiefly command

Across all accounts in this compilation, no visitor describes seeing one individual give orders that others obeyed. The closest is the *George* (1840) account where individuals described as "chiefs" appear to have prevented others from trading, but the observer is uncertain about their status.

#### 2. No visitor describes tribute, redistribution, or provisioning through a central authority

All trade with ships is conducted by individuals swimming out with personal baskets of produce, or by individuals in small canoes. No account describes trade organized or mediated by a chief or central authority. The standard pattern across dozens of visits is: islanders swim or canoe to the ship individually, each carrying their own produce, and exchange directly with sailors.

#### 3. No visitor describes a residence or dwelling distinguished from others as chiefly

Multiple visitors describe houses (Bishop 1795, Dobson 1821, Du Petit-Thouars 1838, Palmer/Portland 1853), and all describe them as similar low structures. No visitor identifies a chiefly residence, a larger house, or a dwelling set apart.

#### 4. No visitor describes ceremonial deference to any individual

Despite multiple accounts of crowds of hundreds to "upwards of a thousand" (Wolfe p.50), no visitor describes any islander showing deference, bowing, stepping aside for, or otherwise treating another islander as a superior.

#### 5. Peard and Wolfe (1825) explicitly looked for authority and found none

This is the most important "significant absence" in the compilation. Two educated observers on HMS *Blossom* specifically noted individuals with distinctive adornment, hypothesized these might be authority figures, tested this hypothesis against observed behavior, and concluded that no authority was discernible:
- Peard: "nor, indeed, as I have said before, were there any who appeared to possess any superiority at least in the way of command over the rest" (p.52).
- Wolfe on adorned individuals: "It was first thought that these might be people having authority, but we could discern no particular respect paid to them" (p.52).

#### 6. Du Petit-Thouars (1838), the most detailed observer, does not mention any authority figures

Du Petit-Thouars' account (pp.69-75) is the longest and most detailed in the compilation. Despite extensive interaction with islanders aboard the frigate and observations of the coastline, he never identifies a chief, leader, or authority figure. This absence is notable given that French naval officers of this period were trained observers and that the expedition's purpose was partly to document Pacific societies.

#### 7. No visitor describes territorial boundaries or named political units

Despite the *Foster* (1822) claim of "two parties ... frequently at war," no visitor describes named groups, territorial boundaries, or political units. The tribal boundaries shown on the Routledge map (illustration p.14) are from much later ethnographic reconstruction, not from observations during this period.

#### 8. No ceremonial or religious authority described

No visitor between 1793 and 1862 describes priests, ritual specialists, or religious ceremonies beyond noting carved images ("idols," "wooden gods"). Orlebar (1830) specifically notes that carved images received "no reverence" (p.59). Palmer (1853/1870) notes household gods "were not worshipped" (p.86).

### Speculative / Uncertain Claims

#### 1. The *Foster* (1822) claim of bipartite warfare (p.37)

"They are divided into two parties, which are frequently at war with each other, and when this happens, they generally massacred all the prisoners."

This is the most politically specific claim in the entire compilation, but its reliability is highly questionable:
- It was reported secondhand in a newspaper (*National Gazette Literary Register*)
- Richards questions how such detailed information could have been communicated during a brief ship visit
- No other visitor in this period (1793-1862) reports witnessing or being told about inter-group warfare
- The claim of massacring "all the prisoners" may reflect European expectations about "savage" warfare

#### 2. Population estimates vary wildly

Different visitors provide dramatically different estimates:
- Captain Chase / *Foster* (1822): "five or six thousand" (p.37)
- Dobson / *Surry* (1821): shore was "lined with them, cheering, hallooing and waving to us"; "some more [tattooed] than the rest who appeared to be chiefs" (p.35). Does not give a specific number.
- HMS *Seringapatam* (1830): "Among the 200, of both sexes, who swam to the ship" (p.58), but this is swimmers to the ship, not total population.
- Peard / *Blossom* (1825): "about a thousand" who gathered (p.40), "The natives accumulated to upwards of a thousand" (p.50).
- Three Brothers (1848): "I should think that before long the island would be over run with Indians for they seem to be very numerous" (p.84).
- Various whaling logs describe large numbers swimming out to ships.

The variation suggests no visitor conducted a systematic census. The population was large enough to generate crowds of hundreds to a thousand at trading encounters.

#### 3. Macy (1833) claim of cannibalism (p.64)

"The inhabitants I am informed are of a ferocious and savage nature and are cannibals." This is explicit hearsay ("I am informed") and likely reflects sailor lore rather than observation. No other visitor makes this claim based on observation.

#### 4. "George Lawrence" (kidnapped boy) on inter-community violence (p.66)

The kidnapped boy told his American captors: "His father had once told him that they had killed a crew of a wrecked ship once" and "the natives would kill him if they did not remember him." These statements suggest a memory of collective violence but provide no information about political organization of that violence.

#### 5. Moerenhout's characterization of "the chief" (1829, pp.57-58)

Moerenhout identifies a tall, impressively tattooed man as "the chief" and reports this individual "strongly urged us to land, boasting especially about the women." Whether this man was actually a chief in any political sense, or simply an impressive individual who took the initiative to board the ship, cannot be determined from the account.

### Second-Pass Additions (2026-03-13)

**SP-1. Du Petit-Thouars (1838) silence on authority as evidence of absence.**

Du Petit-Thouars was a French naval officer trained in systematic observation and accustomed to identifying social hierarchy. He spent hours with islanders aboard his frigate, observed the coastline in detail, recorded monuments, noted canoe construction, described trading behavior, and documented the islanders' insistent collective demand for wood. Despite this extensive and detailed interaction, he never mentions any authority figure, chief, leader, or individual exercising command (pp. 69-75). This silence is analytically significant because of WHO is silent: a trained military observer from a hierarchical society, someone predisposed to notice and record hierarchy. If there had been a visible chief or person of authority, du Petit-Thouars would almost certainly have recorded it, as he recorded every other notable aspect of the encounter. His silence is not the absence of observation but the observation of absence.

**Assessment**: While "Significant Absences #6" notes this, the evidential weight of the silence deserves emphasis. Du Petit-Thouars' account is the most detailed in Richards' compilation and the most conspicuous for its complete absence of authority figures.

**SP-2. Peard and Wolfe (1825) conducted a deliberate hypothesis test of chiefly authority.**

The Blossom observers (Peard and Wolfe) followed a specific epistemological sequence: (1) they observed individuals with distinctive adornment (feathered crowns, body paint), (2) they formed the hypothesis that these individuals might be authority figures, (3) they looked for behavioral correlates of authority (deference from others, obedience to commands, special treatment), and (4) they found none. Wolfe: "It was first thought that these might be people having authority, but we could discern no particular respect paid to them" (p. 52). Peard: "nor, indeed, as I have said before, were there any who appeared to possess any superiority at least in the way of command over the rest" (p. 52). This is a negative result from a deliberate search, epistemologically stronger than mere silence. The observers did not simply fail to mention authority; they actively looked for it and reported its absence.

**Assessment**: Claims "Direct Observations #11-12" and "Significant Absences #5" record these observations. The epistemological point deserves emphasis: this is a hypothesis tested and rejected, not merely an unreported observation.

**SP-3. "Authority was very limited" as a stress test of chiefly power.**

During the Blossom confrontation (1825), two "apparent chiefs, smeared all over one black and the other red, [crowned with feathers] attempted in conformity with our wish to keep back the multitude, but their authority was very limited" (pp. 50-51). This is a stress test of chiefly power: a crisis situation (violent confrontation with armed foreigners) in which individuals who APPEARED to be chiefs attempted to exercise authority and FAILED. If a chief cannot compel obedience during a life-threatening crisis involving armed foreigners, he has no effective coercive power. In Polynesian chiefdoms documented elsewhere, chiefs could command collective behavior during military or diplomatic crises. The failure of these "apparent chiefs" to restrain the crowd is direct evidence of the absence of effective political authority.

**Assessment**: This episode goes beyond mere absence of observed authority. It is a positive observation of authority being attempted and failing, which is stronger evidence against chiefdom organization than silence alone.

**SP-4. Collective wood request (Du Petit-Thouars 1838): horizontally-organized resource negotiation.**

"All the natives repeated frequently and vigorously the word miro" (p. 71). The collective demand for wood was not mediated by a chiefly delegate or spokesperson; everyone made the request simultaneously and with equal urgency. In a chiefdom, foreign resource acquisition would be mediated by political authority: a chief would negotiate on behalf of the community, or a chiefly delegate would present the request. The horizontal, mass character of the wood demand (every individual making the same request independently) is inconsistent with chiefdom-mediated foreign contact and consistent with a community of autonomous individuals with a shared material need.

**Assessment**: Claim "Direct Observations #19a" records this but does not draw the contrast with chiefdom-mediated resource acquisition. The unanimity and non-mediated character of the request is analytically significant.

**SP-5. George Lawrence kidnapping: community-level justice, not chiefly justice.**

The kidnapped boy (taken c. 1839, pp. 65-66) feared "the natives" collectively upon potential return: "He said that the natives would kill him if they did not remember him" (p. 66). His fear was of the community as a collective agent of retribution, not of a chief who would judge and punish. His father had told him "that they had killed a crew of a wrecked ship once" (p. 66), again attributing collective violence to "they" (the community), not to a chief's order. Justice and retribution operated at the community level, consistent with segmentary kinship-based systems in which collective responsibility and collective punishment are the norm, rather than with chiefdom organization in which a chief adjudicates disputes and directs punishment.

**Assessment**: This passage provides indirect evidence about internal social organization. The boy's fear of collective retribution, not chiefly judgment, is consistent with acephalous or segmentary social organization.

## Cross-Reference Notes

### Changes Over Time (1793-1862)

#### Monument condition
- Du Petit-Thouars (1838, p.70): Describes multiple standing moai on platforms near Cook's Bay, including one platform with four statues and another with five. The moai are described as "red statues" topped with "white stones or cornices."
- HMS *Seringapatam* (1830, p.58): Waldegrave mentions "eight standing *moai* statues."
- Palmer via HMS *Portland* (1853, p.86): By this time, Palmer's account discusses moai and ahu but does not clearly state how many were still standing.
- The available accounts suggest some moai were still standing as late as 1838, consistent with the gradual toppling chronology rather than a single catastrophic event.

#### Trading patterns
- Consistent across the entire period: islanders swim or canoe to ships with individual baskets of produce (mainly sweet potatoes, yams, bananas, sugar cane) and exchange for iron goods (hoop iron, nails, fish hooks), cloth, and miscellany.
- No change in trading organization is visible: trade remains individual-to-individual throughout. No visitor describes centralized or chiefly-mediated exchange at any point.
- Wood (*miro*) was consistently the most desired item. Du Petit-Thouars (1838) notes the islanders' insistent and collective demand for wood.

#### Hostility patterns
- Hostility toward visitors appears to increase over time, plausibly as a result of accumulated negative experiences (the Nancy massacre 1806, the Adventure kidnapping 1806, Weeden's massacre 1822, Rugg's possible atrocities 1838-1841, multiple kidnappings).
- Several visitors note violence from islanders (Peard 1825, Dawn 1823, various whaling logs), while others report friendly encounters.
- The pattern suggests situational rather than systematic hostility, possibly related to which part of the island was approached and recent experiences with previous ships.

#### Canoe deterioration
- Bishop (1795): "Two small canoes" with "mean construction" (pp.18-19).
- Dobson (1821): "only two canoes which were very small" (p.36).
- Du Petit-Thouars (1838): "at most a dozen" canoes, "all very small and could only contain two or three people" (p.74).
- Hamilton / *Prudent* (1856): "very few canoes" (p.89).
- Palmer (1853/1870): "From the scarcity of wood, no canoes now exist [in 1868?]" (p.86).
- This progressive canoe deterioration reflects ongoing deforestation and wood scarcity, not political change per se, but it does suggest declining capacity for inter-community maritime communication.

### Discrepancies Between Visitors

1. **Population estimates** range from implications of a few hundred to "five or six thousand." These are impossible to reconcile without understanding that visitors only saw portions of the population and under different circumstances.

2. **Hostility vs. friendliness:** Some visitors describe extremely friendly reception (Dobson 1821, Du Petit-Thouars 1838), while others describe immediate hostility (Dawn 1823, Macy 1833). This likely reflects which part of the island was approached, recent experiences with previous ships, and the circumstances of approach.

3. **"Chiefs" identification:** Where visitors identify "chiefs," they use exclusively visual criteria (tattooing, feathered ornaments, body paint, physical stature). In the one case where observers tested this identification against observed authority behavior (Peard and Wolfe, 1825), they found no evidence of actual authority.

## Summary Assessment

### What These Sources Collectively Tell Us About Political Organization, 1793-1862

The post-La Perouse accounts compiled by Richards provide a remarkably consistent picture across nearly 70 years and dozens of visitors: **no observer between 1793 and 1862 provides clear evidence of centralized political authority on Easter Island.**

The key findings are:

1. **The term "chief" is used by several visitors, but always based on visual markers (tattooing, feathered ornaments, body paint, physical stature), never on observed command behavior.** The most careful observers (Peard and Wolfe on HMS *Blossom*, 1825) explicitly tested the hypothesis that adorned individuals were authority figures and concluded they were not: "we could discern no particular respect paid to them" and "nor ... were there any who appeared to possess any superiority at least in the way of command over the rest."

2. **Trade was consistently individual, never centrally organized.** Across dozens of visits spanning 70 years, produce was brought by individual islanders swimming or canoeing to ships. No account describes a chief organizing, mediating, or taxing trade. This is significant because foreign trade in provisions was the primary economic interaction between islanders and Europeans, and would be the most likely context for chiefly authority to be visible.

3. **The one politically specific claim (bipartite warfare, *Foster* 1822) is secondhand, published in a newspaper, and questioned by Richards himself.** No other visitor corroborates inter-group warfare from direct observation.

4. **The most detailed accounts (Du Petit-Thouars 1838, Peard and Wolfe 1825) are the ones that most clearly suggest an absence of hierarchical authority.** Brief accounts are more likely to use "chief" casually; extended accounts consistently fail to identify actual authority.

5. **Social differentiation existed** in the form of differential tattooing, distinctive feathered ornaments, and possibly red/black body paint. But differential adornment does not by itself constitute evidence of political hierarchy: it may indicate age grades, ritual status, lineage identity, or personal achievement.

6. **The pattern of interaction with ships was collective and decentralized.** Large groups (hundreds to a thousand) gathered at trade encounters. Women and men participated independently. No individual or small group controlled access to the community or mediated relations with outsiders.

### Implications for the Chiefdom Attribution

These accounts are significant for the chiefdom question because they cover the period immediately before the slave raids, when the population was still substantial (likely 2,000-4,000 based on various estimates). If a functioning chiefdom existed, visiting naval officers, naturalists, and traders might reasonably be expected to encounter some evidence of it: organized reception by a leader, centralized provisioning, differential treatment of chiefly individuals, or at minimum, deference to identifiable authority figures. None of this appears in the record.

The strongest counter-evidence comes from the *Foster* (1822) report of "two parties ... frequently at war," which could be read as evidence of competing political units. But this claim is secondhand, unverified, and not corroborated by any other visitor.

### Limitations

1. **The PDF is incomplete.** The Conclusions chapter (p.93) and Appendices A-C (pp.95-127) are missing. Appendix A (Cuming's 1827 account) and Appendix B (Guthrie's 1830 account from *Seringapatam*) may contain additional politically relevant observations.

2. **Most visits were extremely brief** (hours to a day), conducted from ships or boats, with severe language barriers. Visitors saw only coastal areas during trading encounters. The absence of observed political authority may reflect the limitations of observation rather than the absence of political authority.

3. **Visitors had no framework for recognizing Polynesian-style leadership** that might operate through prestige, genealogical seniority, or ritual authority rather than overt command. The European expectation of "chiefs" who give orders and are visibly obeyed may have caused observers to miss more subtle forms of political organization.

4. **Selection bias:** Richards notes that whaling logbooks are "terse" and focused on navigation and trade, not ethnographic observation. The absence of political observations in most whaling logs may simply reflect the logbook genre.

5. **These accounts cannot demonstrate that political authority was absent, only that it was not visible to short-term European visitors.** The argument from absence is suggestive but not conclusive.
