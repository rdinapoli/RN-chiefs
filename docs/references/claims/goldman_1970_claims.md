## I. The Classification Framework (Ch. 1, Introduction: Principles of Status, pp. 1-28)

### Definitions of Traditional, Open, and Stratified

1. **Three basic variants** (p. 20): "The variety of ways in which basic principles and prerogatives of status combine and modify one another gives rise to three basic variants in Polynesian status systems."

2. **Traditional** (p. 20): "In the first, which I call 'Traditional,' seniority is central. As the source of mana and sanctity, senior descent establishes rank and allocates authority and power in an orderly manner. The Traditional is essentially a religious system headed by a sacred chief and given stability by a religiously sanctioned gradation of worth."

3. **Open** (p. 20): "In the second system, which I call 'Open,' seniority has been modified to allow military and political effectiveness to govern status and political control. The Open system is more strongly military and political than religious, and stability in it must be maintained more directly by the exercise of secular powers. In the Open, status differences are no longer regularly graded but tend to be sharply defined."

4. **Stratified** (pp. 20-21): "Finally, the third system, which I call 'Stratified,' is characterized by clearcut breaks in status that are far-reaching in their impact upon everyday life. In the Stratified system, status differences are economic and political. High ranks hold the rule and possess the land titles; the commoners are subjects and are landless."

5. **Stratified as synthesis of Traditional and Open** (p. 21): "The Stratified represents a synthesis of Traditional and Open, combining respect and reverence for hereditary rank via seniority with necessary concessions to political and economic power. The system seems to have been an outcome of the intense status rivalry so characteristic of the Open societies."

6. **Categorical vs. gradual distinctions** (p. 21): "Stratified is the most clearly defined type because of the categorical distinction between landed and landless. The distinction between Traditional and Open, on the other hand, is one of gradation."

### Classification list

7. **Full classification** (pp. 21-22): "Traditional -- Maori, Manihiki-Rakahanga, Tongareva, Uvea, Futuna, Tokelau, Tikopia, Pukapuka, Ontong Java; Open -- Mangaia, Easter, Marquesas, Samoa, Niue; Stratified -- Mangareva, Society Islands, Hawaiian Islands, Tonga."

### The status-power relationship in each type

8. **Traditional: status dominant** (pp. 21-22): "In the Traditional societies, status is dominant and power is subordinate."

9. **Open: power dominant** (pp. 21-22): "in the Open societies, power is dominant and status is subordinate."

10. **Stratified: status and power even** (pp. 21-22): "in the Stratified societies, status and power are at an approximately even level but both are more consequential than in the Traditional societies."

### How Goldman's framework differs from Sahlins

11. **Not an ecological/productivity model**: Goldman's framework is organized around status rivalry, not productivity-stratification correlation (contrast Sahlins 1954/1958). The three pillars are mana, tohunga (specialist), and toa (warrior). Goldman writes (p. 21): "A description of Polynesian status systems is necessarily in political terms, if only because the central conception of status in Polynesia is that of power or capability. The concepts of mana, tohunga, and toa, the three pillars of Polynesian status, refer directly to power."

12. **Critique of productivity-driven models** (p. 23): Goldman notes the "functional-structural theory" that regards political power as "a function of economic productivity" (citing Hogbin 1938 and Sahlins 1959). He rejects this: "Taking a totally abstract view of culture -- ignoring actual motives and the specific histories of cultures -- it is, of course, possible to postulate a social model in which any aspect of culture can be shown to be adaptive, integrative, and contributing to equilibrium."

13. **Status rivalry as the engine** (p. 24): "it is my contention that we cannot account for cultural evolution in Polynesia without full knowledge of the forms and circumstances of status rivalry."

14. **Open not a regression, but a developmental stage**: Goldman presents Traditional -> Open -> Stratified as a developmental sequence, not a hierarchy of complexity. The Open societies are not "failed" Traditional societies but societies in which a specific political dynamic (warrior displacement of hereditary authority) has altered the traditional pattern.

### Methodological notes

15. **Work of interpretation** (p. xiii): "I would be overstating my intentions grossly if I did not make clear that at bottom this is a work of interpretation. Its findings are to be regarded as hypotheses."

16. **Ethnographic limitations acknowledged** (p. xii): "Much of the ethnographic work was carried out well after the aboriginal cultures had gone into decline."

17. **Developmental sequence is hypothetical** (pp. 27-28): "The question I have raised in this book is not of so specific a nature. I have asked rather whether variations in status system and related social structure have moved in some order of regularity from Traditional to Open and Stratified."

## II. Easter Island Chapter (Ch. 6, pp. 93-122)

### Opening classification and rationale

18. **Easter Island as Open society** (p. 94): "By social standards, it stands with the Open societies, which, like Mangaia and the Marquesas, had never evolved a central authority."

19. **Cultural sophistication acknowledged** (pp. 93-94): "the home of an impressively developed civilization. Its monumental stone statues and ceremonial centers and its esoteric script, evidently an 'embryo writing' (Barthel 1958), stamp this remote Polynesian outpost as a 'high culture,' at least by technical standards."

20. **Polynesian, not alien** (p. 94): "When we turn from such special features as scripts and statues to Easter Island culture as a whole, the strangeness and mystery quickly dissipate. The language is eastern Polynesian, closely related to Marquesan and Mangarevan, and the main social institutions and artifacts are also definitely Polynesian."

21. **Pristine internal development** (p. 94): "The fact of relative isolation is particularly important to our understanding of Easter Island society because it suggests that here we have what is perhaps the best example of relatively pristine internal development in all Polynesia."

22. **Population estimate** (p. 95): "aboriginal populations estimated as high as 7,000." Source: ecological survey by the Norwegian Archaeological Expedition.

### Warriors displacing ariki AND assuming religious prerogatives

23. **Warriors overcame ariki** (p. 96): "Like Mangaia, Easter Island is an example of an Open society in which warriors had succeeded in overcoming the authority of the traditional ariki."

24. **KEY: Warriors gained both political AND religious power** (p. 96): "The warriors' supremacy had gone even further on Easter Island because here they had gained not only the political power, but had achieved an ascendant religious position as well. Nowhere else in Polynesia did commoners take upon themselves both the secular authority and prerogatives of mana and tapu which were not ordinarily theirs."

25. **Carrying forward the Marquesan tradition** (p. 96): "This particular development, a carrying forward of the Marquesan tradition, lends special interest to Easter Island history."

### Source assessment

26. **Culture ended abruptly** (p. 96): "Easter Island culture came to an abrupt end shortly after 1863."

27. **Slave raids** (p. 96): "It was in that year that Peruvian slavers took some 1,000 persons off the island and the first missionaries arrived. In 1871 hundreds more left Easter Island for Tahiti and Mangareva."

28. **Contact-era accounts dismissed as superficial** (p. 96): "Early explorers, beginning with Roggeveen in 1722, and including Cook's party in 1774 and La Pérouse in 1746 [sic], did not record culture or traditions." [NOTE: La Pérouse visited in 1786, not 1746. Goldman's error.]

29. **Primary reliance on Metraux** (pp. 96-97): "We are indebted, nevertheless, to Dr. Alfred Metraux, the ethnologist of that expedition, for a most thorough and judicious job of cultural salvage."

30. **Information limited but general outlines available** (p. 97): "We cannot answer many questions about details of Easter Island culture and of its development, but the general outlines are available."

31. **Norwegian Expedition as additional source** (p. 97): "In 1955 the Norwegian Archaeological Expedition under Dr. Thor Heyerdahl began an extensive study of the stone monuments."

32. **Less social structural knowledge than for Mangaia** (p. 97): "we do not know as much about Easter Island social structure as we do about Mangaia, we have, on the other hand, some substantial archeological documentation."

33. **Roussel as "best source on aboriginal culture"** (p. 98): Father Roussel "considered by Metraux to be the best source on aboriginal Easter Island culture."

### Genealogical traditions

34. **Hotu-matua foundation legend** (p. 97): "All accounts agree that an ariki called Hotu-matua, who came from Marae Renga, an unidentified island, was the founder of the social order."

35. **History begins in status rivalry** (p. 97): "Thus, the history of Easter Island begins in status rivalry."

36. **Multiple origin groups possible** (pp. 97-98): Traditions ambiguous on whether settlers were single kin group or multiple distinct parties. Metraux identified five tribes "whose genealogical antecedents are not really known."

37. **Genealogies "exceedingly sketchy"** (p. 98): "Easter Island genealogies are exceedingly sketchy, and most tribes that consider themselves descended from Hotu-matua have no specific pedigrees."

38. **Wars were internecine** (pp. 98-99): "leaves no doubt that the historical Easter Islanders thought of themselves as one stock and that when they fought it was one group of descendants of Hotu-matua against another group of his descendants."

39. **East-west division** (p. 98): "persistent rivalries between the eastern and western districts."

40. **Royalist vs. commoner division** (p. 98): Western side = Tuu (royalist); eastern side = Hotu-iti (commoner).

### Roussel on ariki mau power

41. **KEY: Roussel on loss of ariki authority** (pp. 97-98): Goldman quotes Roussel via Metraux: "These kings who in the beginning were considered deities and had absolute power on the island, did not retain such authority for long, but only the prestige of supernatural power with certain personal privileges (1957:43)."

42. **KEY: Royal authority "nil"** (p. 98): "In late times, although kings were still surrounded with respect by the natives who considered their persons to be sacred, their authority was nil and entirely disregarded. It had passed entirely into the hands of the matatoa who decided everything and carried on the war without beforehand consulting his majesty (1940:135)."

43. **Dual status system like Mangaia** (pp. 98-99): "In its last days, therefore, Easter Island had a dual status system almost identical with that in Mangaia. One aristocratic tribe, the Miru, retained rank and sanctity, while the chiefs of plebeian tribes held the actual power."

44. **Commoners gained sanctity through Bird Man cult** (p. 99): "the commoners' control of the strange cult of the Bird Man gave them sanctity as well."

### The archaeological record

45. **Earliest radiocarbon date** (p. 99): "A radiocarbon date from what may have been a defensive ditch dug across the Poike Peninsula puts the occupancy of Easter Island at about A.D. 386 +/- 100."

46. **Three periods defined** (p. 100): "Early Period, 400-1100; Middle Period, 1100-1680; Late Period, 1680-1868."

47. **Over 200 ahu** (p. 100): "There were well over 200 ahu on Easter Island that varied greatly in size, in quality of construction, and in form as well as in function."

48. **Ahu were local ceremonial centers** (pp. 99-100): "Because of their great number, and the way in which they were distributed all along the coast where the population had been most dense, and in inland areas where it is known that native population had settled, there can be little doubt that the ahu were local ceremonial centers, constructed, that is, by local groups and maintained by them."

49. **Three exceptional ahu** (p. 100): Ahu Tahiri (Hangaroa, west coast), Hekii (La Perouse, north coast), Tongariki (east coast, the largest, 145 meters, 15 statues).

50. **Ahu as lineage structures** (p. 100): Norwegian Expedition: "It seems likely that each ahu belonged to an extended family or lineage. When a family line died out its ahu probably was abandoned and fell into disrepair."

51. **Early Period: quality construction, solar orientation** (pp. 100-101): "The Early Period was notable for the quality of ahu construction. At Vinapu, one of the great ceremonial centers of this period, the ahu were then oriented toward the sun."

52. **Middle Period: crude construction, images** (p. 101): "In the Middle Period, the quality of ahu construction declined, while architectural and artistic interest shifted to the making and setting in place of the great images."

53. **Late Period: toppling, burial cairns** (pp. 101-102): "During this period, which seems to have been one of great violence, the stone images were toppled and the ahu, reduced to mounds of rubble, became burial cairns."

### Moai

54. **600 images** (p. 101): "some 600 images ranging in size from small 3-foot figures to one 70-foot giant that had never left the quarry."

55. **Images as symbols of chiefly mana** (p. 102): "It is certainly reasonable to assume that the figures were, in fact, measures of chiefly mana, and that they represented the chiefs."

56. **Competitive rivalry in image production** (p. 102): "Metraux' information that Easter Islanders vied with one another in producing the largest images brings us back to the idea that whether they represented living or dead chiefs they were indeed specific symbols of the power of chiefs and of their lineages."

57. **500 men to move** (pp. 102-103): "According to Heyerdahl's estimate it would have required up to 500 men to move one large image."

58. **"Walked" by mana of ariki** (p. 103): "The native explanation, which is not without interest, is that the statues 'walked' to their sites propelled by the mana of the ariki."

### KEY: Goldman's assessment of who built the ahu

59. **KEY: No central authority undertook local monuments** (pp. 106-107): "There is no reason to believe, however, that a central authority ever undertook the construction of local monuments. Since all but the few major ahu were local, they must be assumed to have been constructed under local auspices."

60. **No Polynesian precedent for centralized local monument construction** (p. 107): "Everywhere else in Polynesia local maraes were always the product of local labor."

61. **Captive labor, not chiefly redistribution** (pp. 107-108): Goldman offers "an alternative explanation for the construction of image ahu; that is, the use of force or captive labor." He proposes kio (conquered peoples) provided labor: "the chief of a victorious military coalition allotted kio among his allies to post a monument, probably a victory memorial."

62. **Each statue a sign of military victory** (p. 108): "Each statue would therefore be a sign of military victory and in that very specific sense it could be said to have moved to the site by the mana of the chief."

63. **Images toppled when chiefs lost mana** (pp. 108-109): "They were erected in the spirit of status rivalry and they were toppled when the local chiefs were defeated in war or otherwise lost their mana."

### The Orongo cult and Bird Man

64. **Orongo as sole public ceremonial center** (p. 103): "At Orongo, a village on the southwestern tip of the island, the Easter Islanders had constructed their one great public ceremonial center."

65. **Religious evolution parallel to social evolution** (p. 103): Excavation "revealed evidence of a religious evolution, parallel in some degree to the social evolution suggested by the developments in ahu construction and function."

66. **From sun cult to Bird Man cult** (pp. 104-105): Shift "from a sun cult, or some other generalized cult, to a specific dedication to the Bird Man cult of the warriors."

67. **Bird Man competition** (p. 105): "The chief to whom this first egg was brought won the sacred title of Bird Man (tangata manu) and was for the entire year the incarnation of the god Makemake."

68. **Bird Man presented with offerings** (p. 105): "As a god he was secluded, but presented by the entire island with offerings of food."

69. **Terror and plunder by victorious party** (p. 105): "Mrs. Routledge was told that the victorious party also showed its secular might by terrorizing and plundering all who could not resist them."

70. **Ariki mau had no role in Bird Man cult** (p. 106): "The island ariki, the traditional sacred ruler, had no role in the Bird Man cult other than to signal the start of the egg hunt."

### Historical recapitulation: Open society development

71. **Early Period = Traditional society** (pp. 105-106): "The Early Period appears only vaguely in the genealogical traditions and in the archeological record. Nevertheless, the specific information from both sources, meager as it is, does in fact suggest a Traditional society."

72. **Middle Period = shift to Open** (p. 106): "Quality architecture is an expression of highest rank, while mass defines best the interests of lower ranks." Mass production of images = political rivalries of the Open society.

73. **Tongariki (plebeian center) surpassed Vinapu (aristocratic center)** (p. 107): "By such standards of rivalry, Tongariki (the plebeian center with its 15 images) would seem to have surpassed aristocratic Vinapu (with only 6) as the major center of political power during the Middle Period."

74. **Late Period = chaos, not innovation** (p. 107): "The overthrow of the images marks not a reconstruction but the inevitable chaos and violence of the militarized Open society."

75. **Religious centralism ≠ political centralism** (p. 109): "Religious centralism clearly did not imply political centralism, judging only by the short duration of periods of peace."

### The status system

76. **Dual status system** (p. 109): "Easter Island, like Mangaia, had a dual status system: one, traditional, organized around sacred ariki and their descent lines; the other, highly mobile and representing warrior chiefs and their separate descent lines."

77. **Never stabilized** (p. 109): "This complementary division of authority seems never to have stabilized itself."

78. **Progressive constriction of ariki authority** (pp. 109-110): "Once the division was formed, the authority of the ariki was assailed by the matatoa and progressively constricted to its purely religious functions."

79. **Matatoa gained sanctity but remained lower status** (p. 110): "the matatoa, who had begun with a secular authority and had gained sanctity, remained, nevertheless, in a lower status category outside the realm of authentic pedigree."

### Sanctity of the ariki mau

80. **Extreme personal sanctity** (pp. 111-112): "His great personal sanctity persisted well into the European period." Head particularly sacred, hair never cut, touching tapu risked supernatural death, possessions tapu, eating and sleeping private.

81. **Ariki mau controlled crop growth through mana** (p. 112): "The ariki mau controlled the growth of crops and the abundance of edible marine life directly through his mana."

82. **Ariki mau inaugurated all new houses and canoes** (p. 112): "He inaugurated all new houses and all new canoes."

83. **First fruits** (p. 112): "he initiated the harvest of all crops and received first fruits."

84. **Miru shared royal sanctity** (p. 112): "Routledge was told that all Miru tribesmen shared in the royal sanctity."

### Specialists and priests

85. **Craftsmen (maori/tohunga) accorded highest respect** (p. 113): "Craftsmen and experts -- in recent times called maori but currently tohunga -- were accorded the highest respects."

86. **Stone carver guild** (p. 113): "The stone carvers, who had been organized in a 'guild' in which membership went from father to son, had a prestige comparable to that of royalty."

87. **Specialist communities = Open society characteristic** (p. 107): "Such specialist communities -- possibly guilds -- were in Polynesia a distinctive characteristic of Open and Stratified societies. They do not appear in any of the Traditional societies."

### KEY: Redistribution on Easter Island

88. **KEY: Redistribution lay with commoner heads, NOT ariki mau** (pp. 114-115): "Information on wealth and its role in status is sketchy. Such as it is, it suggests that on Easter Island the main role of economic redistribution or recirculation lay not with the ariki mau, but with heads of commoner groups."

89. **Ariki mau distributed only to elites** (p. 115): "When the ariki mau received the tuna catch that was his ritual tithe, he distributed it only among the 'important old men' (Metraux 1940:132)."

90. **Warrior chiefs held honorific role in redistribution** (p. 115): "it was the warriors, the commoners who had achieved high military status or who had acquired wealth, who held the honorific role as centers of economic redistribution."

91. **Three major feast types** (pp. 114-115): paina (memorial for deceased), koro (honoring feast for living kin), areauti (display-of-wealth feast). All were non-chiefly distributions.

### Political hegemony

92. **Thomson vs. Roussel on royal power** (p. 115): "Thomson said that the king was undisturbed by the results of wars and had the authority to remove or put to death any district chief and then to name a successor (1891:472). But this view of the royal power is flatly contradicted by the very able Roussel, who said that the royal authority had become 'nil'."

93. **Ariki mau probably never a powerful secular ruler** (p. 115): "From our knowledge of traditional authority in Polynesia, the ariki mau was probably never a powerful secular ruler, but he may have been the titular holder of lands and the central figure in ritual and property distribution."

94. **Political evolution: not transfer but growth** (pp. 115-116): "The political evolution of Easter Island is perhaps best described not as a transfer of powers from ariki to warrior but rather as a growth in political power resulting from the emergence of the matatoa as an independent force."

95. **No suggestion ariki mau ever presided over conquered populations** (p. 116): "There is certainly no suggestion from any source that the ariki mau ever presided over conquered populations."

96. **Political overlordship became predatory** (p. 116): "There seems little reason to doubt that political overlordship had become largely predatory and had lost whatever constructive and organizing functions it had once had."

### Descent group organization

97. **Easter Island lacked rangatira rank** (p. 116): "Easter Island lacked the transitional rank of rangatira. This position, which in Traditional societies was ordinarily held by segment heads below the rank of ariki, was on Easter held by rankless matatoa."

98. **Political separatism** (p. 108): "the persistence of Segment II as an ahu-building and therefore corporate body bears out the traditional genealogical records on Easter Island political separatism, a major characteristic of the Open society."

99. **Segment I unstable** (p. 118): "The most striking feature of Segment I was its unstable political character."

100. **Easter Island committed only to religious unity** (pp. 118-119): "Mangaia had already established a principle of full political and religious unity, but Easter Island was committed only to the principle of religious unity, political ties remaining tenuous."

101. **Political tradition was separatist** (p. 119): "The political tradition was separatist."

102. **Mata as territorial, not genealogical** (p. 120): Metraux: "The old territorial divisions thus came to be mere districts where the main part of the mata, perhaps the senior line, was settled and had little to do with the actual distribution of the tribes."

103. **Only Miru had traditional organization** (pp. 119-120): "Segment II consisted of the ten or so mata... insofar as we know, maintained no genealogies and had no regular forms of genealogical rank. Each was headed by a warrior chief who had assumed command because of his military successes rather than by genealogical succession."

### Goldman's evolutionary assessment

104. **Open society with divided social structure** (pp. 120-121): "At the level of Segment I, Easter Island presents a divided social structure, one mata conforming approximately to the traditional type, the others having a distinctive structure."

105. **Marquesas as more evolved Open** (pp. 122-123): "If Mangaia and Easter represent two phases of 'dual' status, the Marquesas differ in having evolved an open status system that was unitary."

106. **Easter Island as dualism "in process of being overcome"** (p. 123): "If Mangaia stands as an example of emergent dualism, and Easter is an example of dualism in process of being overcome, we may then consider the Marquesas as an example of synthesis."

### How Goldman uses each source category

**Category 1: Metraux as dominant source (~50 references)**

Goldman's Easter Island chapter is overwhelmingly built on Metraux. All of the following claims derive from Metraux:

- Political organization: dual status system, matatoa displacement of ariki, Roussel's account of ariki powerlessness (Metraux 1940:134-135)
- Social structure: mata composition, lineage organization, land tenure, genealogical traditions (Metraux 1940:20, 74, 124)
- Redistribution: tuna catch distribution to "important old men" only (Metraux 1940:132)
- Status markers: craftsmen, priests, rongorongo (Metraux 1940:136-137, 177)
- Feasts: paina, koro, areauti (Metraux 1940:343, 345)
- Matatoa: professional warriors, tuna offering privilege (Metraux 1940:138)
- Kio (war captives): treatment, tribute (Metraux 1940:139)
- Moai: competitive production, walking by mana (Metraux 1940:307)
- Hotu-matua traditions: settlement, tribal origins (Metraux 1940:74)
- Marriage rules: Miru endogamy (Metraux 1940:124)
- Bird Children seclusion rites (Metraux 1940:105)

Goldman also uses Metraux 1957 for Roussel's statement on ariki losing power (1957:43) and for the Gunther/image dating debate (1957:161).

**CRITICAL ASSESSMENT**: Goldman's reliance on Metraux means his analysis inherits all of Metraux's evidentiary limitations. Metraux conducted fieldwork in 1934-1935 among a population of 456, seventy years after the slave raids. Metraux himself was a careful historian who flagged many of these limitations (see our Metraux 1937/1940/1957 claims files). Goldman accepts Metraux's account as reliable salvage ethnography ("a most thorough and judicious job of cultural salvage," p. 97) without independently questioning the post-raid transmission problem.

**Category 2: Roussel (always mediated through Metraux, ~6 references)**

Goldman never cites Roussel directly. Every Roussel quote is taken from Metraux 1940 or 1957. Goldman uses Roussel for:

- Kings who "in the beginning were considered deities and had absolute power" but "did not retain such authority for long" (via Metraux 1957:43, p. 98)
- Royal authority "nil and entirely disregarded," passed "entirely into the hands of the matatoa" (via Metraux 1940:135, p. 98)
- Matatoa won tuna offering privilege (via Metraux 1940:138, p. 113)
- Kio treatment: forced labor, tribute, cave imprisonment (via Metraux 1940:139, pp. 115-116)

**CRITICAL ASSESSMENT**: Goldman evaluates Roussel as "the best source on aboriginal Easter Island culture" (p. 98), taking this assessment from Metraux. He presents Roussel's claim about original "absolute power" of kings without noting that Metraux himself (1937) dismissed this as an "entirely gratuitous assumption." Goldman also does not note that Roussel arrived just one year after the slave raids and was recording traditions from survivors whose institutional memory had been catastrophically disrupted. Goldman uses Roussel's conflicting testimony strategically: the "nil" authority passage supports the Open classification, while the "absolute power in the beginning" passage supports the Traditional -> Open developmental trajectory. Goldman does not flag the internal contradiction.

**Category 3: Routledge 1919 (~9 references)**

Goldman cites Routledge for:

- Bird Man cult: victorious party "terrorizing and plundering" (p. 105)
- Mother's side could enlist loyalty in war (1919:226, p. 110)
- Miru skulls incised with fish designs as heirlooms (1919:240, p. 112)
- All Miru shared royal sanctity, "fowlheads" (p. 112)
- Ivi-atua as seers; one ivi-atua identified as "poor relation" of Bird Man (1919:264, p. 113)
- Western/eastern division of Orongo ceremonial center (footnote, p. 104, via Metraux 1940:125)
- Population estimate of 7,000 from carrying capacity (p. 108)

**CRITICAL ASSESSMENT**: Goldman uses Routledge selectively. He draws on specific ethnographic details (matrilineal war loyalties, skull heirlooms, Bird Man cult violence) but never quotes or engages with Routledge's strongest findings: "no chiefs nor any form of government" (p. 224), ariki-mau "neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest" (p. 242), or the ao system of rotating clan dominance. Routledge's independent observation that matatoa/matakio were rotating statuses rather than permanent classes is also absent. Goldman's conclusions are consistent with Routledge's findings, but he reaches them through Metraux rather than through direct engagement with Routledge's more detailed account.

**Category 4: Thomson 1891 (~4 references)**

Goldman cites Thomson for:

- 57-generation king list (p. 99, p. 111)
- Rongorongo priests as members of distinguished families (1891:514, p. 113)
- King "undisturbed by results of wars" with authority to remove district chiefs (1891:472, p. 115)

**CRITICAL ASSESSMENT**: Goldman uses Thomson's strongest chiefdom-supporting claim (king with authority to remove chiefs) only to set up the contradiction with Roussel. He introduces it as "Thomson said" and immediately notes it is "flatly contradicted by the very able Roussel." Goldman does not note that Thomson's 12-day visit in 1886 was entirely mediated through Alexander Salmon as translator, or that Thomson's king list of 57 generations is irreconcilable with Roussel's 22 generations (only ~12 overlap). Goldman accepts both the Thomson and Roussel accounts as representing legitimate but conflicting evidence, without examining whether the conflicts might reflect the unreliability of the post-raid informant base.

**Category 5: Heyerdahl 1961 / Norwegian Expedition (~13 references total)**

Goldman cites the Norwegian Expedition reports for all archaeological claims:

- Radiocarbon dates: Poike ditch A.D. 386 +/- 100 (1961:394, p. 99); Poike battle 1680 (p. 99); Rano Raraku quarries 1476 (p. 102)
- Three-period chronology: Early (400-1100), Middle (1100-1680), Late (1680-1868) (pp. 100-101)
- Ahu as lineage structures (C. S. Smith in 1961:183, p. 100)
- Orongo center excavation and religious evolution (Ferdon, pp. 103-105)
- Image emplacement on ahu dating to Middle Period (pp. 101-102)
- 500 men estimate for moving statues (1961:370, p. 103)

**CRITICAL ASSESSMENT**: Goldman's archaeological framework is entirely the Norwegian Expedition's. He adopts Heyerdahl's three-period scheme, radiocarbon dates, and the Poike ditch/battle narrative without qualification. The Poike ditch date (A.D. 386) and the "Long-Ears vs. Short-Ears" tradition are now considered unreliable. The 500-person estimate for statue transport has been challenged by Lipo et al. 2013 (18-person walking transport). Goldman adds his own interpretive layer (quality = traditional; mass production = Open society) but the underlying data is entirely from Heyerdahl's team.

**Category 6: Contact-era accounts (mentioned but NOT used)**

Goldman mentions Roggeveen, Cook, and La Perouse only in the introductory passage (p. 96) to note that they "did not record culture or traditions." He does not use any contact-era observation for evidence about political organization.

Specific contact-era uses:
- Cook: learned that "some images carried the names of ariki" (p. 102). This is Goldman's only substantive use of a contact-era account.
- La Perouse: "took it to be fact that they portrayed chiefs" (p. 102). Used for moai interpretation, not political organization.
- La Perouse date error: Goldman gives "1746" instead of 1786 (p. 96).

**CRITICAL ASSESSMENT**: Goldman's dismissal of contact-era accounts as not recording "culture or traditions" is factually incorrect. Cook's 1774 expedition included detailed observations by Wales and the Forsters. La Perouse's 1786 visit was the most methodologically careful contact-era expedition and explicitly reported on political organization ("no need of a chief," equal conditions, no observable authority structure). Goldman's failure to use these accounts is a significant blind spot. The contact-era accounts would have independently supported his "Open" classification, but Goldman does not appear to have read them carefully.

**Category 7: Minor/tangential sources**

- Barthel 1958: rongorongo script analysis ("embryo writing")

### Summary: Goldman's evidentiary chain

Goldman's Easter Island chapter is built on a **two-source foundation**:

1. **Metraux 1940** (and 1957): The source for virtually all ethnographic claims about social structure, political organization, status system, redistribution, traditions, and customs. Metraux is the funnel through which earlier sources (Roussel, Thomson, Englert) reach Goldman. Goldman rarely goes behind Metraux to check the original sources.

2. **Heyerdahl 1961** (Norwegian Expedition): The source for all archaeological claims. Goldman builds his three-period developmental model (Traditional -> Open) on the Expedition's ahu typology, radiocarbon dates, and Orongo stratigraphy.

**Routledge 1919** provides supplementary ethnographic details but is not systematically engaged. **Thomson 1891** is cited only three times, mainly to establish a contradiction with Roussel. **Contact-era accounts are not used.**

**What this means for our argument**: Goldman and Sahlins both built their Easter Island analyses primarily on Metraux 1940, yet reached opposite conclusions. Sahlins found moderate-high stratification (Group IIA) by asking about productivity-stratification correlation and filling gaps with Polynesian analogy. Goldman found an Open society by asking about the relationship between status and power and taking Metraux's own characterization of ariki powerlessness and matatoa dominance at face value. The divergence is methodological, not evidentiary: same data, different questions, different answers. This is itself significant for the epistemological argument. When the same ethnographic base supports contradictory classifications, the classification is doing more work than the evidence.

Goldman does not use any contact-era observations about political authority. He relies entirely on post-raid sources and the Norwegian Expedition.

## V. Key Claims for Our Argument

### The "never evolved a central authority" statement

124. **The clearest statement** (p. 94): "By social standards, it stands with the Open societies, which, like Mangaia and the Marquesas, had never evolved a central authority." This is the clearest statement by any Polynesian comparativist that Easter Island lacked centralized political authority.

### Scope of "no central authority"

125. **"Central authority" = island-wide paramount**: Goldman's use of "central authority" throughout the book consistently refers to island-wide or supra-local authority. He uses the term when discussing Tongan, Tahitian, and Hawaiian centralization (e.g., pp. 279, 285, 373, 394, 395). For Easter Island, he explicitly states the ariki mau was "probably never a powerful secular ruler" (p. 115) and that "political overlordship had become largely predatory" (p. 116). However, Goldman does describe functioning local-level authority by matatoa within each mata. The "Open" classification is about the character of authority (military vs. hereditary) and the absence of centralization, not about the absence of all leadership.

126. **Local authority by matatoa, not ariki** (p. 120): "all or most of the mata were subdivided." Each plebeian mata "was headed by a warrior chief who had assumed command because of his military successes rather than by genealogical succession." Authority existed at local levels but was achieved, not inherited, and fragmented, not centralized.

127. **Religious unity only, not political unity** (pp. 118-119): "Easter Island was committed only to the principle of religious unity, political ties remaining tenuous."

### Redistribution

128. **Redistribution bypassed the ariki mau** (pp. 114-115): Goldman explicitly states the main role of redistribution lay with commoner heads, not the ariki mau. The ariki mau distributed only to "important old men." This directly contradicts the Sahlins/Kirch assumption that the chief was the redistributive center. [Cross-reference: Sahlins 1954, p. 101: "little direct evidence" of ariki mau redistribution.]

### Assessment of evidence quality

129. **Goldman does not discuss the slave raids' effect on evidence quality** beyond noting the population collapse (p. 96) and the timing of culture's "abrupt end" (p. 96). He does not raise the specific epistemological problem of whether post-raid traditions can reliably describe pre-raid organization. He treats Metraux as reliable and does not flag the post-raid evidentiary problem that our paper identifies.

## VI. Goldman vs. Sahlins vs. Kirch

### How Goldman's "Open" differs from Sahlins's Group IIA

130. **Different classification criteria**: Sahlins classified by productivity-stratification correlation. Goldman classified by the relationship between status and power. Sahlins placed Easter Island in Group IIA (moderate-high stratification). Goldman placed Easter Island as "Open" (power dominant over status; military over hereditary). These are not directly comparable categories, but Goldman's classification is significantly less hierarchical: "Open" societies have no stable political centralization, only competing war leaders.

131. **Sahlins's Group IIA implies functioning chiefdom; Goldman's "Open" implies its absence**: Sahlins's classification assumed redistribution (filled by analogy when evidence was lacking). Goldman's classification explicitly places redistribution with commoner heads, not the paramount.

### Whether Goldman engages with Sahlins

132. **Goldman cites Sahlins 1959** (p. 23): Goldman mentions "a more detailed version of this theory has been put forth by Sahlins (1959)" regarding the productivity-power correlation, and rejects the approach as too abstract. He does not specifically discuss Sahlins's Easter Island placement.

133. **No direct engagement with Sahlins's Group IIA for Easter Island**: Goldman does not name or rebut Sahlins's classification of Easter Island. The two frameworks operate independently.

### Kirch's handling of Goldman

134. **Kirch includes Goldman in Table 2 (p. 37)**: Goldman's "Open" classification for Easter Island is displayed alongside Sahlins's Group IIA.

135. **Kirch never discusses the contradiction in Ch. 11**: The Easter Island chapter (Ch. 11, pp. 264-278) does not mention Goldman's "Open" classification or address why he classified Easter Island differently from Sahlins.

136. **This is a significant omission**: Goldman's framework is an independent assessment by a recognized Polynesian scholar. Goldman's "Open" classification, with its emphasis on military displacement of hereditary chiefs, is consistent with the matatoa-dominated system described by Routledge and Metraux. Kirch's failure to engage with it means the most prominent counter-classification was acknowledged but never addressed.
