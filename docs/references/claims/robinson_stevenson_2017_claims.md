# Claims Extraction: Robinson & Stevenson 2017

**Source**: Robinson, Taylor, and Christopher M. Stevenson. 2017. "The Cult of the Birdman: religious change at 'Orongo, Rapa Nui." *Journal of Pacific Archaeology* 8(2): 88-102.

**Type**: Journal article (archaeological chronology and interpretation)
**Accessed**: PDF (`robinson_stevenson_2017.pdf`)
**Extracted**: 2026-03-16

---

## I. Core Argument and Framing

1. "On the island of Rapa Nui the Cult of the Birdman reflected a very visible expression of political competition and co-operation at the island-wide level. This paper synthesizes recent archaeological and chronological information for the cult's central site, 'Orongo, in order to document the temporal shift in ideology from an emphasis on lineage autonomy to a more integrated leadership." (p. 88, abstract)

2. "While Rapa Nui was experiencing internal pressures from the loss of arable land and territory reconfiguration, brought on by soil nutrient depletion and limited rainfall, we hypothesize it was only the events associated with repeated European contact that were sufficiently disruptive to initiate rapid social change at the collective level." (p. 88, abstract)

3. "The first activities at 'Orongo, occurred during the early AD 1600s. Activities intensified near AD 1800 possibly due to the negative impacts of European contact and it is hypothesized that stone house construction occurred at this time." (p. 88, abstract)

**Assessment**: The paper frames the Birdman Cult as a response to both internal ecological pressures and European contact, but attributes the decisive intensification to post-contact disruption. This is significant for the chiefdom question: if the Birdman Cult's intensification is a contact-period phenomenon driven by European disruption, then it cannot serve as evidence for a prehistoric political transition from hereditary chiefdom to competitive warrior-chief system.

4. "We evaluate chronometric data from the investigations conducted by Ferdon (1961) Cristino (1995), and Orliac & Orliac (1996) and conclude, based on the current evidence, that activities at 'Orongo originated before European contact in AD 1722 but intensified in the last part of the 18th century." (p. 89)

5. "Identifying the beginning of the Birdman Cult in the archaeological record serves as a proxy indicator of the period when Rapa Nui was experiencing a social reformation and was looking for a new ideological pathway and leadership alternatives that crystalized after contact with European navigators." (p. 89)

**Assessment**: Robinson & Stevenson explicitly link the crystallization of the Birdman Cult's leadership model to the post-contact period. If the Birdman institution only "crystalized" after European contact, then the contact-era observations made by Roggeveen, Gonzalez, and Cook were witnessing a society in the process of political transformation driven by European disruption, not a stable prehistoric political system.

## II. Prehistoric Sociopolitical Context

6. "The prehistoric political system of Rapa Nui is inferred to have been a hierarchical chiefdom with its roots in Ancestral Polynesian Society (Kirch 2000)." (p. 90)

**Assessment**: The authors present the chiefdom attribution as an inference, not an established fact. The word "inferred" is notable.

7. "Settlement pattern analyses of megalithic platforms (*ahu*) and residential dwellings (McCoy 1976; Stevenson 1986; Stevenson & Haoa 2008) have revealed spatially demarcated central places and inequalities in house architecture that reflect social ranking (see Morrison 2012 for an alternate interpretation)." (p. 90)

**Assessment**: The authors acknowledge Morrison 2012 as offering an alternative interpretation to the chiefly-hierarchy reading of settlement patterns. The parenthetical acknowledgment suggests awareness that the evidence is not unambiguous.

8. "The largest *ahu* few in number and were often positioned at the location of shallow bays that provided easier access to the ocean. It is hypothesized that these structures were commissioned and produced by corporate groups, or allied descent lines, that marked ritual precincts (Stevenson 1986)." (p. 90)

9. "Archaeological survey of *ahu* at the regional or island-wide level (Englert 1948; McCoy 1976; Stevenson 1986; Martinsson-Wallin 1994) documents uniformity in the form of architectural components (e.g., platforms, plazas, crematoria) and similar building materials (e.g., tuff, scoria) that reflect cooperation in economic exchange, but expressions of an integrated governance, or paramountcy, at the regional level has not been discovered archaeologically or reported in the ethnohistoric oral history." (p. 90)

**Assessment**: This is a critical admission. The authors state that paramountcy, i.e. centralized authority above the local level, "has not been discovered archaeologically or reported in the ethnohistoric oral history." This directly undermines the chiefdom attribution, which typically requires some form of regional political integration above the lineage level.

10. "The largest *ahu* on Rapa Nui located at Tongariki is a potential candidate for the residence of a paramount chief but there are no recognized material expressions of an elevated leadership role at present." (p. 90)

**Assessment**: Even the most plausible candidate for a paramount chief's seat lacks material evidence for that function.

11. "These informants state the population of Rapa Nui was divided into ten tribes along lines of paternal descent. According to Metraux, each of these tribes had a common ancestor who was descended from Hotu-matua, the first 'king' of the island (Metraux 1940:121)." (p. 90) [POST-RAID]

**Assessment**: The ten-tribe political structure is known only from post-slave-raid informants recorded by Routledge (1919) and Metraux (1940). Whether this structure was operative in the pre-contact period, or represents a post-collapse reconstruction of political memory, cannot be determined from these sources alone.

12. "While evidence of warfare is inferential and difficult to place (often people cite *mata'a* obsidian tools as weapons [Flenley & Bahn 2002]; when they are fairly ambiguous as such [Hunt & Lipo 2011; Lipo *et al.* 2016]), this dual division was likely a late development in the wider sociopolitical trajectory of the island." (p. 90)

## III. Peripheral Cults and Ideological Change

13. "On Rapa Nui, the Cult of the Birdman is thought to represent a significant change in the form of leadership. The prehistoric political structure on the island recognized the legitimacy of chiefly authority based both on their descent from the founding ancestors of the island as well as from the gods themselves (Metraux 1940:130)." (p. 90) [POST-RAID]

14. "The *ariki-mau*, or chief, was seen to possess *mana*, a type of supernatural power that separated him from the commoners and legitimized his leadership role." (p. 90) [POST-RAID]

**Assessment**: The description of the *ariki-mau* and *mana*-based leadership derives from Metraux 1940, a post-slave-raid source. This is the standard Polynesian chiefdom model applied to Rapa Nui via ethnographic analogy. Whether it accurately reflects pre-contact political organization is precisely the question.

15. "Assuming that Metraux is correct, the Cult of the Birdman took advantage of the idea that people, not directly descended from the gods, could also increase their own level of personal *mana*. Every year, the selected Birdman was considered to possess an elevated level of *mana* not because of his genealogical position, but because the god *Makemake* chose that particular man as a physical manifestation of himself for the year (Metraux 1940:336)." (p. 90) [POST-RAID]

**Assessment**: The phrase "Assuming that Metraux is correct" is a significant qualification. The authors' interpretation of the Birdman Cult as a challenge to hereditary authority depends entirely on Metraux's post-slave-raid ethnographic account. The authors do not independently verify this account.

16. "Participants in peripheral cults are often not those of lower social rank, but rather 'a class of socially mobile people whose ambitions are at odds with their traditional obligations' (Lewis 1975:131)." (p. 91)

17. Routledge (1998:260) "described the cult participants as 'men of importance'. Metraux (1940:333) interpreted this as the category of *matatoa*, the warrior, of which only dominant, victorious tribes took part in the quest for the egg and the attendant ceremonies, for 'the privileges at stake were too important to be vested in an inferior or defeated group'." (p. 91) [POST-RAID]

18. "The *matatoa* were socially mobile; although they already held the political responsibility for leading the lineages in day-to-day activities, they could never be higher than the chief. The cult's emphasis on the annual finding of the first egg highlighted a basis in annual achievement as ordained by the god *Makemake*. Through such a ceremony, the *matatoa* found a way to compete with each other, as well as to live with a level of supernatural *mana* comparable to the chief, if only for a year." (p. 91)

**Assessment**: This passage reveals a key tension. The *matatoa* already "held the political responsibility for leading the lineages in day-to-day activities" yet were formally subordinate to the chief. The Birdman Cult allowed them to claim comparable supernatural status. This suggests the political reality may have involved distributed leadership at the lineage level with a largely ceremonial paramount, rather than the hierarchical chiefdom the model implies.

19. "Lewis (1975:130) proposes that religious movements that involve direct spirit possession arise as peripheral cults to central religions. Such central religions are usually characterized as rigid and dogmatic, removing religious experience from people and placing it in the control of expert priests. Peripheral cults arise as a mechanism for individuals in society to practice agency and to react to changing circumstances when the central religion has become too rigid in its dogma to react appropriately to an unstable situation." (p. 91)

20. "We propose that contact with Europeans beginning in the early 18th century resulted in pressures in both the psychological and biological domains that over several generations led to a post-contact distress that was addressed through a change in ritual practice." (p. 91)

## IV. Contact History and Disease

21. "It appears from the navigator accounts that interactions were welcomed until approximately AD 1806 when an abrupt change in Rapa Nui attitude arose and Europeans were met at the shoreline with a shower of stones." (p. 91)

22. "We hypothesize that this reversal of attitude reflects the period when biological pathogens began to seriously impact the health of the population who inferred (correctly) that the illness must have come from the outsiders." (p. 91)

23. "Hunt and Lipo (2011) suggest the first major disease outbreak was brought by the Spanish in AD 1770 and the aftermath was witnessed by Captain Cook four years later." (p. 91)

**Assessment**: If disease impacts began as early as 1770, the political disruption visible to Cook in 1774 (including toppled moai) could reflect disease-driven crisis rather than a longer-term prehistoric political transition.

## V. Archaeological Context at 'Orongo

### Complex A

24. "Ferdon's excavations at Complex A revealed a large plaza, two structural foundations, and a number of petroglyphs depicting the god *Makemake*. He argues that this section of 'Orongo was occupied first and is associated with the deity of *Makemake* and pre-Birdman ceremonies." (p. 92)

25. "In relation to the other complexes of the 'Orongo village, Ferdon states that Complex A was abandoned around AD 1420 based on radiocarbon date (540+/-70 BP, T-193, Table 1) taken from a sample of charcoal in a fire pit in front of Structure I (Ferdon 1961:248). Recalibration of this date (Stuiver and Reimer 1986-2011) derived from bulk charcoal with a potential in-built age is not so specific and defines a 2-sigma age range of AD 1300-AD 1617." (p. 92)

**Assessment**: Ferdon's date of "around AD 1420" for Complex A is based on a bulk charcoal sample with a broad 2-sigma range extending to AD 1617. This undermines the precision of the claim that Complex A was abandoned by the early 15th century.

26. "The two structures of Complex A are foundations of *ahu* platforms. Structure I has a boulder pavement leading up to the platform, and behind it is a pit within which the remains of a child were found (Lee 1992:132)." (p. 92)

27. "In addition to the two structures, a supposed solstice marker was positioned in front and west of Structure I. The stones of the solstice marker are adorned with crude petroglyphs depicting faces. The faces are associated with *Makemake*. On one boulder leading up to Structure I, however, Ferdon (1961:231) noted a *Makemake* face with a much more sophisticated petroglyph of a Birdman superimposed upon it." (p. 92)

### 'Orongo Village, Complex B

28. "'Orongo Village is characterized by two rows of about 55 houses, all facing southwest, and constructed with large tabular slabs of basalt." (p. 92)

29. "All the Birdmen petroglyphs in the village were created in bas-relief, a later development from the initial practice of simply pecking the outline of the Birdman. *Komari* (female genetalia) symbols are more frequent at this locality and *Makemake* faces are also present (Lee 1992:135)." (p. 92)

30. "Ferdon (1961) argues that 'Orongo Village was occupied after AD 1540 (see our evaluation below) as a place to live in during religious ceremonies." (p. 92)

### Mata Ngarau, Complex C

31. "Mata Ngarau is another section of 'Orongo with houses, somewhat spatially continuous with the 'Orongo village that were constructed in a semi-circle that faces the offshore islets associated with the Birdman ceremonies." (p. 93)

32. "Lee (1992) notes that Mata Ngarau's petroglyphs are often obscured and covered by the masonry of houses, suggesting that the houses may have been an even later development." (p. 93)

33. "The petroglyphs at Mata Ngarau are of high-quality, showing different steps of development for the Birdman motif from simple pecked forms that allowed for considerable graphic variation to a firmly established later canon of bas-relief depictions (Lee 1992:140)." (p. 93)

34. "The *komari* is thus a later addition to the Birdman depictions." (p. 93)

## VI. Archaeological Chronology: Radiocarbon Evidence

35. "'Orongo has 86% (~413) of the 481 iterations of the birdman motif found on the island (Lee 1992:66). Clearly, 'Orongo had a special role in the prehistory of Rapa Nui." (p. 91)

36. "Of the early navigators, only the physician of the La Perouse expedition in 1786 climbs Rano Kau, and although geographically closer than any previous European visitor, the La Perouse expedition does not verify the presence of 'Orongo in the 18th century." (pp. 93-94)

37. "It is not until nearly three-quarters of a century later that 'Orongo is described by Palmer (1869), Pinart (1878), Geissler (1883), and Thompson (1891); and finally mapped by Routledge (1920)." (p. 94)

**Assessment**: The site of 'Orongo was not described by any European visitor until 1869. The La Perouse physician climbed Rano Kau in 1786 but did not record the site. This means there is no European documentation of 'Orongo before the 1860s, making it impossible to use contact-era accounts to confirm the site's pre-contact or even early contact-period significance.

### Ferdon's Excavations

38. "Ferdon was able to bracket the time period for the emplacement of the compact house construction fill layer with a date from above the deposit in Trench 1 (380+/-100 BP, K-514) and a date from a charcoal deposit in a pit beneath the layer in Trench 2 of AD 470+/-70 BP, T-194). Based upon his interpretation of rapid site formation processes, he favored an AD 1540-1576 date for the emplacement of the fill deposit and beginning of room construction in this area." (p. 94)

39. "However, the bulk samples of charcoal submitted for age assessment are not described in detail and are thus unreliable." (p. 94)

**Assessment**: The standard date of c. AD 1540 for 'Orongo house construction rests on bulk charcoal samples that "are not described in detail and are thus unreliable." This echoes Pollard et al. (2010) and reinforces the fragility of the conventional chronology.

### Orliac Excavations (1995)

40. "In 1995, Catherine and Michel Orliac made two small test excavations on the western sloping apron of the site located in front of the openings to the stone house entrances." (p. 94)

41. "Within Test Unit O88 were 7 mollusk shell fragments, 26 bird bones, 2 fish bones, and 29 pieces of obsidian, intermixed with 6257 charcoal fragments. Similarly, Test Unit O97 yielded 1147 mollusk fragments, 134 bird bones, 65 fish bones, and 153 obsidian flakes/fragments within a matrix containing 3959 pieces of charcoal." (p. 95)

42. "Also present in the test units were carbonized fragments of sweet potato (*Ipomoea battas*) along with two tentatively identified pieces of carbonized yam (*Dioscorea sp.*)." (p. 95)

43. "Radiocarbon dates from the new excavations (Table 1) were on small twigs as noted by the excavators." (p. 95)

**Assessment**: The Orliac dates on short-lived materials (small twigs) are more reliable than Ferdon's bulk charcoal dates.

### Test Unit O88 Dates

44. "A fragment from L.2 labeled O88-JA (Beta-099356) returned an age of 200+/-50 BP. Higher up in the profile from the thick deposit containing L. 4, 5, 7, a sample labeled O88-F returned an age of 210+/-50 BP (Beta-099347)." (p. 95)

45. "If the sandy-clay and gravel layer (L.3) has been correctly interpreted as the fill deposit associated with house construction, then the radiocarbon date of 200+/-50 BP from the sealed context below this layer suggests that the stone houses were constructed after this date (AD 1649-AD 1894)." (p. 95)

**Assessment**: Based on the Orliac dates, house construction at 'Orongo occurred after a date calibrating to AD 1649-1894. The midpoint of this range is well into the contact period. This supports Robinson & Stevenson's argument that stone house construction may be a late 18th-century phenomenon.

### Test Unit O97 Dates

46. "The radiocarbon dates by Orliac and Orliac (1996) are not stratigraphically consistent in Test Unit O97 and point to a mixing of the soil deposits, especially Level 2, where in addition to turbation, modern charcoal has also been introduced." (p. 96)

### Mata Ngarau Dates

47. "Two radiocarbon dates from this unit returned ages of 100+/-50 BP (Beta-099341) and 240+/-60 BP (Beta-099342)." (p. 96)

48. "If the radiocarbon dates from Test Unit O88, O97, and Mata Ngarau taken as a group, with the modern dates eliminated, they suggest activity in front of the houses at Complex B and C occurred in the middle AD 1500s (Figure 6) but the majority of the age determinations (9 of 10) indicates that cooking/burning activities start near AD 1650 or later." (p. 96)

**Assessment**: Nine of ten reliable radiocarbon dates from the Orliac excavations indicate activity beginning near AD 1650 or later. Only one date suggests activity in the middle AD 1500s. The overwhelming majority of dated activity is post-1650.

## VII. Obsidian Hydration Evidence

49. "Obsidian hydration dates from 'Orongo range in age from AD 1608 to AD 1862 with associated errors of 23-35 years based upon a measurement error of +/-0.007 infrared photoacoustic absorbance units." (p. 97)

50. "The distribution of dates (Table 2) did not show any clear increase in age with depth and indicated that prehistoric activity had resulted in a mixed deposit. This patterning indicates the simple stratigraphy exposed by Ferdon (1961) is not a reliable guide to the complex depositional history of this location." (p. 97)

**Assessment**: The obsidian hydration dates confirm that the stratigraphy Ferdon used to construct his chronology is unreliable. The mixed deposits undermine any interpretation that relies on simple stratigraphic layering to establish temporal sequence.

51. "The summed probability distributions indicated the beginning of activity at 'Orongo near AD 1600-1650 and an increase in obsidian discard around AD 1800. This increased frequency of discard is assumed to reflect an intensification of burning/cooking and ritual activity and we hypothesize at this time that the 'Orongo stone houses were constructed." (p. 98)

52. "The late-18th century spike in obsidian dates correlates with the use of 'Orongo based upon the succession of leaders recounted in the oral history; a structure which reflects a formalization of the collective memory." (p. 98)

53. A summed probability distribution of obsidian hydration dates shows "a site occupational range of AD 1600 to the early 20th century and a bimodal distribution with peak usages of obsidian at AD 1653 and AD 1795. The second peak is much larger and suggests an intensification of activities using obsidian at the very end of the 18th century." (p. 97)

**Assessment**: The bimodal distribution with a much larger peak at AD 1795 is the key chronological finding. Peak activity at 'Orongo occurred not in the mid-16th century as Ferdon proposed, but near the end of the 18th century, well into the contact period.

## VIII. Discussion: Reinterpreting the 'Orongo Chronology

54. "In reconsideration of the archaeological context, the bulk of the stratigraphic evidence presented by either Ferdon (1961) or Orliac and Orliac (1996) do not demonstrably link the deposits to stone house construction." (p. 98)

55. "In Trench 1, Level 3, the inferred house construction fill layer of compacted grey-brown soil (Figure 5) is not stratigraphically connected to the house foundation observed in Trench 2. Similarly, the sandy-clay and gravel layer located low in the profile of Test Unit O88 is well removed from the stone houses and only hypothesized to be associated with house construction based upon the coarse nature of the deposit. In each case the connections are inferential and the deposits could have been created by other earth-moving activities or natural processes." (p. 98)

**Assessment**: The authors explicitly state that none of the excavated deposits are demonstrably linked to stone house construction. All connections between dated deposits and construction events are inferential. This fundamentally undermines the standard chronology for 'Orongo's stone houses.

56. "If the obsidian dates are representative of the occupational history of Complex B at 'Orongo, then the creation of midden deposits created by food and charcoal discard begin about the same time as the abandonment of many upland agricultural fields in the middle and late AD 1600s (Stevenson *et al.* 2015) and near the mid-point of the island-wide drought (Canellas-Bolta *et al.* 2013; Rull 2016)." (p. 98)

57. "The late-18th century spike in obsidian hydration dates. But the construction of houses, as reflected by the inferred construction fill layer within the midden of Trench 1, occurred later in time. We propose this period came after the startling interactions associated with European and is reflected by the spike in obsidian hydration dates." (p. 98)

**Assessment**: The authors argue that while some activity at 'Orongo began in the 1600s, the construction of the stone houses and the major intensification of activity both occurred in the late 18th century, after sustained European contact. This is a much later timeline than the conventional AD 1540 date.

## IX. Discussion: Complex A, Ancestor Worship, and the Shift to Birdman

58. "'Orongo's Complex A has an archaeological record that shows a heavy emphasis on the traditional ancestor worship religion in ancient Rapa Nui society. From petroglyphs depicting the face of *Makemake* to the structural foundation of an *ahu* platform, Complex A seems to be oriented to the worship and recognition of *Makemake*, the main deity of Rapa Nui and from whom the chiefs were said to descend (Metraux 1940:311)." (p. 98) [POST-RAID]

59. "Ferdon argues that 'Orongo's Complex A was only occupied up until AD 1420 and then abandoned in favor of 'Orongo village and Mata Ngarau. The abandonment of Complex A and the use of 'Orongo village and Mata Ngarau as ceremonial centers suggest a movement away from ancestor worship toward the Cult of the Birdman, although the timing of this shift now appears to be much later due to a more recent chronological assessment of activities." (p. 98)

**Assessment**: The shift from ancestor worship (Complex A) to the Birdman Cult (Complexes B and C) is the key political-religious transition. Robinson & Stevenson argue this transition occurred much later than previously thought.

60. "It is possible that the statue *Hoa Hakananai'a* was once located at Complex A and later moved to 'Orongo village. *Hoa Hakananai'a* is actually one of the only statues considered to be directly related to worship on the island; it was used for initiation rites for children as well as the feasts of the Birdman (Metraux 1940:306, 335). Furthermore, the statue has both birdmen and *komari* motifs carved into its back, showing that it was being used in association with the Birdman Cult." (pp. 98-99) [POST-RAID]

## X. Discussion: Emergence and Timing of the Birdman Cult

61. "Birdmen petroglyphs were gradually added to the boulders at Mata Ngarau by creation/sponsorship/supervision by *matatoa* warriors interested in achieving greater status in the power void forming as people lost faith in their chiefs and religion." (p. 99)

62. "Gradually, these depictions became more skillful and more people began partaking in the associated annual competition to find the first sooty tern egg. Houses were built at 'Orongo village and Mata Ngarau, and a new priestly class began to distinguish themselves from ordinary participants." (p. 99)

63. "Ancestor worship may have been gradually forgotten, as the deity of *Makemake* was incorporated more and more into the Birdman ceremonies." (p. 99)

64. "After activities at the site had been occurring for about 150 years (AD 1650-1800) it is our hypothesis that stone houses were then built, and the concept of the Birdman was formalized and depicted by petroglyphs." (p. 99)

**Assessment**: Robinson & Stevenson propose that formal stone house construction and the formalization of the Birdman Cult concept occurred around AD 1800, not AD 1540. This pushes the institutional crystallization of the Birdman Cult firmly into the contact period.

65. "'Orongo's two lines of houses were said to be split by which tribes the participants came from; 'those from Hotu-iti dwelt in the western group of houses, and those from Tu'u in the houses to the east' (Metraux 1940:335)." (p. 99) [POST-RAID]

66. "Mata Ngarau was reserved for specialized *rongorongo* chanters, priests who chanted sacred lore and held specialized religious knowledge (Lee 1992:138)." (p. 99)

67. "The spatial division of 'Orongo village and its separation from the new class of priests at Mata Ngarau may be evidence that the Cult of the Birdman at the time of house construction had attained a level of prominence in society that had taken over ancestor worship as the central religious form of the island." (p. 99)

## XI. Discussion: European Contact and Cult Intensification

68. "Add to this scenario an increased frequency of European contact events after AD 1800 (Boersema 2015) and the possible introduction of diseases by the Spanish in AD 1770, and the intensification of cult activities reflects the seriousness of new problems." (p. 99)

69. "Supportive evidence for a connection of historical events to changes in the society's material expressions prior to missionization comes in the form of burial tombs approximating the shape of European ships (e.g., *ahu poepoe*) (Shaw 2000) and the construction of earthen replicas of western vessels (e.g., *miro o'one*) (McCoy 1976; Stevenson & Haoa 2008)." (p. 99)

---

## XII. Reasons for Cult Emergence

70. "The reasons for the cult emergence are not completely clear. We have hypothesized that the Rapa Nui underwent a territorial restructuring in response to soil nutrient depletion in the interior uplands, and a long dry period experienced by farmers, but we presently have no empirical data to connect this situation to the emergence of the Birdman Cult directly." (p. 99)

**Assessment**: The authors candidly acknowledge the absence of empirical data connecting the proposed ecological pressures to the Birdman Cult's emergence. The causal chain from environmental degradation to political-religious transformation is hypothetical, not demonstrated.

71. "While long-term economic problems may have been an impetus for early ritual activities at 'Orongo, the intensive collective ritual behavior occurs well after European contact and may have been a response to the detrimental effects of pathogens possibly introduced by the Spanish in AD 1770 (Hunt and Lipo 2011)." (p. 99)

**Assessment**: The authors distinguish between early ritual activity (possibly linked to ecological pressures) and the intensive collective ritual that characterizes the formalized Birdman Cult. The latter is attributed to the post-contact period and to the impacts of European disease.

---

## XIII. Conclusion: Revised Timeline

72. "Our analysis of the chronometric data from 'Orongo suggests that the original temporal time line is later than previously determined." (p. 99)

73. "Radiocarbon and obsidian hydration dating of deposits in front of the stone houses indicates that middens found in association with an extensive petroglyph complex and stone houses did not begin until around AD 1600." (p. 99)

74. "The 16th century radiocarbon dates from the work of Ferdon (1961) likely stem from bulk charcoal samples with a large in-built age that made the assessments too early (Mulrooney 2013)." (p. 99)

75. "Our results point to an intensification of site activities in the very late 18th century that may have included the construction of the stone houses." (p. 99)

76. "This sequence of events is in contrast to earlier archaeological reconstructions but is supported by the island oral history." (p. 99)

---

## XIV. Conclusion: 'Orongo's Role as Peripheral Cult

77. "'Orongo's role as the site of a peripheral cult likely began when Complex A was abandoned and ancestor worship moved to 'Orongo village. Birdmen petroglyphs were gradually added to the boulders at Mata Ngarau." (p. 99)

78. "The archaeological record at 'Orongo in Complex B and Complex C does argue for some type of collective activity emerging from longer term internal economic problems. But the construction of houses, as reflected by the inferred construction fill layer within the midden of Trench 1, occurred later in time." (p. 98)

---

## XV. What Was Not Known

79. "Despite the many investigations and restorations of 'Orongo we know remarkably little about the construction history of the complex, the types of rituals, the extent of food consumption, and the nature of daily activities that actually took place." (p. 99)

80. "This analysis shows that 'Orongo was a dynamic place that expressed the Rapanui's changing view of the world but the emphasis on restoration and the lack of explicit and broad research questions has led to an underdeveloped interpretation of the complex." (p. 99)

81. "This analysis has not been able to assign absolute ages to individual stratigraphic deposits, or house construction events, and thus the arguments are open to revision." (p. 99)

**Assessment**: The authors' candid acknowledgment that their arguments remain provisional is important. The chronological evidence, while pointing toward a later timeline, does not definitively establish it.

## XVI. Bearing on the Chiefdom Attribution

### Claims that support the project argument

- Paramountcy at the regional level "has not been discovered archaeologically or reported in the ethnohistoric oral history" (p. 90). This is a direct admission that the chiefdom model lacks both archaeological and ethnohistoric support for supra-local political integration.
- The standard chronology for 'Orongo (c. AD 1540) rests on bulk charcoal dates that are unreliable, and the deposits are not demonstrably linked to house construction.
- Peak activity at 'Orongo occurred in the late 18th century (c. AD 1795), well within the contact period.
- The formalization of the Birdman Cult and construction of stone houses are hypothesized to have occurred around AD 1800, meaning the political-religious transformation the cult represents is a contact-period phenomenon, not a prehistoric one.
- The authors explicitly attribute the crystallization of the new leadership ideology to European contact, not to internal prehistoric dynamics alone.
- Even the ecological causal chain is acknowledged as lacking empirical support: "we presently have no empirical data to connect this situation to the emergence of the Birdman Cult directly" (p. 99).

### Claims that complicate the project argument

- The authors accept without scrutiny that "The prehistoric political system of Rapa Nui is inferred to have been a hierarchical chiefdom" (p. 90), treating this as background rather than questioning it.
- The paper relies heavily on Metraux (1940) for its characterization of the *ariki-mau*, *mana*, *matatoa*, and the ten-tribe system. These post-slave-raid sources are not critically examined for reliability.
- The Lewis (1975) peripheral cult framework assumes a pre-existing "central religion" against which the Birdman Cult is peripheral. If the prior ancestor worship system was itself less centralized than this framework implies, the model may not apply.
- Some early activity at 'Orongo (AD 1600-1650) does predate European contact in 1722, meaning the cult's origins, though not its formalization, may be prehistoric.

### Relationship to the five-step circularity

The paper inadvertently illustrates the circularity problem. It accepts the chiefdom attribution (citing Kirch 2000) as established fact, then uses post-slave-raid ethnography (Metraux 1940) to describe the political system that the Birdman Cult supposedly transformed. The archaeological evidence is then interpreted within this framework. Yet the paper's own chronological findings suggest the transformation occurred after European contact, which means the contact-era observations used to establish the chiefdom model were made during or after the very transformation the model purports to describe.
