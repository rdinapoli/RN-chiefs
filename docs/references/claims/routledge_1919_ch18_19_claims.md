# Routledge 1919 - Claims Extraction: Chapters XVIII (Legends) and XIX (The Present Position of the Problem)

Source: Routledge, Katherine. *The Mystery of Easter Island*. London: Sifton, Praed & Co., 1919.
Chapters: XVIII (pp. 277-289) and XIX (pp. 290-302)
[POST-RAID] These chapters record traditions collected 1914-1915, approximately 50 years after the slave raids of 1862-1863, from a population that had collapsed from ~3,000 to fewer than 200.

---

## CHAPTER XVIII: LEGENDS

### 1. Routledge's Framework for Assessing These Legends

**Claim 1.1: Legends are only "suggestive," not historical records**
> "It need hardly be said that, like all such legends, they cannot be regarded as more than suggestive; when the mysteries have been solved, it will no doubt be easy to see where they have been founded on fact, and where error has crept in, and essential points distorted or forgotten; meanwhile, the clues they afford can only be partial." (p. 277)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Routledge explicitly cautions that these traditions cannot be taken as straightforward historical accounts. This applies equally to any political organization claims derived from them.

**Claim 1.2: Three separate legend groups whose chronological relationship is uncertain**
> "These legends fall into three groups, which, though they touch at some points, are in reality separate, and their relation to one another in point of time cannot be certainly ascertained." (p. 277)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES. If the chronological relationship between the three major tradition cycles cannot be established, reconstructing a coherent political history from them is problematic.

### 2. The Hotu Matua Arrival Tradition

**Claim 2.1: Origin from Marae Renga, driven by dynastic conflict**
> "The ancestors of the present inhabitants came, it is said, from two neighbouring islands known as Marae Renga and Marae Tohio. Here, on the death of the chief, Ko Riri-ka-atea, a struggle for supremacy arose between his two sons, Ko Te Ira-ka-atea and Hotu-matua, in which Hotu was defeated." (p. 277)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS. Presents the origin as involving a chiefly lineage with dynastic succession conflict. However, this is a standard Polynesian migration charter motif and its historicity cannot be assessed.

**Claim 2.2: Haumaka's dream and the six advance scouts**
Haumaka, who had tattooed Hotu, "had a dream, and during it his spirit went to a far country." Six men were sent on pieces of wood to find this land; they found Motu Nui, Motu Iti, Motu Kao-kao, and the crater of Rano Kao. They planted yams at Rano Kao, walked around the island, and one (Kuku) was killed by a turtle at Anakena and died in a cave. (pp. 277-278)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. The six scouts are a standard Polynesian exploration motif. No political authority structure is described among them.

**Claim 2.3: Two boats arrive; Hotu-matua races Hinelilu to Anakena**
> "The name of the one boat was 'Oteka,' and in it were Hotu-matua and his wife, Vakai-a-hiva; and the name of the other boat was 'Oua,' and in it were a certain Hinelilu and his wife, Avarepua." (p. 278)

Hotu "wished to be the first to reach Anakena" and used supernatural power: "he 'said to himself a word,' which made his own boat go fast and Hinelilu's go slow." (pp. 278-279)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS. Hotu-matua is presented as the superior figure who uses mana to ensure his primacy. But the presence of a second boat with a separate leader (Hinelilu) complicates the picture; this is not a single paramount arriving.

**Claim 2.4: Ariki Tuukoihu among the company; Miru clan subdivisions**
> "Amongst those who came in the boats was the ariki Tuukoihu, the maker of the wooden images; two of his sons and two grandsons have given their names to four subdivisions of the Miru clan." (p. 279)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS. Tuukoihu is explicitly called "ariki." The Miru clan is linked to this founding figure. But note: Tuukoihu is presented as an ariki alongside Hotu-matua, suggesting multiple ariki rather than a single paramount.

**Claim 2.5: Hinelilu described as intelligent, brought writing**
> "Hinelilu was a man of intelligence, and wrote rongo-rongo on paper he brought with him." (p. 279)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Hinelilu is presented as a figure of some importance, a literate leader of the second boat, not subordinate. The tradition of writing arriving with the settlement is notable.

**Claim 2.6: The Oroi story (concealed enemy, murder, cooking)**
Oroi was "an enemy of Hotu, who had killed his children in the place whence they came." He stowed away, got ashore at Anakena, and "killed everyone." Eventually Hotu-matua trapped and killed him: "when, however, they put the corpse in the oven to cook him he came to life again, so they had to take him over to the other side of the island to where the ahu is called Oroi... and there he cooked quite satisfactorily, and they ate him." (p. 279)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Shows Hotu-matua as a personal actor who kills his own enemy, not as a chief commanding others to do so. The story has supernatural elements (Oroi comes to life).

**Claim 2.7: Hotu-matua's sons are ancestors of the clans**
> "Hotu-matua had many sons from whom the different clans are descended, and whose names they bear." (p. 279)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS. Standard Polynesian charter linking clan organization to the founding chief's genealogy.

**Claim 2.8: Hotu-matua quarrels with eldest son, abdicates**
> "He quarrelled with the eldest, Tuumaheki, and with his own wife, Vakai; the two having behaved badly to him, he finally gave up his position to Tuumaheki and retired to the top of Rano Kao." (pp. 279-280)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. There is a "position" to give up, implying a leadership role. But succession here is presented as the result of personal conflict and abdication, not orderly hereditary transfer. Hotu-matua does not choose his successor; he is effectively pushed out by his eldest son's bad behavior and responds by retiring.

**Claim 2.9: Hotu-matua's deathbed scene and the blessing of Hotu-iti**
Hotu-matua was "old and blind" at Rano Kao. His elder sons visited, but he kept asking for Hotu-iti, the youngest, his "favourite." He tested each son by feeling the calf of his leg. When Hotu-iti finally arrived:

> "You are Hotu-iti, of Mata-iti, and your descendants shall prosper and survive all others." And he said to Kotuu, "You are Kotuu, of Mata-nui, and your descendants shall multiply like the shells of the sea, and the reeds of the crater, and the pebbles of the beach, but they shall die and shall not remain." (p. 280)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS in form, UNDERMINES in substance. This is a classic Polynesian origin charter with the dying chief blessing his sons and the island division. But the blessing favors the youngest son (Hotu-iti) over the elder sons and over Kotuu, reversing normal primogeniture. The prophecy that Kotuu's descendants "shall die and shall not remain" provides a mythological charter for Hotu Iti's eventual triumph, which reads as a retrojection from the victorious side.

**Claim 2.10: Death signal and burial**
Hotu-matua stood at the crater edge, looked over Motu Nui toward Marae Renga, and called to four aku-aku in his old home: "Kuihi, Kuaha, Tongau, Opakako, make the cock crow for me." The cock crowed in Marae Renga, and he heard it across the sea. "That was his death signal." He was buried at Akahanga. (p. 280)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. The burial at Akahanga, a specific ahu, connects the founding chief to a known archaeological site. The supernatural elements (hearing a cock across the ocean) indicate mythological elaboration.

**Claim 2.11: Gods/ancestors traveled with Hotu-matua**
> "Many of the gods of Marae Renga, who were the ancestors of Hotu-matua, came with him in his boat, and he knew they were there though the others did not see them. The names of eleven of them were given, four of which were independently quoted as amongst the aku-aku associated with Akahanga." (p. 280)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Ancestral deities associated with the founding chief is standard Polynesian religious structure consistent with chiefly authority, but also consistent with any founding lineage.

### 3. Routledge's Assessment of the Arrival Tradition's Consistency

**Claim 3.1: The sons of Hotu-matua "are not known exactly"**
> "The sons of Hotu-matua are not known exactly. Kotuu is sometimes identified with Ko Tuumaheki, and is sometimes a separate person. Miru occasionally figures as one of them, which is inconsistent with the statement that four of Tuukoihu's descendants are the ancestors of four subdivisions of that clan. Miru is also the name given in all the lists to Tuumaheki's son, the third ariki." (pp. 281-282)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES. Fundamental confusion among informants about who the sons of Hotu-matua were, whether Kotuu and Tuumaheki are the same person, and whether Miru was a son of Hotu-matua or a grandson (son of Tuumaheki). The "lists" of ariki are mentioned but the details are contradictory. If the genealogy of the founding dynasty cannot be reliably established, claims about hereditary chiefly authority rest on shaky foundations.

**Claim 3.2: "Hotu Iti was always a district, never the name of a clan"**
(p. 281)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Distinguishes between territorial and kin-group organization.

**Claim 3.3: The "most interesting point" has "the most vagueness"**
> "On the most interesting point—namely, the origin of the Long Ears—there is the most vagueness." (p. 281)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES. Routledge signals that the most ethnologically significant question cannot be answered from the traditions she collected.

### 4. The Long Ears vs. Short Ears

**Claim 4.1: Long Ears and Short Ears lived "mixed up all over the land"**
> "Now the Long Ears ('Hanau Epe') and Short Ears ('Hanau Momoku') lived together mixed up all over the land." (p. 280)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. No territorial segregation between the two groups initially.

**Claim 4.2: Long Ear cannibalism provoked the conflict**
A Long Ear named Ko Ita at Orongo "had in his house the bodies of thirty boys, whom he had killed to eat. Among his victims were the seven sons of one man, Ko Pepi." Ko Pepi went mad, "and his brothers took their mataa and killed the Long Ears at Vinapu and at Orongo." (p. 280)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. The conflict is triggered by personal grievance (cannibalism of children), not by political contest.

**Claim 4.3: Ditch defense and extermination**
Long Ears retreated to the eastern headland, "dug a ditch and filled it with brushwood in order to make a fire in self-defence." Short Ears attacked from both sides, and the Long Ears "were swept into the ditch of their own making." All were burned except two, who were found in a cave near Anakena and killed. (pp. 280-281)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Describes inter-group conflict but not explicitly about political authority.

**Claim 4.4: The ditch is probably a natural geological feature**
> "[The ditch] is still shown; there is a marked depression running across the island dividing the eastern volcano from the mainland, but after much consideration we came to the conclusion that it was a natural phenomenon due to geological faulting. A mound of earth is, however, to be seen in places on its higher or eastern side, and it is possible that persons holding the mountain may have utilised it for defensive purposes by erecting a rampart in this manner." (p. 281, footnote)

- Type: (a) Direct observation / (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Routledge's archaeological observation that the "ditch" may be natural rather than man-made.

**Claim 4.5: Kilimuti's version: Hotu-matua = Short Ears, Hinelilu = Long Ears**
> "According to Kilimuti, who was a recognised authority, and whose account of the landing has been followed, Hotu-matua and those in his boat were the Short Ears, Hinelilu and the crew of the second boat the Long Ears." (p. 281)

When asked how the two came together, Kilimuti "merely replied that it was in the same way as we ourselves had various nationalities on the yacht." According to this authority, "the destruction in the ditch took place in the time of Hotu-matua's children." (p. 281)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition (from named informant Kilimuti)
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. This version links the two groups to the two boats of the founding voyage, making the Long Ears co-settlers rather than an earlier population. No clear hierarchy between the two groups is described; they simply arrived together.

**Claim 4.6: Alternative version: Long Ears arose through mana of third ariki**
> "Another version, given by three old men in conclave, was that the Long Ears came into existence on the island through the 'mana' of the third ariki." (pp. 281-282)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS (weakly). This version attributes the origin of the Long Ears to the supernatural power of the third ariki, implying that ariki had significant spiritual authority. But the claim itself is inconsistent with other versions and was debated among informants.

**Claim 4.7: Te Haha's wife dismisses the informants' knowledge**
> "Discussion one day waxed quite fierce on the point till Te Haha's wife, who was a shrewd middle-aged woman, turned and said, 'Never mind them. Mama, they don't know anything about it,' which probably summed up the situation." (p. 282)

- Type: (a) Direct observation (of an informant session)
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES. Routledge's own assessment, through the words of a local woman, is that the informants do not actually know the origin of the Long Ears. This is a remarkable admission by the ethnographer of the limits of her informants' knowledge.

**Claim 4.8: "Long Ears" meant naturally long ears, not ear distension**
> "The term Long Ears seemed to convey to the natives not the custom of distending the ears, but having them long by nature." (p. 282)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference based on informant statements
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS.

### 5. Comparison with Thomson's Version of the Arrival Tradition

**Claim 5.1: Thomson's easterly origin not confirmed**
> "The statement made by him [Thomson via Salmon], and repeated by various travellers, probably from the same source, that Hotu-matua came from the east, was never met with by us." (p. 282)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference / (a) direct observation (of what informants did and did not say)
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES (the reliability of Thomson's version). Routledge could not confirm a key detail of Thomson's account, and suggests Thomson's version derives from a single source (Salmon).

**Claim 5.2: Direction of origin uncertain**
> "Kilimuti did not know whence he came; the direction in which Hotu-matua looked when dying would be west, or more accurately, south-west." (p. 282)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition / (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES (certainty about the tradition). Even the best informant did not know the homeland's location.

**Claim 5.3: Thomson's version has no Hinelilu, no triumph of Hotu-iti**
> "The same authority [Thomson] states that Hotu was in the boat which went by the south and east and his wife Vakai in the other; Hinelilu does not appear. Hotu is depicted as dividing the land between his sons, but there is no mention of the ultimate triumph of the descendants of Hotu-iti over those of Kotuu, which, as told to us on more than one occasion, was the chief point in the story." (p. 282)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference (comparing versions)
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Thomson's version includes land division among sons (supporting chiefly authority to distribute land) but omits what Routledge considered "the chief point" of the story (Hotu-iti's ultimate victory), and omits Hinelilu entirely. The divergence between the two versions, collected ~25 years apart, shows significant variation in the tradition.

**Claim 5.4: Thomson places Long Ears much later; Routledge's informants differ**
> "The Long Ears [in Thomson's account] suddenly appear on the island at a much later time." Thomson is quoted in footnote: "The tradition continues by a sudden jump into the following extraordinary condition of affairs. Many years after the death of Hotu-matua the island was about equally divided between his descendants and the long-eared race." (p. 282, quoting Smithsonian Report, 1889, p. 528)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference (comparing versions)
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES (reliability of either version). The two accounts cannot both be correct about the origin and timing of the Long Ears.

### 6. The Kotuu vs. Hotu Iti Wars

**Claim 6.1: Kainga as "great man" triggers the wars**
> "Kainga was a great man, and he lived near Tongariki." (p. 283)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Kainga is called "a great man" but not an ariki. His authority appears to be personal and military, not hereditary or institutional.

**Claim 6.2: The war between the two sides of the island**
> "Now there was war between one side of the island and the other side. The Koro-orongo, the Tupahotu, the Ureohei and Ngaure fought the Haumoana, Miru, Marama, Hamea, and the Raa." (p. 284)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. The war is described as between named clans organized by territorial halves (consistent with a moiety system), not as a rebellion against chiefly authority or a competition between chiefs. Leadership in the war is personal (Kainga) rather than institutional.

**Claim 6.3: Kainga commands a large boat-building project**
> "Kainga then said, 'Bring me much wood to make a boat'; and all men worked at the boat of Kainga, and he gave them much food—chickens and potatoes and bananas, sugar-cane, hens and fish and eels—but they did not make it well. Then Kainga sent for Tuukoihu, the chief who lived at Ahu Tepeu, on the western side." (pp. 283-284)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS/SUPPORTS. Kainga commands labor and redistributes food in return, which could be read as chiefly redistribution. But note: (1) Tuukoihu is called "the chief" (an ariki) who lives on the western side, suggesting multiple authorities; (2) this same Tuukoihu supposedly arrived with Hotu-matua, which is chronologically inconsistent (see Claim 6.7); (3) the initial workers "did not make it well," suggesting Kainga's command did not extend to ensuring quality, requiring a specialist.

**Claim 6.4: Scale of violence in the tradition**
After Hotu Iti's initial defeat: "they killed all the people of Hotu Iti, thousands and thousands and thousands, women and children and little children, big children and young men, and old men who could not walk away quickly." (p. 284)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Epic scale of violence is a common feature of oral traditions. The "thousands and thousands" is likely formulaic rather than demographic.

**Claim 6.5: Poie as "big man" (not ariki)**
> "Poie, who was one of the Haumoana and a big man, came to live at Ana Havea, the cave near Tongariki." (p. 285)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Poie is called "a big man" (tangata nui?), not an ariki. His authority is exercised through military dominance and personal power, not institutional rank.

**Claim 6.6: Cannibalism in the war traditions**
Poie goes to Marotiri and says to Vaha, "Give me men to cook." Vaha "gave him one thousand in the boat." Poie "gave each of the men of Anakena a man to eat; he took thousands of children by the leg and dashed them against the stone. Every day he did the same again, and brought a thousand men from Marotiri." (p. 285)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. The extreme violence and cannibalism may reflect actual practices or formulaic exaggeration. The numbers are clearly formulaic.

**Claim 6.7: Chronological inconsistency identified by Juan**
> "Our informant, Kapiera, was quite positive that the events took place during the time of Ngaara's grandfather, and refused to be dislodged from his position because Juan pertinently pointed out that this was inconsistent with the boat being made by Tuukoihu, who landed with Hotu-matua." (p. 289)

- Type: (a) Direct observation (of informant interview)
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES. The internal chronology of the traditions is inconsistent. Events attributed to "Ngaara's grandfather" (perhaps 18th century) involve a character (Tuukoihu) from the founding voyage. This suggests either telescoping of the tradition, confusion of characters, or mythological rather than historical time.

**Claim 6.8: Hotu Iti ultimately victorious, fulfilling Hotu-matua's prophecy**
> "Now, when Poie was dead, Kotuu was finished and Hotu Iti victorious according to the words of Hotu-matua." (p. 288)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS (weakly). The narrative is structured as a fulfillment of Hotu-matua's deathbed prophecy. This is a common charter myth structure: the present political order is legitimized by reference to the founding ancestor's wishes. But this structure itself reveals the tradition as a political charter rather than a straightforward historical account.

**Claim 6.9: The sixty victorious men divide captured women**
> "Men and old men, old women and children they killed all, but the fine women they took; the sixty men divided the women between them. A man would say to a woman, 'Do you like me?' and if she said 'No,' then he killed her." (p. 287)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. The division of women is done among sixty men collectively, not allocated by a chief. Kainga directs the groups to settle separately afterward but does not distribute women hierarchically.

**Claim 6.10: Kainga directs settlement after victory**
> "Kainga told the men from Te Ava-nui to go to one place, and the men from Marotiri to go to another, and live with their wives and beget children, and so they did." (pp. 287-288)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS (weakly). Kainga directs settlement patterns, consistent with a leader distributing land. But Kainga is never called ariki; his authority is military and personal.

### 7. King List / Ariki Succession (What Routledge Reports)

**Claim 7.1: No formal king list provided in these chapters**
Routledge does NOT provide a numbered king list comparable to Thomson's 57 names or Roussel's 22. She mentions ariki in passing: Hotu-matua as the first, Tuumaheki (the eldest son) as the second (to whom Hotu-matua "gave up his position"), and Miru as "the third ariki" (as "the name given in all the lists to Tuumaheki's son"). She refers to "all the lists" (p. 281), implying she collected multiple genealogical lists, but does not reproduce them in these chapters.

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. The existence of "lists" of ariki implies informants could produce genealogical sequences, but Routledge does not present them, and the inconsistencies she describes (whether Kotuu = Tuumaheki, whether Miru was a son or grandson of Hotu-matua) suggest the lists were not reliable. The mention of "all the lists" agreeing on Miru as the third ariki suggests some consistency on this point.

**Claim 7.2: "Ngaara's grandfather" as a chronological reference**
One informant (Kapiera) placed the Kotuu-Hotu Iti wars "during the time of Ngaara's grandfather." Another old man "definitely asserted that they took place in the time of the grandfather of the last ariki" (p. 300 in Ch. XIX). Ngaara is thus implied to be the last ariki.

- Type: (b) Reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS (weakly). The use of the ariki genealogy as a chronological framework implies that the ariki line was a recognized institution. But the chronological confusion (Tuukoihu from the founding era appearing in events two generations before the last ariki) undermines confidence in the genealogy's accuracy.

## CHAPTER XIX: THE PRESENT POSITION OF THE PROBLEM

### 8. Routledge's Interpretive Framework for Political Organization

**Claim 8.1: Working hypothesis of two migration waves**
> "It is impossible as yet to give with any certainty a connected account of the early history of Easter Island, but as a working hypothesis the following may perhaps be assumed. There was an original negroid element which brought with it the custom of distending the ear, the wooden figures, and also the bird-cult. A whiter wave succeeded which mingled with the first inhabitants, and the next generation adopted the fashion of the country in stretching the lobe of the ear, and carried on the bird-cult." (pp. 298-299)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. This is Routledge's racial-migration framework, typical of early 20th century anthropology. She reads the Long Ear/Short Ear tradition as reflecting ethnic replacement. The framework is no longer accepted, but it shows how she interpreted the traditions.

**Claim 8.2: Miru and allied clans as Polynesian settlers; ariki as "quite white"**
> "It seems reasonable to look to the Miru, who are settled in that part of the island, and perhaps also to the allied clans of the Marama and Haumoana, who together form the chief inhabitants of the district of Kotuu, as the more direct descendants of the Polynesian settlers. In confirmation of this we find that the ariki, or chief, the only man who was necessarily of pure descent, is said to have been 'quite white.' The inscribed skulls, which are those of the Miru, are reported to be of the Polynesian type." (pp. 298-299)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS. This is a key passage. Routledge reports that the ariki was "the only man who was necessarily of pure descent," implying a hereditary requirement for racial/genealogical purity. The ariki is described as "quite white" (lighter-skinned), and this is connected to Polynesian ancestry via the Miru clan. However, this information is itself derived from post-raid informants describing a pre-raid institution.

**Claim 8.3: Ariki did not participate in the bird-cult**
> "It is a somewhat striking fact also that the ariki, in spite of his prominent position in the island, took no part in the bird-cult ceremonies." (p. 299)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference / (b) reported tradition
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES standard chiefdom model. The ariki held "a prominent position" but was excluded from (or did not participate in) the bird-man cult, which was the island's most important annual ritual competition. This separation between the hereditary ariki and the annual ritual-political competition suggests a more complex political structure than a simple paramount chiefdom. The bird-man cult produced its own form of authority independent of the ariki line.

**Claim 8.4: The Miru ariki's connection to the tablets**
> "It would be satisfactory, in view of the relation of the Miru ariki to the tablets, and the tradition that they came with Hotu-matua, if internal evidence could show that it was of Polynesian origin. Unfortunately for this theory, the Melanesian bird figures largely among the signs." (p. 302)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. The ariki had a special relationship to the rongorongo tablets, which were a marker of elite knowledge. But Routledge notes the inconvenient fact that the script contains Melanesian motifs, complicating the Polynesian chiefly interpretation.

### 9. Routledge's Assessment of Informant Reliability

**Claim 9.1: Legends "cannot be regarded as more than suggestive"**
(See Claim 1.1 above, p. 277)

**Claim 9.2: "The most definite and agreed points are the most incomprehensible"**
> "Such is the outline of these stories; the most definite and agreed points are the most incomprehensible—namely, the landing of the six men prior to that of the main wave, and the concealed arrival of Oroi." (p. 281)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES. Routledge notes that the elements on which informants agreed most firmly are the ones she finds most difficult to interpret. The elements most relevant to political organization are among the most contested.

**Claim 9.3: Te Haha's wife: "they don't know anything about it"**
(See Claim 4.7 above, p. 282)

**Claim 9.4: Thomson's versions "from Salmon" may be a single-source artifact**
> "The statement made by him, and repeated by various travellers, probably from the same source, that Hotu-matua came from the east, was never met with by us." (p. 282)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES (reliability of Thomson). Routledge suggests Thomson's version traces to a single informant/interpreter (Salmon), and that claims "repeated by various travellers" are not independent but derivative.

**Claim 9.5: Kilimuti as "a recognised authority"**
Routledge identifies Kilimuti as her primary informant for the arrival tradition: "Kilimuti, who was a recognised authority, and whose account of the landing has been followed." (p. 281)

- Type: (a) Direct observation (about informant status)
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. Shows Routledge was aware of differential informant authority and chose to follow a particular source. But "recognised authority" among a post-raid population does not guarantee accurate transmission of pre-raid traditions.

**Claim 9.6: Juan's knowledge "a step further from the original"**
> "Juan put the home of the first immigrants in the Paumotu; as a young man his knowledge of legend was a step further from the original, but it was often useful as summing up the general impression he had received." (p. 282)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES. Routledge explicitly acknowledges generational knowledge loss in her younger informants.

### 10. Comparative Polynesian Context

**Claim 10.1: Easter Islanders are "undoubtedly connected" to Pacific peoples**
> "They are undoubtedly connected with those found elsewhere in the Pacific; much of their culture is similar; and even the earliest voyagers noted that their language resembled that found on the other islands." (p. 291)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS (weakly). Polynesian cultural connections are affirmed, which provides the baseline for the Polynesian analogy.

**Claim 10.2: Skulls are more Melanesian than Polynesian**
Dr. Keith's report: "The Polynesian type is fairly purely represented in some of the Easter Islanders... but they are absolutely and relatively a remarkably long-headed people, and in this feature they approach the Melanesian more than the Polynesian type." (p. 295)

Capt. T. A. Joyce: "I could not get away from the conclusion that in their measurements and general appearance these skulls were far more Melanesian than Polynesian." (p. 296)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference / scientific evidence
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES simple Polynesian chiefdom model. If the population was significantly Melanesian in physical type, the applicability of the Polynesian chiefdom model based on Hawaiian/Tongan comparisons is weakened. (Note: early 20th century racial categories are no longer used in this way, but the observation of phenotypic diversity is relevant.)

**Claim 10.3: Cultural parallels with Melanesia, not just Polynesia**
Routledge catalogs multiple Melanesian cultural parallels: ear distension, double-outrigger canoe, obsidian blade type, wooden figures with prominent ribs, and especially the bird-cult (paralleled in Solomon Islands with the Frigate bird). (pp. 296-298)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES. If significant cultural elements are Melanesian rather than Polynesian, the Polynesian chiefdom model cannot be applied straightforwardly.

**Claim 10.4: Mangarevan tradition of finding inhabitants on Easter Island**
> "According to the account of Admiral T. de Lapelin, there is a tradition at Mangareva in the Gambier Islands to the effect that the adherents of a certain chief, being vanquished, sought safety in flight; they departed with a west wind in two big canoes, taking with them women, children, and all sorts of provisions." One man returned to Mangareva and reported that "the fugitives had found an island in the middle of the seas, and disembarked in a little bay surrounded by mountains; where, finding traces of inhabitants, they had made fortifications of stone on one of the heights. A few days later they were attacked by a horde of natives armed with spears, but succeeded in defeating them. The victors then pitilessly massacred their opponents throughout the island, sparing only the women and children." (pp. 294-295)

- Type: (b) Reported tradition (external to Easter Island)
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. An independent tradition from Mangareva suggesting Easter Island was already inhabited when Polynesian settlers arrived. This would complicate the Hotu-matua founding charter. Routledge notes the parallel with the Long Ear/Short Ear tradition.

### 11. Routledge's Conclusions about Social Organization

**Claim 11.1: Hotu-matua story represents "the Polynesian immigration"**
> "If these suppositions are so far correct, the story of the landing of Hotu-matua and the establishment of his headquarters at Anakena refer to the Polynesian immigration." (pp. 298-299)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS. Routledge interprets the Hotu-matua tradition as a historical account of Polynesian settlement, with the Miru/Marama/Haumoana clans as the more direct Polynesian descendants. This is the foundation for treating the ariki institution as Polynesian-derived.

**Claim 11.2: Wars between Kotuu and Hotu Iti as "comparatively recent"**
> "The legendary struggles between Kotuu and Hotu Iti, in which Kainga played so prominent a part, are always spoken of as comparatively recent history, and one old man definitely asserted that they took place in the time of the grandfather of the last ariki, which may be as far back as the eighteenth century." (p. 300)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: AMBIGUOUS. If the wars occurred in the 18th century, the overthrow of statues and the devastation of the island may have occurred between Roggeveen (1722) and the Spanish visit (1770).

**Claim 11.3: Statues probably represent ancestors; hats may be "badges of rank"**
> "It is, however, probably safe to regard ahu statues as being in general representations of ancestors, either nearer or more distant, this does not necessarily exclude the idea of divinity. The hat may have been a badge of rank; warriors in Tahiti wore a certain type of hat as a special mark of distinction." (p. 301)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: SUPPORTS (weakly). Ahu statues as ancestor representations, with hats as rank markers, is consistent with chiefly ancestor veneration. But Routledge hedges: "probably safe," "may have been." The Tahitian comparison is offered tentatively.

**Claim 11.4: Statues had diverse functions**
Routledge suggests some statues were gods, some ancestors, some bird-man memorials, some boundary markers, and some "simply ornamental, 'to make it look nice.'" She compares the diverse uses to the Latin cross. (p. 301)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES (monolithic chiefly-command interpretation). If statues served multiple functions including boundary markers and decoration, they are not exclusively monuments to chiefly power.

**Claim 11.5: "Impossible as yet to give with any certainty a connected account"**
> "It is impossible as yet to give with any certainty a connected account of the early history of Easter Island." (p. 298)

- Type: (c) Routledge's inference
- Chiefdom relevance: UNDERMINES. Routledge's own summary judgment is that a connected historical account cannot be constructed from the available evidence. This is from the most thorough early ethnographic investigation of the island, conducted while some older informants were still alive.

## SUMMARY: Key Passages for the Chiefdom Paper

### Most relevant for arguing against the chiefdom attribution:

1. **Routledge's explicit caution about the legends** (Claim 1.1, p. 277): "they cannot be regarded as more than suggestive"
2. **Te Haha's wife** (Claim 4.7, p. 282): "they don't know anything about it"
3. **Contradictory genealogies** (Claim 3.1, pp. 281-282): Sons of Hotu-matua "not known exactly," Kotuu/Tuumaheki confusion, Miru as son vs. grandson
4. **Chronological impossibility** (Claim 6.7, p. 289): Kapiera places events near Ngaara's grandfather but involves Tuukoihu from the founding voyage
5. **Ariki excluded from bird-cult** (Claim 8.3, p. 299): separation of hereditary and ritual-competitive authority
6. **"Impossible" to construct connected history** (Claim 11.5, p. 298)
7. **Thomson/Routledge discrepancies** (Claims 5.1-5.4): major differences between versions collected 25 years apart
8. **Melanesian physical and cultural elements** (Claims 10.2-10.3): complicating the Polynesian chiefdom analogy

### Most relevant for the chiefdom attribution:

1. **Ariki as hereditary office requiring "pure descent"** (Claim 8.2, p. 299): "the only man who was necessarily of pure descent"
2. **Land division by Hotu-matua** (Claim 5.3, per Thomson's version, p. 282)
3. **Clan origins from sons of Hotu-matua** (Claim 2.7, p. 279)
4. **Ariki Tuukoihu named as such** (Claim 2.4, p. 279)
5. **Hotu-matua has a "position" to abdicate** (Claim 2.8, pp. 279-280)
6. **Ariki lists exist** (Claim 7.1, p. 281): "all the lists" agree on Miru as third ariki
7. **Kainga commands boat-building and redistributes food** (Claim 6.3, pp. 283-284)

### Key methodological observation:

Routledge herself, the most thorough early ethnographer of Easter Island, repeatedly signals uncertainty about her informants' knowledge and the reliability of the traditions she collected. She calls them "suggestive," notes that the most important questions produce the most vague or contradictory answers, and concludes that a connected historical account cannot yet be constructed. This caution from the primary collector of these traditions is directly relevant to the paper's argument about the epistemological foundations of the chiefdom attribution.

The traditions do contain elements consistent with Polynesian chieftainship (hereditary ariki, clan origins from founding chief's sons, genealogical purity requirements). But these elements are embedded in narratives that are internally contradictory, that differ significantly between Thomson's and Routledge's versions, and that Routledge herself assessed as unreliable on precisely the points most relevant to political organization.
