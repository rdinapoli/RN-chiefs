# Claims Extraction: Métraux 1940 *Ethnology of Easter Island*

## Source Information

- **Author**: Alfred Métraux
- **Title**: *Ethnology of Easter Island*
- **Publication**: Bishop Museum Bulletin 160, Honolulu, 1940
- **Fieldwork**: 1934 Franco-Belgian expedition
- **Source type**: Comprehensive ethnographic monograph (463 pages)
- **Post-raid status**: [POST-RAID] 72 years post-raids; population ~450-500; fieldwork conducted under Franco-Belgian expedition with key informants including Tepano (shared with Routledge) and Victoria Rapahango
- **Relationship to other Métraux publications**: Expands substantially on the 1937 JPS article "The Kings of Easter Island" and the 1937 "Easter Island Sanctuaries." Same fieldwork, fuller treatment of clan structure, marriage, classes, warfare, religion, bird cult, and stone image significance.

## A. King Lists and Genealogies (Book pp. 88-93, PDF pp. 105-110)

### Claim A1: King list comparison table (Table 2)
**Page**: pp. 88-89 (PDF pp. 105-107)
**Summary**: Table 2 presents four king lists side-by-side: Roussel (23 names), Jaussen (32 names), Thomson (57 names), and Métraux (30 names). Only **7 names are common to all four lists**. 12 of 23 Roussel names appear in Thomson's list; Thomson includes 22 of Jaussen's 32 names; Roussel and Jaussen share only 9 names.
**Quote**: "the sequence of names in Roussel's list presents 'striking parallels with the first and last quarter of Thomson's list, although the intermediate sections do not correspond at all'"
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's comparative analysis of four independently recorded lists.
**Relevance**: **Resolves [CITE-CHECK: Métraux 1940:91].** The king list comparison demonstrates that these lists cannot be treated as reliable historical records. The names common to multiple lists "are primarily in the kings' names which also designate tribes or districts, like Marama, Raa, Hotu-iti, Tuu" (p. 88), suggesting tribal/geographic names were incorporated as "kings" rather than recording a dynastic sequence.

### Claim A2: Tuhunga series as inserted priests/experts
**Page**: p. 88 (PDF p. 105)
**Quote**: "If we accept Jaussen's spelling, we may consider these kings as a group of experts or priests (tuhunga) who have been inserted in the list of kings by mistake or for some obscure reason. It is noteworthy that identical names are given in the same order in two lists of quite different origin."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's textual analysis.
**Relevance**: Demonstrates that the king lists conflate political, religious, and occupational categories, mixing priests, tribal eponyms, and possibly mythological figures into a single "dynastic" sequence.

### Claim A3: Mythological figures interpolated into king lists
**Page**: pp. 88-89 (PDF pp. 105-106)
**Summary**: Tiki-te-hatu and Te Riri-katea are identified as god-names, not historical kings. Only three names have independent corroboration: Hotu-matua (founding ancestor), Nga-ara (Routledge's informant testimony), and Maurata/Kaimakoi (historical persons known from the slave raids).
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's critical analysis.

### Claim A4: Miru as consistently the third ariki
**Page**: p. 89 (PDF p. 106)
**Quote**: "Miru is the name of a king and of a lineage of which he was probably the ancestor. The Miru were the most important group on the island since they had the largest territory. They reserved to themselves the title of ariki because the king was always a Miru... There is thus a certain consistency in the different lists which tends to prove that Miru was probably the third ariki."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition, confirmed across multiple lists. [POST-RAID]

### Claim A5: Last genuine king and end of kingship
**Page**: p. 93 (PDF p. 110)
**Summary**: Maurata was the last ariki-mau, kidnapped in 1862. After him "kingship came to an end." Uncertainty exists about whether Kaimakoi or Maurata was actually taken by the slavers.
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition with historical corroboration. [POST-RAID]

## B. Social Organization and Clan Structure (Book pp. 97-142, PDF pp. 115-160)

### Claim B1: Loss of social organization knowledge
**Page**: p. 120 (PDF p. 137)
**Quote**: "Very little information on social organization can be found in the missionaries' accounts of Easter Island. This is not due to lack of interest but rather to the series of catastrophes which befell Easter Island immediately preceding the advent of the missionaries and which removed from the tribes their ancient social importance."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's methodological assessment.
**Relevance**: Métraux himself acknowledges the impoverished state of social organization data available to ethnographers.

### Claim B2: Missionary concentration destroyed territorial connections
**Page**: p. 120 (PDF p. 137)
**Quote**: "The missionaries concentrated the population in a single village and thus broke the last connections of the individuals with their ancestral territory. The memory of the old order survived with remarkable tenacity and as late as 1914 Routledge was able to obtain from aged natives references to lineage groups and their respective distribution."
**Evidential type**: (c) Historical assessment.
**Relevance**: By 1914, social organization was a matter of memory, not functioning practice.

### Claim B3: Named clans and territorial organization
**Pages**: pp. 121-125 (PDF pp. 138-142)
**Summary**: Métraux lists the clans as territorial portions from Hotu-matua's division: Tuu-ma-heke (eldest son), Miru (second son), Marama (third son), Raa (fourth son), Koro-orongo (fifth son), Hotu-iti (sixth son). Additional clans mentioned: Tupa-hotu, Ureohei (Hitiuira), Hamea, Ngaure, Ngatimo, Haumoana.
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]

### Claim B4: Some clans lacked distinct territory
**Page**: pp. 121-125 (PDF pp. 138-142)
**Summary**: Hamea and Raa "had no distinct territory but lived within Miru lands." Tupa-hotu, Ureohei, and Koro-orongo "had no districts of their own but were more or less scattered in the eastern part of the island." The Miru were "the most widely distributed of the mata."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]
**Relevance**: The territorial picture is considerably more diffuse than a chiefdom model of well-defined administrative districts would predict.

### Claim B5: Tumu (patron-client/marriage exchange) relationships
**Page**: p. 123 (PDF p. 140)
**Quote**: "Tumu is a group from which you may get a wife. The tumu is a friend, but always of another group; he plants for you, he works for you. Every group has its tumu... A tumu may be defined, as Routledge surmised, as 'a group of people into which a man may marry.'"
**Summary**: Tumu were not strictly clans but exchange/marriage groups. "Tumu were people of one tribe who stood in a special relation to people of another." The Hamea and Raa were tumu of the Miru; the Miru were tumu of the Tupa-hotu, Ureohei, and Marama.
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition, with Métraux's analytical interpretation. [POST-RAID]

### Claim B6: Marriage restrictions minimal
**Pages**: pp. 123-125 (PDF pp. 140-142)
**Summary**: "There were no marriage restrictions among tribes, save those of the tumu." The only restriction was marriage before the fourth generation. One persistent tradition: it was considered improper for a man of another group to marry a Miru woman, with supposed supernatural punishment.
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]
**Relevance**: Minimal marriage restrictions are inconsistent with a ranked hierarchical society maintaining rank through marriage rules. The Miru endogamy tradition suggests one clan had a notion of exclusivity, but this was not a general feature of the social system.

## C. Classes of Society (Book pp. 135-140, PDF pp. 152-157)

### Claim C1: Four classes described by Roussel
**Page**: p. 135 (PDF p. 152)
**Quote**: "There were four grades or classes in the Easter Island society: ariki (noblemen), ivi-atua (priests), matatoa (warriors), and kio (servants or farmers). This schematic outline made by Roussel (190, p. 660) is only superficially exact. It does not indicate the actual position of the aristocracy and of the commoners and does not include the specialized groups like those of the experts (maori)."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's critical assessment of Roussel's classification.
**Relevance**: Métraux accepts Roussel's framework as "only superficially exact," noting it omits important categories and misrepresents the actual social structure.

### Claim C2: Ariki-mau as divine chief with mana, not political power
**Page**: p. 135 (PDF p. 152)
**Quote**: "The ariki-mau or king of Easter Island was a divine chief who had mana (supernatural power) and was therefore surrounded by tapus; as a member of the Honga lineage of the Miru tribe he was descended from the gods Tangaroa and Rongo."
**Quote**: "The ruler of the hierarchy was the principal high chief (ariki-mau) whom the missionaries and the present natives designate as 'king of the island.' As descendant of Hotu-matua by right of primogeniture, the king had great prestige which was increased by the sacredness of his person although his political influence was slight."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's synthesis.

### Claim C3: "Certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (KEY PASSAGE)
**Page**: p. 136 (PDF p. 153)
**Quote**: "The ariki-mau was respected because of his mana, his influence over nature, and the magic connected with his person, but **he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief**. As a direct descendant of the first king who settled on the island and thus of the gods, the ariki-mau had a status superior to that of other chiefs or heads of tribes, but he probably had little authority over them. Though the king did not interfere in the intertribal wars, he may occasionally have acted as mediator in quarrels."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's assessment based on all available evidence.
**Relevance**: This is the 1940 monograph version of the key passage from the 1937 article, confirming that the fuller treatment did not change Métraux's core conclusion.

### Claim C4: Historical demonstration of ariki-mau's powerlessness
**Page**: p. 136 (PDF p. 153)
**Summary**: Just before 1862, "The Ngaure tribe defeated the Miru whom they took as slaves (mata-kio). The king, his son Kamakoi, and his grandson Maurata were held in captivity in the territory of the Ngaure and were not rescued until a few years later by the Miru who had in the meantime united with the Tupa-hotu."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition with historical dating. [POST-RAID]
**Relevance**: Same incident reported by Routledge. The ariki-mau and his entire family could be captured and enslaved by another clan. This is incompatible with a functioning chiefdom where the paramount commands institutional authority.

### Claim C5: Definition of kingship (Métraux's synthesis)
**Page**: p. 136 (PDF p. 153)
**Quote**: "In view of all available evidence, kingship on Easter Island may be defined as follows. The king was the man with the most aristocratic pedigree and the most exalted social position on the island. His person was overflowing with mana and his sacredness caused him to be feared and respected. His function in society was to insure through his very being the abundance of crops and the fertility of the ground and to exercise his influence on animal life. Certain religious activities were derived from his sacredness and he held supervisory control over various practices connected with religion."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's definitive assessment.
**Relevance**: Métraux's summary of kingship describes a sacred figure with supernatural functions, not a political ruler. No mention of governance, administration, tribute collection, labor mobilization, or judicial authority.

### Claim C6: Roussel on the transfer of power to matatoa
**Page**: p. 135 (PDF p. 152)
**Quote**: Roussel quoted: "In late times, although the kings were still surrounded with respect by the natives who considered their persons to be sacred, their authority was nil and entirely disregarded. It has passed entirely into the hands of the mata-toa [warriors] who decided everything and carried on the war without beforehand consulting his majesty."
**Evidential type**: (b) Roussel's observation/tradition, quoted by Métraux.

### Claim C7: Matatoa as professional warriors and actual rulers
**Pages**: pp. 138-139 (PDF pp. 155-156)
**Quote**: "Thomson... is mistaken when he assumes that 'there was no class of professional fighters or soldiers; every able-bodied man was supposed to be a warrior and compelled to do duty in time of war.' It is certainly true that most of the men of a tribe participated in a fight but the matatoa were nearly, if not exactly, professional warriors... Their prestige and the fear they inspired placed them in a special category above the average members of their tribes. They probably did no work."
**Quote**: "Legends and missionary accounts fully agree that the matatoa were not only the leaders in war but were also the actual rulers of the tribes... On Easter Island there is no specific record that a matatoa ever ruled over the whole island, but certain legends seem to indicate that some matatoa exercised, at least temporarily, supremacy over all the tribes."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's synthesis from multiple sources.
**Relevance**: NEW beyond the 1937 article. Métraux explicitly identifies matatoa as "the actual rulers of the tribes," confirming that effective political power rested with warriors, not the sacred ariki-mau. However, no matatoa achieved island-wide authority.

### Claim C8: Kio as war captives, not a hereditary class
**Pages**: pp. 139-140 (PDF pp. 156-157)
**Quote**: "The kio were defeated people who were obliged to serve their conquerors or to pay tribute to them with the produce of their lands... When a war was over, the defeated people were kept in caves which they could leave only during working hours. All became slaves and had to plant a certain portion of land for their respective masters."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]
**Relevance**: NEW detail beyond 1937 article. The kio status was created by warfare (defeated peoples), not by hereditary class assignment. This describes a consequence of inter-clan warfare, not a stable hierarchical class system.

## D. Religion and Priests (Book pp. 308-330, PDF pp. 326-350)

### Claim D1: Pre-missionary collapse of religion
**Page**: p. 309 (PDF p. 326)
**Quote**: "Easter Island religion was more deeply affected by the several catastrophes which befell the people than any other phase of their culture. Roussel (190, p. 424) says that the downfall of the religion took place long before the arrival of the missionaries."
**Summary**: Zumbohm "in 1866, found no ceremony or religious cult in honor of any deity." Eyraud "who lived many years among the islanders, declares that he never saw any outward expression of religion."
**Evidential type**: (a) Multiple observers' direct testimony about absence of religion.

### Claim D2: Disagreement on whether priests existed
**Page**: p. 309 (PDF p. 326)
**Summary**: Geiseler and Routledge "declare there were no priests." Thomson claimed "native priests were simply wizards and sorcerers." Roussel, whom Métraux considers "our best authority on Easter Island," explicitly confirms priests existed with rank next to ariki.
**Evidential type**: (b/c) Cross-source comparison.
**Relevance**: The fundamental disagreement among sources about whether an entire social class (ivi-atua) existed underscores the unreliability of post-raid ethnographic data for reconstructing social structure.

### Claim D3: Priestly functions
**Pages**: pp. 309, 341-342 (PDF pp. 326, 358-359)
**Summary**: Priests presided at birth celebrations, drove away evil spirits from the sick ("steaming the sick man"), composed and recited chants for the dead, served as ceremony directors at paina feasts, and "were inspired by the deity who entered their bodies and spoke through their mouths." Priests received "food and carved objects of wood as reward for his services."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]

### Claim D4: Makemake as principal god
**Pages**: pp. 311-312 (PDF pp. 328-329)
**Quote**: "The greatest god of the Easter Islanders was Makemake. He is the only one whose memory has been preserved until the present day."
**Summary**: The great Polynesian gods (Tangaroa, Tu, Hiro, Rongo, Tane) had only "a purely academic existence" on Easter Island, recorded "only as legendary figures or as divine ancestors heading genealogies." Makemake was the active, worshiped deity.
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition with some corroboration from contact-era accounts. [POST-RAID]
**Relevance**: Easter Island's religious system diverged significantly from the pan-Polynesian model, with the major gods of the Polynesian chiefdom complex reduced to genealogical entries while a distinctive local deity dominated actual worship.

### Claim D5: Priestly ventriloquism and manipulation
**Pages**: pp. 311-312 (PDF pp. 328-329)
**Quote**: Zumbohm quoted: "However Makemake gave orders to the men through priests, male and female. These imposters, very able ventriloquists, pretended that the great god spoke through their mouths and asked sacrifices of chickens, sweet potatoes, fish, etc. Of course everything was to the advantage of the representatives of the so-called divinity."
**Evidential type**: (b) Zumbohm's account (1866). [POST-RAID]

## E. Bird Cult (Book pp. 331-341, PDF pp. 350-364)

### Claim E1: Only dominant clans participated
**Page**: p. 333 (PDF p. 350)
**Quote**: "The feast began in the austral spring (probably September or October), when manu-tara were nesting on Motu-nui. Only dominant, victorious tribes took part in the quest for the egg and the attendant ceremonies, for the privileges at stake were too important to be vested in an inferior or defeated group (mata-kio)."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]

### Claim E2: Tangata manu elected for one year alongside hereditary king
**Page**: p. 337 (PDF p. 354)
**Quote**: "Each with an egg on his hand and the new majesty with a bird hanging on his shoulders walked about in the surrounding places singing and dancing." The tangata manu was "elected for a year, without any lack of regard for the king who reigned by right of primo geniture."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]
**Relevance**: Confirms the parallel but distinct nature of the two authority systems. The tangata manu coexisted with but did not replace the ariki-mau.

### Claim E3: Tangata manu privileges
**Page**: p. 337 (PDF pp. 354-355)
**Summary**: The tangata manu received food contributions at feasts; had authority "for daily and nightly marauding and the fooling of the people" and for "spending the year in continuous childish and wanton feasts"; refusal to contribute food "could be punished by the burning of one's house"; adopted a new sacred name which also became the name of the year; had contagious sacredness (tapu) extending to his hopu (servants), wife, and children.
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]
**Relevance**: The tangata manu's privileges were aggressive/predatory (marauding, house-burning threats) and temporary (one year), not administrative or governmental.

### Claim E4: Tangata manu designated human sacrifices
**Page**: p. 337 (PDF p. 354)
**Quote**: "The new king designated two or three persons who must be sacrificed for the prosperity of his reign. It was the choice of the victims which caused a war among the tribes every year."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition. [POST-RAID]
**Relevance**: NEW detail. If accurate, this represents a form of coercive power, but one that provoked warfare rather than maintaining order. The tangata manu system generated inter-clan conflict, not political stability.

### Claim E5: Tangata manu not a secular king
**Page**: pp. 340-341 (PDF pp. 357-358)
**Summary**: Métraux addresses Williamson's suggestion that the bird-man might have been "for the secular kingship," finding this unlikely with no parallel in Polynesia. The bird-man was "an incarnation of the god" Makemake rather than a political ruler.
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's analytical assessment.

### Claim E6: Ao system and dual division
**Page**: pp. 339-340 (PDF pp. 356-357)
**Summary**: The competition was organized along the dual division of Hotu-iti (east) vs. Ko Tuu/mata-nui (west). Motu-nui island was "divided into two sections...the western half being apportioned to the experts from Hotu-iti, the eastern to those from Ko Tuu." The ao refers to competing groups from the two regional divisions.
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition via Routledge, cited by Métraux. [POST-RAID]
**Relevance**: Confirms Routledge's account of rotating competitive dominance organized along dual-division lines, not centralized chiefly command.

## F. Stone Images: Significance (Book pp. 306-307, PDF pp. 323-325)

### Claim F1: True significance unknown; no evidence of worship
**Page**: p. 306 (PDF p. 323)
**Quote**: "**The true significance of stone statues is not known. There is no direct evidence that they were ever worshipped.**"
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's assessment based on all available evidence.
**Relevance**: Critical for the monument-production argument. The ethnographer who produced the most comprehensive study of Easter Island material culture concludes there is no direct evidence the statues were objects of worship.

### Claim F2: Roggeveen's observation "too vague" for worship conclusion
**Page**: p. 306 (PDF p. 323)
**Quote**: "Roggeveen's statement (186, p. 15), so frequently quoted as proof of an ancient cult of the images, is certainly too vague to allow any such conclusion."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's critical assessment of a key contact-era passage.

### Claim F3: Multiple competing explanations considered
**Pages**: pp. 306-307 (PDF pp. 323-324)
**Summary**: Métraux considers: (a) monuments to distinguished persons (Thomson); (b) deified ancestors (possible Marquesan parallel); (c) landmarks indicating boundaries; (d) display and tribal pride; (e) monuments to perpetuate ancestor memory. He does not endorse any single explanation.
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's analysis.

### Claim F4: Tribal pride and competitive instinct as motivation
**Page**: p. 307 (PDF p. 324)
**Quote**: "The desire for display was certainly a predominant motive for the carving of these giant statues, but it is questionable whether this was sufficient to provoke as much energy as that required for the transportation of enormous statues. On the other hand, to assume that the motive for making the images was entirely religious is to underestimate the tribal pride and competitive instinct of Polynesians."
**Evidential type**: (c) Métraux's interpretive assessment.
**Relevance**: Métraux identifies "tribal pride and competitive instinct" as a motive for monument construction, a formulation consistent with competitive signaling among autonomous groups rather than centralized command.

### Claim F5: No worship within living memory
**Page**: p. 307 (PDF p. 324)
**Summary**: "One of the Easter Islanders kidnapped and sent to Peru assured Gill (89, p. 213) that these statues had not been worshipped within the memory of man." Jaussen's informants "conveyed to him the impression that these statues were merely to decorate the ahu."
**Evidential type**: (b) Reported tradition from multiple independent informants. [POST-RAID]

### Claim F6: González expedition members' response equivocal
**Page**: p. 306 (PDF p. 323)
**Summary**: Natives appeared "to 'hold them [the images] in great veneration,' and were 'displeased' when the Spaniards approached to examine them closely. However, this fear might have concerned the ahu itself rather than the images."
**Evidential type**: (a) Contact-era observation (1770), with Métraux's interpretive note.

## G. Convergences and Divergences with the 1937 JPS Article

### What's NEW in the 1940 monograph (not in the 1937 article):

1. **King list comparison table (Table 2)**: The full side-by-side comparison with analysis of only 7 names common to all four lists.
2. **Tuhunga series**: Identification of priests/experts interpolated into king lists.
3. **Clan structure detail**: Named clans, territorial descriptions, diffuse territories.
4. **Marriage rules**: Minimal restrictions; tumu marriage exchange system.
5. **Matatoa as "actual rulers"**: Explicit identification of warriors as the real political power.
6. **Kio as war captives**: Details of enslavement following defeat.
7. **Priestly functions**: Specific ritual duties, healing practices, ventriloquism.
8. **Stone image significance**: Extended analysis concluding no evidence of worship.
9. **Bird cult sacrifice designation**: Tangata manu designated human sacrifices, provoking annual warfare.
10. **Makemake's dominance**: Great Polynesian gods reduced to genealogical entries; Makemake alone actively worshiped.

### What's CONFIRMED from the 1937 article:

1. "Certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (nearly identical wording).
2. Ariki-mau's supernatural functions without political power.
3. Roussel's "absolute power" claim rejected.
4. Capture of the ariki-mau by the Ngaure.
5. Food flows through ariki-mau to tangata-honui (elite-to-elite).
6. Tangata manu as incarnation of Makemake, not secular king.

## Summary of Relevance to the Paper

The 1940 monograph substantially enriches the 1937 article's conclusions while confirming them in every particular:

1. **King list comparison** (Table 2) provides the definitive evidence for the unreliability of dynastic lists. Only 7 of dozens of names appear in all four independently recorded lists. Names that do recur are tribal/geographic eponyms, not historical individuals. This resolves [CITE-CHECK: Métraux 1940:91] and strengthens the circularity argument: the "dynastic succession" used to support chiefdom models is an artifact of conflated tribal names, interpolated priests, and mythological figures.

2. **Social class analysis** reveals Roussel's four-class system as "only superficially exact." The kio were war captives, not a hereditary caste. The matatoa were the actual political power holders, not administrators in a chiefly hierarchy. The ivi-atua's very existence is disputed among sources.

3. **Stone image significance** section concludes there is "no direct evidence" the statues were worshiped, and identifies "tribal pride and competitive instinct" as construction motivations, consistent with collective action/competitive signaling rather than chiefly command.

4. **Clan structure** is considerably more diffuse than a chiefdom model predicts: some clans lacked distinct territories, boundaries overlapped, and marriage restrictions were minimal.

5. **The fuller treatment reinforces, rather than modifies, the 1937 conclusion**: despite documenting more chiefly prerogatives and more social complexity than the article, Métraux reaches exactly the same assessment of the ariki-mau's political power: "certainly not a ruler or civilian chief."
