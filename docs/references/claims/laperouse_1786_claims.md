# Claims Extraction: La Perouse 1786

## Source Information

- **Full citation**: La Perouse, Jean-Francois de Galaup, comte de. *A Voyage Round the World, Performed in the Years 1785, 1786, 1787 and 1788, by the Boussole and Astrolabe*. Translated from the French. Published under the superintendence of L. A. Milet-Mureau. London: Printed by A. Hamilton for G. G. and J. Robinson, 1799. Vol. I, Chapters IV-V (pp. 317-334). Da Capo Press / N. Israel reprint (Bibliotheca Australiana #27).
- **PDF file(s)**: `docs/references/pdfs/european_accounts/04_laperouse_1786/La Perouse - A Voyage Around the World.pdf`; `docs/references/pdfs/european_accounts/04_laperouse_1786/LaPerouse-1786-EasterIsland.pdf`
- **Date of visit/observation**: April 9-10, 1786
- **Duration on island**: Approximately 10-11 hours ashore (day-break to six in the evening)
- **Context of visit**: La Perouse's circumnavigation expedition with two frigates (Boussole and Astrolabe), approximately 70 persons landed. The expedition included naturalists (Dagelet, Lamanon), artists (Duche), a botanist/gardener, and clergy (abbe Monges, father Receveur). La Perouse had expressly planned to bring gifts and livestock to benefit the islanders. He was well-read in Cook's and Forster's prior accounts and arrived prepared for theft. A second party under M. de Langle explored the interior (Chapter V). This is the most extensive and best-provisioned of the four major contact-era visits.

## Cross-Reference Notes

### Internal Consistency
- La Perouse and de Langle's accounts (Chapters IV and V respectively) are remarkably consistent. Both describe communal property, absence of chiefs, and a population of approximately 2,000.
- De Langle's interior exploration (Chapter V) confirms La Perouse's coastal observations: communal houses, district-level organization around morais/platforms, skilled agriculture, and no obvious political hierarchy.
- There is a minor tension between La Perouse's categorical denial of chiefs (p. 323: "they have no need of a chief") and his hedged concession that "There is probably a chief in each district" (p. 324). The resolution appears to be that the "chief" of p. 324 is a horticultural overseer requiring consensus, not a political authority.

### Comparison with Cook 1774
- La Perouse explicitly critiques Cook's interpretation that the person controlling food distribution was a "proprietor" chief (p. 324). La Perouse argues the difficulty in obtaining food reflected the need for "general consent," not chiefly ownership.
- La Perouse notes women were more visible in 1786 than in Cook's 1774 visit but cannot explain why (p. 322).
- La Perouse disagrees with Forster's assessment (from Cook's voyage) that the monuments indicated a formerly more powerful population (p. 321): "his opinion does not appear to me to be well founded."
- La Perouse explicitly notes Cook's expedition arrived in poor condition ("in want of every necessary, and afflicted with the sea scurvy," p. 319) and that "Their narratives were influenced by their situation."

### Comparison with Roggeveen 1722
- The French editor's footnote (p. 329) observes that "Easter Island, discovered in 1722, by Roggewein, appears, as la Perouse observes, to have undergone a revolution in it's population and the productions of it's soil."
- La Perouse noted large monuments as "very ancient" (p. 322), consistent with Roggeveen finding them already in place in 1722.

### Comparison with Gonzalez 1770
- No explicit reference to the Gonzalez expedition in the text.

## Summary Assessment

### What This Source Can Tell Us

1. **Active search for chiefs yielded negative results.** La Perouse did not merely fail to mention chiefs; he actively tried to identify them among 800+ people, distributed medals to candidates, and concluded their "pre-eminence consisted in nothing but their superiority of talents, and greater dexterity in promoting the pillage" (p. 320). This is the strongest negative evidence from any contact-era account.

2. **No coercive authority observed.** Among the largest contact-era landing party (~70 French, ~800 islanders), no individual could restrain the population from theft, no punishment or social sanction was observed for theft, and even supposed "chiefs" participated in stealing.

3. **Communal resource organization.** La Perouse observed that "productions of the earth were in common" (p. 323), houses were communal (p. 323), and distribution of food required "almost a general consent" rather than chiefly authorization (p. 324).

4. **Egalitarian conditions.** The explicit statement that "all conditions are equal" (p. 323) and that there is no "need of a chief" (p. 323) is a direct observation-based inference.

5. **Monument construction had ceased.** Current burial practice consisted of individually-built cairns (p. 322), not collective monument construction. La Perouse interpreted this as reflecting the absence of chiefs worth commemorating.

6. **No religious institutions.** "We did not however observe any traces of religion" (p. 322). No priests, temples, or organized ritual observed.

7. **No surplus or redistribution.** Subsistence required minimal labor ("three days' labour... for a year," p. 323), production was communal, and no redistribution system was observed or described.

8. **Detailed environmental observations.** Deforestation, lack of running water, volcanic soil, limited animal resources, skilled but unmechanized agriculture.

### What This Source Cannot Tell Us

1. **Pre-contact political organization.** La Perouse saw the island for approximately 10 hours in 1786, 64 years after European contact began. He explicitly acknowledged his observations were "nothing but conjectures" about a people "whose language I knew nothing" (p. 323).

2. **Whether chiefs existed but were not visible.** La Perouse's search for authority figures took place in the context of a chaotic mass encounter. Leaders may have been present but chose not to engage with the expedition, or their authority may not have been exercisable in the unusual circumstances of European contact.

3. **Whether the absence of visible hierarchy was recent.** La Perouse noted the monuments were "very ancient" and that current practice had changed. He could not determine whether political organization had changed along with monument-building practices.

4. **Internal social structure.** With no knowledge of the language and only surface observation, La Perouse could not assess kinship systems, lineage hierarchies, ritual authority, or any form of organization not visible to a brief visitor.

5. **Whether "general consent" (p. 324) implies a political process.** The need for consensus to distribute food could reflect either acephalous communalism or a consultative chiefly system. La Perouse could not distinguish these.

### Epistemological Significance

La Perouse's account is the most detailed contact-era observation and the one most often cited for evidence about political organization. Its significance for the chiefdom debate is twofold:

First, it provides the strongest direct evidence AGAINST hereditary chiefdom organization: La Perouse explicitly tried to find chiefs and could not; he described conditions as "equal" and stated there was "no need of a chief."

Second, it demonstrates the limits of contact-era evidence for any conclusion about political organization. La Perouse himself emphasized that his claims were "conjectures" based on a single day's observation without language competence. If the most extensive and best-documented contact-era visit can produce only "conjectures," the thinner accounts of Roggeveen, Gonzalez, and Cook provide even less basis for confident claims about political structure.

The account is particularly damaging to the chiefdom attribution because La Perouse was not ideologically opposed to finding chiefs; he was a French naval officer familiar with hierarchical social orders and actively looking for authority figures with whom to negotiate. His failure to find them is evidence from a sympathetic searcher, not a skeptical one.
