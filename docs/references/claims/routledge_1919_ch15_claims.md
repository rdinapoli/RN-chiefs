# Claims Extraction: Routledge 1919, Chapter XV

## Source Information

**Author**: Katherine Routledge (Mrs. Scoresby Routledge)

**Publication**: *The Mystery of Easter Island: The Story of an Expedition*, Chapter XV: "Native Culture in Pre-Christian Times," pp. 200-235. London: Sifton, Praed & Co., 1919.

**Period on Easter Island**: March 1914 to August 1915 (approximately 17 months). The Mana Expedition.

**PDF file**: `docs/references/pdfs/post_slave_raid/routledge_1919/` (chapter OCR in `docs/working/routledge_ch15_raw.txt`)

**Context**: Routledge arrived on Easter Island approximately 51 years after the 1862-1863 Peruvian slave raids and the demographic collapse that followed. The population at the time of her visit was approximately 250. She worked with elderly informants (about a dozen), some of whom had been children or young adults in the 1860s and could recall the pre-missionary period. Her principal interpreter and collaborator was Juan Tepano, a man of about forty, full-blooded Kanaka who had served in the Chilean army and spoke some pidgin English. Routledge learned the native language during her stay and by the end could conduct interviews directly in Kanaka, with Tepano clarifying.

**[POST-RAID]**: ALL claims from informants in this file derive from a source recorded ~51 years after the 1862-1863 slave raids. The population had collapsed from ~3,000 to 175 by 1871. Routledge's informants were elderly survivors who had been children during the pre-missionary period. She herself notes: "the last of the ariki, or chiefs" were among those carried off to Peru (p. 205). All ethnographic information about political organization comes through this post-catastrophe filter.

---

## Analytical Significance

Chapter XV is the most important single chapter in Routledge 1919 for political organization. It covers social divisions, clans, warfare, marriage, burial customs, and social functions. Unlike Thomson (who relied on Salmon as intermediary), Routledge worked with multiple elderly informants over many months, cross-examined them, and learned the native language. Her methodology was more rigorous than any predecessor. However, the temporal distance from functioning pre-contact society was even greater than Thomson's.

For the chiefdom attribution, the critical passages are: (1) the statement that "with the exception of the Miru, there were no chiefs nor any form of government" (p. 224); (2) the description of ten mata (clans) grouped into two major divisions (p. 221); (3) the absence of any description of tribute, redistribution, or taxation; (4) the description of warfare as "spasmodic and isolated raids" led by any man "expert in war" (p. 224); (5) the clan marriage rules showing no ranked endogamy; and (6) the description of social functions (paina, koro) organized by families, not chiefs.

This chapter simultaneously contains the most explicit denial of centralized authority outside the Miru AND preserves the Miru's exceptional status as having some form of chiefly rank. This ambiguity is central to the chiefdom question.

## 1. Sources of Information and Methodology

### 1.1. Assessment of early voyagers [POST-RAID context but about historical sources]

"While attention has been drawn to the importance of these early narratives, it must be remembered that all the visits were of very short duration, and that the old voyagers were not trained observers. The Dutchmen, for instance, deliberately tell us that the statues have no arms." (p. 205)

**(c) Routledge's own assessment.** She explicitly qualifies the reliability of the contact-era accounts: short visits, untrained observers, demonstrable errors.

**Valence for chiefdom question**: Undermines uncritical reliance on contact-era accounts for political organization claims.

### 1.2. The slave raids carried off the last chiefs

"The natives say that one thousand in all were so removed from the island, and, unfortunately, there were amongst them some of the principal men, including many of the most learned, and the last of the ariki, or chiefs." (p. 205)

**(b) Reported tradition from informants.** Routledge records that the slaves raids removed "the last of the ariki." This is critical: it means her informants were not themselves chiefs, nor had they lived under functioning chiefly authority.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. Confirms ariki existed in some form pre-raid but simultaneously explains why no living informant could describe how the system actually functioned. All subsequent descriptions of chiefly authority in this source are reconstructions from memory, not descriptions of lived experience.

### 1.3. Thomson's account and Salmon as intermediary

"He [Salmon] spoke the language of the island, and when the U.S.S. Mohican arrived in 1886, he was the source of much of the information which they subsequently published. It is an important but difficult matter to know how far the material thus gleaned thirty years ago was carefully obtained and reproduced." (pp. 208-209)

**(c) Routledge's assessment.** She explicitly questions the reliability of Thomson's account by noting Salmon's mediating role and doubting the care of the collection process.

**Valence**: Undermines Thomson 1891 as an authority on political organization. Routledge adds: "he appears to have taken little interest in the surviving customs and failed to understand them" (p. 209).

### 1.4. Informant methodology

"On our arrival there were still a few old people who were sufficiently grown up in the sixties to recall something of the old life; with the great majority of these, about a dozen in number, we gradually got in touch." (p. 211)

**(a) Routledge's direct description of her methods.**

### 1.5. Cross-examination method

"The witness had to be heard, and discreetly cross-examined without any doubt being thrown on his story, which would at once have given offence; then allowed to forget and again re-examined, his story being compared with that of others who had been heard meanwhile." (p. 213)

**(a) Routledge's description of her methodology.** This is a sophisticated approach: separate interviews, cross-checking, re-examination after intervals.

**Valence**: Supports treating Routledge's ethnographic data as more carefully collected than Thomson's, though the same fundamental limitations apply (post-catastrophe informants reconstructing a social system they experienced only as children).

### 1.6. Reliability caveat

"Though great care has been exercised, it must be remembered, when a particular account is quoted, as, for example, that of Te Haha regarding the annual inspection of the tablets, while it is believed to rest on fact, its absolute accuracy cannot be guaranteed." (p. 213)

**(c) Routledge's own qualification.** She acknowledges the limits of her data.

### 1.7. Memory limitations

"Deliberate invention was rare, but, when memory was a little vague, there was a constant tendency to glide from what was remembered to what was imagined." (p. 212)

**(c) Routledge's assessment.** Critical methodological observation about the tendency of elderly informants to blend memory and imagination.

### 1.8. Juan Tepano as principal interpreter

"Any real success in intercourse was, however, due to the intelligence of one individual who was known as Juan Tepano. He was a younger man about forty years of age, a full-blooded Kanaka, but had served his time in the Chilean army... before the end he and I were able to understand each other entirely in Kanaka, and he made clear to the old men anything I wished to know, and explained their answers to me." (p. 214)

**(a) Routledge's description.** Tepano was not himself an elderly informant but a translator and facilitator. He was of the Tupahotu clan (identified in the photograph caption, p. 212).

### 1.9. Named informants and their clans

From photograph captions (p. 212) and text:
- **Te Haha**: Clan Miru (informant on tablets, old age)
- **Juan Tepano**: Clan Tupahotu (interpreter, ~40 years old)
- **He**: Clan Marama (old man, made mataa for the expedition)
- **Viriamo**: Clan Ureohei (oldest woman living, detailed life history given)
- **Porotu**: Old man (helped carry Ko Tori's body)
- **Kapiera**: Parents lived at Anakena (born during his mother's attendance at a funeral)
- Various unnamed lepers at the settlement north of Hanga Roa

**(a) Routledge's direct identification.** The clan affiliations of informants are significant: they come from multiple clans (Miru, Tupahotu, Marama, Ureohei), reducing the risk of a single clan's perspective dominating the account.

## 2. Clan (Mata) Divisions

### 2.1. Ten clans with territorial associations

"They were divided into ten groups, or clans ('mata'), which were associated with different parts of the island, though the boundaries blend and overlap; members of one division settled not infrequently among those of another. Each person still knows his own clan." (p. 221)

**(b) Reported tradition, confirmed by Routledge's direct observation** (that each person still knew their clan). Key details: (1) ten clans; (2) territorial association not rigid ("boundaries blend and overlap"); (3) inter-clan residence common ("not infrequently"); (4) clan identity still known in 1914-15.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. Clan divisions are consistent with both chiefdom and non-chiefdom models. The flexible territorial boundaries and frequent inter-clan residence argue against rigid territorial control by chiefs.

### 2.2. Kotuu and Hotu Iti division

"These ten clans were again grouped, more especially in legend or speaking of the remote past, into two major divisions known as Kotuu (or Otuu), and Hotu Iti, which correspond roughly with the western and eastern parts of the island. These divisions were also known respectively as Mata-nui, or greater clans, and Mata-iti, or lesser clans." (p. 221)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Critical details: (1) the dual division was spoken of "more especially in legend or speaking of the remote past," suggesting it was a structural concept rather than an active political reality in living memory; (2) the west = Kotuu = Mata-nui (greater clans) and east = Hotu Iti = Mata-iti (lesser clans) alignment is given; (3) "roughly" western and eastern.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. Dual divisions are common in Polynesia and can reflect either ranked moieties (supporting chiefdom) or non-hierarchical groupings. The "greater/lesser" terminology could imply ranked status or simply relative size. Routledge does not describe any political functions of the division.

### 2.3. Kotuu clans: territory and composition

"In Kotuu, the Marama and Haumoana inhabited side by side the land running from sea to sea between the high central ground and the western volcano Rano Kao. They had a small neighbour, the Ngatimo, to the south, and jointly with the Miru spread over Rano Kao and formed settlements by the margin of the crater lake. The Miru lived on the high, narrow strip between the mountain in the apex and the cliff, and mixed up with them was a lesser people, the Hamea. To the east was another small clan, the Raa, which is spoken of in conjunction with the Miru and Hamea." (pp. 221-223)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Kotuu clans enumerated: Marama, Haumoana, Ngatimo, Miru, Hamea, Raa (six clans in the western division). Note: the Hamea are described as "a lesser people" mixed with the Miru, and the Ngatimo is "small." This suggests size differentiation among clans, which could imply unequal status.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. The Miru's position at the apex/cliff (near Orongo) and their association with smaller dependent groups (Hamea, Raa) could suggest privileged status. But the arrangement could also reflect simple geographic proximity.

### 2.4. Hotu Iti clans: territory and composition

"The principal Hotu Iti clans were the Tupahotu, the Koro-orongo, and the Hitiuira. The last were generally known as the 'Ureohei'; they inhabited jointly the level piece of ground from the northern bay to the south coast, and had some dwellings on the eastern headland. Next to them on the south coast was a small group, the Ngaure." (p. 223)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Hotu Iti clans: Tupahotu, Koro-orongo, Hitiuira/Ureohei, Ngaure (four clans in the eastern division).

### 2.5. Significance of clans for immigration question

"The particular importance of the clans lies in the fact that, while they may be merely groups of one body, they may, on the other hand, represent different races or waves of immigrants. If there have been two peoples on Easter Island, these divisions are one place where we must at least look for traces for it." (p. 223)

**(c) Routledge's inference.** She is interested in the clans primarily as evidence for or against multiple immigration waves, not as evidence for political hierarchy. She does not describe the clans as ranked relative to each other (except the Miru, discussed separately).

### 2.6. Clan names and their Polynesian meanings (footnote)

"'Raa' means the sun and 'Marama' the light. The signification of the others is not equally clear... Mr. Ray gives the following interesting information from other Polynesian sources. 'Haumoana' means the sea-breeze; 'Hitiuira' is probably 'hiti-ra' or sunrise; and 'Ure-o-hei' another version of 'ura-o-hehe,' or red of sundown. 'Koro-orongo' is doubtless from 'Koro-o-Rongo,' or the ring of Rongo (a well-known Polynesian deity), that is the rainbow. 'Kotuu' appears to be a contraction of 'Ko Otuu,' meaning 'The Hill.' 'Hotu' is another form of the word for hill and 'Iti' signifies small, it presumably refers to Rano Raraku." (p. 223, footnote)

**(c) Routledge's interpretation with linguistic analysis from Mr. Ray.** The derivation of "Hotu Iti" as meaning "small hill" (referring to Rano Raraku) rather than a personal name is notable.

## 3. Leadership, Chiefs, and Political Authority

### 3.1. "No chiefs nor any form of government" except the Miru [THE CENTRAL CLAIM]

"With the exception of the Miru, of which more will be said, there were no chiefs nor any form of government; any man who was expert in war became a leader." (p. 224)

**(b) Reported tradition.** THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT SINGLE STATEMENT IN THE CHAPTER FOR THE CHIEFDOM QUESTION. Routledge's informants from multiple clans (Tupahotu, Marama, Ureohei, Miru) told her that outside the Miru clan, there were no chiefs and no government. Leadership was achieved, not ascribed: "any man who was expert in war became a leader."

**Valence**: Strongly undermines the chiefdom attribution. If the informants are correct, centralized political authority was confined to one clan (Miru) and even within that clan Routledge does not describe the mechanisms of authority. This is inconsistent with an island-wide chiefdom. It is consistent with either: (a) a weak paramount chief whose authority was nominal outside his own clan, or (b) no real paramount at all, with the Miru's "chiefly" status being ceremonial/ritual rather than political.

**Cross-reference**: Directly contradicts Thomson 1891, who describes an "arbitrary monarchy" with a king reigning "over the entire island" (Thomson p. 472). However, Thomson himself qualifies this by noting "no tax was exacted of the people" and "it does not appear that any great homage was paid the king" (Thomson p. 473), which partially supports Routledge's account.

### 3.2. Miru set apart but powers unspecified

"With the exception of the Miru, of which more will be said..." (p. 224)

**(c) Routledge's framing.** She promises more detail about the Miru but IN THIS CHAPTER provides none. The Miru are set apart from the general statement about the absence of chiefs, but the nature of their distinction is not elaborated here. Chapter XV does not describe ariki mau powers, succession rules, or specific political functions of the Miru.

**Valence**: The deferral is itself significant. If the Miru had held obvious, well-described paramount authority, one would expect it to be presented here in the chapter on social organization. The fact that Routledge separates it out and promises later treatment suggests the Miru's status was complex, ambiguous, or primarily ceremonial (likely connected to the tangata manu bird-man cult discussed in a later chapter).

### 3.3. The "last real ariki" was white

"The last real ariki, or chief, was said to be quite white." (p. 221)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The reference to color is in the context of Routledge discussing the mixed racial composition of the islanders. The phrase "last real ariki" implies: (1) there was an ariki; (2) the lineage had ended; (3) this person was the last one considered genuinely legitimate. No powers or functions of this individual are described.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. Confirms ariki as a recognized category but provides no evidence about what powers the ariki held.

### 3.4. Ngaara as "last great chief"

"this latter was the reason given for the burial of the last great chief, Ngaara, who was interred in one of the image ahu on the western coast." (p. 230)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Ngaara is identified as the "last great chief" and was buried in an image ahu to protect his body from being burned by enemies. This implies: (1) the chief's status warranted special burial treatment; (2) the chief was vulnerable to enemies, suggesting limited actual power.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. Confirms chiefly status was recognized but the need to hide the burial from enemies suggests the chief's authority did not extend to protection from rival groups, inconsistent with effective paramount authority.

### 3.5. Warfare: no permanent military leadership

"The warfare consisted largely of spasmodic and isolated raids; an aggrieved person gathered together his neighbours and descended on the offenders." (p. 224)

**(b) Reported tradition.** War leadership was ad hoc: an "aggrieved person" (not a chief) gathered neighbors for retaliatory raids. This is raiding, not organized warfare directed by a political authority. The description of "spasmodic and isolated raids" is inconsistent with the organized military campaigns that chiefdom models predict.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom attribution. No war leader appointed by chiefs; no standing military organization; raids motivated by personal grievance, not political objectives.

### 3.6. Warfare between and within clans

"Legend tells of continual wars between Hotu Iti and Kotuu. In recent times general fighting seems to have been constant, and took place even between members of one clan." (pp. 223-224)

**(b) Reported tradition and (c) Routledge's characterization.** "Continual wars" in legend between the two major divisions, but in "recent times" (presumably the period her informants remembered) fighting was intra-clan as well as inter-clan.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. If fighting occurred even within clans, clan-level political authority was insufficient to maintain internal order, let alone island-wide authority.

### 3.7. Matatoa: warrior class ahu dedication

"One of those on the west road was reported as specially dedicated to mata-toa which signifies victors or warriors—and the same was said of a differently made ahu on the south coast." (p. 231)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Certain ahu were dedicated to matatoa. This confirms the matatoa as a recognized social category with associated ritual/commemorative infrastructure, but does not describe political functions.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. Matatoa (warriors/victors) as a recognized class is consistent with both chiefdom (where warriors serve chiefs) and non-chiefdom (where warriors achieve status independently) models.

## 4. Social Organization and Rank

### 4.1. Ivi atua mentioned in passing

"the original direction for the celebration came from a supernaturally gifted individual known as an 'ivi-atua.'" (p. 233)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The ivi atua is mentioned only in connection with the paina ceremony. The ivi atua gave the "original direction" for the celebration, suggesting a ritual/ceremonial role. No political functions are described.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. Confirms ivi atua as a recognized social role, but its association with ceremonial direction rather than political command is consistent with a religious specialist role rather than a political authority.

### 4.2. Marriage: no clan restrictions

"In remembered times there were no group restrictions on marriage, which took place indiscriminately between members of the same or of different clans. The only prohibition had reference to consanguinity, and forbade all union nearer than the eighth degree or third cousins." (p. 221)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Critical for the chiefdom question. No endogamy, no exogamy, no ranked marriage rules. Marriage between any clans was permitted. The only restriction was consanguinity (not nearer than third cousins). This is inconsistent with ranked chiefdom systems where marriage between social strata is regulated (hypergamy, endogamy of elites, etc.).

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom attribution. The absence of marriage restrictions between clans or social ranks is inconsistent with a hierarchical society where rank is maintained through marriage rules.

### 4.3. Patrilineal clan membership

"The children belonged to the father's clan, and are often distinguished by his name being given after their own." (p. 226)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Patrilineal descent for clan membership. But this was not rigid: "the clan of the mother was not ignored, and a man would sometimes fight for his maternal side" (p. 226).

### 4.4. Polygamy

"Polygamy was usual, but many seem to have had only one wife." (p. 226)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Polygamy was practiced but not universal. No indication that polygamy was restricted to elites or chiefs.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. Polygamy is common in both chiefdom and non-chiefdom Polynesian societies.

### 4.5. Marriage as arrangement with bridewealth

Viriamo's account: "Her first union was a matter of arrangement, the husband, who was also of the Ureohei, giving her father much food, and, if she had refused to accept the situation, she would, she said, have been beaten. There was no ceremony of any kind, no new clothes nor feasting; her father simply took her to her new home and handed her over." (p. 227)

**(b) Reported tradition from Viriamo (clan Ureohei).** Marriage involved food-giving to the father, suggesting a form of bridewealth. No ceremony. No chief or religious figure involved. Marriage was a family arrangement, not a state function.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. Marriage as a purely family transaction without any involvement of political or religious authority is inconsistent with chiefdom models where marriages (especially among elites) are politically significant events.

### 4.6. Marriage as debt payment

"She was handed over to him as a matter of family arrangement, in discharge of a debt." (p. 228)

**(b) Reported tradition from Viriamo about her third marriage.** Women could be transferred between families to discharge debts. This was described matter-of-factly, not as something requiring chiefly approval.

### 4.7. Viriamo's inter-clan marriages

Viriamo (Ureohei) married: (1) a man of the Ureohei; (2) a man of the Miru clan at Anakena; (3) a Tupahotu near Tongariki. (pp. 227-228)

**(b) Reported tradition.** A single woman married into three different clans over her lifetime, including the Miru. No indication that the Miru marriage was exceptional or required special permission, nor that marriage into the "chiefly" clan conferred or required special status.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. If an ordinary Ureohei woman could simply go live with a Miru man, the Miru were not maintaining exclusive elite status through marriage restrictions.

### 4.8. No social classes described in marriage or daily life

Routledge describes no system of social classes (ariki, ivi atua, matatoa, kio) as a structured hierarchy. The terms appear individually but are not organized into a ranked system. The kio (servant/slave class reported by Thomson and Métraux) is NOT MENTIONED AT ALL in this chapter.

**Valence**: Significant absence. If a hereditary servant class (kio) existed, one would expect Routledge's extensive interviews to reveal it. Its absence from her account undermines claims of rigid social stratification.

## 5. Warfare

### 5.1. Weapons

"A wooden sword, or paoa, was used, but the chief weapon was made from obsidian, and took from it the name of 'mataa.'" (p. 223-224)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Obsidian mataa as primary weapon, wooden paoa as secondary.

### 5.2. Anecdote of finding "war going on"

"It is related incidentally that one man, going along the south coast, 'found war going on,' one set of men having blocked up another in a cave." (p. 224)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The casual quality of this anecdote ("found war going on") reinforces the picture of sporadic, localized violence rather than organized campaigns.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. This describes small-scale feuding, not organized warfare directed by political authority.

### 5.3. The Toa-toa cave story: six men against a hundred

"six men, called Gwaruti-mata-keva, of the clan Tupahotu, who lived in a cave in a certain hillock on the south coast, known as Toa-toa. They went round in a boat to Hanga Piko, stole fish, and returned rapidly to their cave. A hundred men from Hanga Piko then came overland to punish the robbery." (p. 224)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Warfare as retaliatory raid for theft. A hundred men mobilize to punish six fish thieves. No chief organizes either side; the "hundred men from Hanga Piko" appear to be a spontaneous retaliatory force.

### 5.4. Secret society / fraud gang at Toa-toa

"They were, it was said, in the habit of going about after dark with their faces painted red, white, and black, and visiting houses, where they declared they were gods, and demanded food, which the inhabitants accordingly gave them. The fraud, however, finally came to light when one day a man, who was travelling with his servant, saw them washing paint off their faces, 'so they knew that they had deceived the people, and the people gathered together and killed them.'" (p. 224)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Routledge connects this to "the secret societies found elsewhere in the Pacific." The group used supernatural claims to extract food. When exposed, "the people" collectively killed them. No chief was involved in either the fraudulent extraction or the punishment.

**Valence**: Complex. The use of supernatural claims to extract food could be seen as a proto-taxation mechanism. But the fact that it was a fraud, recognized as illegitimate, and punished by collective action suggests that routine extraction of surplus by claiming divine authority was NOT considered acceptable in Rapa Nui society.

### 5.5. House burning in warfare

"In these internecine fights fire was very generally set to the enemy's dwellings. 'He often burnt houses,' a young man said, pointing to an older one, and the impeachment was not denied." (p. 224)

**(a) Routledge's direct observation of an exchange between two informants.** A living person's involvement in house-burning raids was casually acknowledged. This indicates the violence described was recent and personal, not legendary.

### 5.6. Ahu raided and bodies burnt

"The ahu, too, were raided and bodies burnt, which seems to be the cause of the burnt bones recorded by certain travellers; there is no reason to suppose there was cremation or sacrifice on Easter Island." (pp. 224-225)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Routledge's inference.** Ahu (burial platforms) were attacked and corpses burned as an act of warfare. Routledge explicitly denies cremation and sacrifice.

### 5.7. Last statues overthrown in warfare

"It was in this sort of warfare that the last images were overthrown." (p. 225)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Routledge's inference.** The final toppling of moai occurred during inter-clan raids, not during some organized political upheaval.

### 5.8. Cannibalism fading before Christianity

"While legends record how many people were eaten after each affray, all living persons deny, with rather striking unanimity, not only that they themselves have ever been cannibals, but that their fathers were so. If this is correct, the custom was dying out for some reason before the advent of Christianity; their grandfathers, the old people admit, ate human flesh." (p. 225)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Cannibalism existed but was dying out independently of missionary influence, suggesting two generations before living memory (~1800-1820s).

### 5.9. Miru-Ureohei revenge narrative

"The great-grandmother of an old man of the Miru clan was, according to his account, killed on the high central part of the island by the Ureohei and eaten. In revenge for the outrage, one of her sons, Hotu by name, killed sixty of the Ureohei." (p. 225)

**(b) Reported tradition from a Miru informant.** A revenge killing narrative involving Miru vs. Ureohei. The scale (sixty killed) is probably exaggerated. The revenge was personal/familial (a son avenging his mother), not directed by a chief.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. Even in a narrative involving the Miru (the "chiefly" clan), the motive for war is familial revenge, not political command.

## 6. Burial Customs and Social Differentiation

### 6.1. No ceremony at burial

"The missionaries were impressed with the fact that there was no ceremony of any kind at a burial." (p. 229)

**(b) Reported tradition attributed to the missionaries.** Confirmed by Routledge's informants. Burials lacked formal ceremony.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. If even chiefly burials lacked ceremony, there was no ritual differentiation of rank in death. However, this may refer to ordinary burials only.

### 6.2. Elaborate rites for slain men (tangata ika)

"Most elaborate spells were, however, performed in connection with a man who had been slain, known as 'tangata ika,' or fish-man; the corpse was kept from resting either day or night while his neighbours went in pursuit of vengeance." (p. 229)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The tangata ika rites were not markers of rank but of manner of death (slain in conflict). The "chief man in charge of the ceremony was known as the 'timo'" (p. 229). This is a ritual role, not a political one.

### 6.3. Tangata ika corpse exposure

"On this, it was said, had been seated the naked body of a man belonging to the district, Kota-vari-vari by name, who had been killed at Akahanga on the south coast. One man kept the corpse from falling, while two others sat behind and chanted songs to aid the avengers. These watchers were covered with black ashes, wore only feather hats, and carried the small dancing-paddle known as 'rapa'; the chief man in charge of the ceremony was known as the 'timo.'" (p. 229)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The timo was the leader of the corpse-vigil ceremony. This is a specific ritual role associated with vengeance, not a permanent political office.

### 6.4. Dual mode of disposal: exposure and burial

"burial was also practised, the mode of disposal being a matter of choice." (p. 230)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The choice between exposure and burial was based on practical considerations (risk of ghost, risk of enemy burning the body), not on social rank.

### 6.5. Chief Ngaara buried specially to prevent enemy desecration

"the body, if left in the open, might be burnt by enemies; this latter was the reason given for the burial of the last great chief, Ngaara, who was interred in one of the image ahu on the western coast." (p. 230)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Ngaara's burial was motivated by fear of enemies, not by chiefly protocol. An image ahu was chosen, which could indicate special status, but the reason given was defensive, not honorific.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. The burial of "the last great chief" in an image ahu could indicate elite burial differentiation. But the motivation (fear of enemy desecration) suggests the chief's authority did not extend to guaranteeing the safety of his own remains.

### 6.6. Ahu dedicated to warriors (matatoa)

"One of those on the west road was reported as specially dedicated to mata-toa which signifies victors or warriors." (p. 231)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Some ahu were associated with warriors specifically. This confirms matatoa as an achieved-status category with associated burial/memorial infrastructure.

### 6.7. Memorial cairns (pipi-hereko) taboo

"Others of these independent memorials were in the shape of cairns about 6 feet in height, known as 'pipi-hereko,' and were formerly surmounted by a white stone. Many of them still exist... the taboo for the dead (or pera) extended to them, and no one went near them in the daylight, on penalty of being stoned, till the period of mourning had been terminated with the usual feast." (p. 232)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Taboo (pera) surrounding memorials enforced by collective sanction ("on penalty of being stoned"), not by chiefly authority.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. Taboo enforcement through community sanction rather than chiefly decree suggests collective enforcement mechanisms.

### 6.8. Corpse carried to multiple ahu

"If a man had sons by more than one wife, after his death each claimed the body of his father to lie on the ahu of his mother's clan, and the corpse might thus be carried to several in turn, finally returning to its own destination." (pp. 226-227)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Burial location was contested among maternal clan affiliations of a man's children. This is a family negotiation, not a chiefly decision.

## 7. Social Functions and Festivals

### 7.1. Funerals as major social events

"Any deficiency at marriages, in the way of social festivity, was made up at funerals. These were attended by persons from all over the island, for 'when they were not fighting, they were all cousins.' In answer to the remark that 'considering the population their whole time must have gone in this way,' it was cheerfully observed that 'they had nothing else to do, so they all went, everybody took food and everybody ate.'" (p. 228-229)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Funerals were island-wide events with communal food sharing. The informants' characterization ("everybody took food and everybody ate") describes potlatch-style communal consumption, not redistributive feasting organized by a chief.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. Everyone bringing food and everyone eating describes symmetric exchange, not asymmetric redistribution through a chiefly center.

### 7.2. Tablet chanters at funerals

"The men who knew the tablets went also and sang, but there seems to have been little or nothing in the way of rites." (p. 229)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Tablet experts (tangata rongorongo) attended funerals and sang, but "little or nothing in the way of rites." The tablet chanters were specialists, not political authorities.

### 7.3. Paina: family-organized festival

"The 'paina,' which means simply picture or representation, was given by the family as a testimonial of esteem to a father, or possibly a brother who might be either alive or dead; it was a serious matter, and the original direction for the celebration came from a supernaturally gifted individual known as an 'ivi-atua.'" (p. 233)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The paina was: (1) organized by the family (not by a chief); (2) honoring a father or brother (not a chief or political figure); (3) initiated at the direction of an ivi atua (religious specialist, not a political leader). The honoree could be alive or dead.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. The paina was a family event, not a chiefly redistribution ceremony. The ivi atua's role was to give the "original direction" (likely a ritual prescription), not to command as a political authority.

### 7.4. Paina: held at image ahu

"the interesting point in connection with the paina is that the usual place for erection was in front of an image ahu on its landward side, and at most, or all, of the large ahu, there can still be seen, in the grass at the foot of the paved slope, the holes where the paina have stood." (p. 233)

**(b) Reported tradition confirmed by archaeological observation.** Paina figures were erected at the image ahu. This connects the ahu to ongoing social life (feasting, family commemoration) rather than exclusively to chiefly authority.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. Ahu as community gathering places for family-organized feasts could support collective action models (ahu as communal infrastructure) rather than chiefdom models (ahu as elite monuments).

### 7.5. Paina scale and frequency

"The feast was held in the summer, and lasted from two to four days; at any given ahu there might be only one in the season or as many as five." (p. 233)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Multiple paina at a single ahu per season indicates frequent community use of ahu for feasting events, organized by different families.

### 7.6. Koro: extended house-party

"Another great festivity, given for a father either living or dead, was the 'koro.' This was a house-party on a very extended scale. A special dwelling made with poles and thatched was put up, and, according to accounts, which surround it no doubt with a halo from the past, measured some hundreds of feet in length and 20 feet in height. An old man stated that at a celebration at which he was present there were 'a hundred guests,' a number which is probably a guess, but the addition that there were 'ten cooking-places' sounds like memory." (p. 234)

**(b) Reported tradition from an eyewitness (an old man who was present).** The koro was organized for a father (alive or dead), a large-scale event with elaborate temporary construction. "Presents of food were brought to the hero who distributed them to the party" (p. 234).

**Valence**: Complex. The honoree distributing food to guests resembles redistributive feasting, a key chiefdom behavior. However: (1) it was organized by the family, not by a political office; (2) the "hero" was the person being honored, not a chief; (3) it honored a father, not a political leader qua leader. This could support either model depending on interpretation.

### 7.7. Koro duration and entertainment

"They seem to have lasted indefinitely, going on for months, and the time was passed with various entertainments. The old people sang, the young people danced, and the host, who lived in a little house near, came and looked on." (p. 234)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Multi-month festivals with the "host" (not a chief) presiding.

### 7.8. Koro: house broken down with carved lizards

"On the last day there was a great feast, and the house was broken down with the aid of the carved wooden lizards, which are associated with the island." (p. 234)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The ritual destruction of the temporary feast house connects to Polynesian patterns of ceremonial destruction.

### 7.9. Entertainment for mother (kaunga)

"There was yet another entertainment which is said to have been in honour of a mother, as a koro was of a father. In at least four different places on the island are to be seen a dancing-ground known as 'kaunga.' It is a narrow strip paved with pebbles, over 200 feet in length by 2 feet in width." (p. 234)

**(b) Reported tradition confirmed by archaeological observation.** The kaunga honored mothers specifically. At least four permanent dancing grounds existed.

### 7.10. Pre-dance seclusion for complexion

"In connection with some or all of the kaunga there was a house where the party remained indoors for a long time previous to the dances, in order to 'get their complexions good,' a touch which shows that a white skin was admired." (p. 235)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Seclusion for skin-lightening before dances. Routledge notes this as evidence of aesthetic preference for lighter skin.

### 7.11. Feasts timed by astronomical observation

"These feasts were held in certain months only, determined by the appearance of the heavens after nightfall. On the extremity of the eastern headland there is an outcrop of boulders, one of which is incised with a spiral design; the place is known as 'Ko Te Papa-ui-hetuu,' or, 'The Rock-for-seeing-stars,' and here the old men came to watch the constellations." (p. 235)

**(b) Reported tradition confirmed by physical remains.** Feast timing was determined by stellar observation by "old men" (not by a chief). The astronomical observation site and star map are physical evidence of organized calendrical knowledge held by specialists ("old men"), not by political authorities.

**Valence**: Ambiguous. Centralized calendrical knowledge could support chiefdom (priests maintaining calendar for chiefs) or collective action (community specialists maintaining shared knowledge).

## 8. Property and Land Tenure

### 8.1. Clan territorial association but flexible residence

"They were divided into ten groups, or clans ('mata'), which were associated with different parts of the island, though the boundaries blend and overlap; members of one division settled not infrequently among those of another." (p. 221)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Land was associated with clans, but residence was flexible. No description of chiefs allocating or controlling land.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. The absence of described mechanisms for chiefly land control, combined with flexible inter-clan residence, is inconsistent with a chiefdom in which a paramount controls land allocation.

### 8.2. Fish bartered for vegetables

"Fish are not very plentiful, as there is no barrier reef, but they also were an article of diet, and were bartered by those on the coast for the vegetable products obtained by those further inland." (p. 218)

**(b) Reported tradition / (c) Routledge's characterization.** Coastal-inland exchange through barter. No mention of this exchange being mediated by chiefs or requiring chiefly redistribution. This is direct, horizontal exchange.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. Exchange through barter rather than redistributive networks is inconsistent with chiefdom models where surplus flows through a chiefly center.

### 8.3. Chicken houses as most elaborate domestic architecture

"chickens played an important role in native life, and the remains of the dwellings made for them are much more imposing than those for human beings." (p. 218)

**(a) Routledge's direct observation / (b) reported tradition.** That chicken houses were more elaborate than human dwellings suggests chickens were a primary form of wealth, but no chiefly control over chickens is described.

### 8.4. Stone booty from raids

"Even the heavier stones were at times seized upon as booty in enemy raids; one measuring 15 feet was pointed out to us near an ahu on the south coast, which had been brought all the way from the north side of the island." (p. 216)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Worked house foundation stones were seized as war booty. This indicates that architectural stone was valuable and that raids targeted material resources, not just people.

---

## 9. Houses and Living Arrangements

### 9.1. Houses shared by related families

"They were often shared by related families and held anything from ten to thirty, or even more, persons." (p. 216)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Multi-family residences holding 10-30+ persons. No indication that house size correlated with chiefly rank.

### 9.2. Largest house with unique features

"The largest of these houses, which had some unique features, measured 122 feet in length, with an extreme width of 12 feet; but some 50 feet by 5 feet or 6 feet are more usual measurements." (p. 216)

**(a) Routledge's measurement.** The largest hare paenga was 122 feet long. Routledge notes its "unique features" but does not say it belonged to a chief. No direct association between house size and political rank is drawn.

---

## 10. Ko Peka Figure (Spanish Account Confirmed)

### 10.1. Clothed figure confirmed from living memory

"the natives were asked if they had ever known a similar figure, and an old man at once replied that there had existed one just like the description, made of reeds, as a memorial of a dead wife or 'fine' child; it stood in front of the house, or was sometimes carried to a hillock where the people assembled to mourn." (p. 234)

**(b) Reported tradition confirming the Spanish 1770 observation.** The ko peka figure was a memorial for a dead wife or child, organized at the household level.

## 11. Significant Absences

### 11.1. No king list provided in this chapter

Routledge does NOT provide a king list in Chapter XV. She mentions "the last real ariki" (p. 221) and "the last great chief, Ngaara" (p. 230), but presents no genealogical sequence of paramount rulers. If she obtained one elsewhere, it is not in this chapter.

### 11.2. No description of tribute or redistribution

Nowhere in Chapter XV does Routledge describe tribute payments to chiefs, redistributive feasting organized by chiefs, or systematic extraction of surplus. The feasts described (paina, koro, kaunga) are organized by families for family members.

**Valence**: Strongly undermines chiefdom. The absence of tribute and redistribution from the most extensive ethnographic account of pre-Christian Rapa Nui is a major gap in the evidence for a chiefdom attribution.

### 11.3. No description of succession rules

Routledge does not describe how the ariki mau or any chief succeeded to office. There is no mention of primogeniture, abdication at son's marriage (Roussel), eldest son inheritance (Thomson), or brother-to-brother succession (Geiseler).

**Valence**: The absence of succession rules from Routledge's account is notable because she collected genealogies and had detailed interviews with elderly informants over many months. If succession rules had been well-established and memorable, one would expect them to appear.

### 11.4. No mention of kio (servant/slave class)

The kio class described by Thomson ("serfs" or servants) and later by Métraux does not appear in Routledge's Chapter XV.

**Valence**: Undermines the claim of rigid social stratification. The absence of the kio from Routledge's account is especially significant given that she interviewed informants from multiple clans, including the Miru.

### 11.5. No description of ariki mau powers

Although Routledge acknowledges the Miru's exceptional status and references the "last real ariki" and the "last great chief, Ngaara," she does not describe what powers these figures held, what functions they performed, or how they exercised authority. The Miru exception is noted but not elaborated in this chapter.

### 11.6. No description of chiefly role in monument construction

Routledge provides no account of chiefs directing moai carving, ahu construction, or moai transport. This entire topic is absent from the social organization chapter.

## 12. Evidence Relevant to Collective Action Models

### 12.1. "Any man who was expert in war became a leader"

(p. 224) Achieved leadership based on competence, not inherited rank. This is a classic collective action signature: leadership emerges from demonstrated ability and operates through consent/voluntary following.

### 12.2. Community-organized feasting

The paina, koro, and kaunga were organized by families, not chiefs. Food was contributed by all ("everybody took food and everybody ate" at funerals, p. 229). This describes symmetric, communal resource pooling, not asymmetric redistribution.

### 12.3. Communal enforcement of taboo

The pera (death taboo) around memorial cairns was enforced by "being stoned" (p. 232), a collective sanction, not a chiefly decree.

### 12.4. Collective punishment of the Toa-toa fraud

When the Toa-toa gang's impersonation of gods was discovered, "the people gathered together and killed them" (p. 224). Collective enforcement, not chiefly justice.

### 12.5. Direct barter without chiefly mediation

Fish-for-vegetables exchange between coastal and inland residents through barter (p. 218) represents horizontal exchange without centralized redistribution.

### 12.6. Flexible inter-clan residence

"members of one division settled not infrequently among those of another" (p. 221). Voluntary mobility between clan territories without described chiefly permission.

---

## 13. Genealogical and Life History Data

### 13.1. Viriamo's life history: inter-clan mobility and multi-marriage

Viriamo (Ureohei) married three men from three different clans (Ureohei, Miru, Tupahotu) over her lifetime (pp. 227-228). Her movements: from eastern volcano area (Ureohei territory) to Anakena (Miru territory) to Tongariki (Tupahotu territory). This represents high inter-clan social and geographic mobility for an ordinary woman.

### 13.2. Genealogical depth limitation

"Only in one case was it possible to get back beyond the great-grandfather of our informant." (p. 227)

**(a) Routledge's direct finding.** Genealogical depth of 4-5 generations maximum. This fundamentally limits what can be known about political succession from oral tradition.

### 13.3. Two hundred names placed in family groups

"some two hundred names were in this way placed in their family groups, with details of clan, place of residence and colour, and some knowledge obtained with regard to many more." (p. 227)

**(a) Routledge's direct description.** She collected substantial genealogical data but explicitly states it is "not of course enough ground on which to found any theory."

---

## 14. La Perouse's Key Observation (Quoted by Routledge)

### 14.1. "No chief great enough" for a memorial

"he sees no reason why they should not still be made even under existing conditions; he thinks that a hundred people would be sufficient to put one of the statues in place. The objection he sees is that the people have no chief great enough to secure such a memorial." (p. 204)

**(c) Routledge quoting La Perouse 1786.** La Perouse (in 1786) observed that the islanders had no chief prestigious enough to warrant a new statue. This is a contact-era observation suggesting the absence of effective paramount authority as early as 1786.

**Valence**: Undermines chiefdom. If by 1786, before the slave raids, there was already "no chief great enough" to commission a statue, the chiefly system was either non-existent or in decline well before the 1860s catastrophe.

### Routledge vs. Thomson on political authority

| Topic | Thomson 1891 | Routledge 1919 |

### Routledge vs. Roussel on succession

Routledge provides NO succession rules. This silence is notable against:
- Roussel (1869): abdication when son marries
- Thomson (1891): eldest son nominated
- Geiseler (1883): brother-to-brother

The disagreement among the three other sources and Routledge's silence after 17 months of fieldwork suggests that either succession rules had been forgotten, were never standardized, or were invented/reconstructed differently by different informants for different inquirers.

## Summary Assessment

Routledge's Chapter XV provides the most detailed and carefully collected account of pre-Christian social life on Rapa Nui from a post-raid source. Her methodology (multiple informants from multiple clans, cross-examination, learning the native language, 17-month residence) was substantially more rigorous than Thomson's (12 days, single interpreter, evening sessions).

**What this source can tell us about political organization:**
- Clan (mata) structure: ten named clans with territorial associations but flexible boundaries
- Dual division (Kotuu/Hotu Iti) existed at least as a conceptual framework
- The Miru clan had some exceptional status, but its nature is not described in this chapter
- Warfare was endemic but consisted of raids motivated by personal grievance, not organized campaigns
- Leadership in war was achieved through ability, not inherited
- Marriage was unrestricted between clans
- Social functions (paina, koro) were family-organized, not chiefly-directed
- Exchange occurred through direct barter, not redistribution

**What this source cannot tell us:**
- The nature of Miru/ariki authority (deferred to later chapter)
- Pre-contact political organization (informants could recall only to the 1840s-1860s at best)
- Whether the absence of described chiefly authority reflects pre-contact reality or post-raid loss of knowledge
- The genealogical sequence of paramount leaders

**Key finding for the chiefdom critique:**
The most extensive ethnographic fieldwork ever conducted on Rapa Nui, by the most methodologically careful pre-modern researcher, found "no chiefs nor any form of government" outside one clan (p. 224), no tribute or redistribution, no succession rules, no social classes, no marriage restrictions between groups, and warfare led by achieved rather than ascribed leaders. This is fundamentally inconsistent with a chiefdom attribution.

However, the Miru exception must be investigated in the chapter where Routledge elaborates on it. The strength of the chiefdom critique from Routledge depends on what that later treatment reveals.
