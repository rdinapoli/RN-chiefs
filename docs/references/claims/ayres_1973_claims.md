# Claims Extraction: Ayres 1973

**Source**: Ayres, William S. 1973. "The Cultural Context of Easter Island Religious Structures." PhD dissertation, Tulane University. 402 pp.

**Type**: PhD dissertation; first systematic study of ahu in cultural context; pre-chiefdom-model archaeological analysis

**Date processed**: 2026-03-16

---

## I. Political Organization and Authority

### A. Ariki Mau Characterization

1. "At the time of European contact the king was not a political ruler but more of a spiritual one who presided over the entire island but did not engage in intertribal feuding." (p. 25, citing Métraux 1940:129)

2. "The warrior leaders eventually took over the actual political control of the tribes" (p. 26, citing Métraux 1940:138; Goldman 1970:116)

3. "The religious and political leader for the entire population of the island was the king (ariki), the head of the Honga lineage of the Miru mata, a nonunilinear kin group, consisting of several hundred people. This was a hereditary position." (p. 335)

4. "The prerogatives of rank were no longer as closely associated with status lineages, like the Honga and Miru, as they were during the earlier phases, but increasingly with military power." (p. 337)

5. "Goldman believes that at this time it was impossible for the culture to continue to operate as a Traditional Society and that the military supremacy, accompanied by reinterpretation of supernatural sanctions, led to island wide control by military leaders of lower status than the Miru Ariki." (p. 342)

### B. Social Classes

6. "Four classes of Easter Island society have been reported: ariki, ivi-atua, matatoa, and kio (Métraux 1940:129). However, the classification is a superficial one and the reality of clear-cut classes is in doubt." (p. 25)

7. "There appears to have been a class of people who were professional warriors (matatoa)." (p. 26)

8. "Serfs, or kio, defeated people who worked as slaves, were a fourth social class according to Métraux (1940:139)." (p. 26)

9. "Other important experts or craftsmen, not recognized by Métraux as a social class, were called maori or tahonga similar to the tohunga of other islands in Polynesia." (p. 26)

### C. Warfare and Conflict

10. "According to tradition warfare or feuding was mainly related to rivalry between the mata groups of the western and eastern ends of the island. Shifting alliances among these kin groups seemed to have been common." (p. 26)

11. "A very slight incident could cause large-scale conflict since the groups would most often be involved as units. Defeated groups were chased and killed or taken for slaves and many captives were eaten." (p. 27)

12. "The tremendous proliferation of spear points during this period indicates the severity of inter-mata warfare, often associated with attempts to tip over statues of other ramages." (p. 344)

## II. Ahu and Social Organization

### A. Ahu as Kin-Group Structures

13. "Land ownership is important because the associated social unit would be the one whose membership was involved in constructing and maintaining ahu on their land. Land use rights were related to use of agricultural plots but land ownership must also have been a prerequisite for ahu construction." (p. 329)

14. "Ahu would have maintained their importance for nonunilinear kin groups which were apparently increasing in size and in the ability to gather large labor forces for the construction of ahu like Tongariki, Hekii, and Mahatua in the eastern end of the island." (p. 339)

15. "The tribes included a number of lineages or subtribes (Métraux 1940:126), which were localized to a degree within the larger mata and were associated with specific ahu." (p. 25)

16. "Lineages held fairly large hereditary portions of land which were divided among households. Villages, probably consisting of the majority of the people of the lineage, were commonly located directly behind the ahu." (p. 25)

17. "Named levels of nonunilinear descent groups can be demonstrated for Easter Island from the descriptions of that culture at the time of contact." (p. 328)

### B. Ahu Construction and Labor

18. "The increased size of ahu constructed during this time, such as Ahu Hekii, Ahu Tongariki, Ahu Mahatua, reflect both the growing population and the power of leaders to make people work on what was basically an altar and offering to the deceased father of a new ariki and a symbol of his temporal power." (p. 337)

19. "Goldman suggested that the ability of kin groups to build such large ahu was based on the use of slaves, kio, defeated in battle. This being the case, it would appear that the priests also became increasingly involved in interpreting the outcome of forthcoming battles and preparing leaders for battle." (p. 337)

20. "A craft guild was responsible for carving them [the standardized statues]." (p. 338)

21. "Regional quarry areas are indicated for the early phases; apparently the red scoria statues of the Tahai region came from the quarry Heyerdahl described on Otuu hill and from nearby Punapau; early statues in other areas are of Rano Raraku tuff and, in some cases, of basalt from unknown quarries." (p. 336)

### C. Ahu Religious Function

22. "The significance of the statues erected on the rectangular stone platforms of Expansion Phase ahu is that of commemorating deceased ariki for whom the entire ahu was a votive offering." (p. 339)

23. "The ahu also served as the site for the investiture of a new ariki, and probably, as in other Polynesian marae, as a site for ritual marking other important events in the life of the ariki." (p. 339)

24. "Priests in the Settlement Phase were primarily heads of kin groups, lineage (ivi), ramage (mata), or tribe, who acted as religious specialists for the social unit." (p. 335)

25. "I consider the entire complex of central platform and court to be the equivalent of the marae found in other parts of Polynesia." (p. 43)

26. "There are strong indications of rituals associated with a sun cult in the solar orientation of early ahu." (p. 336)

27. Goldman "referred to a 'generalized sun cult' as characteristic of the Early Period (1970:107), when the ahu had 'a religious function unrelated to the use of statues' (1970:101)." (p. 336)

## III. Ahu Typology and Chronology

### A. Typological Classification

28. Four ahu types following Métraux 1937: (1) image ahu, largest with statues and cut stone; (2) semi-pyramidal ahu (ahu paepae), uncut stone, some built over ruined image ahu; (3) ahu poepoe (boat-shaped), fitted stone; (4) rectangular ahu avanga, simple platforms with burial vaults. (pp. 43-46)

29. "The image ahu consists of three main parts: 1) the 'central platform,' a rectangular shaped platform on which the statues stood, 2) an inclined ramp in front (landward side) of the central platform...3) a court or plaza on the landward side of the ahu." (p. 43)

30. Thomson "located 113 ahu around the coast, and found 555 statues, of these 238 were associated with ahu." (p. 41)

31. Routledge "located just under 100 of the image ahu and counted 231 statues associated with them (Routledge 1919:168)." (p. 42)

### B. Cultural Phase Sequence

32. Settlement and Developmental Phase (A.D. 400-1000): "Ahu during this time consisted of an open court and an elevated platform with dressed, upright slab walls and rubble fill. Statues may have appeared prior to A.D. 700." (p. 47)

33. Expansion or Ahu Moai Phase (A.D. 1000-1680): "Ahu increase in size, variety, and show a culmination of dressed stone architecture early in the phase, followed by increasing stress on larger, standardized images (moai) placed on the ahu platform. The quality of stone masonry shows some decline." (p. 47)

34. Decadent or Huri Moai Phase (A.D. 1680-1722): "No image ahu were constructed during this phase and most ahu built earlier were destroyed or allowed to fall into ruin. Semi-pyramidal ahu were built in place of the image ahu and these were used for mass burial." (p. 47)

35. "The latest actual radiocarbon date for construction of an image ahu is A.D. 1460 from Ahu Akivi." (p. 342)

36. "Neither the major East Polynesian gods, who at one time were of considerable importance, nor the old ancestral gods, whose place of worship was the ahu itself, would have played a major role in the religious substantiation or verification of the supremacy of the bird men during this [Decadent] phase." (p. 343)

## IV. Theoretical Framework

### A. Goldman's Classification Adopted

37. Ayres explicitly adopts Goldman's (1970) "Traditional Society" vs. "Open Society" framework. No use of "chiefdom" terminology anywhere in the dissertation. (passim)

38. "Goldman (1970) maintained that the ability to construct such large structures in the Expansion Phase (the beginnings of his 'Open Society') was possible through the use of slave labor, gained through military supremacy rather than the religious or sacred superiority of ariki who constructed ahu in the earlier phases, his 'Traditional Society.'" (p. 340)

39. Goldman "even referred to a 'generalized sun cult' as characteristic of the Early Period (1970:107)." (p. 336)

### B. Kinship Theory

40. Extensive discussion of nonunilinear descent groups drawing on Goodenough (1955), Murdock (1948, 1949), Gifford (1929), Firth (1936, 1957), Sahlins (1958), and Davenport (1959, 1969). (pp. 330-335)

41. "It seems clear then, that such a form of social organization, including kin units of varying size levels in relation to property use rights, was characteristic of the early Polynesian settlers." (p. 335)

42. Frake (1956) objected that Goodenough had not demonstrated nonunilinear descent groups widely enough for Proto-Malayo-Polynesian reconstruction, "since his examples were restricted mainly to Micronesia and Polynesia." (p. 333)

43. Lane (1961) hypothesized that "generation systems characteristic of Polynesia" resulted from "break downs of lineage systems" caused by "rapid and drastic depopulation." (p. 334)

### C. Polynesian Comparative Framework

44. "Easter Island's Relationship With Other Polynesian Islands": Ayres argues for Polynesian settlement from earliest occupation, contra Heyerdahl's South American origin hypothesis. Linguistic evidence (glottal preservation) indicates early isolation from East Polynesian. (pp. 326-328)

45. "The archaeological record points to cultural continuity from the time of initial settlement to historic times so that on this basis we may project the features of the Polynesian religious system back into the Settlement and Developmental phases of the island's occupation." (p. 328)

## V. Ethnographic Sources and Their Limits

### A. Source Dependencies [POST-RAID]

46. All ethnographic claims about Easter Island social organization derive from post-slave-raid sources: Métraux (1940, 1937), Thomson (1891), Routledge (1919), Roussel (1926), Englert (1938). No contact-era primary sources cited for social organization. (passim)

47. "The Peruvian slave raids, beginning in 1859 and culminating in 1862, resulted in the capture and abduction of nearly 1000 Easter Islanders for work on the guano fields off the coast of Peru (Métraux 1940:42-43). This depletion of the population and especially the loss of the captured ariki and maori or 'bards' resulted in a tremendous loss of knowledge and interest in the Polynesian culture, traditions and religious beliefs." (p. 22)

48. "Unfortunately very little knowledge of the native religion of Easter Island remained to be recorded in the protohistoric and historic periods." (p. 27)

49. "The role of priests in the Easter Island religious system is completely obscure. How closely they were organized into a priesthood is not known." (p. 28)

### B. Bird-Man Cult

50. "The person who found the first egg of the sooty tern for his chief placed that chief in the envied position of tangata manu or 'bird man' for one year. He was the virtual ruler of the entire island during this time." (p. 29)

51. Makemake "took over the features associated earlier with Tane, became the most important single god in conjunction with the temporal lords." (p. 343)

---

## VI. Settlement and Population

52. "According to Métraux, Easter Island was populated by 10 tribes or mata (1940:121)." (p. 24)

53. "These separate social groups were, fictitiously perhaps, all related by tracing their origin to the king, Hotu-matua (Métraux 1940:123)." (p. 25)

54. Population at time of Eyraud's arrival (1864) estimated at about 4000 by Zumbohm; Roussel census (1867-1870) showed 650. "The decline was due to slave raids, introduced diseases such as tuberculosis and smallpox, and famine." (pp. 22-23)

55. "Roussel put the maximum pre-contact population between 5000 and 6000 because the interior of the island was thought to be unoccupied (Roussel 1926:426)." (p. 23)

---

## VII. Archaeological Observations

56. "Nucleated settlements were fairly clearly delineated in this phase with the semi-circular spread of boat-shaped houses behind the ahu court." (p. 339)

57. "Small pits for depositing cremated bones represent a previously unrecognized feature of ahu in this phase. The five cremation pits that have been dated (A.D. 1440-90, 1380, 1220, 1210 and 1210) indicate a long use of this method." (p. 338)

58. "The process of tipping statues over, however, must have begun considerably before this time [Decadent Phase], possibly as early as the middle of the Expansion Phase when the Tahai and Ko te Riku statues were tipped over." (p. 342)

59. "The last great ariki of the traditional Miru line, Nga'ara, died shortly before the Peruvian slave raids in the 1860's, but by that time the ahu no longer functioned in any important way other than as a burial place." (p. 345)

60. Rongo-rongo "was apparently in full development at that late date and supports Emory's contention that it was a post-contact innovation, and one not closely related to the ahu, which were actually defunct from the time of the inception of the writing." (p. 345)

---

## VIII. Absences and Gaps

61. **No use of "chiefdom" terminology**: The word "chiefdom" does not appear in the dissertation. Ayres uses Goldman's (1970) "Traditional" / "Open" / "Stratified" framework instead. Service (1962) is not cited.

62. **No redistribution discussion**: No mention of redistribution, tribute, or centralized economic coordination as mechanisms for ahu construction or social organization.

63. **No contact-era primary sources for social organization**: All claims about political structure, social classes, and ahu function derive from post-slave-raid ethnographic sources.

64. **Social class reality questioned**: Ayres notes four reported classes but states "the reality of clear-cut classes is in doubt."

65. **Priestly organization unknown**: "The role of priests in the Easter Island religious system is completely obscure."

## Summary of Relevance to Chiefdom Attribution

Ayres 1973 is significant for the chiefdom attribution question because it represents the most detailed study of Easter Island ahu in cultural context BEFORE the Kirch 1984 chiefdom model was applied. Key findings:

1. **No chiefdom framework**: Ayres works entirely within Goldman's (1970) classification, which placed Easter Island as an "Open" society that "had never evolved a central authority." Service 1962 is not cited.

2. **Ahu as kin-group structures**: Ahu construction and maintenance are attributed to nonunilinear descent groups (mata, lineages) controlling land, not to chiefly command or redistribution.

3. **Ariki mau as spiritual, not political**: The king is explicitly described as spiritual, not a political ruler. Actual political control resided with warrior leaders (matatoa).

4. **No redistribution mechanism**: The dissertation contains no discussion of redistribution, tribute networks, or centralized economic coordination for ahu construction.

5. **Labor mobilization attributed to kin groups and slavery**: Large ahu construction attributed to growing kin groups and (following Goldman) to use of slave labor from military conquest, not to chiefly redistribution.

6. **Phase sequence shows decentralization, not centralization**: The trajectory moves from sacred/religious authority toward military/warrior control, the opposite of what a chiefdom consolidation model predicts.

7. **Pre-chiefdom baseline**: This is what Easter Island archaeology looked like before the chiefdom model was imposed. When Kirch (1984) later positioned Easter Island within his Polynesian chiefdom framework, he was reframing an archaeological record that the most detailed prior study had interpreted without chiefdom concepts.
