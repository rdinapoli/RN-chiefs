# Claims Extraction: Geiseler 1883

## Source Information

**Author**: Kapitanleutnant Geiseler, Imperial German Navy

**Period on Easter Island**: 19-23 September 1882 (four days). Geiseler commanded S.M. Kbt. "Hyane," dispatched by the Kaiserliche Admiralitat to investigate the island.

**Publication**: *Die Oster-Insel: Eine Statte prahistorischer Kultur in der Sudsee.* Berlin: Siegfried Mittler und Sohn, 1883. Report to the Kaiserliche Admiralitat.

**Language**: German. This claims extraction is based on the German original. An English translation exists (Ayres & Ayres 1995, *The Easter Island Statue [Moai]: A Monument Reexplored in Context*) but has not yet been consulted for this project. All translations below are working translations from OCR of Fraktur (Gothic) script and should be treated as approximate until verified against the Ayres translation.

**PDF file**: OCR of Google Books scan. Fraktur script produces highly variable OCR quality; German quotations in the extraction are approximate.

**Context**: Geiseler visited Easter Island approximately 19-20 years after the 1862-1863 Peruvian slave raids. The population had collapsed from roughly 3,000 to 150 souls (67 men, 39 women, 44 children), of whom approximately 20 were Tahitians brought by the Brander firm from Papeete (pp. 5, 19). Alexander P. Salmon, a half-Tahitian related to the Tahitian royal family who operated a livestock ranch at Mataveri, served as the primary translator and cultural intermediary for the visit. Salmon spoke Rapanui fluently (p. 24), arranged native performances, questioned informants on Geiseler's behalf, and provided his own interpretive commentary on origins and customs. The entire ethnographic content of the report is filtered through Salmon.

**[POST-RAID]**: ALL claims in this file derive from a source recorded approximately 20 years after the 1862-1863 slave raids. The population at the time of Geiseler's visit was 150, including approximately 20 Tahitians. The slave raids removed "the last king Maurata and his brother Kaimokoi" along with other leaders (p. 22). Geiseler directly observed that "a king does not exist" (p. 5) and that "every man is his own master" (pp. 22-23). His informants were survivors and descendants of survivors of a demographic catastrophe that had destroyed the island's political system.

**Critical intermediary note**: Salmon served as the primary translator and cultural broker for both Geiseler's visit (September 1882) and Thomson's visit (December 1886), four years later. Where Geiseler's reported traditions converge with Thomson's, this may reflect Salmon's consistent framing of island history rather than independent corroboration from different informants. Any claim that Geiseler and Thomson independently confirm each other must account for the fact that the same intermediary filtered both accounts. Salmon was himself related to the Tahitian royal family and familiar with Tahitian political models; his interpretive framework likely shaped how Rapanui traditions were presented to both European visitors.

## Analytical Significance

Geiseler's report is the earliest extended European account after the mission period (Roussel departed 1873) and precedes Thomson's visit by four years. It provides direct observation of conditions in 1882 combined with reported traditions obtained through Salmon. For the chiefdom attribution, the critical sections are: (1) the direct observation of complete political dissolution, with no king, no effective chiefs, and every man his own master (pp. 5, 22-23, 42); (2) the reported tradition of an earlier "almost despotic" regime of kings with chiefs as advisors (p. 42); (3) the succession rule that kingship passed to "the brother," not the son (p. 42), which contradicts both Thomson's primogeniture claim and Roussel's abdication-at-son's-marriage rule; (4) the description of the tangata manu / egg-fetching competition as a food-procurement activity with associated combat rather than a political succession mechanism (pp. 17-18); and (5) the observation that land ownership is individual/familial, hereditary, and fiercely maintained, with no mention of chiefly allocation (pp. 10, 42).

The report's most significant contribution to the chiefdom question is the direct, detailed observation of complete political collapse by 1882. Geiseler explicitly compares Rapanui unfavorably with other Pacific islands: "this complete dissolution of all political community on a hitherto so isolated island must appear remarkable, when the conditions on any other island opened to commerce for a century are considered" (p. 23). His observation that remaining "old chiefs" are "nothing less than chiefs" and show "nothing of the refined deportment of the old Samoan chiefs" (pp. 22-23) provides a contemporary Polynesian comparison that finds Rapanui political organization entirely absent, not merely diminished.

The king list comparison is analytically important. Thomson records 57 kings; Roussel records 22 generations (23 names); Geiseler reports that succession passed to "the brother" rather than the son (p. 42). These three sources, all recorded from the same small surviving population within a 17-year span (1869-1886), describe fundamentally different succession rules and produce irreconcilable king lists. This pattern is consistent with post-catastrophe reconstruction of lost political knowledge, not with the transmission of a stable institutional tradition.

## Claims Extraction

### 1. Political Authority

**1.1. No king exists** [POST-RAID]

"A king does not exist, since the last one died in the Peruvian slavery." (p. 5)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** This is Geiseler's baseline observation. There is no functioning kingship on the island. The statement is matter-of-fact and unqualified.

**1.2. The last king died in Peruvian slavery** [POST-RAID]

"The last King (Sep) of the island, who traced his descent from the first immigrant Ta'u-itu [Hotu Matu'a?], died in 1863 on the Chincha Islands, where he had been dragged with a portion of his subjects by Peruvian slavers for forced labor." (p. 1, introductory letter)

**(b) Reported tradition / historical narrative.** The last king's genealogical claim (descent from the first immigrant) is reported tradition. The death in Peruvian slavery is historical fact corroborated by multiple sources.

**1.3. Power and prestige of kings and chiefs entirely gone** [POST-RAID]

"Of the power and prestige of the kings and chiefs barely a century ago and even earlier, at the time of the manufacture and the cult of the old stone idols, nothing remains. The prestige has fallen still further and ceased entirely with the deportation of the last king Maurata [Kamakoi?] and his brother Kaimokoi to the Peruvian slavery, which coincided with the arrival and permanent settlement of the Europeans." (p. 22)

**(b) Reported tradition combined with (a) direct observation.** Geiseler observes the absence of chiefly power and attributes it to the slave raids and European settlement. The passage implicitly acknowledges that chiefly prestige was already declining before the slave raids ("fallen still further"), suggesting the raids accelerated rather than initiated the collapse. The reference to "barely a century ago" places the period of kingly power at roughly the late 18th century, the contact era.

**1.4. "Old chief" as a title without substance** [POST-RAID]

"Since that time every man is his own master, who recognizes no authority over himself and seeks his own justice. The chiefs who still remained on the island after the last king, who were already at that time very restricted in their power, lost this entirely and have today no advantage over any man. One says only that this or that person is an 'old chief' and that is that. These so-titled people appear to be nothing less than 'chiefs'; almost childish, they show for example nothing of the refined deportment of the old Samoan chiefs; they are not at all respected on account of their age, and when one hears talk of such a man, they say at most that he was formerly a good swimmer, to fetch sea-bird eggs from Motu Nui, or a great fighter and killed such-and-such a number of people." (pp. 22-23)

**(a) Direct observation (1882) combined with (b) reported tradition.** THIS IS ONE OF THE MOST ANALYTICALLY IMPORTANT PASSAGES IN GEISELER'S REPORT. The description of "old chiefs" as empty titles is direct observation. The comparison with Samoan chiefs reflects Geiseler's own Pacific experience and provides a contemporary cross-Polynesian evaluation that finds Rapanui chiefly authority entirely absent. The criteria by which surviving "old chiefs" are remembered (swimming ability for egg-fetching, warrior prowess) are personal achievements, not hereditary rank or administrative function. Note especially that egg-fetching from Motu Nui (the tangata manu competition) is cited as one of the two things an "old chief" might be remembered for, linking whatever residual prestige exists to competitive achievement rather than genealogical status.

**1.5. Complete dissolution of political community** [POST-RAID]

"This complete dissolution of all political community on a hitherto so isolated island must appear remarkable, when the conditions on any other island opened to commerce for a century are considered. On all of these we still find a certain political independence..." (p. 23)

**(a) Direct observation and comparative assessment.** Geiseler explicitly notes that Rapanui's political dissolution is exceptional even among contact-disrupted Pacific islands. Other islands retained some political structure; Rapanui had none. This observation complicates the argument that Rapanui had a robust chiefdom system that the slave raids merely disrupted: if the system had been deeply institutionalized, one might expect some structural residue, as on other islands.

**1.6. A Queen Moreto is unknown** [POST-RAID]

"A Queen Moreto, as Pinart names her in 1867, was not known; the only woman of this name, now dead, was a chief's wife [Hauptlingsfrau] and had no prestige, as women in this respect are generally not respected on Rapanui." (p. 22)

**(a) Direct observation and informant testimony.** This directly contradicts Pinart's 1877 identification of a queen. The correction indicates that European visitors could misidentify the political status of individuals they encountered.

**1.7. No political life since the last king was taken** [POST-RAID]

"A political life has not existed since the deportation of the last king, as every man is his own master, and a national consciousness seems to have been entirely lost." (p. 42)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** This reiterates the observation in 1.4 and 1.5. The phrase "national consciousness seems to have been entirely lost" goes beyond the absence of political structure to suggest the loss of collective identity.

**1.8. Earlier regime was "almost despotic"** [POST-RAID]

"In earlier times one distinguished an almost despotic regime of the kings toward the common people. After them the chiefs had the most prestige and formed the advisors in all cases. At death, after the burial of the old king, the dignity passed without further ado and without special ceremonies to the heir, the brother." (p. 42)

**(b) Reported tradition (via Salmon as intermediary).** This is the strongest claim about pre-raid political authority in Geiseler's report. The term "almost despotic" (fast despotisch) implies centralized, coercive authority. However, several cautions apply: (i) this comes through Salmon, who was familiar with Tahitian political models and may have projected them; (ii) the succession rule described here (to the brother, not the son) contradicts both Thomson's primogeniture claim (p. 527 of Thomson 1891) and Roussel's abdication-at-marriage rule (Roussel claims 2.2); (iii) the phrase "without special ceremonies" contradicts the elaborate succession practices described in some Polynesian chiefdom models. The reported description of chiefs as "advisors" (Rathgeber) rather than independent rulers is consistent with a centralized model but is uncorroborated.

**1.9. After the decline, organization based solely on the family** [POST-RAID]

"After the decline of political independence, the entire organization rests solely on the family. Each family lives for itself according to the arrangements of the family head, who is the highest person." (p. 42)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** Family-based social organization is what Geiseler actually observed. No supra-familial political authority existed. The "family head" (Familienoberhaupt) was the highest authority anyone recognized.

**1.10. Chiefs have no power to punish theft** [POST-RAID]

"Since no authority exists, and the chiefs also have no power, there remains for the one robbed only to seek justice for himself. [...] A chief would seek justice for himself in the same way, if something were stolen from him; but they cannot punish others for theft." (pp. 28-29)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** Chiefs have no judicial or coercive power. Self-help is the only recourse. This parallels Thomson's observation that "the people avenged their own injuries" (Thomson claims 1.6, p. 473), but Geiseler is more explicit: chiefs themselves must resort to self-help.

**1.11. Every family head has the right to declare war** [POST-RAID]

"Thus every family father has the right to declare war or feud and combat, as soon as he, his family, or kindred is insulted and receives no satisfaction." (p. 42)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) observation.** The right to declare war is vested in every family head, not in a chief or king. This describes a decentralized system in which the family, not the polity, is the unit of political action.

**1.12. The island was formerly divided into many districts** [POST-RAID]

"One speaks in Rapanui of a time in which the land was richly populated, divided into many individual districts, and every foot of ground was cultivated." (p. 23)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This is a general statement about former conditions. No details are given about who governed these districts or how they related to each other or to a central authority.

### 2. Social Structure

**2.1. Special class of idol-makers existed formerly** [POST-RAID]

"There existed at that time a special class among the inhabitants, the so-called idol-makers, who practiced this craft professionally and stood in high esteem, similar to the boat-builders on Tonga and Fiji. This consciousness, of being an idol-maker, or of descending from the family of one, has been inherited to this day, and a man was shown, of whom it was known that his great-grandfather had been an idol-maker." (pp. 13-14)

**(b) Reported tradition with (a) direct observation.** Geiseler directly observed a man identified as the descendant of an idol-maker. The comparison with Tongan and Fijian boat-builders is Geiseler's own analogy. The description is of a respected craft specialization, not of chiefly-commanded labor. The idol-makers "stood in high esteem" on their own account, like artisans, not as servants of a political authority. Compare Thomson's claim that "the image-makers were exempt from all other kinds of work, and the fishermen were taxed for their chief support" (Thomson claims 5.3, p. 533), which implies a different arrangement; Geiseler's description does not mention exemption from other work or taxation to support them.

**2.2. No special occupational classes remain** [POST-RAID]

"Special occupational classes, as in earlier times the idol-makers, fishermen etc., are no longer distinguished, since the Rapanui people, without any political cohesion, have disintegrated within themselves, so to speak only vegetate and do only what is strictly necessary for sustenance." (p. 19)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** Occupational specialization has disappeared along with political organization. The description of the population as having "disintegrated" and merely "vegetating" reflects both direct observation of social dissolution and a colonial evaluative framework.

**2.3. Marriage restricted by rank; chiefs arranged marriages** [POST-RAID]

"The right to enter into a marriage was formerly very restricted and is still today to a certain extent. Particularly among the chiefs' families, permission for the children always depended on the chief, without the son or daughter having been asked. Much importance was placed on connections of equal rank, and so-called mesalliances are said to have occurred only when marriages befitting one's station could no longer be made due to the shortage of women." (pp. 29-30)

**(b) Reported tradition.** This describes rank-endogamy and chiefly control of marriage among elite families. The qualifier "is still today to a certain extent" suggests Geiseler observed some residual concern with rank in marriage. The acknowledgment that mesalliances occurred when women were scarce indicates the system was flexible under demographic pressure.

**2.4. Common people observed the same marriage rules** [POST-RAID]

"The lower people observed in general the same rules as the chiefs, i.e. marriage took place in earliest youth; however there were many cases where young girls remained unmarried and served the common good, since they were highly regarded in this position." (p. 30)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Marriage rules were reportedly similar across ranks. The institution of unmarried women "serving the common good" and being "highly regarded" for it is not further explained.

**2.5. Punishment for adultery varied by rank** [POST-RAID]

"If adultery was discovered, the punishment differed according to rank; among chiefs, the wife was usually killed by the husband or his brother; in less serious cases she had to endure the most severe beatings or was expelled from the house... Among the common people, the rod punishment was usually applied." (p. 30)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Differential punishment by rank is consistent with a stratified society. However, the punishment is administered within the family (by the husband or his brother), not by a chiefly authority or judicial system.

**2.6. Persons claiming old chief descent carry authority staffs** [POST-RAID]

"Persons who can boast of descending from an old chief's family carry on these occasions [festivals] also the so-called authority-tokens [Hoheitszeichen] wrapped in bast, up to 6-foot-high staffs, topped with a carved double-faced head; however these are quite rare." (p. 32)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** Geiseler observed the use of authority staffs at festivals. The staffs are markers of genealogical descent from chiefs, not of current political authority. Their rarity is noted. Compare with 1.3 (in section 1c of the extraction): "The chiefs use their authority-staffs as clubs" (pp. 5-6), where the staffs have been repurposed as weapons, suggesting the ceremonial function has been emptied of meaning.

### 3. Tangata Manu / Bird-Egg Ceremony

**3.1. Make-Make represented by sea-bird eggs; egg-fetching from Motu Nui** [POST-RAID]

"Besides the god for fruits of the land, one worshipped a chief god, called Make-Make, who had power over everything. This god was represented by the eggs of the sea-birds, which laid them on the small island, a few hundred meters southwest of Rana Kao, called Motu Nui, at all seasons. They are strictly tabu in all months of the year, excepting the months of July, August and September, during which time alone they may be fetched." (pp. 17-18)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The eggs are described as representing the supreme deity Make-Make and are subject to strict seasonal tabu. The religious framing places the egg-fetching within a ritual system rather than a political succession mechanism.

**3.2. The strongest men gathered to fetch eggs; competition and combat** [POST-RAID]

"Before the introduction of European provisions, the eggs of the sea-birds formed a very sought-after food, at many times even a staple food, and therefore the strongest and most capable people gathered at the southwest slope of Rana Kao, from where they climbed down the steep, approximately 300-400 m high rock wall, swam through the sea, and fetched the eggs from the named rock." (p. 18)

"With the combativeness that formerly dwelt in them... there frequently came to fights between the more or less fortunate swimmers, whereby one sought to protect oneself at night from ambushes through those narrow entrances [of the stone houses]." (p. 18)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Geiseler describes the egg-fetching as primarily a food-procurement activity that attracted competition and violence. This is notably different from Thomson's and Roussel's descriptions of the tangata manu as a ritual conferring authority or prestige. In Geiseler's version, the emphasis is on the practical importance of the eggs as food, the physical prowess required to obtain them, and the fighting that resulted. There is no mention of political authority being conferred on the successful egg-finder. The connection to "old chief" status in 1.4 above (where an old chief might be remembered as "a good swimmer, to fetch sea-bird eggs from Motu Nui") suggests the activity conferred personal prestige through demonstrated physical ability, not institutional authority.

**3.3. Special club for defense during egg-season** [POST-RAID]

"For defense in these cases they even had a special club in the form of a rake, split at the front, which was quickly thrust out of the entrance upon the enemy's approach." (p. 18)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The existence of a specialized weapon for the egg-season suggests the competition was serious and violent. This is consistent with a competitive, potentially dangerous ritual, not an orderly political succession ceremony.

### 4. Property and Land

**4.1. Every place has its owner** [POST-RAID]

"Here too every place and every old site has its owner, without whose permission nothing damaging to the place may be undertaken." (p. 10)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** Individual or familial ownership of land and sites was actively maintained in 1882. Geiseler experienced this directly when a native controlled access to an archaeological site on his property. Ownership was enforced without chiefly mediation.

**4.2. Every piece of land has a special name and owner** [POST-RAID]

"On the entire island every piece of land has its special name and its owner. They hold fast very much to this land ownership and inherit it from family to family." (p. 42)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** Named, individually owned parcels inherited within families. No mention of chiefly allocation, royal land, or tribute obligations attached to land tenure. This is the most direct description of Rapanui land tenure in any 19th-century source and describes a system of family-based hereditary ownership.

### 5. Religion and Priests

**5.1. No priests; kings formerly served as priests** [POST-RAID]

"They have no priests and are said never to have had them. In earlier times, when the kings still had considerable power and prestige, these [kings] served as priests, who led and directed the religious festivities." (p. 33)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The claim that kings served as priests combines religious and political authority in the ariki figure, consistent with Polynesian concepts of sacred chieftainship. The assertion that there were "never" separate priests contradicts the ivi atua category recorded by Roussel (Roussel claims 3.1). The claim that the king "led and directed religious festivities" is uncorroborated and may reflect Salmon's projection of Tahitian models where chiefly and priestly roles were combined.

**5.2. Make-Make as supreme god** [POST-RAID]

"Make-Make made everything: earth, sun, moon, and stars; beyond that one seems to know nothing." (p. 33)

**(b) Reported tradition.** A creator deity with universal power. The acknowledgment that "one seems to know nothing" beyond this basic claim suggests the loss of detailed religious knowledge by 1882.

**5.3. Two chief gods: Make-Make and Haua** [POST-RAID]

"Their chief gods are Make-Make, the progenitor, and Haua. To these two deities they sacrifice the first of all products of the land." (p. 31)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The sacrifice of first fruits to the gods. No mention of first fruits going to the king or chief. Compare Roussel's description of first fruits taken "to the Chief" (Roussel claims 6.5, p. 48): Geiseler attributes the first-fruits obligation to the gods, not to a political leader.

**5.4. Stone idols served as intermediary gods** [POST-RAID]

"All the stone idols of the island thus served in earlier times as subordinate gods, through which the chief god was worshipped." (p. 33)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The moai are described as religious intermediaries, not as representations of chiefs or ancestors. Compare Thomson's claim that moai were "effigies of chiefs or distinguished persons" (Thomson claims 7.4, p. 508): Geiseler's informants (through Salmon) described them differently, as mediators of worship rather than portraits of leaders.

**5.5. Worship always took place outdoors; no temples** [POST-RAID]

"Worship always took place under open sky, formerly before the standing stone idols, now at the chief festivals." (p. 33)

**(b) Reported tradition / (a) observation.** No temples or dedicated religious structures. Worship was conducted at moai and at festivals. The absence of temples is consistent with some Polynesian patterns but distinct from the marae complexes of other eastern Polynesian societies.

**5.6. Make-Make kills and eats the souls of the wicked** [POST-RAID]

"Make-Make is at the same time the most feared god; he kills and eats the souls of the dead, if they did evil in life, and protects the souls of the good, who under his care are allowed to sleep on the island after death." (p. 31)

**(b) Reported tradition.** An afterlife theology with moral judgment. The souls of the good remain on the island; the souls of the wicked are consumed. This is not directly relevant to political authority but indicates a religious system with ethical content beyond simple ancestor worship.

### 6. Traditions and Origin Stories

**6.1. Divided traditions about origin** [POST-RAID]

"Regarding the origin of the Rapanui people, who also call their land Te Pito [te henua], the views are divided. So it is said that they came in a large boat in early times from Rapo, also called Oparo [Rapa], an island of the Tuamotu [Austral] group, and are said to have landed at Vinapu in the southeast of Rana Kao." (p. 43)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Multiple competing origin traditions existed in 1882. The lack of consensus is itself significant: a single authoritative origin tradition would be expected in a centralized polity with controlled genealogical knowledge.

**6.2. Salmon reports alternative tradition from Galapagos** [POST-RAID]

"Herr Salmon, asked about this point, remarked that among the natives there also exists a tradition, according to which they came in a large boat from one of the Galapagos Islands with the trade-wind and landed at Anakena in the north, but did not conceal that this tradition too stands in contradiction with the opinions of other natives, who hold to an immigration from the west." (p. 43)

**(b) Reported tradition (via Salmon).** Three distinct origin traditions are identified: from Rapa/Oparo, from the Galapagos, and from the west. Salmon functions as curator and organizer of these traditions, deciding which to present and in what order. His willingness to acknowledge contradictions is notable but also reveals his role as active interpreter rather than neutral translator.

**6.3. Salmon's own view: Polynesian origin from Tuamotus** [POST-RAID]

"Herr Salmon himself, a Tahitian, who is thoroughly familiar with the customs and habits of the Polynesians, expressed the view that in his conviction and knowledge it was possible and even more than probable that the first Rapanui people came over from the Tuamotu Islands." (pp. 43-44)

**(c) Salmon's interpretive opinion.** This is explicitly labeled as Salmon's own view, not native tradition. It reveals Salmon's willingness to offer his own interpretive conclusions alongside native accounts. If Salmon was this forthcoming with his own opinions about origins, his views likely shaped the presentation of political and social traditions as well.

**6.4. Age of last idols estimated at about 350 years** [POST-RAID]

"The time of manufacture of the last idols the natives trace back to approximately 350 years." (p. 14)

**(b) Reported tradition.** If calculated from 1882, this places the end of moai production at approximately 1530, which is roughly consistent with some archaeological estimates but based solely on oral report.

**6.5. Last idols on the west side of Rano Raraku estimated at 250 years** [POST-RAID]

"[The age of the last idols on the west side is] estimated by the oldest present-day natives at approximately 250 years [before 1882, i.e. ca. 1630]." (p. 44)

**(b) Reported tradition.** A different estimate from different informants for a different set of moai. The discrepancy between 350 and 250 years illustrates the imprecision of oral chronology.

### 7. Warfare

**7.1. Blood revenge was the norm** [POST-RAID]

"The same [blood revenge] occurred more frequently in earlier times, now less often and even rarely. It was always demanded for a slain relative." (p. 28)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Blood revenge is described as a familial obligation, not a state function. The decline by 1882 may reflect reduced population and/or European influence.

**7.2. Challenge ritual with singing and noise-making** [POST-RAID]

"The one so charged challenged his opponent publicly, by placing a rough wreath made of horsehair on his head and making noise with broad wood and bone plates struck together under continuous singing of songs of insult and revenge." (p. 28)

**(b) Reported tradition.** A formalized challenge ritual. The mention of "horsehair" indicates this tradition incorporates post-contact elements (horses were introduced by Europeans), suggesting the practice was modified or the description conflates different periods.

**7.3. Cannibalism of war captives** [POST-RAID]

"The oldest people still remember this and know that war captives were consumed. They were, when captured in greater numbers, brought to the dwellings specially built for this purpose before the old stone idols and held captive until the victory celebration and provided with food. On the occasion of these festivities they were killed in honor of the gods and consumed." (pp. 30-31)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Cannibalism of war captives is described as ceremonial (in honor of the gods) and linked to stone idol worship. The captives were held in special dwellings before the moai. No chiefly role in organizing or directing this practice is mentioned.

**7.4. Women and girls captured in war given to unmarried warriors** [POST-RAID]

"Women and girls captured on such raids, however, were not killed, but given to young unmarried warriors, which in turn may have its cause in the small number of available women." (p. 31)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Captured women were distributed to warriors, not to chiefs. The distribution is described as going to "young unmarried warriors," suggesting military merit rather than political rank determined access to captured women.

**7.5. Skulls of high-ranking enemy warriors were burned** [POST-RAID]

"Slain warriors of high rank, who fell into the enemies' hands in war, were dishonored by burning their skulls, as if to burn away the soul." (p. 31)

**(b) Reported tradition.** The burning of enemy skulls as ritual dishonor implies recognition of rank among warriors. The practice targets "high rank" warriors specifically, indicating social differentiation based on military distinction.

### 8. Significant Absences

**8.1. No mention of tribute, redistribution, or taxation.** Geiseler's account contains no reference to any system of tribute, tax, or redistribution. First fruits are offered to the gods (5.3), not to political leaders. Land is owned by families (4.2), not allocated by chiefs. This absence is significant because tribute/redistribution is a defining feature of chiefdom models. Compare Thomson's internal contradiction: "no tax was exacted of the people" (Thomson claims 1.5) alongside the claim that fishermen were "taxed" to support image-makers (Thomson claims 5.3).

**8.2. No mention of the ariki exercising political authority.** The term "ariki" appears only once in Geiseler's report, in reference to a deceased person buried in a special tomb (p. 10). It is not recorded as a title for a living political leader. The word is used to identify the buried dead, consistent with the ariki as a sacred/ancestral category rather than a political office.

**8.3. No succession ceremony described.** Succession passed "without further ado and without special ceremonies" to the brother (1.8, p. 42). The absence of investiture ceremonies is inconsistent with Polynesian chiefdom models, where elaborate installation rituals typically mark the transfer of chiefly authority.

**8.4. No tangata manu as political succession mechanism.** Unlike Roussel (who describes the birdman as acquiring "the chief's authority" for one year; Roussel claims 4.4) and Thomson (who describes an annual ceremony with the king presiding; Thomson claims 3.2), Geiseler's account describes egg-fetching as a competitive food-procurement activity with associated combat (3.2). No political authority is said to be conferred on the successful swimmer.

**8.5. No mention of the ariki's role in agriculture, fishing, or resource management.** There is no description of the king directing economic activities, allocating resources, or mediating access to land or marine resources. The only economic role attributed to political authority is the former chiefs' control over marriage arrangements (2.3).

### 9. European Categories Imposed

**9.1. "Almost despotic" regime.** The term "fast despotisch" (p. 42) is a European political category applied to the reported pre-raid system. There is no Rapanui equivalent provided. The term may reflect Salmon's description (he would have been familiar with European political categories through his Tahitian royal connections) or Geiseler's interpretation of what was described.

**9.2. "King" (Konig) and "chief" (Hauptling).** Geiseler uses the German Konig for the paramount and Hauptling for subordinate leaders, imposing a hierarchical distinction. He also uses Furst (prince) in the burial context (3b in the extraction: "only chiefs and princes are buried in such tombs," p. 11), introducing a third-tier European rank category with no attested Rapanui equivalent.

**9.3. Samoan comparison as evaluative framework.** Geiseler's explicit comparison of Rapanui "old chiefs" with "the old Samoan chiefs" (pp. 22-23) applies a Polynesian standard from a different society. The comparison is useful because it comes from a contemporary observer with Pacific experience, but it also reveals Geiseler's assumption that Polynesian chieftainship should look a certain way, with Rapanui measured against a Samoan standard.

### 10. Material Culture Relevant to Political Authority

**10.1. Authority staffs (Hoheitszeichen)** [POST-RAID]

"The chiefs use their authority-staffs [Hoheitszeichen] as clubs." (pp. 5-6)

At festivals: "Persons who can boast of descending from an old chief's family carry on these occasions also the so-called authority-tokens wrapped in bast, up to 6-foot-high staffs, topped with a carved double-faced head; however these are quite rare." (p. 32)

Inventory: "Nr. 2. Authority-staffs of the chiefs. Carried on special occasions. Very rare. Long staffs, topped with carved head on both sides, as well as eyes of bone and obsidian. 2 specimens." (p. 48)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** The staffs exist as objects. Their daily use as clubs and their ceremonial use at festivals are both directly observed. The objects have survived while the authority they symbolized has not. The rarity is noted multiple times.

**10.2. Breast-shield with script characters from royal family** [POST-RAID]

"The same [breast-shield] came from the royal family that died on the Chincha Islands and had been inherited in this [family] since earliest times." (p. 39)

**(b) Reported provenance (via Salmon).** An inscribed breast-shield attributed to the extinct royal family. The provenance claim comes through Salmon and cannot be independently verified.

**10.3. Feather hats and distinction markers** [POST-RAID]

"Formerly, at the time of the old and last kings, there were authority-staffs, feather-hats [Federmutzen] with greater ornamentation, and the already-mentioned bast-cloth mantles of especially fine workmanship." (p. 39)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Status markers described as belonging to "earlier times." Compare Thomson's mention of feather hats as insignia of chiefs (Thomson claims 1.4, p. 472).

**10.4. Special rank insignia no longer in use** [POST-RAID]

"Special insignia, which are said to attest to high rank or birth, one no longer has now." (p. 39)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** The material markers of rank have disappeared along with the rank system itself.

**10.5. Script tablets known only to kings and chiefs** [POST-RAID]

"This script-language was known only to the former kings and the chiefs; the common people understood it neither to write nor to read." (p. 24)

**(b) Reported tradition (via Salmon and "old chief").** Restricted literacy as a marker of elite status. This parallels Thomson's claim (Thomson claims 5.4, p. 514). Both claims derive from the same intermediary (Salmon) and the same small surviving population, so they do not constitute independent confirmation.

**10.6. Tablets used for genealogies and communication between chiefs** [POST-RAID]

"The application [of the script] occurred only in two cases: first, to transmit short notes on important matters to chiefs of other villages, and then in the form of the well-known wooden tablets, to record genealogies." (p. 24)

**(b) Reported tradition (via Salmon).** Two functions are attributed to the script: inter-village communication between chiefs and genealogical record-keeping. Both functions presuppose an elite that could read and write. However, by 1882, no living person could actually demonstrate this ability (compare Thomson claims 7.1 on Ure Vaeiko's inability to read the tablets).

### 11. Tabu

**11.1. Tabu system still strong** [POST-RAID]

"The natives in general hold their tabus very strictly, and it is said to occur only in the rarest cases that it is violated. On the strict observance and also on the imposition of certain tabus for the dead rests a great part of mutual respect; the violation can only be avenged with blood." (p. 28)

**(a) Direct observation and (b) reported tradition (1882).** The tabu system survived the political collapse. Enforcement is through blood vengeance (self-help), not through chiefly authority. This is consistent with tabu as a cultural/religious institution independent of political hierarchy.

**11.2. Burial places (ahu) are tabu** [POST-RAID]

"Furthermore all the gods are Tabu, above all the old stone idols, then the burial places (ahu)." (p. 28)

**(a) Direct observation (1882).** Geiseler records "ahu" as the native term for burial places/platforms and notes their tabu status. The tabu on ahu is maintained without chiefly enforcement.

---

### 12. Burial and Rank

**12.1. Only chiefs and kings buried in special stone tombs** [POST-RAID]

"Only chiefs and princes are buried in such tombs, while the common people are placed under the open platforms and here have enough free air to escape from the god Make-Make, as soon as he wants to seize a soul." (p. 11)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Differential burial by rank. The distinction between elite stone tombs and commoner burial under open platforms indicates recognized social stratification. However, differential burial practices are consistent with both chiefdom and non-chiefdom systems; they demonstrate social differentiation but not necessarily centralized political authority.

**12.2. Deceased persons of higher rank placed in specially built stone houses** [POST-RAID]

"Deceased persons of higher rank were formerly placed in a specially built stone house with two round holes for the escape of the souls." (p. 31)

**(b) Reported tradition.** Reiterates 12.1 with additional detail about the two holes for soul escape (linked to the Make-Make belief in 5.6).

## Cross-Reference: King List and Succession Rule Comparison

| Feature | Roussel 1869 | Geiseler 1882 (via Salmon) | Thomson 1886 (via Salmon) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Number of kings | 22 generations (23 names) | Not enumerated | 57 kings |
| Succession rule | Abdication when son marries | Passes to the brother | Eldest son nominated |
| Last king | Tepito, then Gregorio (child) | Maurata (taken by slavers) | Maurata (taken by slavers) |
| Intermediary | Missionary informants (3+ years' contact) | Salmon (4-day visit) | Salmon (12-day visit) |

Key discrepancies: The three sources, all recorded from the same small surviving population within a 17-year span (1869-1886), describe three different succession rules: (a) abdication when the son marries (Roussel), (b) succession to the brother (Geiseler via Salmon), and (c) nomination of the eldest son (Thomson via Salmon). These are mutually incompatible. The fact that both Geiseler and Thomson obtained their information through Salmon, yet Salmon reported different succession rules four years apart, demonstrates either that Salmon's own understanding evolved, that he provided different accounts to different visitors, or that no stable institutional memory of the succession rule existed by the 1880s. In any case, the king lists and succession rules cannot be treated as reliable evidence for a stable hereditary political system.
