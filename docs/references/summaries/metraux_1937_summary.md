# Source Summary: Métraux 1937

## Overview

Alfred Métraux visited Easter Island in 1934 as part of the Franco-Belgian expedition, producing several publications on Rapanui culture. "The Kings of Easter Island" (*Journal of the Polynesian Society*, Vol. 46, No. 2, June 1937, pp. 41-62) is a focused article on kingship, distinct from and preceding his 1940 *Ethnology of Easter Island* monograph. Métraux was a trained Polynesian ethnographer with comparative knowledge of Mangareva, the Society Islands, and the Tuamotus. His principal informants included Juan Tepano (Tupahotu, ~60, who had served as Routledge's interpreter in 1914-1915) and Victoria Rapahango (Miru descent-group). He also drew extensively on the recently published notes of Father Roussel (Annales des Sacrés-Coeurs, 1926), which he was the first researcher to analyze systematically.

## Sources Consulted

- Métraux 1937, "The Kings of Easter Island," *Journal of the Polynesian Society* 46(2):41-62. (PDF: `docs/references/pdfs/post_slave_raid/metraux_1937/Metraux_1937.pdf`)
- The article engages directly with Roussel (1926 Annales text), Routledge (1919), Thompson (1891), Geiseler (1883), Williamson (1924), and several contact-era sources.

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**The ariki-mau had no political power.** Métraux's central conclusion: "Nothing is known about the activities and duties of the king to suggest any great political power. He was not a ruler in the common sense of the word" (p. 58). His final summary: "His political power was little" (p. 61). His most extended formulation: "The *ariki-mau* was respected because of his pedigree which related him with the gods and because of the sacredness of his person, but he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief. He transmitted the *mana* of the god, and it was necessary for the welfare of the island to keep him, but beyond this usefulness he was not allowed to enjoy any real power other than this more or less strictly religious and aristocratic prestige" (p. 60).

**Independent confirmation of Routledge's conclusion.** Métraux endorses Routledge's characterization of the ariki-mau as "neither a leader in war nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest" (p. 58, citing Routledge p. 242). This is the second trained ethnographer, working 20 years after Routledge with partly overlapping but also additional informants, to reach the same conclusion about the absence of political authority.

**More prerogatives described, same conclusion.** Métraux describes more chiefly functions than Routledge: first-fruits ceremony (Roussel eyewitness), harvest tapu, tuna distribution to tangata-honui, tūra/haka-pāpa servant classes, tattooing inspection, twin-naming, and a full fertility chant retranslated from Thompson. Despite this richer picture of the ariki-mau's role, Métraux reaches the same assessment of no political power. The additional functions are all supernatural, ceremonial, or supervisory over religious activities, not political command.

**Critical source assessment.** Métraux evaluates his predecessors with unusual directness:
- Roussel "best understood the true nature of kingship" (p. 43), but his claim of ancient absolute power is "an entirely gratuitous assumption" (p. 58)
- Geiseler is "not a very reliable source" and "certainly mistaken" about brother succession (pp. 52, 58)
- Gana's report should be discarded as "a badly misinterpreted compilation" of Roussel (p. 59)
- Thomson's "arbitrary monarchy" passage actually supports limited power when read carefully, since Thomson himself notes no tax, no great homage, and independent clans (p. 59)

**Food flowing through the king: elite-to-elite, not downward redistribution.** Métraux adds three new pieces of evidence for food flowing through the ariki-mau:
1. Vaka-vaero tuna presented to king, who distributed to tangata-honui (important old men) (p. 50)
2. First tuna after tapu-lifting tasted by king, rest given to tangata-honui (p. 50)
3. First-fruits ceremony with harvest tapu controlled by king (Roussel eyewitness, pp. 54-55)

The pattern is consistent: food flows fishermen/farmers → king → tangata-honui (elite old men) or rongorongo men (at the annual assembly). This is elite-to-elite circulation through the sacred office, not the downward redistribution to the general population that chiefdom models require. The first-fruits/harvest tapu operates through supernatural sanction ("Alas for the man who dared to violate the *tapu*"), not political taxation.

**Servant classes were themselves ariki.** The tūra (providers/fishers) and haka-pāpa (valets) who attended the king were "noblemen (*ariki*)" (p. 49). This describes a sacred court or retinue of high-status attendants, not a commoner labor force under chiefly command.

**The ariki-mau's function was supernatural, not political.** The fertility chant (pp. 52-54) enumerates what the king "makes fertile": sweet potatoes, fish, ferns, turtles, stars, sun, moon, chiefs, earwigs. The king's connection to nature was total and supernatural. Food plants "vanished from the island with the kings who controlled them" (p. 54). Ariki-paka skulls made fowls lay eggs (p. 54). This is a sacred mediator between gods and nature, not a political administrator.

**Tangata manu was not a "secular king."** Métraux rejects Williamson's hypothesis of a dual kingship with the tangata-manu as secular counterpart. He characterizes the tangata-manu as "a man incarnating provisionally the god Makemake and entitled to rights of an aggressive nature" (p. 60), based on Thompson, Routledge, and Roussel.

## Context and Limitations

**[POST-RAID]** Métraux visited 72 years after the slave raids. The population had recovered to approximately 450-500 from a nadir of ~111 in 1877. All claims about pre-contact political organization are reported traditions transmitted through the demographic catastrophe.

**Tepano as shared informant.** Juan Tepano served as interpreter for both Routledge (1914-1915) and Métraux (1934). The convergence between the two accounts may partly reflect Tepano's consistent narrative rather than fully independent confirmation. By 1934, Tepano was approximately 60 years old. However, Métraux also worked with Victoria Rapahango and other informants, and independently analyzed Roussel's published texts. The convergence between Métraux and Routledge is therefore partially but not entirely attributable to their shared reliance on Tepano.

**Métraux's Polynesian comparative framework.** Métraux interpreted Rapanui kingship through a Polynesian comparative lens, connecting the Honga lineage to Tangaroa and Rongo, noting parallels with Society Islands and Tuamotu chiefly genealogies, and situating the ariki-mau within a pan-Polynesian pattern of divine chieftainship. This framework is productive for understanding the supernatural dimensions of the office but risks importing structural assumptions from other Polynesian societies.

**"King" terminology.** Métraux uses "king" and "kingship" throughout the article while arguing the ariki-mau had no political power. This terminological tension is itself significant: European political categories are imposed on an institution Métraux's own analysis shows was not political. The title "The Kings of Easter Island" frames the ariki-mau in a way Métraux's conclusions contradict.

**Roussel's 1926 Annales text.** Métraux was the first researcher to systematically analyze Roussel's notes, published in *Annales des Sacrés-Coeurs* in 1926, "not hitherto utilized" (p. 43). His access to this new textual source gives him material Routledge did not have.

**Métraux's caution about his own evidence.** He acknowledges "information about the powers of the *ariki*, his functions, and his authority is extremely deficient" (p. 43), and that the changes-of-residence tradition he "was unable to check" (p. 51).

## Relevance to the Paper

Métraux 1937 is critical for the chiefdom attribution argument for five reasons:

1. **It provides independent confirmation of Routledge's conclusion about the ariki-mau's lack of political power.** A second trained ethnographer, working 20 years later with a different (though partly overlapping) informant base and with access to Roussel's recently published texts, independently concludes "he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (p. 60). The convergence between Routledge and Métraux strengthens both accounts.

2. **It demonstrates that more evidence does not produce a chiefdom.** Métraux describes more chiefly prerogatives than Routledge (first-fruits, harvest tapus, tūra/haka-pāpa servants, tuna distribution, tattooing inspection, twin-naming), yet reaches the same conclusion about no political power. This refutes the potential objection that the limited-power conclusion reflects absence of evidence. The argument is based on the character of the evidence: supernatural and ceremonial functions, not political command.

3. **It directly labels Roussel's "absolute power" claim as an "entirely gratuitous assumption."** This is a trained Polynesian ethnographer making a methodological judgment about the very source most often cited for chiefly authority. Métraux accepts Roussel's observation that authority was "nil" in historical times but rejects the extrapolation backward to ancient absolute power as unsupported. This anticipates and supports our argument about the five-step circularity: the projection of power into the past has no evidential basis.

4. **It complicates but does not support the redistribution claim.** The tuna/tangata-honui and first-fruits evidence represents food flowing through the king, but the pattern is elite-to-elite circulation (king → tangata-honui) and ceremonial mobilization (rongorongo assembly), not the systematic downward redistribution that chiefdom models predict. The harvest tapu operates through supernatural sanction, not political taxation. The paper must acknowledge this evidence while analyzing the redistribution pattern precisely.

5. **It provides the strongest source assessment of any post-raid ethnographer.** Métraux's direct evaluations of Roussel, Geiseler, Gana, and Thomson, and his engagement with Thomson's "arbitrary monarchy" passage (reading Thomson's own qualifications as evidence against the very characterization Thomson provides), anticipate our argument about the internal contradictions in the chiefdom-supporting sources.
