## Overview

This 9-page chapter is the published version of Stevenson's 1984 Penn State dissertation (377 pages), condensed and placed within Kirch's programmatic edited volume on evolutionary approaches to island societies. The chapter presents the same southern coastal settlement data (57 platform ahu, 24 burial structures), the same five-type ahu classification, the same 13-phase chronological framework, and the same finding of four politically autonomous groups. It retains the corporate descent group analytical framework, the Ibo ethnographic analogue, and the Goldman dual status system.

The chapter does NOT use the term "chiefdom." Service 1962 is not cited. Kirch 1984 is not cited, despite the chapter appearing in Kirch's own volume. In these respects, the 1986 published chapter is continuous with the 1984 dissertation.

However, the 1984-to-1986 transition involves four categories of change that, taken together, represent the beginning of interpretive drift: (1) the theoretical infrastructure that positioned the corporate group analysis as an alternative to neoevolutionary typology is removed; (2) the strongest anti-chiefdom qualifications are dropped; (3) the finding that the system reverted to non-ranked structure is reframed as corporate solidarity being "weakened"; and (4) the environmental deterioration narrative is strengthened with new palynological evidence.

## The 1984-to-1986 Transition

This is the central analytical contribution of processing Stevenson 1986. The transition is more subtle than a simple terminological adoption of "chiefdom." It operates through decontextualization, selective attrition, and editorial reframing.

### What changed

**1. Theoretical infrastructure removed.** The 1984 dissertation's intellectual genealogy ran through British social anthropology (Fortes 1953/1959), Polynesian kinship theory (Firth 1936/1957), the conical clan concept (Kirchhoff 1955), and corporate group archaeology (Hayden & Cannon 1982). These constituted an explicit theoretical alternative to the Sahlins-Service-Kirch neoevolutionary tradition. In 1986, every one of these citations is absent. What remains is the method (identify corporate groups through ritual architecture, using Ibo analogy) without the theory that positioned it as an alternative framework. The corporate descent group analysis, stripped of its theoretical grounding, appears as a technique compatible with the chiefdom framework rather than as a rival intellectual tradition.

**2. Strongest qualifications dropped.** Of the 11 explicit hedging statements in the 1984 dissertation, the following are absent from 1986:
- "not assumed a priori that the prehistoric social system... is headed full tilt on a course of increased social complexity" (1984, p. 50)
- "The three models... are not presented as an evolutionary trajectory" (1984, p. 50)
- "tentative in nature and require verification through future research" (1984, p. 20)
- "preliminary model which needs to be evaluated by additional research" (1984, p. 181)
- "it will not be possible to draw boundaries between political units" (1984, p. 162)
- "The use of an arbitrary criteria for establishing political divisions... is probably misleading" (1984, p. 162)

The dropped items are not random: they are the methodological commitments (rejection of evolutionary trajectory, models as alternatives not stages, boundaries as indeterminate) and the calls for verification. What survives is detail-level hedging ("preliminary," "hypothesized," "tentative") that softens specific claims without challenging the overall interpretive direction.

**3. Reversion reframed as weakening.** The 1984 dissertation reported that after ~1680 AD, "the ranked corporate descent group structure is not present" and the system "reverted to a non-ranked corporate structure" (p. 168). In 1986, this becomes: "the corporate solidarity of these communities had been weakened in response to a decline in the availability of productive agricultural land" (p. 77). The shift is from structural flexibility (the system can adopt simpler forms) to degradation (the system declined from a norm). Reversion implies that non-ranked organization was a viable alternative state; weakening implies that ranked organization was the proper condition from which the system fell.

**4. Environmental narrative strengthened.** The 1986 version adds Flenley and King 1984 (radiocarbon dates from crater lake cores showing deforestation by 990 ± 70 bp) and develops a causal chain: population growth + environmental deterioration -> agricultural land shortage -> corporate territory breakdown -> competitive warfare strategy -> replacement of ancestral ahu by Orongo cult. While not identical to the ecocide narrative that Bahn & Flenley (1992) and Diamond (2005) would later popularize, this degradation-warfare-collapse sequence is compatible with and feeds into that narrative.

**5. Three-model framework absent.** The 1984 dissertation evaluated three alternative models of corporate group structure (non-corporate, lineage-specific, ranked) and explicitly stated they were not evolutionary stages. The 1986 chapter presents a single narrative trajectory without alternatives. The intellectual apparatus for evaluating non-evolutionary possibilities has been removed.

### What stayed the same

**Core data unchanged.** Same study area, same ahu typology (Types 1-5), same 13-phase system, same population growth curve, same settlement trajectory.

**"Chiefdom" absent.** Stevenson did not adopt the chiefdom label. Service and Kirch are not cited.

**Four autonomous groups, no higher integration.** The basic political organization finding is unchanged, and the important hedge that higher-level integration "has not been detected archaeologically" (p. 77) survives.

**Ibo analogue retained extensively.** 2.5 of 9 pages devoted to the African ethnographic case study, with an explicit statement that Polynesian ethnographic data are "limited and often anecdotal" (p. 70).

**Goldman dual status system.** The hereditary chief/warrior chief distinction from Goldman (1970) is retained, though the "Open" classification is not named.

**Some hedging retained.** "Preliminary reconstruction," "hypothesized," "tentative," and the absence of archaeological evidence for supra-local integration.

## What This Means for the Chiefdom Attribution

The 1984-to-1986 transition illustrates how interpretive drift operates without explicit terminological change. The mechanism is not that Stevenson adopted "chiefdom"; he did not. Instead:

1. **Decontextualization**: The findings are separated from the theoretical tradition that made them an alternative to the chiefdom interpretation.
2. **Recontextualization**: The same findings are placed in an edited volume whose title, editor, and organizing framework position them as contributions to chiefdom-evolution scholarship.
3. **Selective attrition**: The strongest anti-chiefdom commitments (rejection of unilinear evolution, models as non-evolutionary, reversion as structural flexibility) are dropped. Some hedging survives, but it is detail-level rather than framework-level.

The result is that subsequent citations to "Stevenson 1986 in Kirch (ed.)" read the findings as chiefdom evidence. The four autonomous groups become four chiefdom-level polities. The Type 1 ahu become chiefly centers. The elite villages become chiefly residences. The late-period "weakening" becomes the "Decadent" phase of chiefdom collapse. None of these readings are in Stevenson's text, but the volume's framing invites them.

This parallels the Sahlins 1954-to-1958 pattern: tentative, qualified findings are progressively stripped of caveats and absorbed into a framework the original author did not use. The difference is that with Sahlins, the author himself drove the hardening (from "little direct evidence" to flat assertion of redistribution). With Stevenson, the hardening is driven partly by space constraints, partly by the editorial context, and partly by the absence of the theoretical framework that would have made the alternative interpretation legible. The later publications (Stevenson 1997, 2002) complete the transition with the "Chiefdom Integration" periodization that the 1986 chapter does not yet contain.

## Relevance to the Five-Step Circularity

Stevenson 1986 is a direct illustration of **Step 3** in action: an independent archaeological analysis is absorbed into the Polynesian chiefdom framework through editorial context rather than through the author's own theoretical adoption.

**The counter-example that becomes the example.** In 1984, Stevenson's dissertation was the strongest existing counter-example to the chiefdom interpretation of Rapa Nui: no "chiefdom," no Service, no Kirch, explicit rejection of unilinear evolution, four autonomous non-ranked groups, reversion to simpler organization. By appearing in Kirch's 1986 volume, the same data become part of the chiefdom literature. The editorial frame transforms the meaning without changing the content. This is Step 3 operating at the level of publication context rather than analytical argument.

**What this shows about how circularity operates.** The circularity does not require that every contributing scholar adopt the chiefdom label. It requires only that independent findings be placed in contexts where they are read as confirming the chiefdom model. Stevenson 1986 demonstrates that the editorial apparatus of academic publishing (volume framing, editor reputation, press prestige) can perform the interpretive work that the author's own text does not.

## Connections to Other Processed Sources

- **Stevenson 1984**: Direct predecessor. The 1986 chapter is a condensed version of the same data with the changes documented above.
- **Kirch 1984**: Volume editor's major work. Not cited by Stevenson, but the volume frames all contributions within Kirch's evolutionary archaeology program.
- **Goldman 1970**: Cited in 1986 for dual status system. The "Open" classification, which would challenge the chiefdom framing, is not named.
- **McCoy 1976**: Cited in both 1984 and 1986. McCoy's own anti-chiefdom finding (chiefdom "retarded" by warrior class) is not mentioned; McCoy is cited only for settlement data and environmental deterioration.
- **Sahlins 1954/1958**: Not cited in 1986 (or 1984). The Polynesian stratification framework is absent.
- **Service 1962**: Not cited in 1986 (or 1984). The band/tribe/chiefdom/state typology is absent.
- **Robinson & Stevenson 2017**: Three decades later, Stevenson acknowledges that paramountcy was "not discovered archaeologically or reported in the ethnohistoric oral history" (p. 90), consistent with the 1984/1986 finding that higher-level integration was not detected.

## Data Presented

- 57 platform ahu from southern coast (Hanga Hahave to Runga Va'e), dated by obsidian hydration
- 24 above-ground burial structures (semipyramidal, rectangular, amorphous)
- 33 complete ahu analyzed by cluster analysis (14 architectural attributes, Ward's Method)
- Five ahu architectural types (Table 8.2)
- Six phase maps showing ahu and burial structure distribution (fig. 8.2a-f)
- Population growth curve for Akahanga area (fig. 8.3)
- 13 one-hundred-year phases (AD 700-1864)

## Methodological Notes

- Obsidian hydration dating with re-evaluated rates (Michels et al. 1984), same method as 1984 dissertation
- Corporate group as unit of analysis, identified through ritual architecture
- Ibo ethnographic analogue for corporate group identification (2.5 pages of a 9-page chapter)
- Cluster analysis for ahu typology (Ward's Method, Wishart 1978)
- No independent dating of residential sites in this chapter; elite houses dated by associated obsidian
