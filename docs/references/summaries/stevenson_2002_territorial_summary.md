## Overview

This 17-page chapter presents a model of sixteenth-century Rapa Nui territorial divisions based on cluster analysis of 164 ahu using Martinsson-Wallin's (1994) architectural attribute data. Stevenson identifies seven architectural clusters, selects the most elaborate (Cluster 4, n=27) as "district centers," maps their distribution with associated "elite villages" (hare paenga clusters), and proposes 11 territorial units with boundaries drawn by midpoint interpolation between centers, "following the traditional Hawaiian model."

The paper is the single most consequential document in the Stevenson progressive hardening trajectory. Its opening sentence states: "Rapa Nui society evolved into a well-developed chiefdom involving numerous descent groups and several levels of political office (Goldman 1970; Kirch 1984; Sahlins 1958; Stevenson 1984)." This sentence enlists four sources to support a claim that none of them actually make in the form stated. It is the moment where the chiefdom label transitions from hypothesis to declared fact in Stevenson's work, and where the territorial map that subsequent researchers inherit as spatial infrastructure is formally published.

---

## Relevance to the Chiefdom Attribution

The paper contributes to the chiefdom attribution in five ways:

1. **Declarative chiefdom claim**: The opening sentence declares Rapa Nui "a well-developed chiefdom" as established fact, not hypothesis. This is the first time Stevenson states this unqualified.

2. **Fabricated citation authority**: The four-source citation chain (Goldman, Kirch, Sahlins, Stevenson 1984) creates apparent consensus. Goldman 1970 classified Easter Island as "Open" (contradicting "well-developed chiefdom"); Stevenson 1984 explicitly avoided chiefdom terminology. The citations are mobilized to support a claim their original authors did not make.

3. **Territorial map as infrastructure**: Figure 7 maps 11 named territorial units with geometric boundaries, becoming the spatial framework that the 2001 paper (which cited this paper as "in press") and subsequent researchers adopt as given. Once territories appear on a map, they function as data, not interpretation.

4. **Hawaiian model import**: The ahupua'a model is explicitly identified as the framework: "Following the traditional Hawaiian model, a set of proposed territorial subdivisions is shown in Figure 7" (p. 224). The Polynesian analogy is stated openly.

5. **Paramount chief suggestion**: Stevenson escalates from district chiefdoms to the possibility that "a paramount chief may have created an integrated island chiefdom" (p. 213), based on the distribution of "highly stylised ahu architecture" and Tongariki's size.

## The Fabricated Citation Chain

The opening sentence, "Rapa Nui society evolved into a well-developed chiefdom involving numerous descent groups and several levels of political office (Goldman 1970; Kirch 1984; Sahlins 1958; Stevenson 1984)," is the paper's most consequential claim and its most problematic.

| Source cited | What source actually says | Supports "well-developed chiefdom"? |
|---|---|---|
| Goldman 1970 | Classifies Easter Island as "Open" (lowest stratification, no centralized authority) | **No, contradicts it** |
| Kirch 1984 | Applies Hawaiian chiefdom model to Polynesia broadly; does not present Rapa Nui-specific evidence | Assumed via Polynesian analogy, not demonstrated |
| Sahlins 1958 | Provides comparative stratification framework; rates Easter Island low on most variables | Framework, not Rapa Nui-specific claim |
| Stevenson 1984 | Uses "corporate descent groups"; explicitly states boundaries "cannot be drawn"; avoids "chiefdom" | **No, contradicts it** |

Two of the four cited sources directly contradict the claim they are enlisted to support. The remaining two provide general frameworks, not Rapa Nui-specific evidence for a "well-developed chiefdom." This citation chain is a documented case of source misrepresentation in the service of interpretive hardening.

## The Post-Raid Contradiction

Stevenson explicitly acknowledges the problem with post-raid sources more directly than in any of his other papers:

- "Virtually all information on Rapa Nui political systems was obtained by brief European contact after AD 1722" (p. 214)
- Population reduced from ~10,000 to ~1,000 by 1866, to 110 by 1877 (p. 214)
- "such a drastic population decline also resulted in the loss of much oral history and detail on organizational structure" (p. 214)
- Post-raid ethnographic data "reveal little about the organization of the chiefdom prior to the impacts of western culture" (p. 214)

Having stated that these sources cannot reveal pre-contact political organization, Stevenson then uses Routledge 1919 (Figure 2), Métraux 1940, Thompson 1889, and Englert 1974 as the ethnohistoric framework for his entire analysis. The acknowledgment is rhetorical, not methodological: it is stated but does not constrain the analysis.

---

## The Hawaiian Model Import

The paper makes the Polynesian analogy explicit in a way the 2001 paper did not:

- Regional ahu studies "have developed out of the idealized model of Polynesian settlement presented by Kirch (1984, 1985) and Kirch and Green (1987) that relies heavily on the Hawaiian ahupua'a model of settlement (Handy and Pukui 1972)" (p. 217)
- "This model states that self-sufficient lineage units occupied pie-shaped slices of the island terrain that included marine and inland resource locations" (p. 217)
- "Whether this model actually holds for Rapa Nui has yet to be ascertained" (p. 217)
- "Following the traditional Hawaiian model, a set of proposed territorial subdivisions is shown in Figure 7" (p. 224)

The paper acknowledges the model is untested for Rapa Nui, then applies it anyway. The boundaries are drawn by placing them "approximately midway between the district centers of the two adjacent territories" (p. 224), a geometric construction that assumes the ahupua'a model rather than deriving boundaries from evidence.

## Martinsson-Wallin's Overridden Negative Finding

The paper's analytical core involves re-analyzing Martinsson-Wallin's (1994) 164-ahu dataset. Her original conclusions were:

- Ahu platforms were "highly homogeneous with respect to their architectural elements" (pp. 218-219)
- "A uniform spatial dispersal with no strong correlation to territorial size. Had the territories been long-term stable entities a correlation of this type would have been expected" (p. 219)
- She "was reluctant to use architectural data to define complex and socially correlated ahu structural types" (pp. 218-219)

These are negative results for the territorial hypothesis. Stevenson re-analyzes the same data using Ward's method cluster analysis on 15 (of the original 37) attributes, producing a seven-cluster solution. Three clusters (n=44, 27%) contain only destroyed or undocumented structures. The paper does not explain why Martinsson-Wallin's original analysis was wrong, only that the re-analysis produces a different result. The methodological question of why the same data yields opposing conclusions is not addressed.

The trajectory across four publications spans 18 years and documents the transformation of a cautious empirical finding ("corporate descent groups," "boundaries cannot be drawn") into a declared conclusion ("well-developed chiefdom," 11 mapped territories). Each step hardens the interpretation while the underlying evidence base remains the same post-raid ethnographic sources and the same archaeological survey data.

## The Ethnohistoric-Archaeological Mismatch

A finding that Stevenson reports but does not treat as problematic: his archaeological model of 11 territorial divisions (Figure 7) shows "little correspondence" with the ethnohistoric divisions from Routledge 1919 (Figure 2) (p. 224). Three twentieth-century districts have access to two coastal sections; single principle centers are not unique to each territory. Stevenson interprets this as evidence of "a significant restructuring" after the sixteenth century (p. 225) rather than as evidence that stable territorial divisions may not have existed in either period.

The alternative reading: if the archaeological model and the ethnohistoric model do not correspond, this is not evidence that two different territorial systems existed at different times. It may be evidence that the territorial model itself does not fit Rapa Nui's political organization.

## Claims Contradicted by Current Evidence

1. **Ahu placement**: Stevenson argues "social/religious considerations" drove ahu location rather than environmental factors. DiNapoli et al. 2019 found ahu placement strongly predicted by proximity to freshwater.

2. **Elite coordination required**: Stevenson claims architectural complexity "could only be conducted by elite members" (p. 222). Lipo et al. 2025 found autonomous, redundant quarry workshops inconsistent with centralized elite management.

3. **Population**: "historically high level estimated to be around 10,000." Davis et al. 2024 estimated maximum carrying capacity ~3,900.

4. **Construction timeline**: DiNapoli et al. 2020 found steady ahu construction through contact, not the AD 1000-1600 window implied.

5. **"Well-developed chiefdom"**: Goldman 1970, cited as support, actually classified Easter Island as "Open."

## Bibliographic Profile

The bibliography (41 sources) reveals a narrow theoretical frame:

**Chiefdom/neoevolutionary framework**: Goldman 1970, Kirch 1984, Kirch 1985, Sahlins 1958. No chiefdom critics cited (no Feinman & Neitzel, no Pauketat, no Yoffee). No collective action literature.

**Hawaiian/Polynesian comparative**: Handy & Pukui 1972, Kirch & Green 1987, Kirch 2000. Only Hawaii is used as comparator, despite Goldman covering multiple Polynesian cases.

**Self-citations**: 8 of 41 sources (20%). Settlement model, agricultural data, and chronology all derive from Stevenson's own prior work.

**Post-raid ethnographic**: Thompson 1889, Routledge 1919, Métraux 1940, Englert 1974, Lavachery 1936/1954. Cited openly (unlike 2001, where they were hidden), but limitations acknowledged then bypassed.

**Absent**: Service 1962 (chiefdom typology creator), Sahlins 1963 (all-Polynesian chiefdom classification), Sahlins & Service 1960 (general/specific evolution), all British social anthropology sources from 1984, Hunt & Lipo 2001, Earle 1997. The theoretical framework has narrowed to Kirch's Polynesian chiefdom model as the sole interpretive lens.

**New references not on checklist**: Rounds-Beardsley 1990 (PhD, ahu spatial analysis; important for circularity argument), Golson 1965 (Heyerdahl critique), Smith 1961 (ahu temporal sequence), McCall 1981 (different from McCall 1979), Lavachery 1936/1954, Ayres 1988, Martinsson-Wallin & Wallin 2000, Stevenson 2000.

## Connections to Other Processed Sources

- **Stevenson 1984**: Direct predecessor. The 2002 paper contradicts the 1984 finding that political boundaries cannot be drawn, that corporate groups are the appropriate analytical unit, and that the system reverted to non-ranked organization. The 2002 paper cites 1984 as supporting "well-developed chiefdom," which it does not.
- **Stevenson 1986**: Cited for prior south coast ahu analysis. The 2002 paper completes the transition that the 1986 editorial reframing began.
- **Stevenson et al. 2001**: The 2001 paper cited this paper as "Stevenson in press" and previewed its chiefdom districts map. The temporal relationship is: the 2002 model was circulating before publication, and the 2001 paper already treated it as established.
- **Martinsson-Wallin 1994**: Data source. Stevenson re-analyzes her 164-ahu dataset to produce results she did not reach and that contradict her own conclusions about ahu homogeneity and lack of territorial correlation.
- **Kirch 1984**: The Hawaiian chiefdom model that Stevenson explicitly imports via the ahupua'a framework. First time Stevenson cites Kirch 1984 directly.
- **Goldman 1970**: Misrepresented. Goldman's "Open" classification (accurately cited in 1984) becomes evidence for "well-developed chiefdom" in 2002.
- **Van Tilburg 1994**: The consolidation of chiefdom as default in the mid-1990s provides the context for Stevenson's adoption.
- **DiNapoli et al. 2019**: Directly contradicts the claim that social/religious factors, not environmental ones, determined ahu location.
- **Lipo et al. 2025**: Directly contradicts the elite coordination claim.

## Post-Slave-Raid Dependency

The entire ethnohistoric framework (Figure 2, pp. 214-215) derives from post-slave-raid sources: Routledge 1919, Métraux 1940, Thompson 1889, Englert 1974. Stevenson explicitly acknowledges these sources "reveal little about the organization of the chiefdom prior to the impacts of western culture" (p. 214), then uses them as the comparison framework for his archaeological model. The ethnohistoric territorial divisions, recorded 50-130 years after the slave raids that reduced the population to 110 people, are treated as a valid representation of a political system that may never have existed in its described form.
