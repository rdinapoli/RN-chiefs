# Source Summary: Thompson 1891

## Overview

Paymaster William J. Thomson of the USS Mohican spent twelve days on Easter Island in December 1886, producing the most extensive 19th-century treatment of the island. His report combines archaeological survey (113 platforms catalogued, excavations at Orongo, Tongariki, and elsewhere), collection of material culture for the National Museum, and evening sessions recording traditions through the translator Alexander P. Salmon. Published as a Smithsonian report in 1891, Thomson's account became the standard English-language reference on Easter Island for the next three decades, until Routledge's 1919 work.

## Sources Consulted

- Thomson 1891, *Te Pito te Henua, or Easter Island*, Report of the National Museum, 1888-'89, pp. 447-552 (PDF: `docs/references/pdfs/post_slave_raid/thompson_1891/Thompson_1891.pdf`)

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**"Arbitrary monarchy" with no structural supports.** Thomson's "Government" section (p. 472) describes Easter Island's political organization as an "arbitrary monarchy" with a sacred hereditary king, subordinate hereditary district chiefs, and the royal prerogative to remove chiefs. However, Thomson immediately qualifies this with statements that undermine the chiefdom model: "no tax was exacted of the people," "it does not appear that any great homage was paid the king," each clan was "independent of all the rest," and "the people avenged their own injuries." The description of a monarchy without taxation, without regular homage, and without effective authority over independently warring clans is, structurally, not a description of a chiefdom.

**King list of 57 names.** Thomson records 57 kings from Hotu-Matua to Maurata (pp. 533-534), the longest list from any source. This list is irreconcilable with Roussel's 22-generation list recorded just 17 years earlier (1869): only about 12 of 23 Roussel names appear in Thomson's 57, and few are in the same relative position. The final kings differ (Thomson: Maurata; Roussel: Gregorio). These discrepancies between lists recorded within a single generation from the same small population demonstrate that the king lists cannot be treated as historical records of a stable dynasty.

**Tangata manu without political authority.** Thomson's description of the bird-egg ceremony (pp. 482-483) explicitly states that the winner received "no especial authority." This contradicts Roussel's description of the birdman as exercising "the chief's authority" for one year. Thomson describes the winner as receiving food contributions and spiritual approval, not political power. The king's role was limited to starting the race.

**Land division as origin charter.** Thomson records the tradition that Hotu-Matua divided the island into six districts for his six sons (pp. 527-528). This origin narrative for territorial organization has been influential in later interpretations of mata/clan structure but functions as a legitimizing charter rather than a historical record of land allocation.

**Image-makers supported by fishermen.** One passage states that during the reign of the seventeenth king, "the image-makers were exempt from all other kinds of work, and the fishermen were taxed for their chief support" (p. 533). This is the only reference to a tax-like arrangement in Thomson's account and contradicts his own statement that "no tax was exacted of the people" (p. 473). If read carefully, it describes craft specialization supported by a specific inter-group arrangement, not a general tribute system administered by a paramount chief.

## Context and Limitations

**[POST-RAID]** Thomson visited 24 years after the slave raids. The population was 155 (68 men, 43 women, 17 boys, 27 girls), having recovered from a bottleneck of approximately 111 in 1877. All "officials and persons in authority" had been kidnapped by the Peruvians (p. 514).

**Twelve-day visit.** Thomson's stay was brief. Most time was spent on archaeological survey and excavation. Traditions were collected during evening sessions at Salmon's residence, under time pressure to complete work before the Mohican sailed.

**Intermediated through Salmon.** All traditions were translated by Alexander P. Salmon, a foreign resident with commercial interests on the island. Salmon's interpretive framework, linguistic competence, and potential biases are unexamined filters on all the ethnographic content.

**Coercion in collecting.** The session with Ure Vaeiko, the claimed tablet reader, involved coercion: Thomson's party found the old man at home on a stormy night when "escape [was] impossible," then provided alcohol "as the night grew old and the narrator weary" (p. 515). The ethical problems aside, traditions collected under these conditions are not reliable records.

**Ure Vaeiko could not read the tablets.** Thomson directly observed that Ure Vaeiko recited memorized traditions associated with named tablets but could not identify individual characters. When a different tablet photograph was substituted, "the same story was continued without the change being discovered" (p. 516). This demonstrates that the knowledge system described in the traditions (restricted literacy among the elite) had been completely destroyed by 1886.

**Distinction between observation and tradition.** Thomson's archaeological observations (platform measurements, excavation results, material culture descriptions) are direct and generally reliable. His ethnographic content is entirely reported tradition, filtered through Salmon's translation, from a population of 155 people who were survivors and descendants of survivors of a 96% population decline.

## Relevance to the Paper

Thomson's account is critical for the chiefdom attribution argument for three reasons:

1. **The "Government" section is the primary source for the "arbitrary monarchy" characterization** that later scholars repackaged as "chiefdom." Thomson's own qualifications (no tax, no homage, independent clans) have been systematically overlooked in subsequent citations.

2. **The king list disagreement with Roussel is devastating to both lists' credibility.** If a stable hereditary dynasty had existed for 57 (or 22) generations, independently recorded lists from the same small population should converge. They do not. The lists appear to be reconstructions or elaborations produced by post-catastrophe informants, not records of a functioning institution.

3. **The tangata manu description directly contradicts Roussel.** Thomson says "no especial authority"; Roussel says the winner exercised "the chief's authority." These accounts, recorded 17 years apart from overlapping informant pools, cannot both be correct. The disagreement undermines the use of either as evidence for pre-contact political organization.

The paper should cite Thomson as the source of the "arbitrary monarchy" framing while noting that Thomson's own account simultaneously negates the structural requirements of centralized authority.
