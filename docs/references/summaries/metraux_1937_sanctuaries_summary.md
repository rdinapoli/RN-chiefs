# Summary: Métraux 1937 "Easter Island Sanctuaries"

## Source Information

- **Author**: Alfred Métraux
- **Title**: "Easter Island Sanctuaries (Analytic and Comparative Study)"
- **Publication**: *Etnologiska studier* 5, Göteborg, 1937, pp. 104-153
- **Fieldwork**: 1934 Franco-Belgian expedition
- **Type**: Archaeological and comparative architectural study

## Overview

This 50-page article provides the most detailed analytical study of Easter Island ahu (burial platforms/sanctuaries) available from the early 20th century. Métraux classifies ahu into four types, describes construction techniques and materials in detail, surveys the distribution of monuments across the island, provides comparative analysis with religious structures throughout Polynesia (Mangareva, Marquesas, Tuamotus, Tonga, Hawaii, New Zealand), and addresses the transportation and erection of stone statues.

## Key Findings for Political Authority

This article contains **no claims about political authority directing monument construction**. This absence is significant because:

1. **Comprehensive yet apolitical**: Métraux describes ahu architecture, construction techniques, stone-working, distribution, comparative parallels, and statue transport across 50 pages without once attributing these accomplishments to centralized political direction or chiefly command.

2. **Clan-level organization implied**: Each lineage "distinctly remembers an ahu of its own where the dead of the group were buried" (p. 105). When an ahu's owning family died out, the monument decayed; no political authority maintained orphaned monuments. La Pérouse (cited approvingly) described the island as "divided into as many districts as there are morais," implying decentralized political units corresponding to ahu locations.

3. **Walking statues legend dismissed**: Métraux identifies the "walking statues" myth as an invention "to satisfy inquirers." Roussel's own alternative explanation describes each tribe dragging "its family gods... to their respective districts," implying decentralized, clan-level transport organization, not centralized command.

4. **Transport demystified through Polynesian parallels**: Marquesan platforms used boulders of 3-10 tons moved by sling-and-pole methods; Maori hauled 60-ton vessels; Tongan trilithon pillars of 30-40 tons were moved with ropes, skids, levers, and combined muscular power. Métraux argues Easter Island statue transport requires no special explanation beyond known Polynesian techniques: "This problem has been unnecessarily complicated by those who want to add mystery to the Island."

5. **Weight overestimation corrected**: Using measured tuff density (2.48), Métraux estimates that typical 4-5 meter statues weigh only 4-5 tons, well within the range of stones moved by other Polynesian societies without centralized political authority.

## Context and Limitations

- The architectural descriptions are based on direct observation of ruins and are reliable as archaeological data.
- Claims about lineage associations, ahu names, and construction traditions are [POST-RAID], derived from informants 72 years after the slave raids.
- The comparative Polynesian framework is carefully deployed without projecting other islands' political systems onto Rapa Nui. Métraux compares architectural forms and construction techniques, not political organization.
- The article does not address social organization or political authority directly; these are treated in the companion 1937 JPS article ("The Kings of Easter Island") and the 1940 monograph.

## Relevance to the Paper

1. **Monument-production argument**: The most detailed study of ahu architecture by a trained ethnographer documents sophisticated construction without attributing it to chiefly authority. This is significant for the argument that monumental architecture does not require hierarchical command.

2. **Distribution patterns**: Ahu concentration near coves and economically advantageous locations is consistent with community-level construction (each community building near its settlement) rather than centralized planning.

3. **Ahu Vinapu and diffusionism**: Métraux explicitly rejects the Inca-connection hypothesis for Vinapu's fine masonry, arguing it is "unique on Easter Island" and represents local stone-cutting development, not outside influence.

4. **Walking statues legend**: Métraux's treatment of this legend is relevant to the circularity argument. The legend has been cited as evidence of chiefly power ("the great chief... ordered the statues to walk"), but Métraux dismisses the magical element as myth, and Roussel's own prosaic explanation (each tribe dragging its family gods to its district) implies clan-level organization.
