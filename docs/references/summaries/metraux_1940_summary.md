# Summary: Métraux 1940 *Ethnology of Easter Island*

## Source Information

- **Author**: Alfred Métraux
- **Title**: *Ethnology of Easter Island*
- **Publication**: Bishop Museum Bulletin 160, Honolulu, 1940 (463 pages)
- **Fieldwork**: 1934 Franco-Belgian expedition
- **Type**: Comprehensive ethnographic monograph

## Overview

This is the most comprehensive ethnographic monograph on Easter Island from the early 20th century, covering social organization, kingship, religion, bird cult, material culture, stone images, and traditional history. Based on Métraux's 1934 fieldwork, it substantially expands his two 1937 articles ("The Kings of Easter Island" and "Easter Island Sanctuaries") with full chapters on clan structure, marriage, social classes, warfare, religion, and the bird cult.

## Key Findings for Political Authority

### 1. King List Comparison (Table 2)

The side-by-side comparison of four independently recorded king lists (Roussel, Jaussen, Thomson, Métraux) reveals:
- Only **7 names** are common to all four lists
- Names that recur across lists are primarily tribal/geographic names (Marama, Raa, Hotu-iti, Tuu), suggesting tribal eponyms were incorporated as "kings"
- Priests/experts (tuhunga series) and mythological figures (Tiki-te-hatu, Te Riri-katea) appear interpolated into the lists
- Only three individuals have independent historical corroboration: Hotu-matua, Nga-ara, Maurata

This table provides the definitive evidence that the king lists cannot sustain the weight placed on them by chiefdom models seeking a stable dynastic succession.

### 2. Ariki-mau Assessment

Métraux's conclusion, with access to more evidence than any predecessor, is identical to his 1937 article: "he was certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (p. 136). The ariki-mau possessed mana and supernatural functions (crop fertility, tapu control, rongorongo supervision) but "probably had little authority" over other chiefs. He "did not interfere in the intertribal wars" and "may occasionally have acted as mediator in quarrels."

The capture and enslavement of the ariki-mau and his family by the Ngaure clan, detailed here as in the 1937 article, demonstrates the office's political impotence.

### 3. Matatoa as Actual Rulers

New to the monograph: Métraux explicitly identifies the matatoa (warriors) as "the actual rulers of the tribes," contradicting Thomson's claim that there was no warrior class. The matatoa were "nearly, if not exactly, professional warriors" who "probably did no work." However, "there is no specific record that a matatoa ever ruled over the whole island."

### 4. Social Classes as "Only Superficially Exact"

Roussel's four-class hierarchy (ariki, ivi-atua, matatoa, kio) is described as "only superficially exact." The kio were war captives (a consequence of inter-clan warfare), not a hereditary servile caste. Whether priests (ivi-atua) even existed as a class is disputed: Geiseler and Routledge deny it, Thomson reduces them to "wizards," while only Roussel affirms their status.

### 5. Stone Image Significance

Métraux concludes: "The true significance of stone statues is not known. There is no direct evidence that they were ever worshipped." He considers multiple explanations (ancestor memorials, boundary markers, display, tribal pride) and settles on "tribal pride and competitive instinct" as a likely motivation, a formulation compatible with competitive signaling among autonomous groups rather than chiefly command.

### 6. Bird Cult

The tangata manu institution is confirmed as: one-year duration, coexisting with but separate from the hereditary ariki-mau, organized along the dual division (Hotu-iti vs. Ko Tuu), with privileges limited to aggressive/predatory behaviors (marauding, food extraction, house-burning threats) and temporary sacred status. New detail: the tangata manu designated human sacrifices, provoking annual inter-clan warfare.

## Context and Limitations

- All social organization data is [POST-RAID], collected 72 years after the slave raids from a population recovered from a bottleneck of ~111
- Key informants (Tepano, Victoria Rapahango) were shared with or overlapped Routledge's sources, creating partial dependence between the two accounts
- Métraux had access to Roussel's published texts (1926 Annales), which Routledge did not
- The monograph treats social organization as a memory system, not a functioning practice: "The missionaries concentrated the population in a single village and thus broke the last connections of the individuals with their ancestral territory"
- Métraux is unusually self-aware about the limits of his data, noting "very little information" on social organization and questioning whether the "ancient priesthood was always loosely organized or whether the original institutions were forgotten after the Peruvian raid"

## Relevance to the Paper

1. **King list table resolves [CITE-CHECK: Métraux 1940:91]**: The comparison of four independently recorded lists, showing only 7 shared names with tribal eponyms dominating the overlaps, provides definitive evidence against treating these lists as dynastic records.

2. **Strengthens the "no political power" convergence**: Routledge (1919) and Métraux (1940), the two most comprehensive ethnographers, independently conclude the ariki-mau had no political authority, despite Métraux having access to more sources and more informants than Routledge.

3. **Complicates the class-hierarchy picture**: The four-class system, often cited as evidence for a ranked chiefdom, is revealed as "only superficially exact" even by the ethnographer who recorded it. The kio were war captives, the matatoa were the actual rulers, and the ivi-atua's existence is disputed.

4. **Stone images and monument production**: The conclusion that there is "no direct evidence" of statue worship and that "tribal pride and competitive instinct" motivated construction supports the collective action/competitive signaling alternative to chiefly-command models.
