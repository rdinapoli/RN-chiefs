## Overview

This paper presents the first formal, spatially-explicit point-process model of image-ahu placement on Rapa Nui. Using 93 image-ahu locations in the eastern portion of the island and spatial data on three classes of subsistence resources (rock mulch agricultural gardens, marine resource access points, and freshwater sources including coastal groundwater seeps), the study tests which resources best explain the spatial distribution of monuments. The analysis employs inhomogeneous Poisson point-process models (PPMs) compared via AIC and BIC model selection, preceded by spatial Kolmogorov-Smirnov (SKS) tests and L-function analyses.

The central finding is that ahu locations are most parsimoniously explained by an additive model incorporating distance from the coastline and distance from freshwater sources (model 5, BIC weight = 0.675, AIC weight = 0.35). Rock mulch garden locations do not improve model fit. Marine resource locations show a significant exploratory association with ahu but add no explanatory power once coastline and freshwater are accounted for. Model validation (residual L-function, MAD and DCLF tests) confirms a good fit, with no need for second-order interaction terms (clustering or repulsion).

## Key Findings

### Freshwater explains ahu placement

Ahu intensity (spatial density) decreases with distance from freshwater sources and from the coast (Table 2, p. 15). The best model combines these two covariates. The freshwater variable alone (model 2) performs better than rock mulch alone (model 4) but worse than the combined coastline + freshwater model. The coastline-only model (model 1) is substantially worse than the combined model, indicating that ahu are not simply coastal but are specifically concentrated near coastal freshwater seeps.

The paper documents that Rapa Nui's porous volcanic bedrock channels rainfall into unconfined aquifers that discharge as coastal groundwater seeps (CGD), which were the primary accessible freshwater source for precontact populations (pp. 6-7). Freshwater survey data were collected using salinity meters at 10 m intervals along the coastline, with seeps defined as locations with salinity at or below 28 ppt (a 20% reduction from ambient seawater at ~35 ppt).

### Rock mulch gardens do NOT explain ahu placement

The distribution of image-ahu is not explained by proximity to rock mulch gardens (p. 16). This finding directly contradicts the longstanding argument of Stevenson and colleagues that ahu placement tracks intensified agricultural field systems and reflects elite management of agricultural production. While monument construction and field system development may be temporally coeval, the spatial analysis shows that ahu were not built to mark control or territoriality over agricultural resources.

### Marine resource correlation is spurious

Exploratory SKS tests show a significant ahu-marine association (D+ = 0.65, p < 0.0001), but this variable adds no explanatory power in the PPM framework. Marine resource locations and freshwater seeps both occur in coastal settings, and the apparent marine correlation is an artifact of geographic proximity. This challenges Kirch's (2017) argument that ahu were placed to visually control access to marine resources.

### No inter-ahu clustering or repulsion

After accounting for the inhomogeneous first-order intensity (driven by freshwater and coastline), the ahu pattern shows no second-order clustering or dispersion at distances under ~1500 m (Fig. 3B, p. 12). There is some evidence for dispersion at distances greater than 1500 m. The absence of competitive spacing dynamics means no inter-polity territorial competition model is required to explain the pattern.

## Significance for the Chiefdom Question

This paper bears on the chiefdom attribution in several ways:

**What it demonstrates:**

1. The spatial distribution of ahu is best explained by proximity to freshwater, specifically coastal groundwater seeps. This is a formally tested, quantitative result using information-theoretic model selection across 15 candidate models.

2. Ahu placement does not track agricultural field systems. The empirical basis for the claim that ahu mark elite agricultural territories (Stevenson et al.) is not supported.

3. The marine resource control hypothesis (Kirch 2017) loses its spatial basis once the underlying freshwater and coastline variables are accounted for.

4. The lack of second-order interaction in the ahu pattern means no competitive territorial dynamics between polities need be invoked to explain monument distribution.

5. Ahu are spatially offset from domestic activity areas (100-200 m seaward), positioned directly at freshwater access points rather than at the center of residential zones.

**What it does not demonstrate:**

1. The paper does not claim to disprove the chiefdom model. It demonstrates that the spatial evidence is consistent with community-level territorial signaling over freshwater, which does not require hierarchical political organization but also does not exclude it.

2. The paper does not resolve whether ahu were built by centralized command or by collective action. The spatial analysis identifies *where* ahu were built and *what environmental variable best predicts their locations*, not *who organized their construction*.

3. Temporal dynamics are unresolved. The analysis is spatial only; the lack of precise radiocarbon dates for most ahu prevents spatio-temporal modeling.

4. The western portion of the island was excluded due to incomplete freshwater survey data. Results apply to the eastern study area (93 image-ahu).

## How It Bears on the Traditional Interpretation

The traditional interpretation holds that ahu mark chiefly territorial boundaries and that their distribution reflects the political geography of competing chiefdoms (drawing on McCoy 1976, Kirch 1984/2017, Stevenson 2002, Van Tilburg 1994, Simpson 2009). This interpretation assumes that ahu placement was determined by political considerations, specifically, that chiefs directed monument construction at locations that would display control over critical subsistence resources.

DiNapoli et al. 2019 does not directly test the political interpretation, but its findings reframe the question. If ahu placement is most parsimoniously explained by freshwater access, then invoking chiefly territorial boundaries as an additional explanatory factor is unnecessary for explaining the spatial pattern. The territorial-boundary interpretation is not refuted, but the parsimony argument shifts the burden of proof: advocates of the chiefly-territorial model must explain what additional variance their model accounts for beyond what freshwater proximity already explains.

The paper also notes that raw material access for monument construction (obsidian, basalt, moai tuff, pukao scoria) shows no evidence of localized control by any group (pp. 4-5), and that moai and pukao construction and transport did not require large labor forces (p. 19, citing Lipo et al. 2013 and Hixon et al. 2018). Together, these findings weaken the inferential chain from "monuments exist" to "chiefs must have directed their construction."

The paper's own interpretive framework uses costly signaling theory at the community level: monuments as conspicuous displays of collective community capacity near vital resources, not as expressions of individual chiefly authority. This framing is explicitly compatible with collective action models (DiNapoli and Morrison 2017, DiNapoli et al. 2018).

## Relationship to Other Sources in This Project

- **McCoy 1976**: McCoy anticipated the freshwater-settlement connection, writing that "water, rather than soil fertility, then, seems to have been the major limiting factor in the exploitation of uninhabited lands" (p. 142). DiNapoli et al. 2019 provides the formal quantitative test McCoy's observation required.

- **Kirch 2017**: Kirch's claim that ahu are placed to "visually control" marine resources is directly challenged. Marine resources are a spurious correlate in the spatial model.

- **Stevenson et al.**: The longstanding argument linking ahu to agricultural field management is not supported by the spatial analysis. Rock mulch gardens do not predict ahu locations.

- **Contact-era accounts**: Cook's observation that Rapanui drank brackish coastal water is reinterpreted as evidence for CGD use, connecting the ethnohistoric record to the hydrogeological data.
