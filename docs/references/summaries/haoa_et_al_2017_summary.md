# Summary: Haoa Cardinali et al. 2017 (eds.)

**Source**: Haoa Cardinali, Sonia, Kathleen B. Ingersoll, Daniel W. Ingersoll, Jr., and Christopher M. Stevenson, eds. 2017 [2018]. *Cultural and Environmental Change on Rapa Nui*. Routledge Studies in Archaeology 30. London: Routledge.

---

## Overview

This edited volume brings together eleven chapters by Rapa Nui researchers addressing environmental change, subsistence, settlement, ritual, contact-era accounts, and the collapse debate. The contributors include both proponents and critics of the collapse/ecocide narrative: Stevenson (a principal architect of the chiefdom-integration/collapse periodization) coedits the volume alongside Ingersoll and Ingersoll, who explicitly reject ecocide and advocate a resilience interpretation. The volume does not focus directly on political organization or the chiefdom concept, but several chapters bear on the question through their treatment of archaeological evidence, ethnohistoric interpretation, and the collapse narrative.

## Contents and chapter-level relevance

**Chapter 1 (Stevenson, Ladefoged, and Chadwick)**: Settlement and agriculture on the Hiva Hiva lava flow. Obsidian hydration dating suggests population growth from the 13th century through the pre-contact period, with rock garden intensification expanding in parallel. No evidence of pre-contact population crash in this study area.

**Chapter 2 (Mieth and Bork)**: Deforestation history based on palm root molds in soils. Documents the disappearance of the Jubaea palm forest, arguing for human-caused deforestation but acknowledging the timeline is uncertain.

**Chapter 3 (Ingersoll and Ingersoll)**: Palm extinction by disease (Phytoplasma). Proposes that palm blight, not human overexploitation, may have caused palm extinction. Challenges a core element of the ecocide narrative.

**Chapter 4 (Flenley and Butler)**: Pollen data reinterpretation. The authors support the collapse model in their broader work but this chapter focuses on environmental reconstruction.

**Chapter 5 (Wozniak)**: Documents sophisticated rock garden and manavai systems. Shows continuity between prehistoric and modern farming techniques. The extent and ingenuity of agricultural engineering is consistent with sustained community investment.

**Chapter 6 (Vogt and Kuhlem)**: Excavation at Ava Ranga Uka A Toroke Hau, featuring monumental architecture, hydraulic installations, and ritual features along the island's only streambed. The authors interpret the site as controlled by an elite and invoke Metraux's account of the ariki mau's role in drought rituals. This chapter contains the strongest pro-chiefdom interpretive claims in the volume.

**Chapter 7 (Lee et al.)**: Analysis of secondary petroglyphs on pukao (topknots). Treats the transition from ahu moai phase to huri moai phase as reflecting the breakdown of hereditary chiefly authority and rise of the birdman cult. Assumes chiefly ownership of monuments as a premise rather than a conclusion.

**Chapter 8 (Boersema)**: Systematic review of all four 18th-century expedition accounts. Concludes that the accounts describe a healthy, food-sufficient population without evidence of warfare, starvation, or collapse. The most directly relevant chapter for our paper's treatment of contact-era evidence.

**Chapter 9 (Richards)**: Documents 79 foreign visits before 1862, mostly whaleships. Describes a prosperous society producing agricultural surplus for trade. Notes a "King" controlling shore access in 1805 and "chiefs" with distinctive tattoos in 1823.

**Chapter 10 (Ingersoll, Ingersoll, and Bove)**: Challenges Western stereotypes about Rapa Nui, including the ecocide narrative. Argues the near-extinction of 1877 resulted from slave raids and disease, not environmental collapse. Polemical but makes valid points about the origin of the population crash.

**Chapter 11 (Reflections)**: The editors acknowledge that the collapse scenario continues to shape archaeological interpretation despite its weak evidentiary basis. They explicitly identify the interpretation of Orongo houses, ana kionga caves, and mata'a as cases where the framework has been applied without adequate support.

## Key findings relevant to the chiefdom question

### What the volume demonstrates

1. The 18th-century accounts do not describe a collapsed or warring society. Boersema's review (Ch. 8) finds no evidence of widespread warfare, starvation, or societal breakdown in any of the four expedition accounts (1722, 1770, 1774, 1786). Weapons are absent in 1722 and 1770 and described as modest in 1774 and 1786.

2. The whaling-era accounts (1805-1862) describe a society producing agricultural surplus sufficient for regular trade with visiting ships (Ch. 9). This is inconsistent with a society that had undergone catastrophic collapse in the 17th century.

3. Even Stevenson's own field data (Ch. 1) show population growth and agricultural intensification through the pre-contact period without evidence of a sharp pre-contact decline.

4. The editors themselves (Ch. 11) acknowledge that the collapse framework has shaped interpretation of multiple feature types (Orongo houses, ana kionga, mata'a) without adequate independent evidence.

5. Agricultural systems (Chs. 1, 5, 6) demonstrate sophisticated engineering that does not inherently require centralized direction, though it is consistent with it.

### What the volume does not demonstrate

1. No chapter provides independent archaeological evidence for hierarchical chiefdom organization. Where chiefdom authority is invoked (Chs. 6, 7), it is either assumed as a premise or derived from Metraux's post-slave-raid ethnography.

2. The volume does not directly test the chiefdom model as an explanation for Rapa Nui political organization. The closest it comes is testing (and finding unsupported) the collapse-driven settlement shift that the chiefdom model predicts, but this is done through other contributors' work (Mulrooney 2013) rather than new data in this volume.

3. The existence of recognized authority figures ("King" in 1805, "chiefs" in 1823) is documented from European observers' accounts (Ch. 9) but the nature, extent, and institutional basis of that authority is not characterized.

## Bearing on the five-step circularity

The volume provides material relevant to several steps of the circularity argument:

**Step 1 (European accounts generate categories)**: Boersema (Ch. 8) demonstrates that English-language collapse authors selectively read the contact accounts through Cook's unfavorable comparison of Rapa Nui with other Pacific islands. Richards (Ch. 9) documents European use of the term "King" for Rapanui authority figures.

**Step 3 (Archaeological evidence read through the framework)**: The editors (Ch. 11) explicitly identify cases where archaeological features have been interpreted through the collapse/chiefdom lens without independent support: Orongo as 16th-century (unsupported by reliable dates), ana kionga as refuge caves (based on a single Metraux informant), mata'a as weapons (contradicted by morphometric analysis).

**Step 4 (Circular confirmation)**: Vogt and Kuhlem (Ch. 6) interpret an ahu-adjacent feature as a chief's seat by analogy with Society Islands marae, then cite Metraux on the ariki mau's ritual role, then interpret the entire site complex as confirming elite control of water resources. The archaeological features do not independently establish the political interpretation; the political interpretation is imported from ethnographic analogy and then used to characterize the features.

## Source quality and caveats

This is an edited volume with uneven analytical rigor across chapters. Boersema's systematic source review (Ch. 8), Richards's whaler-account compilation (Ch. 9), and Stevenson's fieldwork data (Ch. 1) provide solid empirical contributions. Lee et al. (Ch. 7) and Ingersoll et al. (Ch. 10) are more interpretive and, in the latter case, explicitly polemical. Vogt and Kuhlem (Ch. 6) present valuable excavation data but their interpretive framework assumes what needs to be demonstrated.

When citing this volume, distinguish carefully between the empirical data presented and the interpretive frameworks applied. Several chapters present data that are valuable for our argument while wrapping them in interpretive framing that we contest.
