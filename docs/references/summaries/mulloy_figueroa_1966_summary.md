# Summary: Mulloy & Figueroa 1966

**Full citation**: Mulloy, William T., and Gonzalo Figueroa G. 1966. "The Archaeological Heritage of Rapa Nui (Easter Island): Its Character, Description, and Conservation." Report prepared for UNESCO, Contract No. CLT-0768. 111 pp.

**Type**: UNESCO heritage conservation report. Comprehensive survey of archaeological monuments with proposals for investigation, conservation, and restoration. Fieldwork March-April 1966, drawing on both authors' combined ~4 years of previous field experience on the island.

## Overview

A systematic survey of Easter Island's archaeological heritage prepared for UNESCO as part of a broader island development plan. Part One provides background on the island's geography, environment, culture history, and previous archaeological work. Part Two proposes a detailed program of archaeological investigation, conservation, and restoration, organized by monument class: ahu, domestic structures, caves, petroglyphs, Orongo, Rano Raraku quarry, Puna Pau, Ana Kai Tangata, and Poike. The report describes individual monuments with assessments of their condition and restoration potential.

## Key Arguments

### Settlement as Independent Kin Groups
The population was "scattered widely along the coasts and adjacent interior areas of the island in relatively small independent or partially independent kin groups of some sort" (pp. 12-13). Each group possessed its own ahu, which were "variants of a widely diffused kind of Polynesian marae" (pp. 13-14). Domestic establishments extended inland from coastal ahu. The typical settlement pattern was "a segment of coast and its adjacent inland zone" (p. 15). No centralized authority, administrative centers, or chiefly compounds are described.

### Ahu Variation as Kin-Group Expression
Ahu variation is attributed to chronological change and "individual preferences of kin groups and a sense of competition among them" (pp. 15-16). Critically, "the size and ostentation of features of many ahu reflected the numbers and capacity of the kin groups that built them" (p. 16). Rebuilding "may have resulted from increased capacity of a kin group and a desire for a more impressive structure" (p. 16). This is a kin-group capacity model: ahu size reflects the size and resources of the building group, not the power of a chief or the operation of a redistributive economy.

### No Political Hierarchy
The entire 111-page report contains no mention of "chiefdom," "chief" (as political term), "hierarchy," "rank," "stratified," "redistribution," or "tribute." Service (1962), Sahlins (1954/1958), and Goldman (1970) are not cited. Political vocabulary is entirely absent: the report does not mention ariki, matatoa, or social classes. This is consistent with the report's heritage-conservation focus but significant in demonstrating that the two most experienced field archaeologists on the island described its monuments without chiefdom-related frameworks.

### Orongo as Competitive Annual Ceremony
The birdman ceremony is described as competitive, temporary, and non-hereditary. "Representatives of the various lineage groups" competed annually; the winner's sponsor occupied "a position of special ceremonial importance" for one year (pp. 52-53). No permanent political office, no chiefly administration, no hereditary authority associated with the ceremony.

### Environmental Decline Narrative
The pre-contact decline narrative (population peak, resource scarcity, warfare, statue toppling) is already present: "over-population resulting in a scarcity of food and eventually fomenting internal warfare" whose "most characteristic goal" was destroying enemy groups' ahu (p. 17). This narrative predates Kirch 1984 by 18 years but is framed as inter-kin-group warfare, not chiefdom collapse.

### Post-Raid Knowledge Loss
The slave raids "carried off" about a thousand islanders, including "all of the maori rongo rongo, or the local priesthood," destroying religious knowledge and literacy (p. 18). This is an early recognition of the catastrophic knowledge loss that makes post-raid ethnographic sources unreliable.

## Source Dependencies

The report cites Thomson (1889), Routledge (1919), Métraux (1936-57), Englert (1948), Lavachery (1935), Heyerdahl and Ferdon (1961), Wright and Diaz (1962), and Skottsberg (1920) for background information. No social organization theoretical framework cited. Empirical/descriptive orientation throughout.

## Significance for Chiefdom Attribution

1. **Pre-chiefdom baseline**: The most comprehensive archaeological heritage assessment before Kirch 1984, written by the two archaeologists with the most field experience. Monument descriptions and interpretations use no chiefdom-related concepts.

2. **Kin-group capacity model**: Ahu size attributed to "numbers and capacity of the kin groups that built them," not to chiefly power. Competition is between kin groups, not directed from above. This passage is cited by Kirch 1984 himself (pp. 270-271) but recontextualized within the chiefdom framework.

3. **Independently working crews**: Mulloy & Figueroa describe ahu variation as resulting from kin-group preferences and competition. Kirch 1984:270-271 cites a later Mulloy & Figueroa publication (1978:134) for "independently working crews, probably sent periodically by their kin groups," demonstrating continuity of this interpretation.

4. **Mulloy school pattern**: This report, combined with Mulloy 1979, Ayres 1973, McCoy 1976 (who concluded chiefdom was "retarded"), and Stevenson 1984 (corporate descent groups without chiefdom terminology), establishes that the entire Mulloy school interpreted Easter Island's archaeological record without chiefdom concepts. The chiefdom attribution came from outside this tradition.

## Ahu Data

- ~350 ahu total on the island
- ~150 image ahu with 1-15 fallen statues
- Platforms range to over 150 meters long
- Statues up to ~10 meters high
- Masonry from large precisely fitted blocks to rough rubble
- Ahu predominantly coastal with some inland examples
- Complicated rebuilding sequences common

## Limitations

- Heritage conservation report, not a political organization study; absence of political terminology may reflect scope rather than analytical position
- Pre-contact decline narrative adopted without independent verification
- Chronology based on limited radiocarbon dates (earliest reliable: Vinapu Ahu No. 2, 857 AD ± 200)
- Poike Ditch date (380 AD ± 100) noted as "seriously questioned"
- Hare paenga described as "traditionally said to have been occupied by members of a priesthood," an unexamined ethnographic assertion
