# Summary: Robinson & Stevenson 2017

**Source**: Robinson, Taylor, and Christopher M. Stevenson. 2017. "The Cult of the Birdman: religious change at 'Orongo, Rapa Nui." *Journal of Pacific Archaeology* 8(2): 88-102.

---

## Overview

Robinson and Stevenson synthesize radiocarbon and obsidian hydration chronological data from the ceremonial site of 'Orongo to reassess the temporal development of the Birdman Cult on Rapa Nui. The paper draws on excavation data from Ferdon (1961), Orliac and Orliac (1996), and Cristino (1995), combined with 23 obsidian hydration dates from Cristino's Test 6. The authors conclude that the conventional chronology, which places the construction of 'Orongo's stone houses and the formalization of the Birdman Cult in the mid-16th century (c. AD 1540), is too early. They propose instead that initial activities at 'Orongo began around AD 1600-1650, with peak activity and probable stone house construction occurring in the late 18th century (c. AD 1795), well within the European contact period. The intensification of the cult is attributed to the combined effects of internal ecological pressures (soil nutrient depletion, drought) and external stresses from European contact, particularly the introduction of disease.

## Methods and Data

The paper compiles all known radiocarbon dates from 'Orongo (Table 1: 16 dates from three investigations) and 23 obsidian hydration dates from Cristino's Test 6 excavation. The radiocarbon dates include both Ferdon's 1960s bulk charcoal samples (unreliable due to potential in-built age and lack of species identification) and Orliac's 1995 dates on short-lived materials (small twigs, more reliable). The obsidian hydration dates were processed using infrared photoacoustic spectroscopy and calibrated against depth-sensitive soil temperature estimates. The authors note that the obsidian dates do not show clear age-depth relationships, indicating mixed deposits that undermine Ferdon's simple stratigraphic interpretation.

## Key Findings

### Revised chronology for 'Orongo

The summed probability distribution of obsidian hydration dates shows a bimodal pattern with peaks at AD 1653 and AD 1795, with the later peak being "much larger" (p. 97). The site's occupational range spans AD 1600 to the early 20th century. Nine of ten reliable radiocarbon dates from the Orliac excavations indicate cooking/burning activity beginning near AD 1650 or later (p. 96). Only one radiocarbon date suggests activity in the mid-1500s.

### Unreliability of Ferdon's chronology

The 16th-century dates from Ferdon (1961) are on bulk charcoal samples that "are not described in detail and are thus unreliable" (p. 94). The connection between dated deposits and house construction events is "inferential" in every case, and "the deposits could have been created by other earth-moving activities or natural processes" (p. 98). Ferdon's extrapolation from Trench 1 dates to construction events in Trench 2 is identified as a weakness.

### Absence of demonstrable links to house construction

None of the excavated stratigraphic deposits are demonstrably linked to the construction of the stone houses. The "construction fill" layers are hypothesized associations based on the coarse nature of sediments, not on direct stratigraphic connections to house foundations.

### Sequence of religious change

The paper proposes a three-phase sequence at 'Orongo: (1) Complex A was oriented toward Makemake ancestor worship and included ahu platform foundations; (2) after Complex A was abandoned, activities shifted to 'Orongo Village (Complex B) and Mata Ngarau (Complex C), with Birdman petroglyphs gradually added to the latter; (3) stone houses were constructed and the Birdman Cult was formalized around AD 1800, after approximately 150 years of preceding ritual activity.

## Relevance to the Chiefdom Question

### What the paper demonstrates

1. The conventional chronology for 'Orongo (c. AD 1540 for house construction and cult formalization) rests on unreliable bulk charcoal dates and inferential stratigraphic connections. More reliable evidence points to a much later timeline.

2. Peak activity at 'Orongo, including the probable construction of stone houses, occurred in the late 18th century, within the European contact period. This means the formalized Birdman Cult, the institution most often cited as evidence for a political transition from hereditary chiefdom to competitive warrior-chief leadership, crystallized after European contact, not during a prehistoric "Decadent Phase."

3. The authors explicitly state that "expressions of an integrated governance, or paramountcy, at the regional level has not been discovered archaeologically or reported in the ethnohistoric oral history" (p. 90). This is a significant admission from a co-author (Stevenson) who has been central to Rapa Nui archaeological research.

4. The authors candidly acknowledge that they "presently have no empirical data to connect" ecological pressures "to the emergence of the Birdman Cult directly" (p. 99). The causal chain from environmental degradation to political-religious transformation remains hypothetical.

### What the paper does not demonstrate

1. A precise date for stone house construction. The evidence points to the late 18th century, but the authors acknowledge that "this analysis has not been able to assign absolute ages to individual stratigraphic deposits, or house construction events" (p. 99).

2. That the Birdman Cult had no prehistoric antecedents. Some ritual activity at 'Orongo appears to date to AD 1600-1650, before European contact in 1722. The argument is that the cult's formalization and intensification were contact-period phenomena, not that the site was unused before contact.

3. What the Birdman Cult's political structure actually was. The paper relies entirely on Metraux (1940) for the characterization of the ariki-mau, matatoa, and the cult's relationship to chiefly authority. These post-slave-raid ethnographic claims are not independently verified.

4. The mechanisms by which European contact produced religious change. The link between disease introduction, social disruption, and cult intensification is hypothesized but not demonstrated by the chronological data.

## Bearing on the Five-Step Circularity

Robinson and Stevenson do not address the circularity problem directly, but their findings are relevant at several points.

The paper accepts the chiefdom attribution as background (citing Kirch 2000), then characterizes the Birdman Cult as a transformation of this chiefdom system using Metraux's post-slave-raid ethnography. This is an instance of the circularity: the Polynesian analogy generates the interpretive framework (hereditary chiefdom), which is then used to characterize the political change (from chiefdom to competitive warrior-chief), which is then used to interpret the archaeological evidence (Orongo as the site of this transition). Yet the paper's own chronological findings suggest the transition occurred after European contact, meaning the contact-era accounts that established the chiefdom model were observing a society already transformed by the very process the model purports to explain.

The paper's most useful finding for our argument is that the formalization of the Birdman institution, the political-religious transformation that the chiefdom-to-warrior-chief narrative describes, is a contact-period phenomenon. If the Europeans who arrived in 1722-1786 were witnessing a society in the early stages of this transformation (not its stable endpoint), then their accounts cannot serve as evidence for either a functioning chiefdom or a post-chiefdom political order. They observed a society under active disruption.

## Limitations and Cautions

The obsidian hydration dates carry non-trivial uncertainties (23-35 year errors on individual dates, plus systematic uncertainty from soil temperature estimation). The radiocarbon dates from the Orliac excavations are more reliable (short-lived samples) but come from deposits that are not demonstrably linked to construction events. The paper's interpretive framework (Lewis's peripheral cult model, Metraux's political categories) is itself dependent on assumptions about pre-contact political organization that the chiefdom question puts at issue. The sample sizes are small and the authors acknowledge that "the arguments are open to revision" (p. 99).
