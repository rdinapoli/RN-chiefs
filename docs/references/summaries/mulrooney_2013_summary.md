# Summary: Mulrooney 2013

**Source**: Mulrooney, Mara A. 2013. "An island-wide assessment of the chronology of settlement and land use on Rapa Nui (Easter Island) based on radiocarbon data." *Journal of Archaeological Science* 40: 4377-4399.

---

## Overview

Mulrooney presents the first island-wide assessment of radiocarbon dates from archaeological contexts on Rapa Nui, compiling a GIS database of over 300 determinations and analyzing their spatial and temporal distributions. The study was designed to test a specific prediction of the collapse scenario: that inland areas, particularly those containing intensified agricultural field systems, were abandoned prior to European contact due to the breakdown of the chiefly economy. The paper assigns reliability classifications to dates following criteria adapted from Spriggs and Anderson (1993) and Wilmshurst et al. (2011), compares archaeological date distributions against artificial datasets reflecting both continuous and collapse-pattern occupation, and individually calibrates dates from inland agricultural and residential contexts to assess whether they predate or postdate the proposed collapse boundary of AD 1680.

## Methods

The database includes 313 dates from archaeological contexts (out of 453 total, excluding palaeoecological and museum specimen dates). Dates were assigned to three reliability classes: Class 1 (n = 51, most reliable: identified short-lived species from secure contexts), Class 2 (n = 200, reliable: unidentified wood charcoal from secure contexts), and Class 3 (n = 62, least reliable: problematic materials, laboratories, or contexts). The island was divided into 500 m distance bands from the coast, and dates were assessed spatially and temporally by distance band and associated feature type. Artificial datasets (one reflecting continuous occupation, one reflecting the expected collapse pattern with abandonment at AD 1680) were created and calibrated for comparison against the archaeological distributions.

## Key Findings

### Settlement continuity, not collapse

The central finding is that the radiocarbon evidence supports continuous settlement and land use from early in the cultural sequence through European contact and the post-contact period. Inland areas up to 3.5 km from the coast show no evidence of the wholesale abandonment predicted by the collapse model. Dates from inland agricultural contexts (both Class 1 and Class 2) have probability distributions that largely fall within or extend into the post-contact period, contradicting the expectation of pre-contact abandonment. Dates from inland residential contexts likewise fail to meet the collapse scenario's expectations, with multiple dates from the HHPA interior falling predominantly in the post-contact period.

### Calibration curve effects

Apparent gaps in the archaeological date distribution (particularly in the latter halves of the 15th and 17th centuries) are shown to mirror features of the radiocarbon calibration curve rather than reflecting real behavioral or demographic changes. An artificial dataset of evenly spaced dates produces the same dips when calibrated, demonstrating that previously claimed evidence for cultural disruption may be a methodological artifact.

### Sampling bias

The study documents a severe coastal bias in Rapa Nui radiocarbon dating: 72.8% of all dates from archaeological contexts come from within 500 m of the coast, and over a quarter of the total sample comes from a single site ('Anakena). This bias means that claims about inland abandonment were made on the basis of an absence of evidence from areas where research was never systematically conducted.

### Chronology of initial settlement

The overall corpus suggests permanent and widespread settlement by at least AD 1200, with initial colonization possibly somewhat earlier. The earliest reliable Class 1 date from 'Anakena has a 1 SD range of AD 1054-1279. This is broadly consistent with the late colonization model (Hunt and Lipo 2006), though the precision limits of the earliest dates do not resolve the early vs. late colonization debate.

## Relevance to the Chiefdom Question

Mulrooney does not directly address political organization or the chiefdom concept, but her findings bear on the chiefdom-collapse narrative in several ways.

First, the collapse scenario that her data contradict is explicitly tied to the chiefdom model. Stevenson and Haoa Cardinali (2008) framed their 5-phase settlement model around the concept of "Chiefdom Integration" (AD 1425-1680) followed by "Warfare and Fragmentation" (AD 1680-1750). The predicted settlement shift (abandonment of inland intensified agricultural areas) was a direct consequence of the supposed "breakdown of the chiefly economy" (p. 4377). Mulrooney's finding that the predicted shift did not occur removes the primary settlement-pattern evidence claimed for the chiefdom's disintegration.

Second, the paper demonstrates that the chronological frameworks used to periodize Rapa Nui prehistory into chiefdom-related phases were built on chronometric data that had never been systematically assessed for reliability. Many determinations used to construct these frameworks are unreliable by modern standards.

Third, the demonstration that calibration curve effects can produce apparent gaps in date distributions that mimic cultural disruption raises the possibility that the chronometric "evidence" for a pre-contact crisis was always an artifact of method rather than a reflection of behavioral change.

## What the Paper Demonstrates vs. What It Does Not

**Demonstrates**:
- Inland areas (1-3.5 km from coast) were not abandoned prior to European contact as predicted by the collapse model
- Intensified agricultural areas in the HHPA and Hiva Hiva continued to be used into the post-contact period
- Apparent temporal gaps in the radiocarbon record can be attributed to calibration curve effects rather than real behavioral changes
- Previous cultural chronologies were built on uncritically assessed chronometric data, including unreliable dates
- There is a severe coastal sampling bias in the Rapa Nui radiocarbon corpus

**Does not demonstrate**:
- What happened in the far upland areas (>3.5 km from coast), where the data are too thin to distinguish between abandonment and insufficient sampling
- The nature of political organization at any period; the paper addresses settlement and land use, not sociopolitical structure
- That no social or political changes occurred in the late pre-contact period; continuity in land use does not preclude political reorganization
- The precise date of initial colonization; the data are consistent with late colonization but cannot rule out earlier settlement

## How the Chronological Evidence Bears on the Chiefdom-Collapse Narrative

The chiefdom-collapse narrative for Rapa Nui posits a sequence: chiefdom integration during the Middle Period, followed by environmental degradation, warfare, the rise of the warrior class, collapse of chiefly authority, and settlement disruption including the abandonment of inland managed agricultural zones. Mulrooney tests the final link in this chain (settlement disruption) and finds it unsupported. This does not disprove the entire narrative, but it removes the settlement-pattern evidence that was supposed to confirm it. If the chiefly economy collapsed, that collapse did not produce the archaeological pattern its proponents predicted. This leaves the collapse narrative without its primary independent archaeological correlate, relying instead on the ethnohistoric and analogical arguments whose circularity our paper examines.
