## Overview

Sahlins's Columbia PhD dissertation (advisor: Julian Steward) argues that differences in social stratification across fourteen Polynesian societies are adaptive responses to differences in productivity, mediated by redistributive economies. Polynesian cultures are treated as "a single cultural species adapting itself to varying local habitats" (p. 5). The more productive the techno-environmental interaction, the greater the surplus, the larger the redistributive network, and the more stratified the society.

The dissertation is organized in two parts. Part One classifies societies by "amount of stratification" using structural criteria (number of status levels), economic criteria (land tenure, redistribution, production supervision, consumption differentials), socio-political criteria (authority, sanctions, marriage), and ceremonial criteria (mana-tabu complex, obeisance, life crisis rites). Fourteen islands are sorted into four groups: I (Hawaii, Tonga, Samoa, Tahiti), IIa (Mangareva, Easter Island, Mangaia, Uvea), IIb (Marquesas, Tikopia, Futuna), III (Pukapuka, Ontong Java, Tokelau). This classification is then correlated with productivity, measured primarily by the size and frequency of redistributive networks.

Part Two examines the "form" of social organization, identifying three types: ramage (branching genealogical descent groups, as defined by Firth 1936), descent line (discrete patrilineal groups with titled positions), and atoll (cross-cutting membership groups adapted to low productivity). Each type is interpreted as adapted to specific resource distribution patterns.

The dissertation proposes a three-stage evolutionary sequence: (I) egalitarian kin societies with reciprocal economies, (II) stratified kin societies with redistributive economies, (III) states. This framework, developed from Polynesian data, becomes the empirical foundation for Service's (1962) band-tribe-chiefdom-state typology. The word "chiefdom" does not appear as a type label in the 1954 dissertation; the equivalent category is "stratified kin society."

## Intellectual Genealogy

Sahlins synthesizes two intellectual streams:

**The "technologists"** (White, Morgan, Marx, Childe): Social systems are functions of technological systems. White provided the premise; Sahlins modified it by reintroducing environment as a variable.

**The "ecologists"** (Steward, Fried, Wissler, Kroeber): Technology and environment together determine cultural variation. Steward's cultural ecology framework is the direct antecedent.

**Additional key influences:**
- **Kirchhoff** (1935, unpublished): The "conical clan" concept, distinguishing ranked from egalitarian kinship, provides the structural theory underlying the entire analysis.
- **Firth** (1936): The "ramage" term and concept from Tikopian ethnography.
- **Polanyi**: The "redistributive form of economic integration" as the mechanism linking surplus to stratification.
- **Landtman** (1938): Precedent for ranking societies by amount of stratification.
- **Williamson** (1924): The secondary compilation on which Sahlins most heavily relies for Polynesian data.

**The chain of influence**: Kirchhoff's conical clan (1935) + Steward's cultural ecology (1938-1955) + White's technological determinism (1943-1949) + Firth's ramage concept (1936) → Sahlins 1954/1958 → Service 1962 (chiefdom typology) → Kirch 1984 (archaeological application to Polynesia).

## Methodology

**Rating system**: Qualitative, not quantitative. Sahlins explicitly states that mathematical measurement is impossible given the ethnographic data (p. 22). Islands are comparatively ranked into four groups based on the reviewer's assessment of the ethnographic literature across twelve criteria. The system is not replicable; two reviewers might classify differently.

**Productivity measurement**: The primary measure is the size of the largest redistributive network multiplied by a frequency factor. This measure is inherently circular: the redistributive network is itself a feature of the stratification system being measured. The secondary measure (labor specialization) is called "pitifully inadequate" by Sahlins himself. The tertiary measure (technology/environment charts) is qualitative.

**Source basis**: "Primarily ethnographical" (p. 15). Sahlins relies on Bishop Museum monographs and Williamson's three-volume compilation. Historical and missionary accounts are largely mediated through these secondary works. For Easter Island specifically, all data comes from post-slave-raid ethnographic sources.

**Key assumption**: The "ethnological present" can be recovered from ethnographic sources by filtering out European-introduced traits (p. 14). No examination of whether post-contact sources can actually accomplish this recovery, particularly for societies that experienced catastrophic demographic disruption between contact and ethnographic recording.

## Easter Island Treatment

### Classification

Easter Island is placed in **Group IIa** for stratification (moderate-high, below Hawaii/Tonga/Samoa but above Marquesas/Tikopia/Futuna and atoll societies) and classified as having **ramage** social organization.

Within Group IIa: Mangareva (most stratified) > Easter Island ≈ Mangaia > Uvea (least stratified).

### Source basis

The Easter Island section (pp. 99-106) and all subsequent Easter Island material depend almost entirely on:
- **Metraux 1940** (*Ethnology of Easter Island*): the dominant source, cited ~30+ times
- **Metraux 1937b** ("Kings of Easter Island"): ~8 citations
- **Thomson 1889** ("Te Pito Te Henua"): ~8 citations
- **Routledge 1919** (*Mystery of Easter Island*): ~3 citations
- **Williamson 1924**: ~3 citations

**No contact-era accounts are cited.** Roggeveen, González, Cook, and La Pérouse do not appear in the Easter Island sections or bibliography. Sahlins's entire knowledge of Easter Island is mediated through post-slave-raid ethnographic compilations.

### Key claims

**Status levels**: Two basic levels (chiefs and non-chiefs), with the kio as a potential unstable third. Chiefs were heads of ~10 tribes (mata). The ariki mau was hereditary paramount religious leader. The birdman was temporary secular leader, a "spoil of war."

**Redistribution**: This is the most consequential passage. Sahlins admits: "With regard to distribution, there is little direct evidence that the ariki mau and the birdmen were focal points of accumulation and redistribution" (p. 101). He then fills the gap explicitly by Polynesian analogy: "We may surmise from comparative data that in most cases these members of the upper status level were acting as focal points in accumulation and redistribution." The customary obligation to give surplus to chiefs "does not seem to have been enforced by confiscation."

**Political authority**: Sahlins reports Metraux's conclusion that government was "decentralized and localized in the hands of the tribal chiefs" (p. 104). Chiefs had "few jurisdictional powers." Private kin-group retaliation was the predominant sanction. Thomson reported the ariki mau "was given no great homage." The only sanctions for violating the ariki mau's personal tabus were supernatural.

**Moai**: "Large-scale enterprises of various types, including the transportation of the famous large stone heads, were initiated by members of the chiefly status level" (p. 100). In the Appendix, Sahlins characterizes moai as communal labor "channelized into non-strategic realms" (p. 433), noting this "may also imply that stratification, in particular instances, can precede communal labor."

**Ecological anomaly**: Sahlins acknowledges Easter Island is anomalous for the ramage category. Its concentrated coastal resources should predict descent line organization. He attributes the ramage to "historical alignments to eastern Polynesia" under "slight selective pressure" (p. 362).

### Eleven documented data gaps

Sahlins himself notes the following gaps or uncertainties in the Easter Island evidence:
1. "Little direct evidence" of redistribution
2. "We do not know" who received the ariki mau's fish distributions
3. "Little information" on what became of goods the ariki mau received
4. "Disposition of the share is not clear" (birdman's feast portion)
5. "We could not learn" whether commoners could be freely dispossessed
6. "Political controls disintegrated shortly after European contact"
7. Government was "decentralized and localized" per Metraux
8. "No great homage" for ariki mau per Thomson
9. "Very little information" on status-differentiated life crisis rites
10. "Not clear whether what was accumulated was for his own support or was widely redistributed" (productivity footnote)
11. Matatoa claim "rests on tenuous evidence"

## The Productivity Argument

Easter Island receives a productivity score of **6,000-8,000†** (Table II, p. 190). The dagger indicates Sahlins's own uncertainty. The score is calculated as:

- Redistributive network size: 3,000-4,000 (entire island, per Metraux's population estimate)
- Frequency factor: 2† ("sporadic," with acknowledged uncertainty)

This places Easter Island 5th-6th of 14 islands, below all Group I societies and below Mangareva within Group IIa.

The environmental profile (Chart 5, p. 217) describes: volcanic island of ~62 sq. miles; no permanent rivers; porous soils; rainfall ~50 in/yr; sweet potato as staple; no breadfruit, coconut, or pig; limited seasonal fishing; no barrier reef. Sahlins notes the "small range of crops and relatively poor fishing conditions" (p. 229).

**Critical circularity**: The productivity measure (redistributive network size) presupposes the existence of redistribution, which is the very feature whose evidence Sahlins admits is lacking for Easter Island. The entire island is treated as a single redistributive unit based on Metraux's post-raid population estimate, multiplied by a frequency factor Sahlins cannot determine.

## Part Two Classification

Easter Island is classified as a **ramage society** alongside Hawaii, Tonga, Tahiti, Mangareva, Mangaia, Tikopia, Marquesas, and New Zealand (p. 243).

The ramage features attributed to Easter Island (p. 283): primogeniture, seniority of the eldest-son line, elaboration of eldest-son life crisis rites, ramage fission, and a genealogical tree encompassing the whole island through ~10 mata.

Sahlins acknowledges this classification is problematic on ecological grounds (pp. 355-356, 361-362). Easter Island's concentrated coastal resources do not predict scattered-resource ramage organization. He resolves this by invoking eastern Polynesian cultural heritage.

## What Sahlins Demonstrates vs. What He Assumes

**Demonstrates:**
- A correlation (at the level of gross groupings) between productivity and stratification across Polynesian societies
- That the form of social organization varies with resource distribution patterns
- That Easter Island's political authority was divided between a sacred hereditary leader (ariki mau) and a temporary secular leader (birdman)
- That the ethnographic evidence for Easter Island is thinner and more uncertain than for most other Polynesian societies in his sample

**Assumes:**
- That post-slave-raid ethnographic sources (Metraux 1940, Thomson 1889, Routledge 1919) can recover the "ethnological present" prior to European influence
- That the redistributive system documented for well-studied islands (Hawaii, Tonga, Tikopia) existed in comparable form on Easter Island, despite admitting the evidence is lacking
- That Easter Island's entire population constituted a single redistributive unit
- That moai transport was organized by chiefly command, without examining the archaeological evidence
- That the ramage organization described by Metraux reflects pre-contact social structure rather than post-contact reconstruction

## Significance for the Chiefdom Attribution Chain

This dissertation is **Step 1** in the Sahlins → Service → Kirch chain that produces the chiefdom attribution for Rapa Nui.

**What Kirch and later scholars inherit from this framework:**

1. **Easter Island placed on a Polynesian stratification gradient**: Group IIa, moderate-high, comparable to Mangareva and Mangaia. This placement becomes the baseline assumption that Easter Island is a "chiefdom" when Service (1962) renames the category.

2. **The redistributive mechanism**: The claim that chiefs served as "focal points of accumulation and redistribution" becomes foundational, despite Sahlins's own admission that the evidence for Easter Island redistribution was lacking.

3. **The ramage classification**: Easter Island as a genealogically ranked ramage society with primogeniture and seniority. This becomes the structural model Kirch applies archaeologically.

4. **Moai as evidence of chiefly command over communal labor**: Sahlins's brief claims about stone head transport (p. 100) and non-strategic communal labor (p. 433) become, in later literature, the central archaeological evidence for chiefdom-level organization.

5. **The post-raid ethnographic sources as the evidential base**: Every claim about Easter Island in this dissertation traces to Metraux, Thomson, Routledge, or Williamson. No contact-era accounts are consulted. This source dependency is inherited by all subsequent scholarship in the chain.

**The feedback loop**: Sahlins uses Polynesian ethnographic data to build the stratification-productivity correlation → Service generalizes this into the chiefdom evolutionary stage → Kirch applies the chiefdom concept back to Polynesia archaeologically → the archaeological interpretation "confirms" the ethnographic framework from which it was derived. The circularity begins here, in the gap where Sahlins admits evidence is lacking and fills it by Polynesian analogy.

**Cross-reference with Kirch 1984** (processed 2026-03-16): Kirch inherits the Group IIA placement and ramage classification unchanged. He does not acknowledge the redistribution gap. The population estimate is raised from 3,000-4,000 to ~7,000 (unsourced). Sahlins's moai claim ("initiated by members of the chiefly status level," p. 100) is contradicted by Kirch's own citations of Mulloy & Figueroa on "independently working crews" (1984, pp. 270-271). Kirch reverses Sahlins's functionalist redistribution argument (1984, p. 281), arguing chiefs were inherited from APS rather than developing because of redistribution needs. Goldman's "Open" classification for Easter Island appears in Kirch's Table 2 (p. 37) but is never discussed in the Easter Island chapter. The contact-era source gap persists: neither Sahlins nor Kirch cites any primary observer of Easter Island before the slave raids.

## Honest Complications

1. **Sahlins is sometimes more careful than later scholars.** He admits the redistribution evidence is lacking, he notes the ariki mau "was given no great homage," he reports Metraux's conclusion of decentralized government, and he challenges the matatoa claim. These qualifications disappear in later citations of his work.

2. **The matatoa challenge is significant.** Sahlins argues the matatoa were likely ramage heads of junior lines who usurped chieftainship, not a separate professional warrior class. This is a nuanced reading that complicates the simple "chief vs. warrior" narrative.

3. **The ecological anomaly is acknowledged.** Sahlins does not force Easter Island into his model; he notes it is a poor fit for the ramage prediction and attributes its classification partly to historical heritage. This honesty is rarely noted by those who cite his framework.

4. **The "other factors constant" proviso is real.** Sahlins acknowledges that alternative mechanisms for managing surplus exist and that his Polynesian correlation may not generalize. This caveat is absent from Service's universalized chiefdom category.

5. **The Group IIb societies challenge the Easter Island placement.** The Marquesas, rated Group IIb (below Easter Island), had a richer ethnographic record showing very weak chiefly authority. Multiple early observers described Marquesan government as "patriarchal," "democratic," or a "republic." If Easter Island had comparable observer documentation, it might have been rated similarly.

## Comparison with 1957 JPS Article

The 1957 *Journal of the Polynesian Society* article ("Differentiation by Adaptation in Polynesian Societies," Vol. 66, No. 3, pp. 291-300) condenses Part Two of the dissertation only (form of social organization: ramage vs. truncated descent line). Part One (amount of stratification, productivity measurement, island-by-island analysis) is not covered. Full claims extraction at `docs/references/claims/sahlins_1957_claims.md`.

### Checklist Results

1. **Easter Island stratification rating**: Not addressed. The Group IIa classification does not appear in the article, which covers only organizational form, not stratification level.
2. **Productivity measure**: Not addressed. No productivity scores or redistributive network calculations appear.
3. **Ramage classification**: Retained unchanged. Easter Island is listed among ramage societies (p. 291). The ecological anomaly is retained: Easter Island is one of three exceptions (with Tikopia and Uvea) where ecological conditions are "intermediate" and ramage organization is explained by cultural heritage from central Polynesia (p. 295).
4. **Redistribution gap**: Not addressed for Easter Island. The article discusses redistribution as a general ramage feature (p. 294) without noting the "little direct evidence" admission from the dissertation (p. 101). The critical data gap is invisible.
5. **Sources**: Drastically reduced. The only Easter Island sources cited are Routledge 1919:218 (fn. 9, for exchange practices) and Sahlins 1955, "Esoteric Efflorescence in Easter Island" (*American Anthropologist* 57:1045-1052). No Metraux, Thomson, or Williamson. No contact-era accounts.
6. **Qualifications**: The ecological anomaly is retained (p. 295). All ten other data gaps documented in the dissertation are absent. This is not dishonest (the article covers only Part Two and directs readers to the monograph) but means scholars citing the published version inherit a framework stripped of its caveats.

### Additional Finding

The article's discussion of catastrophic population loss on atolls (p. 296) provides a direct parallel to the 1862-1863 slave raids. Sahlins describes how the 1903 Raroia hurricane caused "profound cultural changes" through "the loss of old time political and religious leaders." If hurricanes produce this effect, the slave raids (which killed or removed the ariki mau and most knowledgeable elders) would have been at least as disruptive, undermining the assumption that post-raid ethnographic sources can recover pre-contact organization.
