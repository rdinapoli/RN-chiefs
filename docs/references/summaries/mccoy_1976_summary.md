# Summary: McCoy 1976

**Source**: McCoy, Patrick Carlton. 1976. *Easter Island Settlement Patterns in the Late Prehistoric and Protohistoric Periods*. Bulletin Five, Easter Island Committee, International Fund for Monuments Inc. 163 pp.

---

## Overview

McCoy's monograph presents the results of a systematic archaeological survey of the south corner of Easter Island conducted in 1968, covering approximately 1,973 hectares and recording 1,738 sites in six quadrangles. It is the first comprehensive settlement pattern analysis for Rapa Nui and the first study to attempt an archaeological model of Easter Island's social organization. The survey documented households, settlements, ahu, burials, agricultural features, and their spatial relationships, producing four settlement models: three synchronic (household, settlement, and areal patterns) and one developmental model covering ca. A.D. 1500-1865.

## Significance for the Chiefdom Attribution

McCoy 1976 is routinely cited as one of the earliest archaeological studies to frame Rapa Nui within a chiefdom model. This characterization is inaccurate. McCoy's actual conclusion on the chiefdom question is explicit and unambiguous: "If there existed any tendencies toward the development of a chiefdom, the political flux caused by the ascendancy of the warrior class to positions of authority clearly retarded it" (p. 145). McCoy describes a ranked society under environmental and demographic stress, one in which the warrior class (*matatoa*) had displaced hereditary political authority and was driving social fragmentation, not consolidation.

The gap between McCoy's actual conclusions and how subsequent scholars have used his work is significant. His settlement data (house type distributions, site density patterns, ahu-settlement relationships) was later reread through Kirch's chiefdom framework as confirming what McCoy said did not develop.

## Key Findings

### Settlement pattern

Villages were partially dispersed settlements composed of a central core of communal houses, communal structures, and an ahu on the seaward side, with more scattered family residences further inland (p. 109). The seaward residential complex was associated with high-ranking persons; interior habitations belonged to commoners. This spatial pattern was constant across the survey area.

Site density decreased with increasing distance from the shoreline, with a sharp boundary between high and low density zones at 1,000-1,500 meters from the coast (p. 135). Density correlated directly with accessibility to drinking water, marine resources, and the community center.

### Household organization

The household was the smallest unit of production and consumption (p. 148). Gardens and chicken houses were rarely far from dwellings. McCoy concluded that "production of individual households was supervised by household heads rather than directly through chiefs" (p. 148, citing Sahlins 1958: 55).

### Social stratification evidence

McCoy identified a "dual status system" in domestic architecture, distinguishing between commoners' houses (simple elliptical thatch huts) and chiefly/priestly houses (*hare paenga*, with dressed curbstone foundations and larger pavements) (p. 149). The classification depends entirely on ethnohistoric descriptions of who occupied which house type, not on independent archaeological evidence (e.g., differential artifact assemblages or mortuary data).

The absence of garden enclosures and chicken houses at sites with *hare paenga* was interpreted as evidence that "upper status members were exempt from production" (p. 150). This interpretation is circular: houses are first classified as "chiefly" based on ethnohistoric accounts, then the absence of production features is treated as confirming elite status.

### Tribal territories

McCoy maps tribal territories from Routledge (1919) and Métraux (1940), then superimposes these on his archaeological density data. He finds correlations between ethnographic tribal boundaries and site density patterns. The analysis does not demonstrate that these correlations are independent; the territories are ethnographic constructs, not archaeologically derived.

### Ahu

43 ahu were recorded in the survey area. McCoy classified them into seven types: image ahu (18), semi-pyramidal (9), ahu poepoe (3), rectangular (1), upright slabs (3), enclosed court (1), and unclassified (10). He proposed a relative chronology based on masonry styles and statue sizes, with vertical/well-fitted masonry being the oldest and semi-pyramidal forms being Late Period conversions after statue toppling.

### Environmental pressure and warfare

McCoy's developmental model postulates population pressure from ca. A.D. 1500-1600 as the antecedent cause of warfare, which in turn drove adaptive and maladaptive settlement changes. He estimates a maximum population of 7,000+ (based on 18th-19th century estimates and carrying capacity calculations). Environmental deterioration, particularly forest depletion, loss of wood for boats and houses, and soil erosion, prompted innovation in subsistence structures (chicken houses, garden enclosures) and contributed to intensified warfare.

Warfare dispersed populations, destroyed property, and shifted settlement patterns. The warrior class (*matatoa*) rose to political prominence, displacing hereditary authority. McCoy argued this secular political shift "clearly retarded" any chiefdom development (p. 145).

## Methodological Significance

McCoy is explicit about his methodology: "Ethnographic data is the basis for much of the temporal-functional interpretation and societal reconstruction in this study" (p. 2). He describes his approach as projecting ethnohistoric data back in time and then checking archaeological data against this baseline (p. 70). This is precisely the methodology our paper identifies as creating the circularity in the chiefdom attribution.

McCoy was aware of the circularity risk and claimed his site cluster analysis was conducted independently of ethnohistoric data (p. 117). However, his interpretive categories (commoner vs. chiefly houses, tribal territories, redistribution centers) are all ethnohistorically derived.

He also noted that Sahlins (1958) himself treated Easter Island as an "exception" to his model of ranked lineage systems, explaining Easter Island's settlement pattern as a matter of "continuity in a traditional system" rather than a product of the environmental variables that predicted settlement patterns elsewhere in Polynesia (p. 152).

## Notable Anticipations

McCoy's observation that water was "the major limiting factor in the exploitation of uninhabited lands" (p. 142) and that settlement density correlated with freshwater access anticipates DiNapoli et al. (2019) by four decades. His emphasis on household-level production "supervised by household heads rather than directly through chiefs" (p. 148) is compatible with the collective action model later developed by the same research group.

## What McCoy Does and Does Not Demonstrate

**Demonstrates**: Systematic spatial patterning in settlement distribution; correlation between site density and coastal proximity; house type variation; dispersed settlement pattern with ahu-centered communal zones; environmental constraints on settlement.

**Does not demonstrate**: That the dual house type system corresponds to a hereditary social hierarchy (inferred from ethnohistory); that ahu functioned as redistribution centers (asserted, not demonstrated archaeologically); that tribal territories visible in ethnographic accounts correspond to archaeological realities (superimposed, not independently derived); that the settlement pattern reflects a chiefdom organization (explicitly denied by McCoy).
