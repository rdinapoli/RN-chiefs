## Overview

This is the most comprehensive single-volume treatment of Rapa Nui archaeology with the chiefdom as its organizing framework. Published for both academic (Smithsonian) and general (British Museum) audiences, it consolidates Van Tilburg's 1986 UCLA doctoral dissertation into a widely accessible synthesis. The book covers settlement, the birdman cult, built environment, political organization, subsistence and ecology, aesthetics (including rongorongo), moai iconography and transport, and an epilogue on the island's cultural heritage. It sits at a pivotal node in the chiefdom attribution chain: the moment when the tentative hypothesis of the 1986 dissertation hardens into the default description of Rapa Nui political organization.

---

## Relevance to Argument

Van Tilburg 1994 is Stage 4-5 evidence in the chiefdom attribution chain. In the self-referential citation network (Kirch 1984 -> Stevenson 1986 -> Van Tilburg 1986 -> Van Tilburg 1994 -> Martinsson-Wallin 1994 -> all subsequent work), this book is where the chiefdom label becomes the default. Anyone citing Van Tilburg 1994 for Rapa Nui political organization inherits the chiefdom framework as established knowledge. The book's wide distribution (two prestigious presses) and accessible format made it the standard reference for non-specialist readers, policy documents, and subsequent syntheses.

For the paper's Section II ("The Chiefdom Attribution: Origins and Development"), Van Tilburg 1994 documents the final stage of progressive hardening: what was "assumed" in 1986 becomes definitional fact in 1994 ("the Rapa Nui was a tribal society organised as a Polynesian chiefdom," p. 94). No new archaeological evidence supports this transition. The hardening is rhetorical, not evidential.

## The Chiefdom Framework as Presented

Van Tilburg opens Chapter 6 with a definitional statement: "The Rapa Nui was a tribal society organised as a Polynesian chiefdom" (p. 94). The chiefdom is neither hypothesis nor interpretive framework but established fact. She describes a simple chiefdom (few thousand people, one level above local community, graduated status ranking) and assigns Rapa Nui to this category using a population estimate of 7,000-9,000 (contradicted by Davis et al. 2024: max ~3,900).

The political organization as described: the Miru mata held paramount status through the ariki mau, a hereditary spiritual leader whose person contained dangerous mana. The ariki mau was surrounded by tangata honui, received first fruits, and distributed fish. Below the ariki paka aristocracy were the priestly ivi atua, the tangata rongorongo, the carver guild (tangata maori), the matatoa warriors, commoners (hurumanu), and temporarily landless kio. An east-west confederacy divided the island into higher-ranked Ko Tu'u and lower-ranked Hotu Iti.

Every element of this description derives from post-slave-raid ethnographic sources, primarily Métraux 1940. Van Tilburg herself acknowledges "there is no evidence, however, that Europeans ever actually saw the ariki mau" (p. 96). This admission is epistemologically devastating to the chiefdom model but its implications are not drawn.

## Progressive Hardening: 1986 → 1987 → 1994

The transition from the 1986 dissertation through the 1987 journal article to the 1994 book is the clearest documented case of progressive hardening in the chiefdom attribution chain.

**The chiefdom label**: In 1986, the Polynesian cultural matrix was explicitly "assumed to underlie and illuminate" the evidence (p. 299). In 1994, "Precontact Rapa Nui was a traditional, hierarchical society" (p. 30) is stated as background fact. The assumption has become invisible.

**Goldman's "Open" classification**: In 1986, Goldman's classification that Easter Island "had never evolved a central authority" (1970:94) was acknowledged but reframed as applying only to the late period (Stage 3+). In 1994, Goldman is dismissed as "overly simplistic and not fully accurate" (p. 100). The most significant counter-classification within the Polynesian comparative tradition is moved from acknowledged-but-reframed to explicitly rejected.

**Miller & Tilley theoretical engine**: In 1986, the ideology/power framework from Miller & Tilley 1984 was the explicit interpretive apparatus: "the origin, purpose and function of ideology [is] the generation of ideas which misrepresent reality" (1986, p. 344). In 1994, Miller & Tilley disappears entirely from the bibliography. The theoretical infrastructure that generated the chiefdom interpretation becomes invisible.

**Moai-as-paramount-chief**: In 1986, Van Tilburg offered "two possible suggestions" for what the statues represent (p. 348): individual lineage ancestors or the ariki mau. In 1994, the hypothesis is retained and strengthened to "I believe."

**New evidence**: None. The same Métraux passages, the same Polynesian analogies, the same iconographic interpretations appear in 1994 as in 1986. The progressive hardening is a rhetorical phenomenon, not an evidential one.

## The Moai-as-Chiefly-Power Argument

Chapter 9 ("Spirit in the Stone") presents the most sustained interpretation of moai as expressions of chiefly power. The moai-as-paramount-chief hypothesis from the 1986 dissertation survives and is strengthened. Three new interpretive concepts appear:

1. **"The Perfect Chief"**: Moai represent the Polynesian mythic hero Tahaki, an idealized fusion of genealogical and achieved qualities. This synthesizes the 1986 four-stage power shift model into a single concept.

2. **"Neutral vessel"**: Moai as a flexible container of mana that could be activated or deactivated through ritual. This actually weakens the paramount-chief hypothesis by making moai a communal resource rather than a symbol of specific authority. It is more compatible with collective action.

3. **"Sky Propper"**: A cosmogonic framework linking moai to sky separation myth and cosmic order.

All iconographic interpretations (head as sacred mana, hands as tapu powers, hami as sovereignty, dorsal design as birdman/sovereignty, pukao as acquired mana, red color as sacred) derive from Polynesian analogy, not from independent Rapa Nui evidence. The interpretive method from 1986 continues: identify material feature -> find Polynesian analogue -> import social meaning -> present as "archaeological evidence."

Van Tilburg's own statue distribution data shows southeast peripheral lineages were "more productive" than those near the supposed Miru power center, undermining centralized chiefly command.

## Source Dependency

**Post-slave-raid sources**: Every political-organization claim rests on Métraux 1940, Routledge 1919, and/or Englert 1948, all recorded 25-80+ years after the slave raids reduced the population from ~3,000 to fewer than 200. No political claim is supported by contact-era evidence alone.

**Contact-era evidence**: Van Tilburg uses Cook's "areekees or kings" report (via the dissertation) and provides stronger methodological caveats about contact-era sources than the 1986 dissertation. But she also admits Europeans never saw the ariki mau, never saw the social class system in operation, and "found it sometimes difficult to distinguish the higher-ranked individuals from the more ordinary" (p. 96).

**Polynesian analogies**: Extended from the 1986 dissertation. Tikopia (Firth) for expert/chief cooperation and sacred canoe ownership; Tahiti for marae and Tahaki; Marquesas for haka'iki contrast; Rotuma for sau comparison (NEW in 1994); Mangaia for birdman cult parallel (extensively developed in Chapter 6). None tested against independent Rapa Nui evidence.

**NEW in 1994: the sau comparison.** Van Tilburg compares the ariki mau to the Rotuman sau, who "exercised no direct political power" (p. 97). This comparison is valuable and honest, but its implications are not followed through: if the ariki mau was like the sau, the chiefdom model predicated on the ariki mau as political authority collapses.

## Claims Contradicted by Current Evidence

1. **Population 7,000-9,000** (p. 94): Davis et al. 2024 (max ~3,900), Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 (~3,000).

2. **Transport: supine with rollers, 55-70 people** (Ch. 10): Lipo et al. 2013 (18 people walking upright).

3. **Centralized chiefly command of production**: Lipo et al. 2025 (30 independent workshops, 4-6 carvers per statue at Rano Raraku).

4. **Ecocide/deforestation narrative** (Ch. 10): DiNapoli et al. 2020 (steady ahu construction tempo), Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 (no population collapse).

5. **Implicit assumption of pre-contact collapse**: Mulrooney 2013 (settlement continuity), DiNapoli et al. 2020 (no disruption in ahu construction rate).

Van Tilburg's warfare skepticism ("virtually no evidence of warfare," p. 116), mata'a caution ("facile to continue to cite the huge number of mata'a alone as evidence," p. 117), and cannibalism caution ("remains to be demonstrated archaeologically," p. 118) have been confirmed by subsequent research (Lipo et al. 2016, DiNapoli et al. 2021).

## Connections to Other Sources

**Kirch 1984**: Remains the primary theoretical framework. Van Tilburg works within the APS inheritance argument without modification. However, Kirch's own limiting statement (ahu "do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority," 1984:270), quoted in the 1986 dissertation, has vanished from the 1994 book.

**Stevenson 1984/1986**: Cited for population estimates and settlement data but Stevenson's corporate descent group model (no chiefdom terminology, Goldman framework, explicit rejection of unilinear evolution) is not cited.

**Goldman 1970**: Explicitly cited and explicitly dismissed ("overly simplistic"). Goldman's classification that Easter Island "had never evolved a central authority" receives the most direct engagement of any counter-evidence in the book, but is rejected without presenting Goldman's full evidentiary basis (redistribution with commoner heads, no central authority over monument construction, separatist political tradition).

**Sahlins 1954/1958**: Cited via Kirch's summary for the Group IIA placement and for redistribution theory. Sahlins's admission of "little direct evidence" for redistribution (1954, p. 101) is not cited. Sahlins 1955 (the only Sahlins article on Easter Island) remains uncited.

**Valeri 1985**: Not cited. The detailed Hawaiian system that provides the content of the chiefdom model remains implicit.

**Van Tilburg 1986**: Explicitly cited as foundation. The 1994 book is a direct revision of the dissertation, with the same interpretive framework but elevated epistemic status.

## Bibliographic Profile

~300-350 references (roughly tripled from the 1986 dissertation's ~115). Key additions: Earle 1991 (updated chiefdom theory), Sahlins 1985 (historiographic framework), Green 1993 (Lapita/East Polynesian settlement), Bahn & Flenley 1992 (ecocide narrative). Self-citation pattern: 13-15 entries, the densest authorial cluster in the bibliography.

Persistent absences: Service 1962, Sahlins 1955, Sahlins 1963, Sahlins & Service 1960, Feinman & Neitzel 1984. The chiefdom concept's genealogy and its critics remain invisible throughout.

## Internal Tensions

The book contains several passages that in tension with the chiefdom framework:

1. **Chapter 2 methodological caveats** about post-raid sources (pp. 29-39) are among the most explicit in the literature but are not applied to the political-organization claims in subsequent chapters.

2. **Europeans never saw the ariki mau** (p. 96) undermines the entire preceding description of chiefly authority.

3. **Sau comparison** (pp. 96-97): ariki mau as ritual figure without political power, directly analogous to the Rotuman sau.

4. **Ch. 6 n. 37**: Moai production described as "society-wide collaborative religious coping enacted on a lineage or multi-lineage political level," language far more compatible with collective action than chiefdom command.

5. **"Implied gain" over coercion** (Ch. 10): Voluntary affiliation for spiritual benefit is the core mechanism of collective action theory.

6. **"Neutral vessel" concept** (Ch. 9): Moai as flexible communal resource rather than symbol of specific authority.

7. **Warfare skepticism** (pp. 116-117): "virtually no evidence of warfare in the sense of large-scale confrontations."

8. **Social complexity "not clearly articulated research question"** (pp. 103-104): Admits the chiefdom label is underexamined even as it is used definitionally.

These tensions are unresolved because the chiefdom label operates as presupposition rather than hypothesis. It structures the analysis but is never tested by it.
