## Overview

This article is the published journal version of key elements from Van Tilburg's 1986 PhD dissertation, integrated with Georgia Lee's petroglyph data from her concurrent UCLA dissertation. It introduces the "symbolic stratigraphy" concept: a temporal linkage between the statue cult and the birdman cult, demonstrated through petroglyph superimposition on red scoria elements (pukao and ahu facia) at 27 documented sites. Published in *World Archaeology*'s rock art special issue.

The article is important for the chiefdom attribution argument because it represents the moment when the dissertation's interpretive claims entered the peer-reviewed journal literature, making them citable. However, the published version is significantly more restrained than the dissertation: the moai-as-paramount-chief hypothesis, the Miller & Tilley ideology framework, and Goldman's "Open" classification all drop out.

---

## Core Argument

Van Tilburg and Lee argue that:

1. The statue cult and birdman cult are "variants on the same cosmic theme" (p. 136), not unrelated phenomena.
2. Bird symbolism is "inherent in anthropomorphic statue design" (p. 136), and the birdman symbol "evolved in situ" (p. 136, Lee's contribution).
3. Petroglyphs superimposed on red scoria pukao and ahu facia at six sites constitute "purposeful, non-random, re-use" (p. 141) that links the two cults temporally and ideologically.
4. The lunate petroglyphs on red scoria are interpreted as "boats" carrying "complex meanings" including chiefly/priestly status, lineage affiliation, death ritual, and mana (pp. 144-147).
5. The pukao represent "a shift in ideological emphasis from chiefly mana, which is inherent in ancestral proximity, to one of chiefly mana which is acquired and demonstrated through prowess" (p. 146).

## New Data (Not in Van Tilburg 1986 Dissertation)

1. **27-site overlap inventory** (Table 1): Sites where both petroglyphs and statues occur, compiled from the combined Van Tilburg + Lee databases.
2. **Six-site red-scoria petroglyph analysis** (Table 2): Detailed inventory of petroglyph forms on pukao and ahu facia at Vinapu, Akahanga, One Makihi, Nau Nau, and Puna Pau quarry.
3. **Ahu Akahanga detailed description**: Three platforms, seventeen statues, petroglyph panels A-G mapped and described (Figs. 3-6).
4. **Puna Pau quarry inventory**: 22 pukao documented with petroglyphs.
5. **Lee's birdman evolution claim**: "the birdman symbol evolved in situ" (p. 136), based on Lee's dissertation analysis.
6. **Gypsum identification**: White substance on Tongariki moai analyzed as gypsum (S. Williams, pers. comm. 1984).

## Relationship to Van Tilburg 1986 Dissertation

The article condenses and publishes the "symbolic stratigraphy" concept from the dissertation's Chapter VII while integrating Lee's independent petroglyph data. Key differences:

**Present in the article and the dissertation:**
- Ahu/moai as male, lineage-based ideology (p. 134 = diss. p. 350)
- Statues as genealogical connection to founding ancestor (p. 134 = diss. p. 353)
- Pukao as shift from inherent to acquired mana (p. 146 = diss. p. 339)
- Red color associated with chiefly power and mana (p. 142 = diss. passim)
- Non-linear statue-to-birdman transition (p. 136 = diss. p. 312)

**Present in the dissertation but ABSENT from the article:**
- Moai-as-paramount-chief hypothesis (diss. pp. 348-349)
- Miller & Tilley ideology framework (diss. pp. 344-345)
- Goldman "Open" classification engagement (diss. p. 343)
- Firth/Tikopia sacred canoe ownership model (diss. pp. 353-354)
- Explicit "chiefdom" terminology
- Region-wide integration beyond lineage level (diss. p. 350)
- Stevenson 1984 population estimate

The published article is a more conservative document than the dissertation. The interpretive framework (Miller & Tilley, Hodder, Tilley) that drives the dissertation is reduced to a single Hodder citation for the definition of "symbol." The Polynesian comparative ethnography (Firth, Williamson, Handy) that supplied the social meanings in the dissertation is largely absent. What remains are the interpretive conclusions presented as descriptive archaeological observations, without the theoretical apparatus that generated them.

## Bibliographic Profile

The bibliography (~35 works) is dominated by:
- **Easter Island archaeological data** (Mulloy series, Ayres, McCoy, Cristino, Heyerdahl & Ferdon, Stevenson 1986): ~60%
- **Van Tilburg 1986a and Lee 1986a** (the two dissertations): central
- **Polynesian comparative ethnography**: Handy 1927 (Marquesas), Firth 1967 (Tikopia, only in bibliography context, not in interpretive argument), Hadden & Hornell 1975 (canoes), Linton 1925 (Marquesas)
- **Post-slave-raid sources**: Métraux 1940, Thomson 1891, Routledge 1919

**Absent**: Service 1962, Sahlins (all), Goldman 1970, Miller & Tilley 1984, Feinman & Neitzel 1984, Stevenson 1984 dissertation. Same bibliographic profile as the dissertation in lacking the Sahlins-Service neoevolutionary tradition, but differs in also lacking the post-processual theoretical framework.

## Relevance to the Chiefdom Attribution Argument

**Moderate relevance.** The article is significant for three reasons:

1. **It brings "chiefly mana" framing into the journal literature** without using the word "chiefdom." Claims about "chiefly power and mana" (p. 142), "chiefly mana" (p. 146), "class/status symbol" (p. 146), and "differential access to important resources" (p. 146) embed the chiefdom interpretation in archaeological description without explicitly invoking the neoevolutionary category.

2. **It documents the publication pathway from dissertation to journal.** The most ambitious chiefdom claims (moai-as-paramount-chief, region-wide integration beyond lineage level) do not survive into the published literature. This is consistent with peer review disciplining the strongest claims while allowing the general framework through.

3. **Polynesian analogy as the operative mechanism.** Every interpretive claim about symbolic meaning is imported from Polynesian parallels, not derived from Easter Island evidence. The statue-damage interpretation comes from the Marquesas (Linton 1925), the mana-transfer model from East Polynesia generally (Handy 1927), the boat symbolism from comparative Polynesian canoe forms, and the red-color-as-chiefly-power claim from Polynesia-wide ethnography. None of these analogies is tested against independent evidence that Easter Island's social organization matched the source societies.

## Connections to Other Processed Sources

- **Van Tilburg 1986 (dissertation)**: This article condenses and publishes the "symbolic stratigraphy" portion. The dissertation's stronger chiefdom claims do not appear.
- **Kirch 1984**: Cited for dating and mana definition, but not for the chiefdom framework that the dissertation relies on explicitly.
- **Lee 1986a**: Co-author's dissertation; provides the petroglyph data that makes the "symbolic stratigraphy" argument possible.
- **Stevenson 1986**: Cited and corrected for terminology (facia vs. lintel/cornice).
- **Lipo et al. 2025**: Decentralized quarry production contradicts any model requiring centralized control of moai production.
- **DiNapoli et al. 2020**: Steady ahu construction tempo is consistent with Van Tilburg's "non-linear transition" and rejection of "sudden abandonment."
