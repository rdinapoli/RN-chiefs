# Source Summary: La Pérouse 1786

## Overview

Jean-François de Galaup, comte de La Pérouse, visited Rapa Nui on April 9-10, 1786 during his circumnavigation. Two frigates (Boussole and Astrolabe), ~70 persons landed. The expedition included naturalists, artists, a botanist, and clergy. This was the most extensive and best-provisioned of the four major contact-era visits, but lasted only approximately 10-11 hours ashore. La Pérouse was well-read in Cook's and Forster's accounts.

## Sources Consulted

- La Pérouse, *A Voyage Round the World* (1799 Milet-Mureau English translation), Vol. I, Chapters IV-V (pp. 312-334). Da Capo Press / Bibliotheca Australiana #27 facsimile reprint. Two overlapping Easter Island chapter extracts in the repo.

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**Active search for chiefs yielded negative results** (p. 320): La Pérouse identified individuals who "appeared to exercise a small degree of authority," distributed medals to them, then "soon perceived that their pre-eminence consisted in nothing but their superiority of talents, and greater dexterity in promoting the pillage." Those who "pretended to defend us" and chased thieves had "not the least intention of overtaking the delinquents." This is the strongest negative finding from any contact-era account: an active search with a negative result, not mere silence.

**Explicit statements against chiefdom**: "All conditions are equal" (p. 323). "No chief at present exists of sufficient consideration" (p. 322). "They have no need of a chief" (p. 323). No chief's dwelling exists (p. 324). A "kind of chief" who received livestock from de Langle "robbed him of his handkerchief with the other" hand (p. 326).

**Communal organization**: Productions of the earth "in common" (p. 323). Houses common "to a whole village or district." The large 310-foot house "is not the dwelling of a chief." Cook's interpretation of a "proprietor" chief reinterpreted: difficulty procuring food reflected the need for "almost a general consent," not chiefly ownership.

**No coercive authority**: Among ~800 islanders and ~70 French, no individual could restrain theft, enforce norms, or exercise command. No weapons except 3-4 wooden clubs. No organized military response to the French.

**Critical methodological disclaimer** (p. 323): "I can offer nothing but conjectures respecting the manners of this people, of whose language I knew nothing, and whom I only saw for a single day."

**Population ~2,000**. Monument construction ceased; current burials are individually-built cairns. No religious institutions observed. Skilled agriculture (ash manure, organized planting) but no centralized management.

**17 significant absences documented**, including no effective authority figures, no chief of sufficient consideration, no traces of religion, no warfare, no weapons, no chiefly dwelling, no authority over women, no surplus production, no redistribution, and no social mechanism for property enforcement.

## Relevance to the Paper

La Pérouse's account is the most detailed contact-era observation and provides the strongest direct evidence against the chiefdom attribution. His active, failed search for chiefs is not absence of evidence but evidence of absence. His methodological disclaimer ("nothing but conjectures... language I knew nothing... only saw for a single day") directly supports the paper's epistemological argument: if the most extensive contact-era visitor acknowledged his claims were conjectural, the inferential chain from contact-era accounts to prehistoric political organization is even more attenuated than usually acknowledged. La Pérouse was a French naval officer accustomed to hierarchy who was actively seeking authority figures to negotiate with; his failure to find them is evidence from a sympathetic searcher.
