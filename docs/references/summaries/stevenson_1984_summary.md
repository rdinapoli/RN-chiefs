# Source Summary: Stevenson 1984

**Source**: Christopher M. Stevenson, "Corporate Descent Group Structure in Easter Island Prehistory." PhD dissertation, Pennsylvania State University, 1984. 377 pp.

**PDF**: `docs/references/pdfs/stevenson_1984/Stevenson_1984.pdf`

## Overview

Stevenson's Penn State PhD dissertation is the first work to systematically connect archaeological settlement data to a model of political organization on Rapa Nui. Drawing on 65 ceremonial centers and 184 residential sites from the southern coastal region, the dissertation constructs a 1300-year settlement sequence (13 one-hundred-year phases) using obsidian hydration dating. The analytical framework comes from British social anthropology (Fortes, Firth) and corporate group archaeology (Hayden & Cannon 1982), not from neoevolutionary typology. The dissertation describes four autonomous political units emerging by approximately 1600 AD that revert to a non-ranked corporate structure after 1680 AD.

Crucially, the 1984 dissertation does not use "chiefdom" as an analytical category. Service 1962 does not appear in the bibliography. Sahlins 1963 and Kirch 1984 are also absent. The theoretical framework is entirely independent of the band-tribe-chiefdom-state typology. The "Chiefdom Integration" framing appears only in later publications (Stevenson 1986 in Kirch's *Island Societies* volume, then 1997, 2002), representing a progressive hardening from tentative corporate group model to confident chiefdom assertion that parallels the Sahlins 1954-to-1958 pattern.

---

## Significance for the Chiefdom Attribution Chain

This dissertation is critical for **Steps 3-4** of the five-step circularity. It is the first attempt to use archaeological settlement data to infer political organization on Rapa Nui. Three features make it significant for our argument:

1. **The chiefdom framework is absent.** The dissertation operates within a corporate descent group model, uses African ethnographic analogues (Ibo, Dahomey) for corporate group identification rather than Polynesian chiefdom models, and explicitly rejects unilinear evolutionary assumptions (p. 50).

2. **No institutionalized rank differences are found.** The archaeological data as analyzed in 1984 do not support hierarchical political organization. Corporate group strength is characterized as "relatively weak" for the majority of the settlement sequence (p. 176), with descent groups exercising a "significant amount of autonomy."

3. **The transition to chiefdom framing occurs after 1984.** The 1986 chapter in Kirch's *Island Societies* volume represents the first reframing of these data within a chiefdom model. This transition, from a cautious corporate group analysis to a confident chiefdom attribution, is itself evidence of interpretive drift under the influence of the dominant Polynesian framework.

## Theoretical Framework

### Corporate descent groups, not chiefdoms

Stevenson's framework draws on Fortes (1953, 1959) for descent group theory, Firth (1936, 1957) for ramage structure, Kirchhoff (1955) for the conical clan, and Hayden & Cannon (1982) for archaeological identification of corporate groups. The organizing question is not "what evolutionary stage did Easter Island reach?" but rather "what is the structure and trajectory of corporate descent groups as reflected in settlement and ceremonial architecture?"

### Explicit rejection of unilinear assumption

The dissertation states that it is "not assumed a priori that Easter Island headed toward increasing complexity" (p. 50). This is a methodological commitment absent from the later chiefdom literature that cites Stevenson's data.

### African, not Polynesian, analogues

Corporate group identification criteria are drawn from Ibo and Dahomey ethnographic cases, not from Polynesian chiefdom models. This is a striking departure from the Sahlins-Kirch tradition in which Polynesian societies serve as both data and analogue.

## Key Findings

### Settlement sequence

The 13-phase settlement maps document the spatial growth and differentiation of residential and ceremonial sites along the southern coast. Key phases:

- **Phases 1-5 (ca. AD 400-900)**: Initial settlement, low density, few ahu
- **Phases 6-8 (ca. AD 900-1200)**: Expansion, increasing ceremonial center construction
- **Phase 9 (ca. AD 1200-1300)**: Four politically autonomous groups identifiable from distribution of multiple lineage centers (p. 163)
- **Phases 10-12 (ca. AD 1300-1600)**: Peak settlement density, elite village formation
- **Phase 13 (ca. AD 1600-1700)**: Reversion to non-ranked corporate structure (p. 168)

### Four autonomous political units

By Phase 9, the settlement data are interpreted as showing four politically autonomous groups, each centered on its own ceremonial centers and residential clusters. These are not hierarchically integrated; they operate independently.

### Reversion to non-ranked structure

After approximately 1680 AD, the system reverts to non-ranked corporate structure (p. 168). This finding is based on burial data and evidence of ahu abandonment. The trajectory is not one of progressive complexity but of a temporary elaboration that does not consolidate.

### Corporate group strength "relatively weak"

For the majority of the settlement sequence, corporate groups exercised "significant amount of autonomy" and corporate group strength is characterized as "relatively weak" (p. 176).

### No institutionalized rank differences

The burial and architectural data as analyzed do not support institutionalized rank differences between groups (p. 180).

### Elite villages

Type 1 ahu are associated with clusters of hare paenga (up to 10 houses) interpreted as elite villages, with residents "freed from subsistence pursuit" (p. 159). This is the strongest finding that could support social differentiation, though it does not require a chiefdom framework.

## Goldman Engagement

Unlike Kirch 1984, Stevenson engages with Goldman's classification of Easter Island. The dual status system is discussed (pp. 16, 178): hereditary chiefs held sacred authority while secular power was achieved. This aligns with Goldman's "Open" classification, in which military and political effectiveness govern status rather than hereditary seniority alone.

---

## Population Estimates

Stevenson estimates population at 1600 AD at 8,927 (p. 172) and peak population at 11,000-12,000 (p. 173). These estimates are based on household multiplier and cultivable area calculations. They vastly exceed current estimates: Davis et al. 2024 calculate maximum agricultural carrying capacity at approximately 3,900, and Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 genetic data are consistent with gradual growth to approximately 1,500-3,000. The population estimates are among the weakest empirical claims in the dissertation.

---

## Hedging and Qualifications

The dissertation contains at least 11 explicit qualifications of its findings, characterized as "tentative" and "preliminary propositions" (pp. 20, 181). This self-conscious tentativeness contrasts sharply with the confidence of the chiefdom claims that grew from this same data in Stevenson's later publications.

---

## Data Presented

- 65 ceremonial centers, 184 residential sites from southern coastal region
- Obsidian hydration dates from experimentally derived rates (plus/minus 55 year margin for most readings)
- 13 one-hundred-year phase maps showing settlement and ahu construction
- Cluster analysis of ahu architectural attributes (5 types)
- Cluster analysis of residential site features (5 types)
- Ahu "use-life" of 129 years estimated from five securely dated sites
- Population growth curve (Figure 30)

---

## Methodological Notes

- Obsidian hydration dating with plus/minus 55 year margin introduces substantial chronological uncertainty
- Ahu "use-life" of 129 years estimated from only five securely dated sites; small sample
- Corporate group identification criteria from African ethnographic analogues, not Polynesian models
- Framework from British social anthropology (Fortes, Firth) and corporate group archaeology (Hayden & Cannon 1982)

---

## Post-Slave-Raid Dependency

All political organization claims derive from post-slave-raid ethnographic sources, primarily McCall 1979 (who synthesized Metraux 1940, Routledge 1919, Goldman 1970). Stevenson explicitly acknowledges this limitation: ethnographic data are to be used "selectively and with caution" (p. 23), recognizing post-raid disruption. Contact-era accounts are cited only for subsistence observations, not political organization. This means the political interpretations rest entirely on the post-raid ethnographic record, despite the acknowledged limitations of that record.

## Source Audit

Stevenson's bibliography contains approximately 70 references.

**Key absences**: Service 1962, Kirch 1984, Sahlins 1963. The theoretical framework is entirely independent of the chiefdom typology.

**Key presences**: Goldman 1970, Sahlins 1958, Fortes 1953/1959, Firth 1936/1957, Fried 1957, Kirchhoff 1955, McCall 1979, McCoy 1976, Metraux 1937/1940. The anthropological framework draws on kinship and descent theory rather than evolutionary stages.

## The 1984-to-1986 Transition

The 1986 chapter in Kirch's *Island Societies* volume represents the critical transition point. Processing of Stevenson 1986 (90 claims; see `docs/references/claims/stevenson_1986_claims.md`) against this 1984 baseline reveals the specific mechanism: "chiefdom" does not appear in Stevenson's 1986 text, Service and Kirch are not cited, and the corporate group framework is retained. But the theoretical infrastructure (Fortes, Firth, Kirchhoff, Hayden & Cannon) is stripped away, the explicit rejection of unilinear evolution is dropped, and "reverted to non-ranked structure" becomes "corporate solidarity weakened." The editorial context of Kirch's volume does the reframing that Stevenson's own text does not. This transition mirrors the broader pattern in the chiefdom attribution chain: tentative, qualified findings are repackaged with greater confidence under the influence of the dominant interpretive framework. Stevenson had a chapter accepted for Kirch's volume while completing the dissertation, placing the transition precisely at the moment when the Kirch chiefdom framework achieved its greatest influence in Pacific archaeology.

## Significance for the Paper

Stevenson 1984 is significant for the paper in three respects:

1. **It demonstrates that the chiefdom framework was not the only available interpretation of Rapa Nui settlement data.** The same data that later became evidence for chiefdom-level organization were originally analyzed within a corporate group framework that found no institutionalized rank differences and described autonomous descent groups.

2. **The 1984-to-1986 transition is a case study in interpretive drift.** The progression from tentative corporate group analysis (1984) through chiefdom reframing (1986) to confident "Chiefdom Integration" periodization (1997, 2002) illustrates how the dominant framework absorbs and transforms disconfirming evidence.

3. **Stevenson's own later acknowledgment undermines the chiefdom attribution.** Robinson & Stevenson 2017 admits that paramountcy has "not been discovered archaeologically or reported in the ethnohistoric oral history" (p. 90). This concession, from the scholar who first connected settlement data to political organization on Rapa Nui, is a significant admission that the chiefdom framework was never empirically grounded in the archaeological record.
