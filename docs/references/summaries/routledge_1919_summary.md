# Source Summary: Routledge 1919

## Overview

Katherine Routledge spent seventeen months on Easter Island (March 1914 to August 1915) with the Mana Expedition, producing the most extensive early ethnographic account of the island. Her book, *The Mystery of Easter Island* (1919), combines archaeological survey, architectural documentation, and extended ethnographic fieldwork with about a dozen elderly informants from multiple clans. She learned the native language, cross-examined informants over many months, and is the first post-raid ethnographer who did NOT work through Alexander Salmon as intermediary. This independence from Salmon is methodologically critical, since Thomson (1886) and Geiseler (1882) both used Salmon as their sole translator and cultural broker.

## Sources Consulted

- Routledge 1919, *The Mystery of Easter Island: The Story of an Expedition*. London: Sifton, Praed & Co. (PDF: `docs/references/pdfs/post_slave_raid/routledge_1919/Routledge_1919.pdf`)
- Key chapters: X (pp. 131-139), XIII (pp. 165-176), XIV (pp. 175-199), XV (pp. 200-235), XVI (pp. 236-271), XVIII (pp. 277-289), XIX (pp. 290-302)

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**"No chiefs nor any form of government" except one clan.** Routledge's informants from multiple clans told her that outside the Miru, "there were no chiefs nor any form of government; any man who was expert in war became a leader" (p. 224). Only the Miru had a chief (ariki-mau), making it one clan out of ten with hereditary leadership. This is the most explicit denial of centralized political authority in any ethnographic source on Rapa Nui.

**The ariki-mau was a ceremonial figurehead, not a political leader.** Routledge characterizes the last ariki-mau, Ngaara, as follows: "he was neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest; he can best be described as the custodian of certain customs and traditions" (p. 242). This description, based on extensive testimony from Te Haha (a Miru who served in Ngaara's entourage), explicitly denies military, judicial, and religious authority. The ariki-mau's functions were supernatural food-increase magic (especially chickens), oversight of rongorongo knowledge (annual assembly at Anakena), rain prayers (delegated to ariki-paka), house inauguration ceremonies, and receiving the annual pua offering. All of these are ceremonial, ritualistic, or supernatural, not political.

**The ariki-mau lacked coercive power.** Two episodes demonstrate this: (1) Ngaara's servant Kaara stole his most valuable tablet (kohau-o-te-ranga) for the Tupahotu clan, and "the Ariki seems to have taken a somewhat feeble line, and, instead of punishing his servant, merely tried to bribe him, with the result that he never again saw his kohau" (p. 249); (2) Ngaara was captured by the Ngaure clan and held captive at Akahanga for five years with his son and grandson, requiring an alliance between the Miru and Tupahotu clans to free him (p. 246).

**The tangata manu was a rotating competitive institution with minimal practical benefits.** Routledge provides the most detailed account of the bird-man cult. The Ao (ascendant group) system rotated clan dominance annually through competition for the first sooty tern egg on Motu Nui. "Only those who belonged to the clan in the ascendancy for the time being could enter on the quest" (p. 258). The tangata manu's benefits: "food was brought as gifts, especially the first sugar-cane, and these offerings seem to have been the sole practical advantage of victory; those who did not contribute were apt to have their houses burnt" (p. 264). The winner spent five months in strict taboo, "sleeping all day," then "returned to ordinary life" after one year (pp. 264-265).

**The ariki-mau and tangata manu were separate, potentially competing institutions.** The ariki-mau "never appeared at Orongo" (p. 260), the center of the bird cult. The Miru could influence Ao selection but could themselves be victimized when another clan held Ao status: "it was to grace one of these gatherings, when the Ureohei were the Ao, that the mother of Hotu, the Miru, was slain" (p. 259). The ariki-mau's capture by the Ngaure during their Ao period demonstrates that Ao dominance could override ariki-mau status.

**Matatoa and matakio were rotating statuses, not permanent classes.** Routledge's footnote: "The men of the ascendant clan are also often spoken of as the Mata-toa, or warriors, the other clans being the Mata-kio, or servants" (p. 259). This rotated with the Ao: whichever clan was ascendant was "matatoa," all others were "matakio." This contradicts Roussel's description of matatoa and kio as permanent hereditary classes.

**No succession rules reliably established.** Routledge notes in passing that the ariki-mau "should resign in favour of his son" when "old and feeble" (p. 241), partially consistent with Roussel (abdication) and Thomson (son), but not with Geiseler (brother). She provides no detailed succession mechanism despite 17 months of fieldwork.

**No king list.** Routledge does not produce a numbered king list. She mentions "all the lists" agreeing that Miru was the third ariki (p. 281) but documents fundamental confusion among informants about basic genealogical questions, including whether Kotuu and Tuumaheki were the same person and whether Miru was a son or grandson of Hotu-matua (pp. 281-282).

## Context and Limitations

**[POST-RAID]** Routledge visited 52 years after the slave raids. The population was approximately 250, recovered from a nadir of ~111 in 1877. The slave raids removed "the last of the ariki, or chiefs" (p. 205). Her informants were elderly survivors who had been children during the pre-missionary period (pre-1868), meaning they were children during or shortly after the slave raids.

**Seventeen-month stay.** The longest research visit of any early ethnographer. Roussel spent seven years but as a missionary, not a researcher. Routledge was the first to approach the island with a systematic ethnographic methodology.

**Direct language acquisition.** Routledge learned the native language and by the end could conduct interviews directly, with Juan Tepano clarifying rather than translating. This is a significant methodological advance over Thomson's and Geiseler's dependence on Salmon.

**Multiple informants from multiple clans.** Te Haha (Miru), Juan Tepano (Tupahotu), He (Marama), Viriamo (Ureohei), Kapiera, Kilimuti, Porotu, Jotefa, and others. This multi-clan coverage reduces the risk of a single clan's perspective dominating the account.

**Cross-examination methodology.** "The witness had to be heard, and discreetly cross-examined without any doubt being thrown on his story; then allowed to forget and again re-examined, his story being compared with that of others who had been heard meanwhile" (p. 213). More rigorous than any predecessor.

**Memory limitations acknowledged.** "Deliberate invention was rare, but, when memory was a little vague, there was a constant tendency to glide from what was remembered to what was imagined" (p. 212).

**Genealogical depth limited.** "Only in one case was it possible to get back beyond the great-grandfather of our informant" (p. 227). Four to five generations at most, fundamentally limiting what can be known about political succession.

**Routledge's own interpretive framework.** Routledge used an early 20th century racial-migration framework, interpreting the Long Ear/Short Ear tradition as reflecting ethnic replacement (Melanesian predecessors, then Polynesian settlers). This framework is no longer accepted, but her primary ethnographic data remain valuable independently of her interpretive overlay.

**Routledge's caution about her own evidence.** "It is impossible as yet to give with any certainty a connected account of the early history of Easter Island" (p. 298). The most thorough early ethnographer concluded that a connected historical account could not be constructed from the available evidence.

## Relevance to the Paper

Routledge 1919 is critical for the chiefdom attribution argument for five reasons:

1. **It is the only detailed account of the ariki-mau's actual functions, and those functions are explicitly non-political.** Routledge's characterization of Ngaara as "neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest" (p. 242) is the most specific description of ariki-mau authority in any source. It directly contradicts the chiefdom model, which requires a paramount chief with political, military, and/or judicial authority.

2. **It demonstrates the ariki-mau's lack of coercive power through specific episodes.** The stolen tablet and the five-year captivity are concrete evidence, not theoretical arguments. If the ariki-mau could not compel his own servant to return stolen property, and could be captured and enslaved by a rival clan, "paramount chief" is a misnomer.

3. **It provides the most detailed account of the tangata manu as a rotating competitive institution** that is fundamentally different from permanent hierarchical chiefdom. The Ao system distributed dominance across clans on a rotating basis, with the tangata manu returning to "ordinary life" after one year.

4. **It reveals the matatoa/matakio distinction as a rotating binary, not a permanent class system.** Roussel's four-class hierarchy (ariki, ivi atua, matatoa, kio) is reframed by Routledge as a binary that rotated with the Ao: the ascendant clan was "matatoa," everyone else was "matakio." This fundamentally undermines the claim of permanent social stratification.

5. **It is the first account independent of Salmon, and it diverges from Thomson on critical points.** Routledge's informants (working without Salmon as intermediary) described a system without monarchy, without taxation, and without district chiefs, while Thomson (working through Salmon) described an "arbitrary monarchy." The divergence raises the question of how much of Thomson's political description reflects Salmon's framework rather than Rapanui tradition.

6. **Chapter XIV's detailed account of moai production contains no evidence for chiefly command.** Routledge's Chapter XIV (pp. 175-199) is the most detailed early account of moai quarrying, carving, and transport. Despite this detail, she provides no evidence that the ariki-mau or any chief directed monument production. The chiefdom model assumes monument construction was organized by chiefly command; the most thorough early researcher documented the production process without finding evidence for this assumption.

The paper should present Routledge as the strongest post-raid source precisely because she spent the most time, used the most rigorous methods, worked independently of Salmon, and still found no evidence for a functioning chiefdom. Her simultaneous documentation of the Miru's exceptional ceremonial status and the ariki-mau's lack of political authority captures exactly the ambiguity that the chiefdom model collapses into a false clarity.
