# Summary: Davis et al. 2024

**[OWN WORK]**

**Source**: Davis DS, DiNapoli RJ, Pakarati G, Hunt TL, Lipo CP. 2024. Island-wide characterization of agricultural production challenges the demographic collapse hypothesis for Rapa Nui (Easter Island). *Science Advances* 10, eado1459. https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.ado1459

---

## Overview

Davis et al. use shortwave infrared (SWIR) satellite imagery from Worldview-3 and machine learning classification to generate the first accurate island-wide estimate of rock garden (lithic mulching) extent on Rapa Nui. The study finds that rock gardens cover approximately 0.76 km^2 of the island, roughly one-fifth of the most conservative previous estimate (4.3-21.1 km^2 per Ladefoged et al. 2013) and less than 4% of the maximal prior estimate. The authors then use this revised agricultural extent to recalculate population size estimates, concluding that rock gardening infrastructure could not have supported populations exceeding approximately 3,900-4,000 individuals under the most optimistic scenarios. The average carrying capacity of rock gardening alone is estimated at approximately 2,000, which rises to approximately 3,000 when marine and non-mulched terrestrial food sources are included. This figure matches contact-era European estimates of the island's population.

The paper directly challenges the Malthusian overshoot/ecocide narrative by demonstrating that the agricultural base was never extensive enough to support the inflated population estimates (up to 17,000) that the collapse model requires. If the population was maintained within environmental carrying capacity at approximately 3,000, there was no overshoot, no resource crisis, and no agriculturally-driven population collapse.

## Methods

The previous island-wide rock garden estimate (Ladefoged et al. 2013) used visual and near-infrared (VNIR) imagery from Worldview-2 with a maximum likelihood classifier, achieving low accuracy (Kappa 0.17-0.22, recall 0.32-0.34, overall accuracy 0.59-0.61). Davis et al. expand the spectral range to include SWIR bands (1.195-2.365 μm), which are sensitive to moisture variations and mineral composition, enabling better discrimination between rock gardens and natural rocky landscape components (lava flows, colluvium, bedrock outcrops). Three classifiers were tested (maximum entropy, random forest, maximum likelihood) on both VNIR and SWIR data. The SWIR maximum entropy model performed best (Kappa 0.79, recall 0.83, precision 0.83, overall accuracy 0.82). The classified output was manually cleaned in ArcGIS to remove obvious errors (modern roads, lava flows misidentified as gardens).

Approximately 25 km^2 of the island has been affected by urbanization and modern agriculture. The authors assessed this area using VNIR imagery and found that most disturbed zones (~17 km^2, primarily in and around Hanga Roa) are in locations where no rock gardens have been discovered through ground survey or remote sensing.

## Key Findings

### Rock garden extent is drastically smaller than assumed

The total area of rock gardens identified is 0.76 km^2. Previous estimates ranged from 4.3 to 21.1 km^2 (Ladefoged et al. 2013). The previous study's misidentifications included modern roads, natural lava flows, colluvial deposits, and vegetation, all classified as rock gardens due to the limited spectral discrimination of VNIR-only data. The spatial distribution of the newly identified rock gardens is consistent with earlier studies in that gardens are primarily concentrated along coastal regions and largely absent at higher inland altitudes.

### Population estimates are substantially lower

Using the same methodology as Puleston et al. (2017) but substituting the revised rock garden area, maximum population estimates drop from approximately 16,000 to approximately 3,900 (continuous cultivation, high nitrogen availability). Under more realistic cultivation scenarios (shifting cultivation with fallow periods), the estimates range from 716 to 3,066. Adding 50% for marine and non-rock-garden terrestrial foods yields an overall carrying capacity estimate of approximately 3,000, which matches the contact-era population estimate of approximately 3,000 from 18th-century European visitors.

### The Malthusian overshoot model is unsupported

The paper argues that significant demographic increases ("overshoot") did not occur, because the agricultural infrastructure was never extensive enough to support the large populations the collapse model requires. The convergence between estimated carrying capacity and contact-era population size suggests the Rapanui maintained a sustainable subsistence system within environmental limits.

### Temporal non-contemporaneity of gardens

The 0.76 km^2 figure represents the cumulative extent of all rock gardens identifiable in modern satellite imagery. Not all of these gardens were necessarily in use simultaneously; some fields may be older than others, and the active cultivated area at any given point in the island's history may have been smaller or different in distribution. This further reduces instantaneous carrying capacity at any given time.

## Relevance to the Chiefdom Question

The paper does not directly address political organization or the chiefdom concept. Its relevance is indirect but substantial, operating through the demographic and agricultural foundations that chiefdom and collapse models have been built upon.

**What it demonstrates:**

1. The agricultural extent of rock gardening on Rapa Nui was far smaller than the estimates that have been used in carrying capacity and population models. This is a well-supported empirical finding based on improved remote sensing methods with substantially better accuracy metrics than prior work.

2. The maximum population supportable by rock gardening infrastructure is approximately 3,900 under optimistic assumptions, not 16,000-17,000. With realistic cultivation scenarios and accounting for other food sources, the estimated carrying capacity is approximately 3,000.

3. This estimated carrying capacity converges with the contact-era population estimate of approximately 3,000, removing the supposed gap between what the island could support and how many people lived there.

4. The ecocide/Malthusian overshoot narrative loses its demographic foundation. If there was no population overshoot, the sequence of chiefly-competition-driven overexploitation leading to collapse does not have the demographic precondition it requires.

**What it does not demonstrate:**

1. The paper does not address political organization directly. The implications for the chiefdom model are downstream consequences of the revised agricultural and demographic estimates, not findings about sociopolitical structure.

2. The paper does not claim that no rock gardens have been destroyed by modern development. It acknowledges this possibility but argues the destruction is likely limited because most developed areas were not in zones where rock gardens were concentrated.

3. The paper does not resolve the absolute pre-contact population size. It revises the upper bound supportable by rock gardening but acknowledges that comprehensive population modeling requires accounting for marine foods, non-rock-garden cultivation areas, seasonal variability, and temporal changes in land use.

4. The paper does not address temporal change in agricultural extent. The satellite data represent a single time slice; the trajectory of agricultural intensification over the centuries of Rapanui occupation is not captured.

## How It Bears on the Chiefdom-Collapse Narrative

The standard chiefdom-collapse narrative for Rapa Nui runs roughly as follows: chiefdom-level political organization drove competitive monument construction, which required deforestation for transport logistics, which degraded soils, which necessitated agricultural intensification through rock gardening, which initially supported a large population (10,000-17,000), but eventually the system exceeded carrying capacity and collapsed, leading to warfare, the fall of the chiefly order, and demographic decline prior to European contact. Davis et al. 2024 attacks the demographic premise of this sequence. If the agricultural infrastructure was never extensive enough to support more than approximately 3,000-4,000 people, then:

- The supposed population growth phase (to 10,000-17,000) did not occur as modeled, because the agricultural base could not have supported it.
- There was no "overshoot" beyond carrying capacity, because the population appears to have stayed within the limits of what the environment could sustain.
- The resource crisis that supposedly caused the collapse lacks its precipitating condition.
- The organizational demands of managing a population of 3,000 are qualitatively different from those of managing 15,000, weakening the argument that the population size itself required chiefly administration.

The paper also indirectly challenges models positing chiefly territorial control over extensive inland agricultural zones. The rock gardens are concentrated in coastal areas, not spread across the island's interior, which does not match the picture of territorially bounded chiefdoms each managing distinct agricultural hinterlands.

## Relationship to Other Sources in This Project

- **Puleston et al. 2017**: Davis et al. use the same population estimation methodology but with corrected agricultural extent inputs, demonstrating that the high population estimates were artifacts of inflated rock garden data rather than methodological errors in the modeling itself.

- **Ladefoged et al. 2013**: The prior rock garden estimate that Davis et al. correct. The paper demonstrates that Ladefoged et al.'s VNIR-based classification had low accuracy and systematically misidentified non-garden landscape features.

- **DiNapoli et al. 2019**: The ahu-freshwater correlation paper found that rock mulch gardens do not explain ahu distribution. Davis et al. 2024 adds to this by showing that the extent of rock gardening was far smaller than assumed, further weakening arguments linking ahu placement to management of agricultural territories.

- **Mulrooney 2013**: Mulrooney found no evidence for the inland abandonment predicted by the collapse model. Davis et al. complement this by showing that the inland agricultural base was limited to begin with, meaning there was less to abandon.

- **DiNapoli et al. 2020 (collapse model)**: The Bayesian radiocarbon/paleoenvironmental analysis that found population resilience rather than collapse. Davis et al. provide independent support from a completely different data source (agricultural extent rather than radiocarbon chronology).

- **Contact-era European accounts**: The convergence between the revised carrying capacity (~3,000) and 18th-century European estimates (~3,000) is consistent with the interpretation that European visitors observed a population operating near (but within) carrying capacity, not the remnant of a collapsed civilization.
