# Summary: DiNapoli et al. 2020

**[OWN WORK]**

**Source**: DiNapoli RJ, Rieth TM, Lipo CP, Hunt TL. 2020. A model-based approach to the tempo of "collapse": The case of Rapa Nui (Easter Island). *Journal of Archaeological Science* 116: 105094. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jas.2020.105094

---

## Overview

This paper uses Bayesian chronological modeling and the "tempo plot" technique (Dye 2016) to examine the timing, rate, and cessation of platform ahu construction on Rapa Nui. The study integrates radiocarbon dates from 11 platform ahu across the island, relative architectural stratigraphy (pre-platform, platform, and post-platform construction phases), and ethnohistoric accounts to build multi-phase Bayesian models in OxCal v.4.3.2. It also constructs a separate Bayesian colonization model using 14C samples with conventional radiocarbon ages greater than or equal to 650 BP from non-ahu contexts.

The central finding is that ahu construction began sometime between the early 14th and mid-15th centuries AD, after a colonization date of approximately 1150-1280 cal. AD, and continued at a steady rate through European contact in 1722 and likely into the post-contact era. There is no evidence for a pre-contact cessation of monument construction. The paper explicitly describes the tempo plot results as "important falsifying evidence that directly challenges core components of Rapa Nui's collapse narrative" (p. 7).

## Key Findings

### Colonization timing

The Bayesian colonization model estimates initial Polynesian settlement of Rapa Nui at 1150-1280 cal. AD (95.4% HPD), consistent with other recent estimates (Hunt and Lipo 2006, Wilmshurst et al. 2011). The model incorporating unidentified charcoal samples with a Charcoal Outlier parameter produces a slightly more precise estimate than the short-lived-only model.

### Onset of ahu construction

Initial platform ahu construction began sometime between the early 14th and mid-15th centuries AD. The earliest estimate comes from ahu Rongo 1 (1305-1490 cal. AD, 55-285 years after colonization). The most secure estimates, from ahu with both short-lived and unidentified charcoal samples in TPQ and TAQ phases, come from Nau Nau (1410-1450 cal. AD), Heki'i (1320-1445 cal. AD), and Te Niu (1415-1615 cal. AD).

These model-based estimates are later than previous ad hoc estimates (Martinsson-Wallin et al. 2013; Wallin and Martinsson-Wallin 2008), which suggested initial construction potentially as early as AD 1100-1250. The revised dates are potentially contemporaneous with temple (marae) construction elsewhere in central East Polynesia.

### Tempo of construction: steady through European contact

The tempo plot (Fig. 4) shows a rapid period of ahu construction from ca. AD 1350-1450, followed by a steady rate of construction events that continues beyond European contact in 1722. The tempo-activity plot (Fig. 5, first derivative of the tempo curve) shows that the rate of new construction events begins a slow decline around AD 1550 but does not cease. Construction activity continues into post-contact times, with the upper bound of the 95% Bayesian credible interval flattening around 1750.

A sensitivity analysis using an AD 1771 cutoff (instead of the preferred AD 1838-1868 cutoff) produces essentially identical results for periods before AD 1700, but suggests a possible cessation around the time of initial European contact. Both cutoff dates indicate that ahu construction continued at least until 1722.

### No evidence for a pre-contact "collapse" in construction

The paper states directly: "The most significant results from our Bayesian analyses are the tempo plots for the duration of ahu construction activities (Figs. 4 and 5), which provide important falsifying evidence that directly challenges core components of Rapa Nui's collapse narrative" (p. 7). The hypothesized cessation of ahu construction in the 17th century (the "Ahu Moai" to "Huri Moai" transition) is not supported by the Bayesian models. This finding is corroborated by ethnohistoric observations: Roggeveen in 1722 and the Spanish in 1770 both recorded active ritual use of ahu platforms.

## Significance for the Chiefdom Question

### What it demonstrates

1. The chronological basis for the "Ahu Moai" to "Huri Moai" phase transition, the proposed pre-contact cessation of monument construction that is central to the collapse narrative, is not supported by formal Bayesian modeling. The tempo plots show continuous construction activity through European contact.

2. The tempo of ahu construction is steady, not escalating. After an initial rapid period (ca. AD 1350-1450), the rate of construction events does not accelerate. This is inconsistent with a narrative of intensifying chiefly competition driving ever-more-elaborate monument building.

3. The revised (later) onset dates for ahu construction compress the timeline for the hypothesized period of chiefly consolidation. If ahu construction began in the early 14th to mid-15th centuries and continued steadily through contact, the window for an "Ahu Moai" phase of competitive escalation followed by collapse is much shorter than previously assumed, and the evidence for escalation itself is absent.

4. Previous chronological frameworks for ahu construction were based on ad hoc visual interpretations of SPD curves and calibrated date lists, not on formal statistical models. The chronological scaffolding on which the collapse narrative rests was never rigorously established.

5. The paper frames ahu construction as "group-level activity" that "served as a vital component of communities necessary for long term sustainability" (p. 7), not as a product of chiefly command or competitive escalation.

### What it does not demonstrate

1. The paper does not directly test whether ahu construction was organized by chiefs or by communities acting collectively. The tempo plot shows when and at what rate construction occurred, not who organized it.

2. The sample of 11 ahu out of approximately 150 known platform ahu is a limitation. The south coast, with its dense ahu distribution, is not represented. It remains possible that construction began earlier or followed different patterns at unsampled locations.

3. The study does not address the elaboration or scale of individual ahu over time, only the rate of construction events. It is possible that individual ahu became larger or more elaborate even as the overall rate of new construction held steady.

4. Many radiocarbon dates are from unidentified charcoal, which may have inbuilt age. This means the model estimates may be biased older, which, if anything, strengthens the finding that construction continued late. However, it introduces uncertainty into the precise timing of the initial onset.

5. The paper does not model population dynamics directly. The inference that steady construction implies sustained social organization is reasonable but indirect.

## How It Bears on Claims That Ahu Construction Reflects Chiefly Political Consolidation

The standard narrative for Rapa Nui's political history runs as follows: Polynesian colonists established chiefdom-like political structures, competed through increasingly elaborate monument construction during the "Ahu Moai" phase, overextended their resource base, and experienced a political and environmental collapse around AD 1600-1680, after which monument construction ceased and statue toppling began (the "Huri Moai" phase). This narrative depends critically on a chronological framework in which ahu construction intensifies, peaks, and then stops before European contact.

DiNapoli et al. 2020 undermines this narrative at the chronological level. The tempo plots show no cessation, no sharp peak, and no collapse in construction activity before European contact. Instead they show a rapid onset followed by steady activity. This pattern is consistent with communities that established a monumental tradition soon after colonization and maintained it continuously for centuries, adjusting the rate of activity gradually but never abandoning the practice until European impacts disrupted the social order.

The paper does not address the chiefly attribution directly, but its results remove the chronological scaffolding on which the consolidation-and-collapse version of the chiefdom narrative depends. If there was no pre-contact construction peak or cessation, then the archaeological record does not show the trajectory (intensification followed by breakdown) that the chiefly-competition model predicts. The evidence is instead consistent with sustained, community-level investment in a tradition of monumental construction that served ongoing social and ritual functions.

The finding that initial ahu construction timing may be contemporaneous with marae construction elsewhere in East Polynesia also weakens the argument that Rapa Nui's monument-building tradition was uniquely intense or required unusually centralized political organization to explain.

## Relationship to Other Sources in This Project

- **DiNapoli et al. 2019**: The ahu-freshwater study provides the spatial analysis of where ahu were built; this paper provides the temporal analysis of when they were built. Together, they show that ahu placement tracks freshwater sources (not agricultural territories or chiefly boundaries) and that construction continued steadily through contact (not peaking and collapsing before contact).

- **Mulrooney 2013**: Mulrooney's island-wide radiocarbon synthesis reached similar conclusions about continued settlement and land use through the contact era. DiNapoli et al. 2020 adds formal Bayesian modeling and the tempo plot technique to the same general argument.

- **Roggeveen 1722 / Gonzalez 1770**: The contact-era observations of active ahu ritual use corroborate the Bayesian results. The paper explicitly cites Roggeveen's description of ritual activity at ahu as consistent with the model's finding of continued construction.

- **Kirch 1984, 2017**: The "Ahu Moai" to "Huri Moai" phase framework central to Kirch's culture-historical model is directly challenged. If construction did not cease before contact, the phase transition that organizes Kirch's narrative of chiefdom consolidation and collapse has no empirical basis in the construction chronology.

- **Martinsson-Wallin et al. 2013**: The SPD-based construction chronology that claimed ahu were "securely in place by ca. AD 1300-1400" with a "destruction phase" around AD 1600 is superseded by the formal Bayesian models, which show later onset and no pre-contact destruction phase.

- **Englert 1948**: The AD 1680 date for the "Huri Moai" transition, derived from Englert's interpretation of post-slave-raid oral traditions, is identified as the basis for the collapse chronology. The Bayesian models show this date has no archaeological support in the ahu construction record.
