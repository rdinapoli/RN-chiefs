## Overview

Goldman's book is the most comprehensive comparative study of Polynesian status systems, covering all eighteen major Polynesian societies in individual chapters (Part I, Chs. 1-19) followed by five analytical chapters (Part II, Chs. 20-24). His framework classifies Polynesian societies into three types based on the relationship between status and power: Traditional (seniority dominant, religious authority), Open (military/political effectiveness dominant, warriors displace hereditary chiefs), and Stratified (categorical economic and political distinctions, landed vs. landless). The developmental direction is Traditional -> Open -> Stratified, driven by status rivalry.

Goldman classifies Easter Island as an **Open** society, alongside Mangaia, Marquesas, Samoa, and Niue. This is the counter-classification to Sahlins's Group IIA (moderate-high stratification) and stands in unaddressed tension with Kirch's (1984) treatment of Easter Island as "a developed form of the Ancestral Polynesian segmentary ramage system."

## The Classification Framework

Goldman's three types are defined by the relationship between status (honor, hereditary worth) and power (secular control):

- **Traditional**: Status dominant over power. Sacred chief (*ariki*) heads a religiously sanctioned gradation of worth. Seniority of descent allocates authority. Examples: Maori, Tikopia.
- **Open**: Power dominant over status. Military and political effectiveness govern control. Warriors have displaced or circumscribed hereditary chiefs. Status differences "sharply defined" rather than graded. Examples: Mangaia, Easter Island, Marquesas, Samoa, Niue.
- **Stratified**: Status and power at approximately even level, both more consequential. Categorical distinction between landed and landless. Represents synthesis of Traditional and Open, an outcome of intense status rivalry. Examples: Mangareva, Society Islands, Hawaii, Tonga.

The framework differs fundamentally from Sahlins (1954/1958) in organizing principle: Sahlins classifies by productivity-stratification correlation (more productive = more stratified = more chiefly). Goldman classifies by the dynamic between ascribed status and achieved power. The practical consequence for Easter Island: Sahlins places it in the middle of a hierarchy of increasingly complex chiefdoms; Goldman places it among societies where the chiefdom pattern has broken down or never consolidated.

## Easter Island (Ch. 6, pp. 93-122): Key Findings

### 1. The "Open" classification

Easter Island "had never evolved a central authority" (p. 94). Warriors overcame the traditional ariki and gained not only political power but religious prerogatives through the Bird Man cult (p. 96). "Nowhere else in Polynesia did commoners take upon themselves both the secular authority and prerogatives of mana and tapu which were not ordinarily theirs" (p. 96). Easter Island is presented as the most extreme example of warrior displacement of hereditary authority within the Open category.

### 2. Dual status system

Easter Island had two coexisting status hierarchies (p. 109): (a) traditional, centered on the Miru ariki and their genealogical descent from Hotu-matua; (b) achieved, centered on matatoa war leaders and their military accomplishments. The traditional system was progressively constricted; the matatoa gained both secular and religious authority. This dual system "seems never to have stabilized itself" (p. 109).

### 3. The ariki mau: sacred but powerless

Goldman follows Roussel and Metraux: the ariki mau had extreme personal sanctity (head tapu, possessions tapu, controlled crop growth through mana, received first fruits) but "probably never a powerful secular ruler" (p. 115). His authority was "nil and entirely disregarded" in later times, having "passed entirely into the hands of the matatoa" (Roussel, quoted pp. 97-98).

### 4. Redistribution bypassed the chief

The most significant finding for the chiefdom critique: Goldman states explicitly that "on Easter Island the main role of economic redistribution or recirculation lay not with the ariki mau, but with heads of commoner groups" (pp. 114-115). The ariki mau distributed his ritual tuna tithe only among "important old men." Warrior chiefs held the honorific role in redistribution. The three main feast types (paina, koro, areauti) were organized by kin groups, not the paramount. This directly contradicts the Sahlins/Kirch framework where the chief is the redistributive center.

### 5. No central authority over monument construction

Goldman explicitly rejects centralized monument construction: "There is no reason to believe, however, that a central authority ever undertook the construction of local monuments" (pp. 106-107). He proposes instead that captive labor (kio) provided the workforce, organized by victorious military coalitions. Local ahu were built by local groups; the Polynesian pattern of local maraes as local labor products applied to Easter Island.

### 6. Political separatism

Easter Island was "committed only to the principle of religious unity, political ties remaining tenuous" (pp. 118-119). "The political tradition was separatist" (p. 119). Segment I (the whole island as a unit) had "unstable political character." Only the Miru maintained traditional genealogical organization; all other mata were headed by warrior chiefs.

### 7. Historical trajectory: Traditional -> Open

Goldman argues the Early Period (400-1100) was probably a Traditional society: Hotu-matua as traditional ariki, high-quality ahu construction, solar cult at Vinapu. The Middle Period (1100-1680) saw the shift to Open society: mass-produced images replaced quality masonry, warriors displaced hereditary authority, the Bird Man cult emerged. The Late Period (1680-1868) was "chaos and violence of the militarized Open society" (p. 107).

## Source Assessment

Goldman's Easter Island chapter rests on a **two-source foundation**. Systematic citation analysis (see claims file, Section III) reveals:

1. **Metraux 1940** (~45 mentions, 15+ page-cited references): The source for virtually all ethnographic claims. Metraux is the funnel through which earlier sources (Roussel, Thomson) reach Goldman. Goldman rarely goes behind Metraux to check original sources.

2. **Heyerdahl 1961** (~9 mentions + 3 Ferdon + 1 C. S. Smith = ~13 total): The source for all archaeological claims. Goldman builds his three-period model on the Expedition's ahu typology, radiocarbon dates, and Orongo stratigraphy.

**Routledge 1919** (~9 mentions) provides supplementary ethnographic details but is not systematically engaged. Goldman does not quote or address Routledge's strongest findings ("no chiefs nor any form of government," p. 224). **Thomson 1891** (~4 mentions) is cited mainly to set up a contradiction with Roussel. **Roussel** (~6 mentions) is never cited directly; all Roussel quotes are mediated through Metraux.

**Contact-era accounts are not used for political evidence.** Goldman mentions Roggeveen, Cook, and La Perouse only to note they "did not record culture or traditions" (p. 96). This is factually incorrect: La Perouse conducted the most methodologically careful contact-era expedition and explicitly reported on political organization. Cook's party included detailed observations by Wales and the Forsters. Goldman's failure to use contact-era accounts is a significant blind spot. They would have independently supported his "Open" classification.

**What this means for the chiefdom critique**: Goldman and Sahlins both built their Easter Island analyses primarily on Metraux 1940 yet reached opposite conclusions. Sahlins found moderate-high stratification (Group IIA) by asking about productivity-stratification correlation and filling gaps with Polynesian analogy. Goldman found an Open society by asking about the relationship between status and power and taking Metraux's characterization of ariki powerlessness and matatoa dominance at face value. Same data, different questions, different answers. When the same ethnographic base supports contradictory classifications, the classification is doing more work than the evidence.

Goldman acknowledges the slave raids ended Easter Island culture "abruptly" (p. 96) and notes the population had been reduced to 456 by 1934, but does not raise the specific epistemological problem of whether post-raid traditions can reliably describe pre-raid organization. He treats Metraux as a "thorough and judicious" cultural salvage ethnographer and works from this baseline.

## Significance for Our Argument

### Goldman as counter-voice

Goldman is the most important dissenting voice within the Polynesian comparative tradition. While Sahlins (1954) classified Easter Island by analogy with the rest of Polynesia and found moderate-high stratification, and Kirch (1984) applied it as part of the Ancestral Polynesian chiefdom inheritance, Goldman independently classified Easter Island based on the relationship between status and power and concluded it "had never evolved a central authority."

### Kirch included but never engaged

Kirch 1984 displays Goldman's "Open" classification for Easter Island in Table 2 (p. 37) alongside Sahlins's Group IIA, but never discusses the contradiction in the Easter Island chapter (Ch. 11, pp. 264-278). This omission is significant: Goldman's classification, by a recognized authority using an independent framework, directly challenges the chiefdom interpretation. Its inclusion without discussion suggests Kirch was aware of it but chose not to address it.

### Goldman aligns with the contact-era evidence

Goldman's "Open" classification, with its emphasis on military displacement of hereditary chiefs, fragmented authority, political separatism, and absence of centralized redistribution, aligns closely with what the contact-era observers actually describe: La Perouse's "no need of a chief," Cook's ignorance of who governed, HMS Blossom's rejection of the chief hypothesis. Goldman reached this conclusion from the post-raid ethnographic record; the contact-era accounts independently corroborate it.

### Goldman aligns with collective action evidence

Goldman's description of decentralized monument production, competitive lineage-level status rivalry, and redistribution by commoner heads rather than the paramount is consistent with the collective action alternative. The archaeological evidence (Lipo et al. 2013, 2025; DiNapoli et al. 2019, 2020) confirms decentralized production without centralized command.

### What Goldman's "Open" still assumes

Goldman's "Open" classification does assume functioning local-level authority by matatoa within each mata. It describes a system with leaders and social differentiation, just not a centralized chiefdom. The distinction is critical: the question is not whether Easter Island had leaders (it did) but whether it had the specific institutional structure of the chiefdom (hereditary paramount, redistributive economy, centralized command over production). Goldman's answer is no.
