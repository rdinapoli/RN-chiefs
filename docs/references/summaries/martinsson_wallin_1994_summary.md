## Overview

This Uppsala doctoral thesis is the most comprehensive study of ahu (ceremonial stone platforms) on Easter Island. Martinsson-Wallin assembled a database of 313 ahu, of which 164 "image ahu" (platforms with statue associations) were subjected to construction analysis using 112 variables in 20 variable groups, correspondence analysis, seriation, and spatial distribution studies across the entire island. The study uses a structuralist theoretical framework, treating material culture as meaningful within its social and ideological context.

The book's conclusions are of central importance to the chiefdom attribution question because they directly contradict the territorial hierarchy model that Stevenson (2002) subsequently constructed using this same dataset. Three findings are critical:

1. **Ahu are architecturally homogeneous**: correspondence analysis showed "a relatively homogeneous pattern" with no clear complex types distinguishable (p. 137-138).
2. **Significant ahu are equally distributed across districts regardless of size**: "generally five to six significant ahu in each district, independent of the size of the district. This may indicate a social equality between different clans" (pp. 95, 106, 139).
3. **Territorial boundaries cannot be established from ahu data**: "it is difficult to establish any borders between different territories" (pp. 86, 90).

## Relevance to the Chiefdom Attribution

### The finding Stevenson overrode

The book's most consequential finding for our argument appears in three separate places (Ch 8.5 p. 95, Ch 8.9 p. 106, Ch 11.2 p. 139): five to six significant ahu exist in each district, independent of district size. Martinsson-Wallin interprets this as indicating "social equality between different clans." When Stevenson 2002 re-analyzed her 164-ahu dataset, he produced a seven-cluster solution identifying 27 "most elaborate" ahu as "district centers" requiring "elite coordination." He did not address or explain why her finding of equal distribution should be disregarded. The re-analysis produced a hierarchy from data that its original analyst concluded showed equality.

### Ahu homogeneity vs. Stevenson's types

Martinsson-Wallin found ahu "rather homogenous" through correspondence analysis (p. 138). She was "reluctant to use architectural data to define complex and socially correlated ahu structural types" because the data did not support such distinctions. Stevenson 2002 used her same data to define seven architectural clusters with social and political interpretations. The methodological question of how the same dataset yields opposite conclusions (homogeneity vs. hierarchical clusters) is not addressed in Stevenson's paper.

### Critique of Stevenson 1984's cluster analysis

Martinsson-Wallin examined Stevenson's 1984 typology in detail and found that "in only 6 cases out of 33 were all type defining attributes present, and in as many as 12 ahu two or more of these attributes were missing" (p. 42). This means fewer than one-fifth of the ahu Stevenson classified had all the attributes their assigned type required. The five-type classification that formed the basis for Stevenson's model of social differentiation rested on incomplete data that did not consistently exhibit the defining features.

She further critiqued his model: "His ethnoarchaeological model concerning society's organisation has a deterministic and general structure, and in my opinion he does not give appropriate weight to the specific cultural historical context of Easter Island" (p. 42).

### Water resources and ahu placement

Martinsson-Wallin notes that "water resources exist at all major ahu and settlement sites" (p. 105) and that "water resources were important factors for localisation of settlements" (p. 139). This observation anticipates DiNapoli et al. 2019's finding that ahu placement is strongly predicted by proximity to freshwater sources, contradicting Stevenson 2002's claim that "social/religious considerations" rather than environmental factors drove ahu location.

### Non-differentiation in the late period

Martinsson-Wallin's interpretation of the late prehistoric period directly challenges the chiefdom model: "the society moved in a non-differentiated direction at the end of the prehistoric phase" (p. 135). The old chiefly authority "probably had very little to do with the political power" (pp. 135, 142) as the bird-man cult replaced hereditary chiefly authority. This contradicts the narrative of a "well-developed chiefdom" that collapsed; instead, MW describes a shift in the basis of power from hereditary rank to achieved status through the bird-man competition.

## Territorial Boundary Skepticism

Martinsson-Wallin expresses consistent skepticism about drawing territorial boundaries from ahu distribution:

- "it is difficult to establish any borders between different territories" (p. 86)
- "it is difficult to estimate the exact borders between the different mata" (p. 90)
- "The division of the lineage groups performed here is hypothetical and further analyses of the material remains are needed to affirm or dismiss such a division" (p. 104)
- Agglomeration points "may be interpreted as the core of a territorial unit, but may as well indicate a border between two units" (p. 86)

Compare Stevenson 2002: boundaries drawn confidently using "midpoint between the district centers of the two adjacent territories" (Stevenson 2002 p. 224). The same data that MW treated with caution, Stevenson treated as definitive.

---

## Equality Finding in Detail

The equal-distribution finding is the most important single result for our argument. MW documents it with quantitative specificity:

- In each district, "generally five to six significant ahu" exist regardless of district size (pp. 95, 106, 139)
- Large structures are "more evenly distributed over the island than small and middle sized" (pp. 106, 139)
- "Ahu with many statues are spread out in all districts, but are mainly situated on the south coast" (p. 139)
- "An even distribution of constructions with the same degree of mana may be explained by the fact that all clans claim to be related to the main ancestor, Hotu Matua" (p. 131, citing Sahlins 1988 pp. 149-150)

The implication is clear: if each territorial unit, regardless of size, produced roughly the same number of architecturally significant ceremonial structures, the political system was characterized by parity among groups, not hierarchical domination by some over others. This is precisely the pattern predicted by collective action models and precisely the pattern that the chiefdom model cannot accommodate.

## Ethnohistoric Sources Evaluation

Martinsson-Wallin acknowledges the post-raid problem directly. She quotes Métraux: "I know of few places in the Pacific where so little remains of the ancient culture" (Métraux 1940 p. 3, quoted p. 33). She notes that "due to the slave raid most of the people with more in-depth knowledge of such things had disappeared" (p. 33). She uses Routledge's territorial divisions (Figure 13, p. 35) throughout the spatial analysis but as an analytical framework, not as demonstrated prehistoric fact. Her use of the ethnographic categories is more cautious than Stevenson's: she consistently describes her territorial analysis as "hypothetical" and notes the limitations of the ethnographic data.

---

## Stevenson 1984 as Evaluated by Martinsson-Wallin

MW's assessment of Stevenson 1984 is significant because it comes before Stevenson adopted the chiefdom label. She summarizes his chronological model:

- AD 1100-1300: "no institutionalised forms of ranking... nuclear families or extended families"
- AD 1300-1500: "organisation based on lineage groups... lineage groups were however not ranked in relation to one another"
- AD 1500-1700: "regional centres emerged... possibly a ranking between different lineage groups"
- After AD 1700: "nothing that indicates the earlier type of ranking... co-operation between the different lineage groups seems to have ended" (pp. 42-43)

This is Stevenson's own 1984 model as characterized by a peer: no ranking in the early period, possible ranking only in the last phase, followed by non-ranking. By 2002, this cautious, hedged model becomes "a well-developed chiefdom" from settlement through the sixteenth century.

## Progressive Hardening Context

| Feature | MW 1994 | Stevenson 2002 |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| Ahu distribution | "Rather homogenous"; equal distribution across districts | Seven hierarchical clusters; Cluster 4 as "district centers" |
| Territorial boundaries | "Difficult to establish" | Drawn by midpoint interpolation |
| District-size correlation | "No strong correlation"; 5-6 significant ahu per district regardless of size | Not addressed; territorial model assumes differential size |
| Social interpretation | "Social equality between different clans" | "Could only be conducted by elite members" |
| Stevenson 1984 types | 6/33 had all defining attributes; "deterministic" | Not discussed; 1984 cited as supporting "well-developed chiefdom" |
| Late period | "Non-differentiated direction"; chiefs "very little to do with political power" | "Chiefdom collapse" |
| Method | Correspondence analysis on 164 ahu, 112 variables | Cluster analysis on same 164 ahu, 15 binary attributes |

The transformation is stark: MW's finding of homogeneity and equality becomes Stevenson's finding of hierarchical differentiation, using the same underlying dataset but with a reduced variable set (15 of the original 112+ variables) and a different clustering method.

## Connections to Other Processed Sources

- **Stevenson 1984**: MW evaluates and critiques Stevenson's cluster analysis in detail. Found attribute data missing in most classified ahu. Summarizes his chronological model, which at this point still showed no chiefdom.
- **Stevenson 2002**: Re-analyzed MW's 164-ahu dataset to produce results she did not reach. Overrode her finding of homogeneity and equal distribution. Did not address why her analysis was wrong.
- **Stevenson et al. 2001**: Used MW's dataset indirectly through Stevenson 2002 ("in press").
- **Rounds-Beardsley 1990**: MW summarizes her study of 42 image ahu finding "four major territories" with "limited" methods.
- **McCoy 1976**: Extensively cited for settlement pattern data and ahu survey work.
- **Van Tilburg 1986/1994**: Cited for statue ideology ("symbols of a dominant male kinship based ideology").
- **Kirch 1984**: Cited for Polynesian settlement model but not prominently.
- **Goldman 1970**: Not prominently cited; MW's framework is structuralist rather than neoevolutionary.
- **DiNapoli et al. 2019**: MW's observation that "water resources exist at all major ahu and settlement sites" anticipates the freshwater finding.
- **Lipo et al. 2025**: MW's equal-distribution finding is consistent with autonomous cooperative production rather than elite-managed hierarchy.
