# Source Summary: Post-La Pérouse Visits (1793-1862)

## Overview

Rhys Richards, *Easter Island 1793 to 1861: Observations by Early Visitors Before the Slave Raids* (Easter Island Foundation, 2008). Compilation of primary source accounts from 79 visits between 1793 and 1862. Most were brief ship contacts; some involved landing parties. This covers the period between La Pérouse and the catastrophic 1862-1863 Peruvian slave raids.

## Sources Consulted

- Richards 2008 complete book (PDF covers printed pages 3-144)
- **Note**: The PDF is missing the Conclusions chapter (p. 93) and Appendices A-C (pp. 95-127), including Hugh Cuming's 1827 account and Surgeon Guthrie's 1830 Seringapatam account. These should be consulted if a complete version becomes available.

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**HMS Blossom (1825), the critical accounts**: Lieutenant Peard and mate James Wolfe independently tested the hypothesis that adorned individuals were authority figures and rejected it:
- Peard: "nor, indeed, as I have said before, were there any who appeared to possess any superiority at least in the way of command over the rest" (p. 52)
- Wolfe: "It was first thought that these might be people having authority, but we could discern no particular respect paid to them" (p. 52)
- Both describe "apparent chiefs" who attempted to restrain a crowd during a confrontation but whose "authority was very limited" (pp. 50-51)

These are trained Royal Navy observers who spent time ashore, experienced a violent confrontation, and still concluded no command hierarchy was visible.

**Pattern across 70 years**: "Chief" appears multiple times but is always assigned based on visual markers (tattooing, feathers, body paint, stature), never on observed command behavior. Trade was consistently individual and decentralized. Du Petit-Thouars (1838), the most detailed observer, never mentions authority figures despite extensive interaction.

**One counter-example**: The Foster (1822) newspaper report claims "two parties, which are frequently at war." Richards himself questions its reliability, and no other visitor corroborates it.

**Consistent across all visits**: No tribute, redistribution, or provisioning through central authority. No chiefly dwelling identified. No ceremonial deference to any individual. No religious authority described. Trade is individual-to-individual throughout.

**Social differentiation existed**: Differential tattooing, feathered ornaments, body paint. But differential adornment does not by itself constitute political hierarchy; it may indicate age grades, ritual status, lineage identity, or personal achievement.

**Changes over time**: Monument condition shows some moai still standing as late as 1838 (Du Petit-Thouars). Canoe deterioration progressive. Hostility toward visitors may have increased due to accumulated negative experiences (massacres, kidnappings).

## Relevance to the Paper

These accounts cover the pre-slave-raid period when the population was still substantial (likely 2,000-4,000). If a functioning chiefdom existed, visiting naval officers and traders might reasonably have encountered evidence: organized reception, centralized provisioning, differential treatment of authority figures, or deference to identifiable leaders. None appears in the record. The Blossom accounts are particularly valuable because trained observers explicitly tested and rejected the chief hypothesis.

The limitation: most visits were brief with severe language barriers. The absence of observed political authority may reflect observational limitations, not actual absence.
