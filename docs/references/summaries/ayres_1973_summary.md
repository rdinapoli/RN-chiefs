# Summary: Ayres 1973

**Full citation**: Ayres, William S. 1973. "The Cultural Context of Easter Island Religious Structures." PhD dissertation, Department of Anthropology, Tulane University. 402 pp.

**Advisor**: Arden R. King. Committee: R. Berle Clay, John L. Fischer, Robert Wauchope.

**Type**: PhD dissertation based on fieldwork under William Mulloy's direction, funded by International Fund for Monuments and Republic of Chile. Includes excavation and restoration of the Tahai ceremonial complex.

## Overview

The first systematic study of Easter Island ahu (ceremonial stone structures) in their full cultural context, combining archaeological excavation data with ethnographic and comparative Polynesian analysis. Ayres places ahu within the broader Polynesian marae tradition, establishes a four-phase cultural sequence, and interprets ahu as kin-group religious structures tied to ancestor veneration, land ownership, and nonunilinear descent group organization.

## Key Arguments

### Ahu as Kin-Group Structures
Ahu construction and maintenance are attributed to nonunilinear descent groups (mata, lineages/ivi) whose membership controlled the associated land. "Land ownership is important because the associated social unit would be the one whose membership was involved in constructing and maintaining ahu on their land" (p. 329). This is the organizing framework for the entire dissertation: ahu reflect kin-group identity and territorial control, not centralized political authority.

### Political Organization
The ariki mau "was not a political ruler but more of a spiritual one who presided over the entire island but did not engage in intertribal feuding" (p. 25). Actual political control shifted to warrior leaders (matatoa) who "eventually took over the actual political control of the tribes" (p. 26). Four social classes are reported but Ayres notes "the classification is a superficial one and the reality of clear-cut classes is in doubt" (p. 25).

### No Chiefdom Framework
Ayres works entirely within Goldman's (1970) "Traditional" / "Open" / "Stratified" classification. The word "chiefdom" does not appear in the dissertation. Service (1962) is not cited. No discussion of redistribution, tribute networks, or centralized economic coordination. This is what Easter Island archaeology looked like before the chiefdom model was applied.

### Cultural Phase Sequence
1. **Settlement and Developmental (A.D. 400-1000)**: Small ahu with open courts; priests as kin-group heads; solar orientation; possible early statues
2. **Expansion / Ahu Moai (A.D. 1000-1680)**: Large image ahu; standardized moai; craft guild production; Goldman's "Open Society" begins; military power increasingly important; kin groups mobilizing large labor forces
3. **Decadent / Huri Moai (A.D. 1680-1722)**: No new image ahu; statue toppling; semi-pyramidal ahu for burial; inter-mata warfare; bird-man cult ascendant
4. **Protohistoric (A.D. 1722-1868)**: Ahu defunct except as burial; Makemake dominant; last ariki Nga'ara dies before slave raids

### Labor Mobilization
Large ahu construction attributed to two factors: growing population and (following Goldman 1970) use of slave labor (kio) from military conquest. "The increased size of ahu...reflect both the growing population and the power of leaders to make people work on what was basically an altar and offering to the deceased father of a new ariki" (p. 337). A "craft guild was responsible for carving" the standardized statues (p. 338). No redistribution mechanism proposed.

### Polynesian Comparative Framework
Ayres argues for Polynesian settlement from earliest occupation, contra Heyerdahl. Extensive theoretical discussion of nonunilinear descent groups drawing on Goodenough (1955), Murdock, Gifford, Firth, Sahlins, and Davenport. Projects Proto-Polynesian social organization (including kin-group land control) back to initial settlement.

## Source Dependencies

All ethnographic claims derive from post-slave-raid sources: Métraux (1940, 1937), Thomson (1891), Routledge (1919), Roussel (1926), Englert (1938). No contact-era primary sources cited for social organization. Goldman (1970) provides the interpretive framework throughout.

## Significance for Chiefdom Attribution

1. **Pre-chiefdom baseline**: Most detailed ahu study before Kirch 1984 imposed chiefdom framework. Demonstrates that the archaeological record was coherently interpreted without chiefdom concepts.
2. **Ahu = kin-group, not chiefly command**: Construction attributed to descent groups controlling land, not to chiefly redistribution or centralized authority.
3. **Goldman over Sahlins/Service**: Adopts Goldman's "Open" classification (no central authority evolved) rather than Sahlins's stratification gradient or Service's chiefdom typology.
4. **Trajectory toward decentralization**: Phase sequence shows shift from sacred/religious authority toward military/warrior control, the opposite of chiefdom consolidation.
5. **No redistribution**: Complete absence of redistribution as explanatory mechanism for monumental construction.
6. **Absorbed into chiefdom framework**: When Kirch (1984) later positioned Easter Island within his Polynesian chiefdom framework, he was reframing an archaeological record that Ayres (and Goldman) had interpreted through a fundamentally different lens.

## Ahu Typology

Four types (following Métraux 1937): (1) image ahu (largest, cut stone, statues), (2) semi-pyramidal / ahu paepae (uncut stone, some over ruined image ahu), (3) ahu poepoe (boat-shaped, fitted stone), (4) rectangular ahu avanga (simple platforms with burial vaults). Thomson counted 113 ahu, 555 statues (238 on ahu); Routledge counted ~100 image ahu, 231 statues.

## Limitations

- Chronology based on limited radiocarbon dates (latest image ahu: A.D. 1460 at Ahu Akivi); "Decadent Phase" dating now challenged
- Relies entirely on post-slave-raid sources for social organization
- "Traditional Society" projection into Settlement Phase is speculative
- Goldman's slave labor hypothesis itself unverified archaeologically
- Rongo-rongo characterized as possible post-contact innovation (following Emory), now debated
