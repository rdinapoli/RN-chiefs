## Overview

Edited volume with Foreword by Leslie White and chapters by Harding, Kaplan, Sahlins, and Service. A theoretical manifesto for cultural evolutionism. Its core contribution is the distinction between **general evolution** (progressive advance through stages) and **specific evolution** (adaptive diversification along particular lines), a framework drawn from biological evolutionary theory and applied to culture. This distinction becomes the intellectual scaffolding for Service's (1962) band/tribe/chiefdom/state typology.

## Key Arguments

**1. General vs. Specific Evolution** (Sahlins, Ch. II)

General evolution: passage from less to greater energy transformation, lower to higher levels of integration, less to greater all-round adaptability. Uses stage/level taxonomy crosscutting phylogeny. Requires absolute (non-relativistic) criteria.

Specific evolution: the adaptive diversification of particular cultures along their own lines. Uses phylogenetic classification. Each adaptation adequate on its own terms.

Both are aspects of one total process, not different concrete realities.

**2. Criteria for Progress** (Sahlins, Ch. II)

Three criteria: (a) thermodynamic achievement (total energy captured, NOT efficiency); (b) level of integration (segmentation, specialization, integration of parts); (c) all-round adaptability (freedom from environmental control, dominance range).

**3. Biological-Cultural Homology** (Introduction)

Sahlins and Service argue cultural and biological evolution are functionally homologous (not merely analogous), because both are energy-capturing systems descended from the same total evolutionary process. This is a strong claim that later scholarship would not generally sustain.

**4. The Stage Sequence** (Sahlins, Ch. II, p. 36)

One passage sketches the evolutionary stages: chiefless bands -> egalitarian tribes -> chiefdoms with status differentiation -> archaic civilization -> nation-state. This is the embryonic form of what Service 1962 formalizes. The word "chiefdom" appears exactly once in the entire volume, in this single passage. Not elaborated.

**5. Service's Law of Evolutionary Potential** (Service, Ch. V)

"The more specialized and adapted a form in a given evolutionary stage, the smaller is its potential for passing to the next stage." Illustrated with macro-historical examples (writing, agriculture, Mediterranean civilizations, Cold War geopolitics). Contains no sociopolitical typology, no Polynesian content.

## Easter Island Content

All 6 Easter Island references appear in Chs. II-III. All derive from Sahlins 1955 ("Esoteric Efflorescence in Easter Island").

**Ch. II (Sahlins, pp. 25-26)**: Extended quotation from Sahlins 1955. Easter Islanders arrived "with a ramage organization [in Fried's (1957) terms, 'ranked lineages'] and a tradition of image carving." Environmental constraints precluded communal labor in subsistence, so labor was "channelled into an esoteric domain of culture," producing moai. The moai are presented as an adaptive specialization, not as evidence for a chiefdom. Easter Island listed alongside Australian section systems, Eskimo technology, Northwest Coast potlatching, and Paleolithic cave art as examples of "cultural bents" better understood as adaptive specializations.

**Ch. III (Harding, p. ~56)**: Carved statuary "assumed important adaptive functions in maintaining Polynesian ramage organization in the unusual environment of Easter Island (Sahlins 1955; see Chapter II)." Follows Sahlins in assuming ramage as baseline.

## Significance for the Chiefdom Concept Genealogy

### What this book does:

1. Establishes the theoretical framework (general vs. specific evolution) that justifies treating sociopolitical forms as general evolutionary stages.
2. Provides the intellectual warrant for classifying societies into levels (bands, tribes, chiefdoms, states) based on organizational criteria independent of phylogeny.
3. Sketches the band/tribe/chiefdom/state sequence (one passage, p. 36) that Service 1962 will formalize.
4. Frames Easter Island moai as an adaptive specialization within a ramage organization, reinforcing the ramage assumption from Sahlins 1955.

### What this book does NOT do:

1. Elaborate "chiefdom" as an analytical category (the word appears once).
2. Define chiefdom in terms of redistribution, centralized authority, or hereditary leadership (those come from Service 1962).
3. Connect Easter Island to chiefdom-level organization (Easter Island appears only as illustration of specific/adaptive evolution).
4. Include any Polynesian data beyond self-citations (Sahlins 1955, 1958).
5. Present Service's sociopolitical typology (Service's chapter is about evolutionary potential, not typological categories).

### Position in the genealogy:

Sahlins 1954/1958 (Polynesian data) -> **Sahlins & Service 1960 (theoretical framework)** -> Service 1962 (formalized typology) -> Sahlins 1963 (big-man/chief binary) -> Kirch 1984 (archaeological application)

This book is the bridge between Sahlins's empirical Polynesian work and Service's formal typology. It provides the theoretical justification for treating the chiefdom as a general evolutionary stage applicable beyond Polynesia, while simultaneously using Easter Island as an example of specific (adaptive, non-progressive) evolution. The irony is that Easter Island, which was one of the ethnographic cases that fed the framework, is treated here as an example of adaptation (specific evolution), not progress (general evolution). The chiefdom label is an artifact of general evolutionary classification, not of the specific adaptive analysis Sahlins himself applied to Easter Island.

## Key Absences

- No Polynesian islands besides Easter Island mentioned (no Hawaii, Tonga, Samoa)
- "Chiefdom" appears once, not elaborated
- No redistribution discussion related to political typology
- No hierarchy, centralization, or political authority discussion
- Service's chapter contains no sociopolitical typology
- No contact-era accounts, no post-raid ethnographic sources
