# Source Summary: Pinart 1877

## Overview

Alphonse Pinart, a French anthropologist, ethnographer, and linguist (age 25), visited Rapa Nui on the warship *Seignelay* from April 1-6, 1877 (six days). Pinart was the most scientifically trained of the four early post-raid visitors. His account provides the most detailed archaeological descriptions of ahu and Rano Raraku, systematic physical anthropology, and a snapshot of the island at its demographic nadir: 111 people, of whom only 26 were women.

## Sources Consulted

- Altman 2004, *Early Visitors to Easter Island 1864-1877*, Parts 4-5 (pp. 104-135): Pinart report with endnotes by Altman and Georgia Lee

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**Colonial, not traditional authority**: The only political authority Pinart encountered was Koreto, widow of the French adventurer Dutrou-Bornier, acting as "regent" for her five-year-old daughter Caroline, whom she designated "the queen." This authority derived entirely from Dutrou-Bornier's self-appointed colonial rule, not from any traditional Rapanui political structure. Koreto claimed ownership of all livestock (except chickens, which belonged to "the Kanacs"), requested French protection, and expressed hostility toward Chileans, Americans, and Germans.

**"Major domo" with baton**: Pinart observed "a kind of major domo with a baton in his hand" maintaining order at Koreto's reception. This figure likely represents a functionary of Dutrou-Bornier's household operation, not a traditional office.

**No traditional political structure described**: Pinart, a trained ethnographer, records no chiefs, no social classes, no governance structure. The complete silence on this topic from a scientific observer is itself significant evidence.

## Context and Limitations

**[POST-RAID]** Visit occurred 14 years after the slave raids. The population had reached its nadir of 111 people. Dutrou-Bornier had removed most of the remaining population to Tahiti to work on his plantation in Mangareva. Traditional social organization had been completely destroyed.

**Total loss of traditional knowledge**: "The present inhabitants of the Easter Island... had retained no memory at all of those who had come before them" (pp. 122-123). Rongorongo tablets were being used as fishing line spools. This level of knowledge loss, just 14 years after the raids, underscores how little of "traditional" knowledge could have survived to be recorded by Thompson (1891) or Routledge (1919).

**Trained observer**: Unlike Eyraud and Roussel (missionaries) or Loti (literary writer), Pinart was a professional anthropologist. His archaeological descriptions are the most precise and systematic of the four accounts.

## Relevance to the Paper

Pinart's account documents the endpoint of the post-raid collapse sequence: Eyraud 1864 (~1,200 people), Roussel 1869 (~650), Loti 1872 (~100), Pinart 1877 (111). By 1877, traditional political structure was completely gone, replaced by the colonial authority of Dutrou-Bornier's widow. The total loss of traditional knowledge that Pinart documents is critical for assessing what Thompson (1891, ~25 years later) and Routledge (1919, ~55 years later) could plausibly have recovered about pre-contact political organization. Pinart's detailed archaeological descriptions of ahu construction, Rano Raraku quarry organization, settlement patterns, and manavai (deep gardens) provide the earliest systematic architectural documentation, useful for independent archaeological analysis.
