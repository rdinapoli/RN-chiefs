# Source Summary: Loti 1872

## Overview

Pierre Loti (pen name of Louis Marie Julien Viaud), a 22-year-old French naval cadet on the warship *La Flore*, visited Rapa Nui from January 3-8, 1872 (five days). His account, published as "Diary of a Cadet," is literary and romantic in style. Loti later became one of France's most celebrated novelists (elected to the French Academy, 1891). His account provides vivid observations of conditions on the island ten years after the slave raids but is not ethnographically systematic.

## Sources Consulted

- Altman 2004, *Early Visitors to Easter Island 1864-1877*, Part 3 (pp. 62-103): Loti diary with endnotes by Altman

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**One informal local "chief"**: Loti refers multiple times to "the old chief" near Hanga Roa who lives in a cave/hut. The old chief trades a stone idol for an admiral's greatcoat. No commanding behavior, no deference from others, no political role described. The term "chief" appears to be Loti's casual label for an elderly local figure.

**Dutrou-Bornier's legacy**: The Danish carpenter Christian Schmidt, who worked for Dutrou-Bornier, serves as Loti's guide. Loti describes Dutrou-Bornier as having "set himself up as a king on the island a few years earlier." By 1872, the political landscape is dominated by Dutrou-Bornier's commercial operation, not by any traditional authority structure.

**No political structure observed**: Loti describes no chiefs, no governance, no organized political activity. Social interactions are individual and informal. The small population (~100 people) is concentrated around Hanga Roa.

## Context and Limitations

**[POST-RAID]** Visit occurred ten years after the slave raids. The population had collapsed from Eyraud's estimate of ~1,200 (1864) and Roussel's census of 650 (1869) to approximately 100 people. Most of the island was deserted.

**Literary, not ethnographic**: Loti was an aspiring novelist, not a scientist. His descriptions prioritize atmosphere, emotion, and aesthetic impression over systematic observation. He romanticizes the islanders and speculates freely (Atlantis, sacred island for Pacific-wide ceremonies). His account contains factual errors: he attributes a visit to "Vancouver," who never visited Easter Island.

**Brief visit**: Five days provides limited ethnographic depth.

## Relevance to the Paper

Loti's primary value is as a witness to the extreme demographic collapse and cultural loss by 1872. The near-total depopulation (only ~100 people) and concentration into a single settlement, the dancing on an overturned moai (suggesting no sacred status), the use of an old man's cave-dwelling as a "chief's" residence, and the deserted landscape with well-worn paths to nowhere collectively document a society in terminal crisis. This context is essential for understanding what later observers (Thompson 1891, Routledge 1919) encountered: not traditional society but a society that had undergone catastrophic collapse and partial recovery. Loti's account also confirms the dominance of Dutrou-Bornier's commercial enterprise over any surviving traditional authority.
