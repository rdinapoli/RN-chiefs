## Overview

This influential article establishes the big-man (Melanesia) vs. chief (Polynesia) distinction as the standard typology for Pacific political organization. Big-men achieve personal power through competitive generosity and faction-building; chiefs inherit office, control redistribution through tabu, command labor, and head permanent administrative establishments. The "Polynesian chief" is built overwhelmingly from Hawaiian (Malo, Fornander), Tahitian (Handy, Henry), and Tongan (Gifford, Mariner) data. Easter Island is mentioned once, as a geographic boundary of the Polynesian triangle (p. 285), and is never analyzed, discussed, or cited.

## Key Argument

Sahlins contrasts Melanesian and Polynesian political orders as two evolutionary types. Melanesian big-men create personal followings through competitive redistribution, but their authority is fragile, faction-dependent, and limited in scale (70-300 persons). Polynesian chiefs inherit positions in a hierarchical structure of ranked offices, command the labor and surplus of households, control great storehouses, subsidize construction and warfare, and maintain administrative cadres and armed retainers. Polynesian polities reached much larger scale (2,000-tens of thousands). Both types generate internal contradictions: big-men overextract from followers; chiefs overextract from the populace. The Polynesian ceiling was higher but still led to cycles of centralization and fragmentation.

## Significance for the Chiefdom Attribution

**What the article does to Easter Island by implication**: By defining "Polynesia" as the domain of chiefs and "Melanesia" as the domain of big-men, the article places Easter Island on the "chief" side without examination. The question is no longer whether Easter Island had chiefs but what kind of chiefdom it was. This is the framing Kirch (1984) inherits.

**The "Polynesian chief" does not match Easter Island's evidence**: Sahlins's characterization of the Polynesian chief (hereditary office, redistributive control, tabu as economic tool, administrative establishment, armed enforcement) does not fit what any source, including Sahlins's own 1954 dissertation, describes for Easter Island. Goldman's (1970) "Open" classification, where military/political power displaces hereditary authority, captures Easter Island's reality far better. In several respects (achieved leadership, personal followings, factional warfare, competitive status rivalry), Easter Island's matatoa-dominated system resembles the "big-man" pattern more than the "chief" pattern.

**Goldman already provides the counter-evidence**: Sahlins cites Goldman (1955, 1957, 1960) for "status rivalry" but does not engage with the implication that Goldman's framework classifies some Polynesian societies as fundamentally different from the "chief" type.

**Contribution to the circularity chain**: Sahlins 1954/1958 classifies Easter Island on a Polynesian stratification gradient with acknowledged data gaps. Sahlins 1963 creates the binary that places all Polynesia on the "chief" side. Service 1962 (cited here as "in press") formalizes "chiefdom" as a universal evolutionary stage. Kirch 1984 applies the formalized concept back to Easter Island. The 1963 article is the step where the category becomes assumed rather than demonstrated.

## Source Assessment

The article is self-consciously a typological exercise, not an empirical generalization. Sahlins calls his method "The Method of Uncontrolled Comparison" and his types "abstracted sociological types," acknowledging "important variants" and "exceptional political forms" (p. 285, fn.). He notes the continuum is a "gradient" not a "step-like, quantum progression" (p. 286). But the article's rhetorical force and subsequent influence overwhelmed these qualifications.
