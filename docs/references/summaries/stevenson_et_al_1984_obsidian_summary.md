## Overview

This 5-page paper reports a preliminary investigation of the Orito obsidian quarry conducted in April 1981, alongside an island-wide analysis of obsidian consumption rates using hydration dating from 184 residential sites near Ahu Akahanga. The paper is contemporaneous with Stevenson's 1984 dissertation and shares its interpretive framework: corporate descent groups, no chiefdom terminology, no neoevolutionary typology. Co-authored with Leslie Shaw and Claudio Cristino.

The paper contributes two distinct datasets: (1) a lithic reduction sequence at the quarry showing how obsidian was processed and distributed, and (2) a consumption curve tracking obsidian use per 50-year phase from AD 1350-1864.

---

## Key Findings

### Quarry organization

The Orito quarry consists of 33 mapped open-pit mines (northeastern sector only; quarrying occurred around the full circumference of the hill). Pits are shallow (<1 m deep), with debris pushed downslope. Two core types were produced: discoidal cores (from thin slabs, for domestic flake tools) and block cores (from thick slabs, for mataa production). Discoidal cores were prepared at the quarry, transported to habitation sites, and flaked as needed. Mataa were manufactured primarily at the quarry.

No evidence of restricted access, specialized production zones, or elite control over obsidian procurement is presented. The quarry description is consistent with open access by multiple groups.

### Obsidian consumption curve (AD 1350-1864)

The consumption ratio (specimens per occupation per phase) rises steadily from 1.00 (Phase 1, AD 1350) to 1.54 (Phase 6, AD 1601-1650), dips slightly in Phase 7 (AD 1651-1700, ratio 1.43), then rises to its highest levels in Phases 8-10 (AD 1701-1850, ratios 1.69-1.72), before dropping sharply after AD 1850.

The Phase 7 dip corresponds to Metraux's oral tradition date of AD 1680 for "major transformations" in socio-political structure. The post-1700 rise corresponds with increased population within the study area.

### Interpretive hypotheses

Two alternative hypotheses are offered for the post-1700 rise in obsidian consumption:

1. Population increase and nucleation resulted in larger residential units and intensified domestic activities.
2. Political and social instability reduced the ability of specific groups to control access to obsidian within their territories, thereby increasing access and lowering acquisition costs.

Both are explicitly left unevaluated. Neither invokes chiefly authority or centralized management.

## Significance for the Chiefdom Question

### Terminological consistency with Stevenson 1984 dissertation

Published the same year as the dissertation, this paper confirms that Stevenson's interpretive framework in 1984 had no place for chiefdom terminology. "Chiefdom," "chief," "hierarchy," "stratification," and "redistribution" are entirely absent. The paper cites McCoy 1976 and Metraux 1940 but not Service 1962, Sahlins, Kirch, or Goldman.

### Quarry access patterns

The quarry description, with 33+ open pits distributed around the hill and no evidence of access restriction, is consistent with the decentralized production model later documented by Lipo et al. 2025 for the moai quarry at Rano Raraku. Both quarries show multiple independent production loci without evidence of centralized coordination or restricted access.

### Territorial control as a qualified hypothesis

The paper mentions territorial control over obsidian flows as a possibility for the later prehistoric period ("may have been a factor"), but this is immediately hedged and attributed to Metraux's conflict traditions. The alternative hypothesis (claim 41b) explicitly proposes that political instability *reduced* territorial control, the opposite of what a chiefdom-consolidation model would predict.

### Obsidian consumption continuity through contact

The consumption curve shows no dramatic disruption at AD 1680 (the traditional "collapse" date); instead, a slight dip is followed by the highest consumption levels in the entire sequence. This pattern is consistent with Mulrooney 2013's finding of settlement continuity rather than collapse, and with the broader post-ecocide revisionist framework.

## Relationship to Other Processed Sources

- **Mulrooney 2013**: Obsidian consumption curve showing post-1680 continuity/increase is consistent with Mulrooney's radiocarbon evidence against pre-contact collapse.

## Limitations

- Single pit excavation; reduction sequence is preliminary.
- All dates depend on the 6.29U²/Yr⁻¹ hydration rate (Michels et al.), subsequently revised. Relative patterns more reliable than absolute dates.
- Akahanga habitation sample described as "small"; quarry-habitation comparison is hypothesis-generating, not conclusive.
- Study area covers only the northeastern sector of Orito; full quarry extent not characterized.
