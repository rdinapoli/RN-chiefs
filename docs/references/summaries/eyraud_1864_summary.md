# Source Summary: Eyraud 1864

## Overview

Brother Eugene Eyraud, lay member of the Congregation of the Sacred Hearts, arrived on Rapa Nui on January 2, 1864, one year after the 1862-1863 Peruvian slave raids. He was the first European to live on the island, staying approximately nine months. He arrived from Tahiti accompanied by six repatriated Rapanui. His letter to the Father Superior, written from Valparaiso in December 1864, is the earliest post-slave-raid account and the closest in time to the demographic catastrophe.

## Sources Consulted

- Altman 2004, *Early Visitors to Easter Island 1864-1877*, Part 1 (pp. 1-33): Eyraud letter with editorial introduction by Georgia Lee and endnotes by Altman
- Altman et al. 2003, "The Sojourn of the First Missionary on Rapa Nui: Eugene Eyraud Among the Kanacs, 1864," *Rapa Nui Journal* 17(1): separate English translation with scholarly annotations

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**No institutional political structure observed.** Eyraud's single most important statement on authority: "It is hard for me to characterize the authority of chiefs on the island. I don't even know the basis for such authority. It seems that authority depended only on the influence that some natives were able to exert over their neighbors and that the people gradually grew accustomed to such displays of superiority" (p. 14). This describes personal dominance, not institutional hierarchy.

**Torometi as "chief"**: Eyraud's primary interaction was with Torometi, a man "of about thirty, tall and strong" who "lorded it over his neighbors" and whom Eyraud identified as "a chief." Torometi claimed ownership of Eyraud and his belongings, provided daily rations of sweet potatoes, and used a combination of coercion, theft, and social pressure to extract Eyraud's goods. When Torometi's behavior became egregious, a community action led by Tamateka burned his hut as collective punishment. This episode demonstrates that "chiefly" authority was contested, personal, and subject to communal correction.

**No organized political reception.** When Eyraud landed, there was no delegation, no formal greeting, no chief coming forward. "I looked for the Kanacs who had come with me from Tahiti. I found them in the middle of the throng and they looked almost as uncomfortable as I was" (pp. 8-9). Compare with La Pérouse 1786, who similarly found no organized reception.

**Collective action without command hierarchy.** The boat-building episode reveals cooperative production: "I had noted this custom of making everyone contribute to a project that was considered important. Nobody would think of not participating" (p. 27). Wood was gathered from across the island through voluntary contribution, not chiefly command.

## Context and Limitations

**[POST-RAID]** All observations were made one year after the slave raids. The introduction notes "a time of considerable anarchy on the island" and that "the chiefs and priests" were among those taken to Peru. Whatever Eyraud observed was a society in crisis, not functioning normally.

**Population approximately 1,200** at the time of Eyraud's visit (his estimate, p. 7), compared to the pre-raid population of approximately 3,000. The population had already been reduced by roughly 60%.

**Eyraud was not a trained observer.** He was a lay missionary whose primary interest was conversion, not ethnography. His language skills were limited. His account prioritizes his personal experiences over systematic observation of social structure.

## Relevance to the Paper

Eyraud's account is significant primarily for what it does NOT describe: no hereditary chiefs, no hierarchical political structure, no tribute system, no formal governance. His description of authority as dependent on personal influence rather than institutional position, recorded just one year after the slave raids, suggests either that (a) the slave raids had already destroyed institutional authority, or (b) the political structure was never as formalized as later ethnographic reconstructions suggest. The boat-building episode provides positive evidence for collective action without chiefly command.
