# Summary: Pollard et al. 2010

**Source**: Pollard, Joshua, Alistair Paterson, and Kate Welham. 2010. "*Te Miro o'one*: the archaeology of contact on Rapa Nui (Easter Island)." *World Archaeology* 42(4): 562-580.

---

## Overview

Pollard et al. examine the archaeology of European contact on Rapa Nui from Roggeveen's arrival in 1722 to the island's conversion to Christianity in 1868. Their central argument is that the contact period should not be read as a "simple trajectory of decline" appended to a prehistoric sequence, but rather as a time of creative cultural agency in which new practices, social orders, and cosmological frameworks emerged through engagement with European material culture and presence. The paper focuses on ship/canoe imagery (petroglyphs, paintings, and earthwork *miro o'one* boat mounds), the *tangata manu* (birdman) cult, and the material record of exchange and ritual innovation during the contact period.

The paper is not primarily about political organization, but it makes several claims that bear directly on the chiefdom attribution for Rapa Nui.

## Key Findings Relevant to the Chiefdom Question

### 1. The birdman cult replaced hereditary chiefly authority

Pollard et al. characterize the *tangata manu* system as a competitive, performative institution that substituted "traditional chiefly authority constructed around genealogically ascribed rank" with "performative leadership structures controlled by warriors and priests" (p. 566, citing Thomas 1990). The winner became "paramount warrior chief for the year" (p. 566). If correct, this means the political system Europeans encountered in the 18th-19th centuries was not a hereditary chiefdom but a competitive warrior-leadership system, precisely the opposite of what the Sahlins-Kirch chiefdom model describes.

### 2. The 'Orongo chronology may be post-contact

The paper critically reassesses the radiocarbon chronology for 'Orongo, the ceremonial center of the birdman cult. The standard date of c. AD 1540 for the construction of Complex B relies on extrapolation from bulk charcoal dates in Trench 1 to construction events in Trench 2, which Pollard et al. call "a tenuous assumption" (p. 567). They argue that the stone structures of Complexes B and C "could equally be argued" to belong to "a horizon of religious and political change that is post-contact" (p. 567), and express the view that new dates would likely support this reading.

If correct, this has, as Pollard et al. state, "profound implications for our understanding of the timing and mechanisms of changing politico-religious practices on the island, effectively moving key events out of the prehistoric sequence" (p. 567). The entire narrative of a prehistoric "Decadent/Restructure Phase" (c. AD 1680-1722 in Van Tilburg's sequence) would need reconsideration.

### 3. Moai toppling may be contact-driven

The paper notes that the statue cult appeared extant as late as 1770 (Gonzalez visit) but that Cook in 1774 already described statues being toppled (p. 565). Pollard et al. suggest that the politically destabilizing actions of the Spanish visit (erecting crosses, firing guns, claiming the island) may have triggered the internal conflicts that led to moai toppling (p. 569). If the end of the statue cult is a contact-period event rather than a prehistoric one, the supposed prehistoric transition from ahu/moai-based chiefly authority to birdman-based warrior authority loses its archaeological anchor.

### 4. Europeans were incorporated into Polynesian cosmological categories

The paper argues that Rapa Nui people understood Europeans through existing Polynesian frameworks in which strangers from across the sea could be semi-divine, and in which high-ranking chiefs "were regarded as strangers or invaders" (p. 568, citing Thomas 1991). The implication is that performances of hierarchy and deference during contact encounters may reflect cosmologically appropriate responses to perceived divine or chiefly strangers, not displays of everyday political organization. This complicates the use of contact-era accounts as evidence for hierarchical political structure.

### 5. Geographic differentiation in ceremonial practice

The distribution of rock art shows Polynesian canoe petroglyphs concentrated on the north coast (Ra'ai to 'Anakena) while European/hybrid ship images concentrate on the south coast, "perhaps implying a division in ceremonial practices that reflects the existence at this time of competing clan confederations" (p. 574). This pattern is consistent with decentralized, competing groups rather than a unified chiefdom.

### 6. Minimal European observation time

Total European contact before 1795 amounted to approximately two weeks over an 80-year period across seven visits (p. 567). Even during the busier 1801-1861 period, ships typically stopped for only a day or two (p. 567). The brevity of these encounters severely limits what observers could have understood about political organization.

## What the Paper Demonstrates vs. What It Does Not

**Demonstrates**: That contact-period material culture on Rapa Nui (ship imagery, *miro o'one* earthworks, exchange objects) reflects creative cultural agency rather than simple decline; that the chronology of 'Orongo is insecure and potentially post-contact; that moai toppling falls within the contact period; that European goods were incorporated into existing Polynesian cosmological and social frameworks; that geographic variation in ceremonial art suggests competing groups.

**Does not demonstrate**: That the birdman cult is definitively post-contact (they advocate for new dating, but have not produced it); that moai toppling was *caused* by the Gonzalez visit (they suggest it, framed as possibility); that the political system at any particular date was or was not a chiefdom (not their question); that the *miro o'one* ceremonies are direct evidence of a particular political structure.

## Bearing on the Five-Step Circularity

Pollard et al. do not address the circularity problem directly, but their arguments undermine several of its key steps:

**Step 1 (European accounts as evidence of political structure)**: The paper shows that European encounters were brief, cosmologically charged events in which both sides performed within their own interpretive frameworks. The accounts describe performances staged for extraordinary encounters, not ordinary political life.

**Step 2 (Polynesian analogy applied to Rapa Nui)**: The paper does not challenge the analogy directly, but by suggesting that key political changes (birdman cult, moai toppling) may be contact-driven, it removes the archaeological evidence that is supposed to confirm the analogical model. If the birdman cult is post-contact, it cannot confirm a model of prehistoric political evolution.

**Step 3 (Archaeological evidence interpreted through the model)**: The paper demonstrates that the 'Orongo chronology, the primary archaeological anchor for the "transition" narrative from hereditary chiefdom to warrior-chief system, rests on insecure radiocarbon dates extrapolated across trenches (p. 567). The archaeological evidence used to structure the political narrative is weaker than usually acknowledged.

**Step 4 (Apparent "confirmation")**: By questioning whether the key political-religious changes are prehistoric at all, the paper removes the possibility of using them as confirmation of a prehistoric political model. If birdman ceremonies and moai toppling are both post-contact, then there is no archaeological evidence for a prehistoric political transition of the kind the chiefdom model predicts.

**Step 5 (Model becomes assumed)**: The paper implicitly challenges this step by showing that the "decline" narrative (which assumes a prehistoric chiefdom that collapsed) has prevented scholars from seeing the contact period as a time of cultural creativity and political innovation in its own right.

## Limitations and Cautions

The paper relies heavily on interpretive analogy with broader Polynesian contact experiences (Tahiti, Hawaii, New Zealand) and on the theoretical framework of Thomas (1990, 1991, 2003) and Campbell (1997, 2003) for understanding culture contact. Its arguments about the cosmological significance of ship imagery, while suggestive, are speculative. The claim that 'Orongo is post-contact remains a hypothesis awaiting new dates. The paper does not present new archaeological data; it reinterprets existing material within a contact-archaeology framework.

For our purposes, the paper's value lies primarily in its critical reassessment of the 'Orongo chronology, its characterization of the birdman cult as a replacement for hereditary chiefly authority, and its argument that the political changes visible in the archaeological record may be contact-period phenomena rather than the culmination of a prehistoric sequence.
