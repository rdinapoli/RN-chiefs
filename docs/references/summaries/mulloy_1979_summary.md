# Summary: Mulloy 1979

**Full citation**: Mulloy, William T. 1979. "A Preliminary Culture-Historical Research Model for Easter Island." In *Las Islas Oceánicas de Chile*, Vol. 1, pp. 105-151. Santiago: Instituto de Estudios Internacionales, Universidad de Chile. Reprinted (excerpted) in *Rapa Nui Notes* 7, Spring 1988.

**Caveat**: This summary is based on a 3-page excerpt published in *Rapa Nui Notes* 7 (Spring 1988), not the full 47-page paper. The full paper may contain additional material, including Mulloy's discussion of Métraux's and Englert's models (briefly noted by the editor).

**Type**: Culture-historical synthesis; last paper Mulloy wrote before his death in 1978

## Overview

Mulloy's final publication outlines a "tentative model" of Easter Island prehistory as a research tool, emphasizing the inadequacy of the archaeological evidence for reliable reconstruction. The paper presents a phase sequence from initial settlement through cultural climax to decline, all occurring before European contact. Mulloy explicitly frames the model as preliminary and expendable, warns against imposing foreign historical sequences on local developments, and acknowledges that post-slave-raid demographic collapse destroyed cultural memory.

## Key Arguments

### Political Organization
Mulloy assumes "a class organized society with enough coercive power to bring large work crews together" but provides no evidence for this characterization and cites no theoretical framework. He does not use the terms "chiefdom," "chief," "hierarchy," or "redistribution." Service 1962, Sahlins 1958, and Goldman 1970 are not cited. The assumption of coercive labor mobilization stands in tension with Mulloy's own earlier work (Mulloy & Figueroa 1966), which attributed ahu variation to kin-group "numbers and capacity" and "a sense of competition among them."

### Post-Slave-Raid Source Unreliability
Mulloy explicitly acknowledges that the demographic collapse (111 people by 1877) destroyed cultural memory: "only a demoralized and to a considerable extent deculturated remnant population has been available as culture-historical informants." He recognizes that "a proportionately much greater reliance has had to be placed on archaeological evidence" than on other Pacific islands. This is a direct statement of the epistemological problem that the current paper develops.

### Archaeological Evidence Insufficiency
Mulloy characterizes the archaeological evidence as "far from adequate" across multiple categories: settlement period evidence "entirely lacking," domestic architecture lacking time perspective, land-use development poorly understood, environmental modification data "sketchy," artifact studies "little developed." This assessment, from the archaeologist with the most field experience on the island, frames the subsequent chiefdom attribution (Kirch 1984) as applied to an evidence base its primary investigator considered inadequate.

### Methodological Caution
The model is presented as "one possible historical appreciation of the facts" that "would be expected continuously to be modified." Mulloy explicitly rejects imposing "a foreign historical sequence which local developments might be expected to parallel," a statement that directly anticipates and counters the Polynesian chiefdom model Kirch 1984 subsequently applied. He writes that future studies "will surely modify the model and may ultimately demonstrate that it should be discarded."

### Ahu Destruction Hypothesis
Mulloy proposes that image ahu may have been "destroyed by elements of the societies of their owners rather than by enemy groups from other localities." If correct, this reframes ahu destruction as internal to kin groups rather than as evidence of inter-polity warfare, which complicates the warfare-driven collapse narrative embedded in the chiefdom model.

## Source Dependencies

The excerpt references Métraux, Englert, Mulloy & Figueroa, McCoy, Ayres, Heyerdahl expedition, and Cousteau Society. No reference to Service, Sahlins, Goldman, Kirch, or any neoevolutionary typological framework.

## Significance for Chiefdom Attribution

1. **No chiefdom terminology**: Mulloy's final paper, representing his mature assessment after 23 years of fieldwork (1955-1978), does not frame Easter Island's political organization in chiefdom terms. The absence is significant because Mulloy was the most experienced field archaeologist on the island.

2. **Epistemic caution vs. subsequent certainty**: Mulloy's explicit framing of his model as tentative, preliminary, and subject to discard contrasts sharply with the treatment of the chiefdom attribution as established fact from Kirch 1984 onward.

3. **Post-raid source problem recognized**: Mulloy independently recognizes the core epistemological problem (demographic collapse destroying cultural memory) that the current paper develops. His recognition that archaeological evidence must bear disproportionate weight undermines subsequent reliance on post-raid ethnographic sources for political organization claims.

4. **Mulloy school pattern**: Mulloy himself, plus his students Ayres (1973), McCoy (1976), and Stevenson (1984), all interpreted Easter Island's archaeological record without chiefdom concepts. Four independent researchers working from the same excavation tradition avoided the chiefdom label. The attribution came from outside this tradition (Kirch 1984, working primarily from Hawaii).

## Limitations

- Only a 3-page excerpt available; full paper may contain additional relevant material
- No detailed discussion of social organization or political structure in the excerpt
- Environmental deterioration / overpopulation narrative present but not developed into chiefdom framework
- "Class organized society" assumption stated without evidence
