## Overview

This paper presents the first comprehensive high-resolution three-dimensional documentation of the Rano Raraku moai quarry on Rapa Nui, generated through UAV-based Structure-from-Motion photogrammetry using over 11,000 images at sub-centimeter pixel resolution (0.86-1.12 cm). The 3D model was used to conduct a systematic spatial analysis of production features across the quarry, with the explicit goal of testing whether moai production was centrally controlled or organized in a decentralized, clan-based pattern consistent with broader evidence from elsewhere on the island.

The study was conducted between June 2023 and January 2024, authorized by the Rapa Nui Comunidad Indigena Ma'u Henua, and funded by NSF Award #2218602. The resulting 3D model and all feature data are publicly available via Zenodo and ArcGIS Online.

Published in PLOS ONE, November 26, 2025.

## Key Findings

### Comprehensive quarry inventory

The 3D model produced the most complete inventory of the quarry to date: 426 sculpted objects (moai in various stages of completion), 341 trenches, 133 quarried voids, 30 quarrying foci, 13 *pu* (post holes for anchor points), 7 *taheta* (rainwater catchment basins), 5 bollards, and 17 other features including 3 *umu* (earth ovens), 1 *hare paenga* (house foundation), 1 ramp, and areas of quarried waste material (Table 1, p. 8). The model also revealed previously undocumented production areas on the crater's exterior slope and a system of bollards associated with *pu* for lowering moai down steep terrain.

### 30 distinct quarrying foci

The central empirical finding is the identification of at least 30 distinct quarrying foci distributed across the volcanic crater landscape (Figs 9-10, p. 7). These workshop areas are spatially continuous zones of redundant quarrying activity, typically delineated by unquarried bedrock or natural geological discontinuities rather than by imposed barriers. Each focus contains a combination of quarried voids (where statues were removed) and figures abandoned in various stages of completion. The spatial redundancy of these workshops, each containing a full complement of production features, indicates that multiple groups worked simultaneously at the quarry.

### Three distinct carving methods

Three quarrying procedures were identified across the quarry (Table 2, p. 10): (1) the face-outline method (143 examples), in which facial details are carved first in supine position, followed by the body; (2) the block method (120 examples), in which rectangular blocks of tuff are outlined first and then shaped; and (3) the lateral method (5 examples), in which carvers work sideways into near-vertical cliff faces. The diversity of techniques suggests adaptation to local geological conditions by autonomous groups rather than standardized procedures imposed by centralized management.

### Small production teams

Physical constraints within each workshop area limited the number of active carvers to perhaps 4-6 working directly on a single moai at one time, with an additional 10-20 personnel for support activities (tool and rope production, provisioning). This scale is consistent with kin-group labor teams, not an island-wide workforce coordinated by a central authority.

### Engineering infrastructure for small-group transport

The model revealed *pu makari* features (carved holes) and anchor stones near the summit of Maunga Eo that facilitated the movement of statues by small work groups, eliminating the need for centralized coordination of large labor forces (Fig 4, p. 7).

## Significance for the Chiefdom Question

### What the paper demonstrates

1. Moai production at Rano Raraku was spatially organized into 30 distinct, redundant quarrying foci, each with varied carving techniques, delineated by natural boundaries rather than imposed barriers. This is the first systematic, empirically documented characterization of quarry spatial organization.

2. The quarry pattern is consistent with the decentralized, clan-based organization documented in Rapa Nui settlement patterns (Morrison 2012), moai transport routes (Lipo and Hunt 2005), lithic tool distributions (Lipo et al. 2010), and population modeling (Lipo et al. 2021). The quarry was the single most important shared resource on the island, and its organization mirrors the decentralized pattern found everywhere else.

3. Standardized moai forms coexisted with organizational autonomy, demonstrating that stylistic convergence can emerge through horizontal information sharing and cultural tradition without hierarchical oversight.

4. The physical evidence is inconsistent with expectations derived from centralized control: there is no evidence of standardized production procedures, restricted quarry access, or hierarchical spatial organization.

### What the paper does not demonstrate

1. The paper does not directly demonstrate that specific clans or *mata* used specific workshop areas. The connection between quarrying foci and ethnographically described social groups is an inference from spatial pattern, not a demonstrated association.

2. The paper does not resolve the temporal relationships between workshop areas. The argument for contemporaneous operation rests on spatial redundancy and the impracticality of sequential use of 30 foci, not on direct chronological evidence (e.g., radiocarbon dating of individual workshops).

3. The paper does not demonstrate the social mechanisms by which quarry access was negotiated among groups. It demonstrates the absence of evidence for centralized access restriction, which is not the same as demonstrating a specific alternative mechanism.

4. The paper does not address the temporal trajectory of quarry organization. Whether production began as centralized and became decentralized (or vice versa), or was decentralized throughout, cannot be determined from the spatial evidence alone.

5. The scaling argument (decentralized production implies decentralized political organization) involves an inferential step. Production could in principle be decentralized at the quarry while still being coordinated at a higher political level through mechanisms not visible in quarry spatial organization. The paper argues this is unlikely given the convergence of evidence from other domains, but it cannot rule it out from quarry evidence alone.

### How it bears on claims that moai production required chiefly coordination

The paper directly addresses and undermines the argument that moai production required centralized chiefly coordination. The logic of the chiefdom model as applied to Rapa Nui depends partly on the premise that monumental statue production at a single quarry site constitutes evidence for island-wide resource control and labor mobilization by a paramount authority. Lipo et al. 2025 shows that the quarry evidence itself is inconsistent with this premise: production was spatially distributed across 30 redundant workshops employing varied techniques, constrained to small team sizes by physical space, and delineated by natural rather than imposed boundaries.

The paper explicitly states: "These findings challenge assumptions that monumentality requires hierarchical control, instead supporting emerging frameworks that recognize how complex cooperative behaviors can emerge through horizontal social networks" (p. 1). It frames the quarry as evidence that "societies can successfully coordinate large-scale productive activities through distributed networks of semi-autonomous groups" (p. 12).

The paper notes a Polynesian parallel: the early phases of large-scale agricultural field systems in Kona and Kohala, Hawaii, likely emerged without centralized control (citing Allen 2004 and Field et al. 2011), demonstrating that even in well-documented Polynesian chiefdoms, monumental-scale production does not always correlate with centralized direction.

## Relationship to Other Sources in This Project

- **Lipo et al. 2013 (walking moai)**: Demonstrated that moai transport required only small teams (18 people minimum). Lipo et al. 2025 extends the small-group argument from transport to production, showing that both phases of the moai cycle were compatible with kin-group-scale labor.

- **DiNapoli et al. 2019 (ahu-freshwater)**: Showed that ahu placement is explained by freshwater proximity, not agricultural territorial control or marine resource control. Lipo et al. 2025 adds the production locus itself to the pattern of evidence against centralized resource management.

- **Lipo and Hunt 2005 (transport roads)**: Documented radial transport paths suggesting separate group efforts rather than a coordinated transport system. The quarry's 30 redundant workshops provide the production-side complement to this transport-side finding.

- **Morrison 2012 (settlement patterns)**: Found spatially discrete community clusters with redundant architectural features. The quarry's redundant workshop areas mirror this settlement pattern at the production center.

- **Lipo et al. 2010 (obsidian tools)**: Showed highly localized patterns of material culture distribution indicating limited inter-community interaction. The quarry evidence for autonomous workshop groups is consistent with this broader pattern.

- **Routledge 1919, Metraux 1940, Englert 1948**: The paper connects its quarrying foci to the *mata* (clan/tribal) divisions documented by these ethnographers. This connection should be noted as relying on post-slave-raid sources. [POST-RAID dependency noted]

- **Pollard et al. 2010 (quarry studies)**: Previous quarry documentation was limited to two-dimensional renderings and partial inventories. Lipo et al. 2025 supersedes these with the first comprehensive 3D documentation.

## Limitations for Use in the Manuscript

1. The ethnographic connection (quarrying foci = *mata* territories) relies on post-slave-raid sources. The spatial pattern itself is strong, but the social-organizational interpretation depends on analogy with ethnographic descriptions of doubtful reliability for pre-contact conditions.

2. The contemporaneity inference is based on spatial redundancy, not direct chronological evidence. Sequential or partially overlapping use of different quarry areas cannot be definitively excluded.

3. The paper's conclusion that decentralized production was "advantageous" in preventing resource overexploitation (p. 12) is interpretive and goes beyond what the spatial evidence demonstrates. Cite the spatial findings, not the adaptive-advantage speculation.

4. As own work, cite with transparency about what the 3D analysis specifically shows (spatial distribution, feature counts, carving variation) versus what is inferred (clan-based organization, contemporaneous operation, negotiated access).
