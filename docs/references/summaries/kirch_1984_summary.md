# Source Summary: Kirch 1984

## Overview

Kirch's book is the foundational archaeological treatment of Polynesian chiefdoms. It proposes to trace the evolution of chiefdom societies from a reconstructed Ancestral Polynesian Society (APS) through three case studies: Tonga, Hawai'i, and Easter Island. The book's core argument is that colonizing Polynesians carried with them a "cultural baggage" of hierarchical institutions (the conical clan with its *ariki, mana, tapu*, first fruits, tribute) that was then transformed in different ways depending on local ecological constraints, demographic trajectories, and competitive dynamics.

The book is organized in four parts: (1) the general Polynesian framework and APS reconstruction (Chs. 1-3), (2) process chapters on colonization, demography, environment, production, and conflict (Chs. 4-8), (3) three case studies (Chs. 9-11), and (4) a synthetic epilogue (Ch. 12).

---

## Significance for the Chiefdom Attribution Chain

This book is **Stage 3** in the Sahlins → Service → Kirch chain. It transforms the chiefdom attribution from an ethnographic classification (Sahlins 1954/1958) into an archaeological narrative. What was a synchronic typological placement becomes a diachronic story of rise and fall. Specifically:

1. **Sahlins placed Easter Island in Group IIA** (moderate-high stratification) based on post-slave-raid ethnographic sources. Kirch inherits this placement and reframes it as a "developed form of the Ancestral Polynesian segmentary ramage system" (p. 272).

2. **The APS argument is Kirch's key innovation.** Rather than arguing (as Sahlins did) that Easter Island's chiefdom status can be inferred from comparison with better-documented islands, Kirch argues that chiefdom institutions were inherited from the common ancestor. This shifts the burden of proof: instead of needing to demonstrate that Easter Island developed a chiefdom, Kirch only needs to show it retained one. The chiefdom is the default.

3. **The ecocide narrative is Kirch's second major addition.** Sahlins's 1954 dissertation does not contain an environmental degradation narrative. Kirch constructs the three-phase scheme (Colonization → Ahu Moai → Decadent/Huri Ahu) around a cycle of growth, environmental overshoot, and collapse. This narrative is subsequently popularized by Bahn & Flenley (1992) and Diamond (2005).

4. **Contact-era source gap inherited.** Neither Sahlins nor Kirch cites any contact-era primary accounts (Roggeveen, González, Cook, La Pérouse) for Easter Island. The entire interpretation rests on post-slave-raid ethnographic sources (Métraux 1940, Englert 1970, Routledge 1919/1920) and archaeological reports.

## The Chiefdom Framework

### Definition and disclaimer

Kirch disclaims neo-evolutionary stages: "I believe that a stadial or stagal approach to evolution in Polynesia is something of a 'dead horse'" (p. 2). Yet "chiefdom" functions as the organizing concept throughout. The disclaimer prevents the term from being pinned to Service's typology while allowing it to operate as an analytical category.

### The conical clan model

The organizational basis is the conical clan (Kirchhoff 1955; Sahlins 1968): a ramified descent group ranked by genealogical seniority, with the senior male of the founding line serving as paramount. Territorial divisions are radial, with each segment running from coast to interior and encompassing multiple resource zones (p. 33). The chief is the divine representative, mediator of *mana*, controller of *tapu*, and ritual director of production (pp. 37-38).

### Redistribution revised

Kirch explicitly reverses Sahlins's functionalist argument about redistribution (p. 281). Rather than chiefs arising because societies needed redistributors, Kirch argues the chieftainship was inherited from APS and that "leadership continually generates domestic surplus" (Sahlins 1972:140, cited p. 161). As stratification increased, the proportion of surplus actually redistributed to the population decreased; in the most stratified societies, surplus was invested in chiefly establishments, craft specialists, and monumental construction (p. 167). This is significant for our argument because it undercuts the functionalist justification for the chiefdom label: if redistribution decreases as chiefdoms develop, redistribution cannot be the defining feature.

## The Easter Island Chapter

### Three-phase narrative

Kirch adopts a modified version of the Norwegian Expedition's three-phase cultural sequence:

- **Colonization and early developments** (to ~AD 1000): Settlement, reduced crop/animal roster, adaptation, earliest ahu construction (~9th century)
- **Ahu Moai Phase** (AD 1000-1500): Full ahu construction, moai carving, population growth to ~7,000, intensive dryland agriculture, "segmentary ramage system" at its peak
- **Decadent (Huri Ahu) Phase** (AD 1500-1722): Overpopulation, environmental degradation, warfare, matatoa ascendancy, statue toppling, birdman cult

### Key passages supporting our argument

The Easter Island chapter contains two passages that are remarkably close to our argument:

**On ahu production** (p. 270): "Virtually all ethnographic and archaeological authorities agree that the *ahu* were conceived, constructed, and used by local descent groups, and do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority."

**On quarry production** (pp. 270-271): The Rano Raraku quarry suggests "the activities of many independently working crews, probably sent periodically by their kin groups, rather than the activities of a single work force under centralized direction" (Mulloy and Figueroa 1978:134).

These passages describe exactly the pattern that Lipo et al. 2025 has confirmed with 3D photogrammetric documentation: decentralized, lineage-level production without centralized management. Yet Kirch maintains the chiefdom label.

### The ariki-mau qualification

Kirch notes the ariki-mau "may have exercised more ritual than secular power" (p. 272). This is consistent with Routledge's and Métraux's stronger findings, but Kirch does not cite their explicit conclusions. The qualification is buried in a clause rather than developed as a significant observation.

### Goldman's "Open" classification

Table 2 (p. 37) displays Goldman's classification of Easter Island as "Open" alongside Sahlins's Group IIA. Goldman's "Open" category is defined as a system where "seniority has been modified to allow military and political effectiveness to govern status and political control... more strongly military and political than religious" (p. 36). This matches the matatoa-dominated system described by Routledge and Métraux. However, the Goldman classification is never discussed in the Easter Island chapter itself.

## What Kirch Inherits vs. Transforms vs. Adds

**Inherited from Sahlins:**
- Ramage classification (retained)
- Post-slave-raid source dependency (no contact-era accounts)
- Redistribution assumed despite evidence gap (gap not acknowledged)
- Moai as product of chiefly system (but see below)

**Transformed from Sahlins:**
- Moai production: Sahlins attributed it to "members of the chiefly status level" (1954, p. 100). Kirch explicitly describes lineage-level, decentralized production (pp. 270-271). This is a significant departure that creates tension with the chiefdom framing.
- Population estimate: Sahlins used Métraux's 3,000-4,000. Kirch raises to ~7,000 (unsourced).
- Matatoa: Sahlins challenged the matatoa claim as "tenuous" (1954, p. 284). Kirch accepts the matatoa as central to the Decadent Phase narrative.

**Added by Kirch:**
- Ancestral Polynesian Society as inherited template (Ch. 3, p. 281)
- Three-phase cultural sequence with ecocide narrative
- Environmental degradation / deforestation story (from Flenley palynology)
- Birdman cult as response to ecological crisis
- "Decadent Phase" as cultural degeneration framing
- Production function model (from Friedman 1979)

## Assessment against Current Evidence

### The ecocide narrative

Kirch's Decadent Phase narrative (overpopulation → degradation → warfare → collapse) has been substantially undermined:

- **DiNapoli et al. 2020**: Steady ahu construction tempo through 1722 contact; no pre-contact cessation. The "Ahu Moai" to "Huri Ahu" transition lacks chronological support.
- **Davis et al. 2024**: Rock gardens cover ~1/5 of prior estimates. Maximum carrying capacity ~3,900, not the 7,000+ that the overshoot model requires.
- **Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024**: Ancient DNA rejects 1600s population collapse. Steady growth from founding.
- **Mulrooney 2013**: Settlement continuity, not collapse. Inland abandonment prediction fails.

### The population estimate

Kirch's "about 7,000" (p. 271) is unattributed and appears inflated. Davis et al. 2024 estimates agricultural carrying capacity at ~3,000-3,900. Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 genetic data is consistent with gradual growth to ~1,500-3,000. The discrepancy between Table 2 (3,500) and Chapter 11 (7,000) is unresolved.

### The chiefdom label

The Easter Island chapter contains internal tension. Kirch describes:
- Ahu as descent group structures, NOT centralized authority (p. 270)
- Quarry production by independently working crews, NOT centralized direction (pp. 270-271)
- Ariki-mau as primarily ritual, not secular (p. 272)
- Goldman's classification of Easter Island as "Open" (military/political over religious, p. 37)

Despite all this, the chiefdom label is maintained through the APS inheritance argument. The chiefdom is applied not because the evidence demonstrates it for Easter Island specifically, but because it is assumed to be the default condition inherited from the common ancestor.

### The decentralized production paradox

The most consequential finding for our argument is that Kirch himself provides the evidence against centralized chiefly command over monument production. The key passages (pp. 270-271) describe exactly the pattern our recent work has confirmed: autonomous descent group production without hierarchical oversight. That these passages appear in the very book that established the chiefdom framework for Polynesian archaeology is a powerful illustration of the disconnect between the evidence and the interpretive label.

## Source Basis and Bibliographic Analysis

The Easter Island chapter (Ch. 11) rests on a narrow source base: three post-slave-raid ethnographies and five archaeological reports. No contact-era primary source is cited. This matters because it determines what the reader can see and what remains invisible.

### What Kirch cites

**Ethnographic sources (all post-slave-raid):**
- **Métraux 1940** (~15 citations): the dominant authority, used for specific ethnographic details (fishing tapu, kio class, agricultural practices, matatoa traditions, statue destruction timeline)
- **Englert 1970** (~8 citations): matatoa traditions, quarry figures, moai as ancestral commemoration, social disintegration narrative
- **Routledge 1919/1920** (~3 citations): birdman cult and Orongo only
- **McCall 1980** (2 citations): birdman cult interpretation

**Archaeological sources:**
- **McCoy 1976, 1979** (~6 citations): settlement, agricultural adaptation, demographic stress
- **Mulloy & Figueroa 1978** (~4 citations): ahu excavation, the crucial "independently working crews" passage
- **Ayres 1973, 1975, 1979** (~6 citations): cultural chronology, statue toppling, fishing
- **Heyerdahl & Ferdon 1961, 1965** (~3 citations): Norwegian Expedition
- **Flenley 1979, 1981** (~2 citations): palynological evidence of prior forest

**Theoretical:**
- **Sahlins 1955** (1 citation): "esoteric efflorescence" hypothesis

### What Kirch does not cite, and what is lost

**No contact-era primary accounts.** Four European expeditions observed Easter Island between 1722 and 1786 (Roggeveen, González, Cook, La Pérouse). Their accounts are the only direct observations of Easter Island society before the slave raids. All four failed to identify institutional chieftainship. La Pérouse (1786) actively searched for chiefs and concluded there was "no need of a chief." Cook (1774) "confess[ed] myself quite ignorant" of who governed. The reader of Chapter 11 has no way to know this.

**Routledge cited only for birdman.** Her broader findings are absent: "no chiefs nor any form of government" (1919:224); the ariki-mau "neither a leader in war, nor the fount of justice, nor even a priest" (1919:242); no kio class, no tribute, no four-class hierarchy. Routledge's is the first ethnography not filtered through Alexander Salmon.

**Métraux's political conclusions absent.** Métraux 1940 is the dominant source, but only for specific details. His overall political assessment is nowhere cited: the ariki-mau was "certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (1937:58; 1940:136); government was "decentralized and localized" (1940:131); Roussel's claim of absolute royal power was an "entirely gratuitous assumption" (1937:58). Métraux 1937 ("The Kings of Easter Island"), his most focused analysis of the kingship question, is not cited at all. Neither is Métraux 1957, which describes monument transport organized by "heads of lineages" through competitive feast sponsorship.

**McCoy's chiefdom conclusion absent.** McCoy is cited for settlement data but not for his explicit conclusion: "If there existed any tendencies toward the development of a chiefdom, the political flux caused by the ascendancy of the warrior class to positions of authority clearly retarded it" (1976:145). He also noted that Sahlins treated Easter Island as an "exception" (1976:152).

**Goldman's classification unengaged.** Table 2 (p. 37) displays Goldman's "Open" classification for Easter Island (military/political effectiveness over religious hierarchy), but this is never discussed in the Easter Island chapter.

**Thomson 1891 not cited.** This means both the chiefdom-supporting "arbitrary monarchy" claim and its self-undermining qualifications (no tax, no homage, independent clans) are absent. The reader encounters neither the strongest post-raid chiefdom claim nor its refutation.

### What this source pattern produces

By relying exclusively on post-slave-raid ethnographies read through the APS inheritance framework, and by not engaging with contact-era accounts that fail to find chieftainship or with the political conclusions of his own cited authors (Routledge, Métraux, McCoy), Chapter 11 presents a chiefdom interpretation that appears empirically grounded while depending on a curated subset of the available evidence. The sources that challenge the interpretation are either absent or cited only for details that do not bear on the political question. This is not necessarily a deliberate omission; it is consistent with a framework in which the chiefdom is the assumed default (via APS inheritance) and disconfirming evidence is invisible because it is not what the framework leads one to look for.

## Significance for the Paper

Kirch 1984 is the book where the chiefdom attribution for Rapa Nui becomes archaeological rather than merely ethnographic. Every subsequent treatment of Easter Island as a chiefdom derives from or builds on this framework. Processing this source reveals:

1. The chiefdom label rests on the assumption that APS institutions persisted through colonization and transformation, not on independent archaeological demonstration.

2. The author himself provides the strongest published evidence for decentralized, descent-group-level production (pp. 270-271), directly contradicting the implication of chiefly command.

3. Goldman's alternative classification ("Open," emphasizing military/political rather than hierarchical organization) is present in the book but not engaged with in the Easter Island analysis.

4. The ecocide narrative that structures the Easter Island chapter has been substantially undermined by post-2013 research.

5. The contact-era source gap persists: no primary observer of Easter Island before the slave raids is cited by either Sahlins or Kirch. The entire chiefdom attribution chain from 1954 to 1984 rests on the same post-slave-raid ethnographic base.
