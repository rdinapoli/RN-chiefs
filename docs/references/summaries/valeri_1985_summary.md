## Overview

The most detailed structuralist analysis of Hawaiian kingship, sacrifice, and ritual hierarchy in the anthropological literature. Valeri, trained by Levi-Strauss and Dumont in Paris and Sahlins's colleague at Chicago, provides a comprehensive account of the Hawaiian political-ritual system: the relationships among gods, kings, priests, and commoners, mediated through sacrifice, the kapu system, mana transmission, and a hierarchical temple network. Originally written in French (1969-1979), reorganized in 1981, and translated for the 1985 English edition.

The book is organized in three parts:
- **Part 1** (Chs. 1-3): Hawaiian theology, sacrifice theory, gods-humans relationship (purity/impurity, kapu/noa, mana)
- **Part 2** (Chs. 4-6): Sacrifice and hierarchy, sacrifice and kingship, hierarchy of temples
- **Part 3** (Chs. 7-8): Detailed analysis of the Makahiki festival and the luakini temple ritual

The Conclusion (pp. 340-347) is a theoretical reflection on ritual as communication, drawing on Searle, Austin, Durkheim, and Bourdieu. It makes no comparative Polynesian claims.

## Relevance to Argument

Valeri provides the detailed Hawaiian kingship model that Kirch's comparative Polynesian framework exports to other islands. When Kirch (1984) applies the "chiefdom" label to Rapa Nui via the Ancestral Polynesian Society inheritance argument, the implicit claim is that something resembling the system Valeri describes for Hawaii existed in attenuated form across Polynesia. Understanding Valeri's source model in detail allows us to specify exactly what the Polynesian analogy claims when projected onto Rapa Nui and to identify the specific features of the Hawaiian system that are absent from the Rapa Nui record.

The book also demonstrates sophisticated source criticism for Hawaiian ethnohistory that parallels our approach to Rapa Nui contact-era sources: Valeri recognizes that the Hawaiian kapu system was "modified and began to weaken even before its abolition in 1819" (p. xvii), that European observers changed what they described (p. xxviii), and that Hawaiian-language texts must be understood on their own terms rather than through Western categories. This awareness of source limitations, however, gets lost when the Hawaiian model is exported to other islands.

Kirch 1984 cites Valeri 1972 (the earlier French article) three times in the Hawaii chapter. The 1985 book provided the comprehensive framework that subsequent scholars assumed when discussing "Polynesian chiefdoms."

## Hawaiian Political-Ritual System per Valeri

### Kapu/Noa System
The kapu/noa opposition marks hierarchical relationships, not inherent properties. Kapu = "marked" (requires attention from inferiors); noa = "unmarked" (no obligation from superiors). Purely relative: no one is absolutely kapu or noa. The system creates a graduated hierarchy in which purity is equated with proximity to the divine type. Superiors pollute inferiors (through their greater mana), and inferiors pollute superiors (through transgression). Active management of these bidirectional pollution risks required constant ritual attention.

### Mana
In Hawaii, mana is substantialized to a degree unusual in Polynesia: conceived as an invisible substance transmitted through spitting, breathing, prayer, and sacrifice. Mana means efficacy, potency, authority, and aptitude for specific tasks. It is divine in origin but depends on human recognition (ho'omana = "to worship" = "to cause to be mana"). The god's mana and existence depend on sacrifice and prayer; gods "who no longer receive human recognition die" (p. 104). Mana circulates through the social hierarchy as a life-giving force connecting gods, king, priests, and commoners.

### Priesthood
Three categories: (1) kahuna pule (temple priests, themselves ali'i of lower rank, in hereditary orders of Ku and Lono); (2) professional kahuna (specialists in canoe building, astrology, medicine, construction, fighting); (3) kaula (prophets, marginal figures in direct contact with gods, often opposing the king). Sorcery priests (kahuna 'ana'ana) were impure, marginal, and anti-hierarchical. The high priest (kahuna nui) controlled the king's religious duties and influenced legitimacy, but the king remained the true head of the cult.

### Divine Kingship
The king was the supreme sacrifier, the closest human instantiation of the major gods. He shared divine predicates: cosmic effects at birth, invisibility, immobility, incestuous marriage, feather regalia identical to divine images, the prostration taboo (kapu moe), association with sharks and celestial phenomena. His body was equated with his kingdom. He alone could consecrate human sacrifice, the act that set him apart from the rest of the nobility. Kingship was always conquered: no clear succession rules, bilateral rank transmission, multiple pretenders, perpetual factional warfare.

### Land Tenure and Redistribution
All land rights flowed from the king, who redistributed at accession. No right was permanent or hereditary. The chain: king -> high nobles -> their supporters -> commoners, subdivided "to the sixth or seventh degree." Each level gave firstfruits to the level above, ascending to the king, who consecrated them to the gods. This hierarchical chain of prestations was the material expression of the social hierarchy and was renewed through temple rituals.

### Temple Hierarchy
816+ documented heiau, organized in a hierarchical system: luakini (exclusively royal, for human sacrifice, associated with Ku) > hale o Lono (agricultural temples) > ko'a (fishing temples) > medical and sorcery temples (associated with Kane). The king owned the principal temple of each type in each district. The annual ritual cycle began with the king consecrating the main luakini, then agricultural temples, after which subordinates could build their own temples according to rank.

## Features Absent from Rapa Nui

The following elements of Valeri's Hawaiian system have no archaeological or ethnohistoric parallel on Rapa Nui:

1. **Human sacrifice** as institutionalized royal prerogative with elaborate treatment of victims
2. **Graduated kapu system** with prostration/sitting taboos and bidirectional pollution management
3. **Divine kingship** with cosmic effects, shared divine insignia, invisibility requirements
4. **Royal incest** producing hierarchically ranked offspring (pi'o, ni'aupi'o)
5. **Hierarchical priesthood** with hereditary orders, formal training, institutional authority
6. **Land redistribution** at royal accession, creating a chain of rights renegotiated each succession
7. **Firstfruits chain** flowing upward through hierarchy to king to gods
8. **Temple hierarchy** (luakini > hale o Lono > ko'a > unu) with restricted access by rank
9. **Annual ritual cycle** with sequential temple consecration by rank order
10. **Mana as substantialized invisible force** transmitted through specific ritual mechanisms
11. **Genealogical verification** (hale naua ceremony) establishing the nobility
12. **Faction-based political competition** with client-patron relationships and perpetual succession warfare

The contact-era accounts of Rapa Nui (Roggeveen 1722, Cook 1774, La Perouse 1786) describe none of these features. The post-raid ethnographies (Thompson 1891, Routledge 1919, Metraux 1940) describe the ariki mau as "certainly not a ruler or civilian chief" (Metraux 1937:58), with no political power, no ability to compel subjects, and no redistribution authority. Goldman (1970) classified Rapa Nui as an "Open" society that "had never evolved a central authority." The gap between Valeri's Hawaiian model and the Rapa Nui record is fundamental, not a matter of degree.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Sahlins 1954/1958**: Used Hawaii as the paradigmatic "highly stratified" Polynesian society to build the stratification gradient. Valeri provides the detailed ethnographic content that Sahlins's framework assumed.
- **Kirch 1984**: Cites Valeri 1972 for the Hawaii chapter. The APS (Ancestral Polynesian Society) inheritance argument assumes something like Valeri's system existed in proto-form across Polynesia.
- **Goldman 1970**: Classified Hawaii as "Traditional" (Tonga-like, highest tier) versus Rapa Nui as "Open" (lowest tier). Valeri's detail shows why Goldman's classification matters: the Hawaiian system has a specific, complex institutional architecture absent from Open societies.
- **Van Tilburg 1986**: Applied "mana" and "chiefly power" to moai interpretation. Valeri's analysis shows that Hawaiian mana is a specific theological concept embedded in a system of sacrifice, ho'omana, and reciprocal god-human relations, not a free-floating property attributable to monumental objects.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Levi-Strauss and Dumont (structuralism, hierarchy theory) -> Valeri -> Sahlins (Chicago colleague) -> Kirch (Sahlins's student, applies framework archaeologically). Valeri also draws on Hegel, Feuerbach, Hubert and Mauss, Durkheim, and Robertson Smith. The book is a structuralist analysis deeply embedded in the French anthropological tradition. When this analysis is transmitted through Sahlins and Kirch into archaeological application across Polynesia, the theoretical sophistication and Hawaii-specific qualifications are stripped away, leaving a generic "chiefdom" template that Valeri himself never claimed was generalizable.
