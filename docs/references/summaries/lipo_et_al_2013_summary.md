# Summary: Lipo et al. 2013

**Source**: Lipo, Carl P., Terry L. Hunt, and Sergio Rapu Haoa. 2013. "The 'Walking' Megalithic Statues (*Moai*) of Easter Island." *Journal of Archaeological Science* 40: 2859-2866.

**Own-work**: Yes. Lipo and Hunt are co-authors on the current project.

---

## Overview

Lipo et al. 2013 presents archaeological evidence and an experimental demonstration that Easter Island's monumental statues (moai) were transported in a vertical position by "walking" them using ropes and small teams of people. The paper combines three lines of evidence: (1) systematic analysis of 62 road moai abandoned along ancient transport roads, including their shapes, fall positions, and breakage patterns; (2) 3D modeling of an actual road moai to analyze center of mass and biomechanics; and (3) a field experiment using a precisely scaled 4.35 metric ton concrete replica of a road moai, demonstrating that 18 people with three ropes could achieve forward movement at approximately 100 meters in 40 minutes.

The paper was published in the *Journal of Archaeological Science* and builds on the moai road analysis in Lipo and Hunt (2005) and the broader argument in Hunt and Lipo (2011).

## Key Findings

### Archaeological evidence from road moai (n = 62)

Road moai differ systematically from ahu moai in ways consistent with engineering for vertical transport. Road moai have statistically wider bases relative to shoulder width, a forward lean of often more than 10 degrees (placing the center of mass over or beyond the front edge), and no carved eye sockets. These features are inconsistent with horizontal transport and inconsistent with the completed form of statues erected on ahu platforms, where the base was narrowed and the center of mass repositioned over the center of the base.

Thirty-seven percent of road moai are broken in patterns consistent with falling from a vertical position. Seventy percent exhibit lateral basal flaking consistent with the rocking motion of walking. The positions of fallen statues are statistically non-random with respect to slope: facedown on downhill segments, on their backs on uphill segments. These patterns correspond to the predicted failure modes of forward-leaning vertical transport, not horizontal transport.

The concave cross-section of moai roads, documented by Love (2001), rules out the use of transverse rollers.

### 3D modeling and biomechanics

A 3D model of road moai 12-220-01 (7.35 m tall, 2.83 m wide, transported 3.54 km from Rano Raraku) revealed that the center of mass is positioned forward relative to the base, just over the front edge. This configuration would cause the statue to tip forward if stood on its base, requiring modification before emplacement on an ahu. The forward center of mass, combined with the rounded ventral edge of the base, creates a surface across which the statue can roll as it tilts from side to side. The mechanics resemble an inverted pendulum: energy is conserved through repeated transitions between potential and kinetic energy, minimizing friction and the sustained force required from rope handlers.

### Walking experiment

The replica was a precise proportional scale model of a road moai (3 m tall, 4.35 metric tons), constructed from density-corrected concrete using a mold derived from the 3D model. This was the first experiment to use a replica shaped for transport rather than for standing on an ahu. Field trials at Kualoa Ranch, Hawaii, demonstrated that three ropes (one rear, two lateral) and as few as 18 people could walk the replica. The rear rope (10 handlers) prevented the statue from falling forward; the lateral ropes (4 handlers per side) initiated and maintained rocking. Once rocking began, the statue's energy conservation reduced the sustained effort required. The team achieved approximately 100 m in 40 minutes, compared to Heyerdahl et al.'s (1989) estimate of 100 m per full day for the "wiggle" method.

The method also handled uphill and downhill slopes of up to 6 degrees, directional changes, and 180-degree rotations.

### Scaling properties

The paper argues that the walking method scales favorably with statue size. Larger moai are taller (providing more leverage for rope handlers), proportionally narrower, and have longer, thinner heads relative to their bodies, giving them a "bowling pin" shape with lower relative volume and a lower center of mass. These features reduce the effort required to initiate rocking. The paper concludes that larger statues were less likely to have been transportable by any method other than walking, since horizontal transport would stress the weakest point (the neck) and require subsequent raising to vertical position.

## Relevance to the Chiefdom Question

### What the paper demonstrates

1. Moai were engineered with systematic shape differences between the transport form (road moai) and the emplacement form (ahu moai), indicating sophisticated planning and engineering skill.

2. Vertical transport by walking is physically feasible with small teams (18 people minimum for a 4.35-ton replica) using only ropes.

3. The archaeological evidence from road moai (shapes, fall positions, breakage patterns, road cross-sections) is consistent with vertical walking transport and inconsistent with horizontal transport on wooden devices.

4. Walking is fast (100 m in 40 min), energy-efficient, and does not require timber.

### What the paper does not demonstrate

1. That all moai were transported by walking. The paper demonstrates feasibility and consistency with the archaeological record; it does not prove universal application.

2. That the scaling argument holds at full size. The experiment used a 4.35-ton replica, smaller than the island mean (ca. 4 m tall). The largest transported moai was approximately 10 m tall. The scaling argument is based on geometric analysis, not experimental verification at full scale.

3. The specific social organization of transport crews. The paper infers small groups and part-time effort from the low labor requirements, but does not directly address how crews were recruited, coordinated, or motivated.

### How it bears on the chiefdom attribution

The paper directly undermines a central pillar of the chiefdom attribution for Rapa Nui: the claim that moai transport required large, coordinated labor forces that could only have been mobilized by chiefly command. The authors explicitly state: "The evidence for *moai* carving and transport points to activities by small-scale social groups rather than the product of laborers unified under a powerful centralized chiefdom. Here monumentality does not imply large-scale social organization as assumed for many cases worldwide" (p. 2866).

The paper also severs the link between moai transport and deforestation by demonstrating that transport required ropes, not timber. This removes the need for the large-scale surplus extraction (clearing forest for agriculture to feed statue workers, felling trees for rollers) that has been attributed to chiefly economic control.

The alternative framing offered is costly signaling and evolutionary bet hedging in a competitive environment, consistent with collective action models in which monument construction reflects inter-group competition among autonomous or semi-autonomous social units rather than top-down command by a paramount chief.

### Limitations as evidence against the chiefdom model

The walking experiment demonstrates that moai transport *could* have been accomplished without chiefly command of large labor forces. It does not demonstrate that it *was* accomplished this way, nor does it demonstrate that Rapa Nui lacked chiefs. The argument is about removing one evidentiary pillar, not about proving an alternative political organization. Small-group transport is consistent with, but does not require, collective action or the absence of hierarchical leadership.

**Own-work note**: As co-authored work by Lipo and Hunt, this paper should be cited with the same transparency applied to any source. The manuscript should be clear about what the walking experiment specifically demonstrates (feasibility with small teams on a scaled replica) and what it does not (universal application, full-scale verification, direct evidence about social organization). Where cited as positive evidence against the chiefdom attribution, the inferential step from "small teams could do this" to "therefore no chiefly command was needed" should be presented as a plausible implication, not as a demonstrated conclusion.
