# Source Summary: Cook 1774

## Overview

Captain James Cook visited Rapa Nui on March 13-16, 1774 during his Second Voyage aboard HMS Resolution. The expedition included naturalists Johann Reinhold Forster and his son Georg Forster. By 1774, Cook had extensive experience with hierarchical Polynesian societies (Tahiti, Tonga, Society Islands). The Tahitian man Mahine (Oedidee) traveled with them as a partial linguistic intermediary, though Cook noted he "understood their language much better than any of us; though even he understood it but very imperfectly." Cook remained at the landing place on the second day due to illness; the inland reconnaissance was led by Lieutenants Pickersgill and Edgecumbe, with astronomer William Wales.

**Note on edition**: The 1846 compiled edition is an abridged narrative derived from the official Admiralty account. The Beaglehole edition (1961) is the scholarly standard. The 1846 text is reliable for Cook's direct observations but may differ in phrasing from the manuscript journals.

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**Cook's admission of ignorance is the single most important statement.** "I have no doubt that all these plantations are private property, and that there are here, as at Otaheite, chiefs (which they call Areekes) to whom these plantations belong. But of the power or authority of these chiefs, or of the government of these people, I confess myself quite ignorant" (Cook 1846, pp. 454-455). Cook, the most experienced Pacific navigator of his era, who identified chieftainship readily in Tahiti and Tonga, could not characterize political authority on Rapa Nui after four days of direct contact. He infers "chiefs" from Polynesian analogy and native vocabulary, not from observed political behavior.

**Ko Toheetai identified as "aree" (ariki)**: The islanders themselves used the Polynesian title (Forster accounts). Cook's inland party independently encountered a man "seemingly of some note" wearing better cloth, whom they "understood to be the chief of the island" (Cook 1846, p. 451). Neither Cook nor his officers describe any commanding behavior by this individual.

**Explicitly non-coercive authority (Forster accounts)**: "We did not observe that he was held in a very high esteem or that he was paid a humiliating homage." The *Memoire* describes him as "rather a father of a family than an absolute ruler." The government was "neither complicated nor troublesome."

**Individual property rights and household-level distribution**: Cook observes a plantation owner stopping theft of his own potatoes (Cook 1846, p. 450). Hut owners individually distributed food to the European party (Cook 1846, p. 450). No centralized redistribution system described by any observer.

**Statues as memorials to named chiefs/heroes, not deities**: Both Cook and the Forsters independently reach this conclusion. Cook reports islanders prefixed "Moi" (burying/sleeping-place) and appended "Areekee" (chief) to statue names. Monument construction attributed to "ingenuity and perseverance," not chiefly command (Cook 1846, p. 455).

**Quarry confirmed by Cook via Wales**, resolving a key discrepancy: Georg Forster's *Voyage* denied finding quarries, but Cook's account and the *Memoire* both report them. This demonstrates editorial shaping in the published Forster accounts.

**Significant absences**: No redistribution, no centralized exchange, no large canoes/fleet, no organized labor, no warrior class, no surplus extraction, no religious specialists, no religion identified, no commanding behavior by "chief." Extreme poverty and tiny population (600-900) noted by all observers.

## Relevance to the Paper

The Cook accounts are the most important and most ambiguous contact-era evidence. With Cook's own narrative now directly available, the ambiguity is even clearer than the Forster accounts alone suggested. The Forsters constructed an elaborate portrait of benevolent paternal governance shaped by Enlightenment philosophy; Cook, by contrast, simply declared himself "quite ignorant" of the nature of chiefly authority. This is devastating for any chiefdom attribution that relies on these accounts: if the most experienced Polynesian observer of his generation could not identify the political system after four days of contact, the evidence does not support confident characterization by researchers working 200 years later from the same textual base.

The Cook accounts also demonstrate the five-step circularity: Cook infers "chiefs" from Polynesian analogy ("as at Otaheite"), the Forsters elaborate this into a philosophical portrait, later researchers cite the Forster characterization as evidence for chieftainship, and the archaeological record is then read through this lens. Cook's own epistemic caution is lost in this chain of transmission.
