# Source Summary: Cook 1774

## Overview

Captain James Cook visited Rapa Nui on March 13-16, 1774 during his Second Voyage aboard HMS Resolution. The expedition included naturalists Johann Reinhold Forster and his son Georg Forster. By 1774, Cook had extensive experience with hierarchical Polynesian societies (Tahiti, Tonga, Society Islands). The Tahitian man Mahine traveled with them as a partial linguistic intermediary. The crew was in poor condition (scurvy, rheumatism).

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**Ko Toheetai identified as "aree" (ariki)**: The islanders themselves used the Polynesian title. Ko Toheetai wore differentiated clothing (mulberry bark cloth dyed with turmeric, feathered "diadem"), distributed food, stood on a hill to receive the Europeans, and accompanied their party. When asked if he was chief of a district or the whole island, "he spread out his arms, as it were to include the whole island, and said Waihu."

**Explicitly non-coercive authority**: "We did not observe that he was held in a very high esteem or that he was paid a humiliating homage, as it is demanded by despots from Asiatic islands." The *Memoire* describes him as "rather a father of a family than an absolute ruler" whose "commands were treated as advices of a tender father." The government was "neither complicated nor troublesome."

**Conflict resolution by family heads**: "Heads of families, with their wisdom and authority, can easily end [quarrels] and soon restore the peace."

**Individual subsistence**: "On this arid island everyone is in need of seeking their subsistence and they must do it using only the strength of their own hands."

**Statues as memorials to named chiefs/heroes** (not deities): Islanders provided names at monuments and "struggled to disillusion us" that these were religious symbols. The *Memoire* attributes monument construction to voluntary gratitude for leaders' good deeds, not coerced labor.

**Significant absences**: No humiliating homage, no redistribution, no centralized exchange, no large canoes/fleet, no organized labor, no warrior class, no surplus extraction, no religious specialists. Extreme poverty and tiny population noted.

**Caution on the *Memoire***: Jakubowska-Vorbrich (2019) identifies it as simultaneously a scientific report and a political manifesto praising Frederick the Great's Prussia. Its depiction of benevolent paternal governance fits Enlightenment philosophy as much as observed reality. Discrepancies with the published Forster works (quarries/unfinished statues, singing/dancing) require caution.

## Relevance to the Paper

The Cook accounts are the most important and most ambiguous contact-era evidence. They provide the only named individual identified by islanders using a Polynesian leadership title, but the Forsters explicitly characterized his authority as non-coercive and simpler than what they observed in Tahiti and Tonga. The accounts support both sides of the chiefdom debate depending on what is emphasized: the existence of an ariki, or the overwhelming evidence of non-hierarchical organization. The critical epistemological question is whether 1774 conditions reflect a diminished version of earlier arrangements or approximate what always existed.
