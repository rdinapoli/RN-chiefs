# Summary: Métraux 1957 *Easter Island: A Stone-Age Civilization of the Pacific*

## Source Information

- **Author**: Alfred Métraux (translated by Michael Bullock)
- **Title**: *Easter Island: A Stone-Age Civilization of the Pacific*
- **Publication**: André Deutsch, London, 1957 (276 pages)
- **Fieldwork**: Based on 1934 Franco-Belgian expedition (same as 1940 monograph)
- **Type**: Popular synthesis for a general readership
- **OCR**: Extracted via tesseract at 200 DPI; no native OCR layer in PDF

## Overview

Written 17 years after the monograph, this book represents Métraux's mature, settled views reframed for a non-specialist audience. The popular format strips away technical apparatus and produces clearer, more direct formulations of his conclusions about Easter Island political organization. The book covers the island's history, social structure, religion, material culture, statues, and script across fourteen chapters.

## Key Findings for Political Authority

### 1. Ariki-mau's Political Authority "Not Clear"

The 1957 formulation is more explicitly uncertain than the 1940 monograph: "The political authority of the ariki-mau is not clear. On this point we are reduced to conjecture. Although he was the civil leader of the Miru, he was not regarded by the other tribes as more than an extremely sacred personage" (p. 93). The "essence of royalty" is defined as supernatural food-increase magic through mana transmitted by divine ancestors (p. 89).

### 1b. King's Authority Temporary

The ariki-mau's authority was not continuous: "the king lost his rank on the birth of his eldest son. The mana that had been in him passed into the little being" (p. 92). The king served as regent "until his heir was old enough to fulfil his functions. This date was determined by his marriage; after which the old king sank into oblivion" (p. 93). Kingship was a temporary custodianship of mana, not a permanent political office.

### 2. Political Power with the Warriors

Métraux makes a stronger comparative claim than in the 1940 monograph: "The political position of the matato'a on Easter Island was much stronger than that of the warriors in the rest of Polynesia, where they were subordinate to the ariki" (p. 97). The caudillo analogy (new to this book) frames the matatoa as personal strongmen operating through force and intimidation, not institutional authority: "Like the South American caudillo, he was both a civil and military leader" (p. 96). "Political power seems to have fallen into the hands of the mata-to'a, who formed an aristocracy of happy warriors" (p. 93).

### 3. Statue Production as Competitive Feast Sponsorship

The 1957 book provides Métraux's clearest model of monument production: "The heads of lineages who wished to raise a new bust on their ahu undoubtedly organized feasts that they prepared long beforehand... The guests harnessed themselves to the statue and strained their muscles to the sound of cries and chants... A half-serious, half-humorous rivalry turned the undertaking into an immense pleasure party" (pp. 164-165). This is a collective action model: lineage-level organization, feast sponsorship as motivation, competitive display between autonomous groups, and festive voluntary participation.

The book also contains the passage closest to a chiefly-command model in Métraux's writings: "The chiefs, who had vast resources at their disposal, employed them in maintaining and paying groups of craftsmen who carved statues to ornament the mausoleums belonging to the various lineages" (pp. 159-160). However, the "chiefs" are plural (lineage heads), the resources serve lineage-level monuments, and the mobilization operates through feast sponsorship, not coercive authority.

### 4. Statues as Profane Objects Without Active Ritual

"The sanctity of the statues was, therefore, temporary and depended on rites that caused the advent of the deities. In the ordinary way, the image was simply an ornament, a lifeless block of stone" (pp. 159-160). "They became noa—profane" (p. 160). This undercuts arguments that monumental statues necessarily indicate permanent religious-political authority.

### 5. Feast System as Competitive Prestige Economy

Chapter X describes a feast system (koro) organized by "chiefs of lineages or extended families anxious to consolidate or enhance their prestige" (p. 171). Obligatory gift circulation operated laterally: "Those who received a chicken immediately gave it away, so that the fowls circulated from one person to another. The obligatory nature of these exchanges was expressed in the belief that a chicken could not be eaten until it had changed hands five times" (pp. 173-174). Hareauti festivals "were organized by individuals in whom ambition or vanity inspired the wish to increase their prestige by a display of generosity" (pp. 174-175). Métraux frames this as "a whole system of contractual gifts analogous to those described in connexion with other Pacific cultures" (p. 174). This is a competitive prestige/gift economy, not centralized redistribution.

### 6. Cultural Break of 1862 Framed as Total

"The year 1862 was decisive in the history of Easter Island. It saw the end of its civilization" (p. 47). "The religious and social system had been destroyed and a leaden apathy weighed down the survivors from these disasters" (p. 48). "The majority of members of the priestly class disappeared, taking with them the secrets of the past" (p. 48).

## Context and Limitations

- This is a popular synthesis, not a primary research contribution. All data derives from the 1934 fieldwork and the same sources as the 1940 monograph.
- Popular framing may simplify nuances, but in this case the simplified formulations are more direct about political ambiguity, not less.
- The 1957 book incorporates some Sahlins-era comparative Polynesian thinking not available in 1940, which may explain the more developed feast-sponsorship model of statue transport.
- OCR quality is generally good but occasional words may be misread.

## Relevance to the Paper

1. **Clearer formulations for quotation**: The popular framing produces quotable passages that are more direct than the technical monograph. "The political authority of the ariki-mau is not clear" and "Political power seems to have fallen into the hands of the mata-to'a" are stronger statements than the 1940 equivalents.

2. **Feast-sponsorship model**: Métraux's reconstruction of statue transport as competitive feast sponsorship by lineage heads is directly relevant to the collective action alternative. This is the most detailed model of monument-production organization from any post-raid ethnographer.

3. **No evolution toward chiefdom model**: Between 1940 and 1957, Métraux's assessment of political authority moved, if anything, toward greater skepticism. The 1957 book is more explicitly uncertain about ariki-mau political power and more emphatic about warrior dominance.

4. **Statues as "noa"**: The concept that statues were only sacred during active ritual and otherwise "profane" blocks of stone undermines arguments that monumental construction necessarily indicates permanent religious-political authority structures.

5. **Feast system as gift economy**: The koro feast system, with obligatory lateral gift circulation and competitive prestige displays by individual lineage heads, describes a gift economy rather than the centralized redistribution chiefdom models predict. Combined with the feast-sponsorship model of statue transport, this provides a coherent alternative to chiefly-command models of monument production.

6. **King's temporary authority**: The explicit statement that kings "sank into oblivion" after their heir's marriage reinforces that ariki-mau authority was a temporary custodianship of mana, not continuous political rule.
