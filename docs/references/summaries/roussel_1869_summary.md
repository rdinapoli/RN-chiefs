# Source Summary: Roussel 1869

## Overview

Father Hippolyte Roussel of the Sacred Hearts of Picpus served as missionary on Rapa Nui from 1866 to 1873. His notes, written in 1869, were published posthumously in 1926 in the Annals of the Sacred Heart. Roussel provides the most ethnographically detailed early post-slave-raid account, including king lists, social class descriptions, property systems, and descriptions of customs. His notes were written 3-6 years after the slave raids.

## Sources Consulted

- Altman 2004, *Early Visitors to Easter Island 1864-1877*, Parts 2-3 (pp. 34-60): Roussel notes with endnotes by Altman and editorial note by Father Alazard

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**Four social classes identified**: ariki (kings), ivi atua (priests), matatoa (warriors), and kio (farmers/servants). Roussel states: "I did not see any evidence of classes on Rapa Nui other than" these four (p. 41). This is the earliest enumeration of the class system that later becomes central to chiefdom attributions.

**Critical admission of power transfer**: "In recent times, even though the kings were still viewed with respect by the common people, who considered them to be something sacred, their authority was, nonetheless, either absent or totally unrecognized: it had passed completely into the hands of the matatoa, who decided everything and fought among themselves without prior consultations with His Majesty" (p. 41). This passage simultaneously claims hereditary royal authority existed AND that it was functionally absent. Whether the transfer to matatoa was recent (post-raid) or longstanding cannot be determined from Roussel's account.

**King list**: Roussel records a succession from Hotu Matua through 22 generations to Gregorio (the last). The endnotes note that this list disagrees significantly with other king lists: "Twelve of the 23 names on Roussel's list are included in Thomson's list; Roussel and Jaussen have nine names in common. Few are given in the same order" (endnote 7, p. 58). The inconsistency across lists undermines their reliability as historical records.

**First-fruits ceremony directly observed**: Roussel witnessed a procession bringing first yams to Gregorio: "A procession was headed by people carrying the vines, followed by numerous natives and boys in two lines, each holding a peeled branch of hau... The procession resembled nothing more than a funeral procession with flaming torches. From time to time, the air resounded with howling and incomprehensible singing" (p. 40). This is the strongest direct evidence for chiefly prerogatives in any post-raid source. However, Gregorio was the last surviving member of the royal line and was a child; the ceremony may have been performative or residual.

**Government as "perpetual thievery"**: Roussel describes governance as follows: "It would be a contradiction in terms to call the perpetual thievery a form of government" (p. 47). He describes the tangata manu competition and notes that the matatoa's "role was limited to marauding day and night."

**Property**: Land ownership existed but was "in an almost inextricable tangle" due to displacement from wars and slave raids. The sea adjacent to land belonged to the landowner, marked by rahui stones. Plantations were "held in common by the members of a single family" (p. 48).

## Context and Limitations

**[POST-RAID]** All of Roussel's observations come from a society that had been devastated by the slave raids 3-6 years earlier. His informants were survivors of a demographic catastrophe that killed or removed the majority of the population, including specifically "the chiefs and priests."

**Population approximately 650** according to "recent censuses" (p. 46), down from Eyraud's estimate of ~1,200 in 1864.

**Distinction between observation and tradition**: Much of what Roussel reports is what survivors told him, not what he observed directly. The king lists, origin stories, descriptions of "absolute power," and accounts of how things were "in the old days" are all reported tradition filtered through traumatized survivors. Roussel does not consistently distinguish between what he observed and what he was told.

**Missionary perspective**: Roussel's interest was saving souls, not recording ethnography. His descriptions of customs are often judgmental ("savage brutality," "immature and licentious"). He arrived from Mangareva, where the Sacred Hearts mission had already imposed a rigid social order, and may have projected Mangarevan political categories onto Rapa Nui.

## Relevance to the Paper

Roussel's account is the single most important post-raid source for the chiefdom attribution. His descriptions of ariki, social classes, king lists, first-fruits ceremonies, and tapu restrictions have been cited by Kirch and others as evidence for pre-contact hierarchical chiefdom organization. The paper must engage seriously with this material while making three critical points:

1. **The king list is reported tradition**, transmitted through a demographic catastrophe, and disagrees with other recorded lists.
2. **Roussel's own account contradicts itself**: the kings had "absolute power" AND their authority "had passed completely into the hands of the matatoa." Both cannot be straightforwardly true descriptions of the same political system.
3. **The first-fruits ceremony Roussel observed** involved a child who was the last of the royal line, in a society reduced to 650 people. This cannot be assumed to represent normal functioning of a pre-contact political system.
