# Source Summary: González 1770

## Overview

The Spanish annexation expedition under Commodore Don Felipe González de Haedo visited Rapa Nui on November 15-21, 1770 (6 days). Two ships: San Lorenzo and Santa Rosalia. The expedition formally annexed the island for Spain under Carlos III. This was 48 years after Roggeveen.

## Sources Consulted

- Aguera's journal (Corney 1903 translation, Expeditions Part 2)
- Herve's narrative (Corney 1903 translation, Expeditions Part 3, pp. 79-94)
- Jakubowska 2014 (analysis of Corney's translation accuracy vs. Spanish originals)

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**No individual described commanding others** at any point during the 6-day visit. All group behavior is collective and undirected.

**Social differentiation limited to age-grading**: Older men had more elaborate body paint, better housing, and precedence in marital matters. No evidence of hereditary rank.

**The annexation ceremony**: "Some of the natives present signed or attested the official document." The plate caption reads "SIGNATURES OF CHIEFS OF EASTER ISLAND" but "chiefs" is editorial. Neither Aguera nor Herve describes the signatories as chiefs or explains how they were selected. The Spanish legal framework required identifiable political authorities for the annexation; the primary accounts do not demonstrate that the signatories had any.

**Vocabulary list**: Includes "tequeteque" for "High chief or lord," but elicited through signs and drawings, making the mapping unreliable for specific political content.

**Jakubowska's translation findings** (critical for the paper):
- Corney translates *hombres mayores* ("elderly men") as "principal men" or "those in authority," inflating age descriptions into political-authority claims
- Corney mistranslates *sin reparo alguno* ("without scruple") as "without making any return," distorting exchange descriptions
- Corney translates *en Ranchos separados* ("in separate groups") as "in loose cloaks," losing a social-organizational observation
- Corney translates *canasto* ("basket") as "crown," importing royal symbolism for pukao

**No tribute, redistribution, directed labor, warrior class, council, feasting, or hereditary succession** described or hinted at.

## Relevance to the Paper

The González accounts demonstrate both the absence of chiefly authority evidence and the mechanism by which European categories were imposed. The annexation ceremony created "chiefs" for legal purposes; Corney's translations systematically inflated age descriptions into political-authority language. Scholars working from Corney's English rather than the Spanish originals have been reading a text that already imposes chiefdom categories.
