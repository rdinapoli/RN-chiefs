## Overview

This dissertation is the earliest scholarly work to explicitly frame moai as evidence for chiefdom-level political organization. Van Tilburg uses Kirch 1984's Ancestral Polynesian Society framework as explicit scaffolding ("assumed to underlie and illuminate," p. 299), applies it to moai iconography and distribution, and produces a four-stage "power shift" model linking statue production to chiefly authority. The dissertation sits chronologically between Kirch 1984 (which positioned Rapa Nui within the Polynesian chiefdom framework via APS inheritance) and the 1990s consolidation (Martinsson-Wallin 1994, Van Tilburg 1994), representing Stage 4 in the chiefdom attribution chain: the moment when the chiefdom model was first applied to the island's most visible material culture.

The 777-page dissertation is primarily a morphological classification and stylistic analysis of monolithic sculpture (Chapters III-V), but its interpretive contribution (Chapters VI-VII) is where the chiefdom implications lie. Processing focused on the interpretive chapters.

## The Four-Stage Power Shift Model

Van Tilburg proposes a four-stage "power shift" (pp. 338-355):

**Stage 1**: Functional distinction between red scoria statues (not on ahu, associated with priestly class) and Rano Raraku tuff statues (on ahu, associated with chiefly class), representing differentiation of priestly and chiefly roles.

**Stage 2**: Superimposition of red scoria pukao on the largest lineage ahu, representing a shift from inherent mana (hereditary, through genealogical proximity) to acquired mana (demonstrated prowess). The priestly hierarchy is postulated as dominated by the Miru lineage.

**Stage 3**: Emergence of birdman cult from subordinate to dominant ideology, replacing the statue cult. A "power struggle of a sort between priests and chiefs" (p. 341). Goldman's (1970) "Open" classification is acknowledged but reframed as describing only this late period, not the overall system.

**Stage 4**: Proto-historic/historic re-use of statuary, extending into the post-contact period.

The model draws its theoretical engine from Miller & Tilley (1984): ideology as misrepresentation of reality in the interests of the ruling class, with symbols making ideology concrete.

## What Van Tilburg Contributes to the Chiefdom Attribution

**1. Moai explicitly become chiefdom evidence.** Before Van Tilburg, moai were described in terms of ancestor veneration and kin-group competition (Métraux 1940, Mulloy & Figueroa 1966, Ayres 1973). Van Tilburg reinterprets the same material culture as expressions of chiefly power: the iconography represents sovereignty, the dorsal design is "symbolic of sovereignty invested in the male heads of lineages" (p. 318), the hands embody the tapu powers of the chief, and monumentality asserts permanence and territorial control.

**2. The moai-as-paramount-chief hypothesis.** Van Tilburg's most ambitious interpretive claim: the statues may represent the ariki mau (paramount chief) rather than individual lineage ancestors, "erected at his behest" (p. 348). She supports this with the observation that "the title ariki was reserved only for the Miru" (Métraux 1940:128) and with a Tikopia sacred canoe ownership model (Firth 1967a:55-56). This is the strongest moai-as-chiefdom-evidence claim in the literature.

**3. Region-wide integration beyond lineage level.** Van Tilburg argues that "the extent of political integration on Easter Island was a great deal more complex than simply that of individual, competing lineages, and the Tahitian model, for example, is indicated" (p. 350). This goes beyond Kirch 1984, who himself stated that ahu "do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority" (p. 270).

## How the Chiefdom Framework Operates in the Dissertation

The Polynesian cultural matrix is "assumed" (p. 299), not demonstrated. The interpretive method works as follows:

1. Identify a material feature (red stone, pukao, dorsal design, hand placement)
2. Find analogues in better-documented Polynesian societies (Tikopia, Tahiti, Marquesas, Tuamotus, New Zealand)
3. Import the social meaning of the analogue (chiefly investiture, mana transfer, priestly authority)
4. Apply that meaning to the Easter Island feature
5. Present the result as "archaeological evidence" supporting the chiefdom model

At no point is the Polynesian analogy tested against independent evidence that Easter Island's political organization matched the societies from which the analogies are drawn. The circularity is explicit: the Polynesian framework is "assumed" and then the archaeological evidence is read through it.

## What Van Tilburg Acknowledges That Complicates the Model

Several passages create tension with the chiefdom interpretation:

1. **Kirch 1984:270 on ahu as descent group structures**: quoted at length (p. 349). Van Tilburg acknowledges the "conventional" interpretation but argues for something beyond it.

2. **Mulloy & Figueroa 1978 on independent quarry work**: accepted as compatible with the model (p. 349), though it undermines centralized command.

3. **Goldman's "Open" classification**: acknowledged (p. 343) but reframed as describing only the late period. Goldman's finding that Easter Island "had never evolved a central authority" (1970:94) is not cited in full.

4. **Not all lineages achieved pukao status**: differential lineage elaboration is accepted as archaeological fact (p. 341). This is compatible with collective action through competitive display without chiefly command.

5. **Ethnographic caution**: "The data must...be approached with extreme caution as it is projected backwards in time, or as it purports to represent pre-contact culture" (p. 39). This caution is stated in Chapter II but not applied in Chapter VII.

6. **"Sudden abandonment" rejected**: Van Tilburg argues for processual continuity and temporal overlap between cults (p. 312), consistent with DiNapoli et al. 2020.

## Relationship to the Five-Step Circularity

Van Tilburg 1986 is a direct illustration of **Step 4**: archaeological evidence (moai) is read through the chiefdom lens that was imported from Polynesian analogy (Step 3) and post-slave-raid ethnography (Step 2).

The circularity is particularly clear in the moai-as-paramount-chief hypothesis:
- The claim that statues represent ariki depends on Métraux 1940:128 (post-slave-raid ethnography)
- The claim that ariki was "reserved only for the Miru" depends on Métraux 1940:128
- The ownership model comes from Tikopia (Firth 1967a), not from Easter Island evidence
- The result is presented as what "the archaeological evidence on Easter Island suggests" (p. 338)

The archaeological evidence (moai exist, they are on ahu, some have pukao, they vary in size) is never analyzed independently of the ethnographic and analogical framework. The material facts could equally support collective action at the lineage level, competitive display without centralized command, or the conventional ancestor-veneration interpretation that Van Tilburg herself acknowledges as the "expected East Polynesian model" (p. 349).

## Connections to Other Processed Sources

- **Kirch 1984**: Van Tilburg takes Kirch's framework as given. Her conclusions go beyond Kirch's own, since Kirch stated ahu were descent-group structures "not reflecting highly centralized authority" (p. 270).
- **Métraux 1940**: Primary ethnographic source; used uncritically for pre-contact social organization despite post-slave-raid context.
- **Goldman 1970**: "Open" classification acknowledged but reframed to apply only to the late period, reversing Goldman's own analysis.
- **Stevenson 1984**: Population estimate of 9,659 cited; contradicted by Davis et al. 2024 (max ~3,900) and Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024.
- **Ayres 1973**: Cited for archaeological data but not for his anti-chiefdom framework or Goldman adoption.
- **McCoy 1976**: Cited but his anti-chiefdom conclusion not engaged.
- **Mulloy & Figueroa 1966/1978**: Independent quarry work acknowledged.
- **Lipo et al. 2013/2025**: Walking moai and decentralized quarry production directly contradict the assumption that moai production required chiefly command.
- **DiNapoli et al. 2019**: Freshwater-based ahu placement contradicts territorial control interpretation.
- **Davis et al. 2024**: Population and agricultural capacity estimates undermine the overpopulation/ecocide framework Van Tilburg adopts.

## Post-Slave-Raid Dependency

All political organization claims, social class descriptions, and chiefdom-supporting ethnographic data derive from Métraux 1940, Englert 1948, Routledge 1919, and Thomson 1891, all post-slave-raid sources. Van Tilburg acknowledges the need for caution (p. 39) but does not apply it in the interpretive chapters. The Miru dominance narrative, the ariki mau description, the ranked lineage order, and the moai-as-chief hypothesis all rest on post-slave-raid testimony. [POST-RAID]

## Assessment Against Current Evidence

Van Tilburg's data contributions (moai classification, stylistic typology, distribution patterns) remain valuable. Her interpretive framework, however, rests on assumptions that have been significantly challenged:

1. **Population estimates**: 9,659 by AD 1800 (Stevenson 1984) contradicted by max ~3,900 (Davis et al. 2024) and steady growth to ~3,000 (Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024).
2. **Ecocide narrative**: Deforestation, soil depletion, overpopulation assumed as context; substantially undermined by multiple independent lines of evidence.
3. **Centralized moai production**: Four-stage model assumes chiefly control of statue production; contradicted by Lipo et al. 2025 (30 independent quarry workshops, 4-6 carvers per moai) and Lipo et al. 2013 (18-person transport teams).
4. **Territorial control model**: Moai as markers of chiefly territory; contradicted by DiNapoli et al. 2019 (ahu placement explained by freshwater access).
5. **Polynesian analogy**: Applied without independent verification; the societies Van Tilburg draws on (Tikopia, Tahiti, Marquesas) are structurally distinct from Easter Island in ways that undermine the comparisons.
