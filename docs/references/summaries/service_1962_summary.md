## Overview

This is the book that formalizes band/tribe/chiefdom/state as universal evolutionary stages. The chiefdom is defined as "redistributional societies with a permanent central agency of coordination" (p. 144), distinguished from tribes by centralized direction, hereditary office, genealogical ranking, craft subsidization, and the ability to deploy public labor. Service draws extensively on Sahlins 1958 for Polynesian data. Polynesia is treated as the paradigmatic chiefdom region.

Easter Island is never mentioned. The chiefdom concept is built from Hawaii, Tahiti, Tonga, the Circum-Caribbean, Northwest Coast, and steppe pastoralists without any consideration of whether it fits all Polynesian societies.

## The Feedback Loop

This book is the middle link in the circular chain:

1. **Sahlins 1954/1958**: Classifies Polynesian societies by stratification, using redistribution as the key mechanism. Acknowledges Easter Island lacks redistribution evidence (1954, p. 101).
2. **Service 1962**: Formalizes Sahlins's Polynesian characterization into a universal evolutionary stage called "chiefdom," defined by redistribution.
3. **Sahlins 1963** (contemporaneous): Creates the big-man/chief binary placing all Polynesia on the "chief" side.
4. **Kirch 1984**: Applies the formalized chiefdom back to Polynesia archaeologically, treating Easter Island as a chiefdom because it is Polynesian and Polynesia defines the chiefdom.

The circularity: Polynesian data (from the most complex cases) constructs the chiefdom concept; the concept is applied back to all of Polynesia as if it were an independent analytical tool. Easter Island's redistribution gap is never resolved; it is simply overridden by the categorical assumption.

## Easter Island vs. Service's Criteria

Easter Island fails every defining criterion of Service's chiefdom:

- **Redistribution from a central agency**: Goldman (1970) shows redistribution lay with commoner heads, not the ariki mau. Sahlins (1954) admitted "little direct evidence."
- **Hereditary office with primogeniture**: Only the Miru maintained hereditary succession; all other mata were headed by matatoa through military success.
- **Chief plans, organizes, and deploys public labor**: Goldman and Kirch both deny centralized monument construction. Archaeological evidence confirms decentralized production.
- **Continuous genealogical ranking**: Easter Island had only two ranks (ariki, huramana), not a continuous gradation. Lacked the rangatira rank found in Traditional societies.
- **Craft subsidization from the redistributive center**: Carvers were paid directly in food by commissioners, not subsidized by a chief.

## Significance

Service completes the conceptual infrastructure that makes the chiefdom label available as a universal category. After this book, calling Easter Island a chiefdom is not just a Polynesian classification but a claim about its evolutionary stage. The label persists because "chiefdom" is the only category between tribe and state, and Easter Island is assumed to be too complex for a tribe. But the defining feature of Service's chiefdom, centralized redistribution, is precisely what the evidence does not support.
