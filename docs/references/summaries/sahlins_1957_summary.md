# Source Summary: Sahlins 1957

**Source**: Marshall D. Sahlins, "Differentiation by Adaptation in Polynesian Societies." *Journal of the Polynesian Society*, Vol. 66, No. 3 (September 1957), pp. 291-300.

**PDF**: `docs/references/pdfs/sahlins_1957/Sahlins_1957.pdf`

**Full claims extraction**: `docs/references/claims/sahlins_1957_claims.md`

---

## Overview

This 10-page article condenses Part Two of Sahlins's 1954 Columbia PhD dissertation (published 1958 as *Social Stratification in Polynesia*). It argues that two characteristic Polynesian descent group forms, "ramage" and "truncated descent line," differentiated by adaptation from a single proto-Polynesian source. Ramages are associated with dispersed resource ecologies requiring household specialization and hierarchical redistribution. Truncated descent lines are associated with clustered coastal resource ecologies where self-sufficient domestic groups compete for status through surplus accumulation. Atoll societies represent further divergence from high-island prototypes.

The article covers only the form of social organization (Part Two). It does not address the amount of stratification (Part One), the four-group classification (I/IIa/IIb/III), productivity measurement, or the detailed island-by-island analysis of political offices, status levels, and evidence gaps.

Sahlins notes in fn. 1 that the article "modifies, in places extends, and generally develops" the dissertation's Part Two. The main modifications are terminological: he departs from Firth's 1957 redefinition of "ramage" (fn. 5, p. 293), preferring the original 1936 usage for a strictly unilineal ranked descent group.

---

## Easter Island Treatment

Easter Island appears three times in the article, all briefly:

1. **Ramage classification** (p. 291): Listed among ramage societies alongside Hawaii, Tonga, Tahiti, Mangaia, Mangareva, Marquesas, New Zealand, and Tikopia. No discussion or qualification at this point.

2. **Exchange practices** (p. 294): Cited as having "comparable exchange practices" to other ramage societies. The only Easter Island source cited is Routledge 1919:218, which actually describes the birdman cult's first-fruits ceremony, not chiefly redistributive exchange.

3. **Ecological anomaly** (p. 295): Listed as one of three exceptions (with Tikopia and Uvea) where the ecological situation is "not clearly one of concentrated nor scattered production, but appears intermediate." Selective pressures are "minimal or indifferent." The ramage classification is explained by cultural heritage from central Polynesia (citing Sahlins 1955).

None of the following dissertation features appear: the Group IIa stratification rating, productivity scores, redistribution evidence assessment, political office descriptions (ariki mau, birdman, matatoa), moai/monument discussion, data gap admissions, or the detailed Metraux/Thomson/Routledge source base.

## Significance

The 1957 article matters for our argument in two ways:

**1. Progressive stripping of qualifications.** The dissertation documents extensive uncertainty about Easter Island's fit within the Polynesian framework: eleven explicit data gaps, an admission that redistribution evidence is lacking, acknowledgment of ecological anomaly. The 1957 article retains only the ecological anomaly note and drops everything else. This is not dishonest on Sahlins's part (the article explicitly covers only Part Two and directs readers to the monograph for full documentation), but it means scholars citing "Sahlins 1957/1958" without consulting the dissertation inherit a framework whose caveats have been removed.

**2. The atoll catastrophe parallel.** Sahlins's discussion of how catastrophic population loss on atolls can produce rapid reorganization of social systems (p. 296) is directly relevant to the 1862-1863 slave raids on Easter Island. If a 1903 hurricane on Raroia caused "profound cultural changes" through the "loss of old time political and religious leaders," the slave raids (which killed or removed the ariki mau and most knowledgeable elders from Easter Island) would have produced comparable or greater disruption. This undermines the assumption that post-raid ethnographic sources can recover pre-contact organization.
