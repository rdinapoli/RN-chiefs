## Overview

This 6-page paper examines prehistoric agricultural production on Rapa Nui through an evolutionary ecological framework, comparing dry lowland (Heki'i) and moist upland (Akahanga/Vaihu) districts. It identifies six types of agricultural features, proposes a three-zone land-use model, and argues that rainfall uncertainty drove agricultural intensification requiring hierarchical political authority. The paper is significant for introducing a map of "Rapa Nui chiefdom districts circa AD 1500" (Figure 2), marking the moment when the chiefdom label becomes cartographic infrastructure.

The paper sits at a critical point in the Stevenson progressive hardening trajectory: 1984 (no chiefdom, corporate descent groups) -> 1986 (no chiefdom in text, editorial reframing in Kirch volume) -> 2001 (chiefdom districts mapped, "apex of chiefdom complexity," "elite managed plantations") -> 2002 ("well-developed chiefdom"). The 2001 paper is where the chiefdom label shifts from interpretive hypothesis to spatial framework.

---

## Relevance to the Chiefdom Attribution

The paper contributes to the chiefdom attribution in three ways:

1. **Cartographic naturalization**: Figure 2 divides the island into named "chiefdom districts," transforming an interpretive claim into a map that subsequent researchers inherit as spatial infrastructure. The district boundaries derive from Routledge 1919, Metraux 1940, and McCoy 1976 (via Stevenson 2002), but none of these post-raid sources are cited in the 2001 paper.

2. **Functional argument for hierarchy**: The paper argues that marginal, uncertain agriculture required "an ideologically strong and strictly ranked hierarchy" (p. 22). This is a functionalist claim: the conditions required hierarchy, therefore hierarchy existed. No independent evidence for the hierarchy is presented.

3. **Terminology hardening**: "Chiefdom" appears at least 8 times in Stevenson's own text, compared with zero uses in 1984 and 1986. "Elite residential complexes" (1984) become "elite managed plantations" (2001). "Reverted to a non-ranked corporate structure" (1984) becomes "chiefdom collapse" (2001).

## The Chiefdom Districts Map

Figure 2 ("Rapa Nui chiefdom districts circa AD 1500, after Stevenson in press") is the paper's most consequential contribution to the chiefdom attribution. The map:

- Shows ten named territorial units (Heki'i, Mahatua, Tongariki, Hanga Poukura, Vinapu, Tahai, Vaihu, Akahanga, Tepeu, 'Anakena)
- Dates them to "circa AD 1500" without stating the evidentiary basis
- Sources them to "Stevenson in press" (= Stevenson 2002 "Territorial Divisions"), which in turn derives boundaries from Routledge 1919, Metraux 1940, and McCoy 1976
- Is introduced not as a hypothesis but as "the distribution of island political units" to which environmental data can be compared (p. 18)

Compare Stevenson 1984 (p. 162): "it will not be possible to draw boundaries between political units" and "The use of an arbitrary criteria for establishing political divisions... is probably misleading." By 2001, boundaries are drawn, named, and used as the analytical framework.

The cartographic presentation is the naturalization mechanism. Once districts appear on a map with dates and names, they function as infrastructure: researchers comparing their own data to the island's spatial organization encounter "chiefdom districts" as given units, not as interpretive claims requiring evaluation.

## The Agriculture-Chiefdom Circular Argument

The paper's central argument has a circular structure:

1. **Premise**: Chiefdom districts existed (from Stevenson 2002, based on ethnographic sources)
2. **Observation**: Agricultural features are differentially distributed across these districts
3. **Inference**: Intensive agriculture required hierarchical authority ("elite managed plantations")
4. **Conclusion**: Agricultural data confirm chiefdom-level political organization

The conclusion restates the premise. Agricultural data organized by chiefdom districts becomes evidence for chiefdom-level organization of agriculture.

The strongest version of this circularity is the "ideologically strong and strictly ranked hierarchy" claim (p. 22). The argument runs: marginal agriculture -> surplus needed for ahu construction -> low returns require hierarchy to compel supra-household labor -> therefore hierarchy existed. Each link assumes the chiefdom model: that surplus was extracted (not voluntarily contributed), that ahu required chiefly direction (not community initiative), and that only hierarchy could mobilize labor above the household level (not collective action).

## Hawaiian Ahupua'a Analogy

The paper explicitly applies the Hawaiian ahupua'a model to Rapa Nui districts (p. 21): "It was based upon the concept of the Hawaiian ahupua'a where each sub-district population segment had access to a strip of land from the coast to the interior and the full range of resources contained within it."

The ahupua'a is a specifically Hawaiian institution involving chiefly management of coast-to-mountain land strips. Applying it to Rapa Nui constitutes Polynesian analogy: the assumption that Easter Island's territorial organization resembled Hawaii's. This assumption is imported through co-authorship (Ladefoged's Hawaiian comparative work: Ladefoged 1995, Ladefoged & Graves 2000) rather than through explicit theoretical argument. The paper notes that the equal-resource assumption of the ahupua'a model is violated by differential rainfall on Rapa Nui, but does not question whether the model itself is appropriate.

| Feature | 1984 | 1986 | 2001 |
|---------|------|------|------|
| "Chiefdom" usage | Absent | Absent from text | 8+ uses |
| Political boundaries | "Cannot be drawn" | No boundary maps | 10 named districts mapped |
| Elite terminology | "Elite residential complex" | Same | "Elite managed plantations" |
| Late-period change | "Reverted to non-ranked" | "Solidarity weakened" | "Chiefdom collapse" |
| Theoretical frame | British social anthropology | Method without theory | Evolutionary ecology |
| Post-raid sources | Cited; used "with caution" | Cited; caveat weakened | Not cited; hidden |
| Polynesian analogy | Rejected (Ibo chosen instead) | Retained Ibo | Hawaiian ahupua'a |

The most striking change is the concealment of the post-raid source dependency. In 1984 and 1986, McCall 1979, Goldman 1970, Metraux 1940, and Routledge 1919 were cited as the basis for political organization claims. In 2001, none appear in the bibliography. The chiefdom districts derived from these sources (via Stevenson 2002) are presented as "island political units" without acknowledging their ethnographic origin. This makes it appear that the chiefdom framework emerges from archaeological survey data rather than from post-slave-raid ethnographic reconstruction.

## Claims Contradicted by Current Evidence

1. **Agricultural extent**: Davis et al. 2024 found rock gardens cover 0.76 km2, one-fifth of prior estimates. Maximum carrying capacity ~3,900, not the large population the paper assumes.

2. **Centralized production**: Lipo et al. 2025 found 30 redundant quarry workshops with varied methods, consistent with autonomous production, not elite management.

3. **Territorial organization**: DiNapoli et al. 2019 found ahu placement explained by freshwater, not chiefly territorial control.

4. **Upland abandonment**: Mulrooney 2013 found settlement continuity, not the abandonment narrative ("virtually all of the island population was living on the island coastal plain" after AD 1700).

5. **"Chiefdom collapse"**: Moreno-Mayar et al. 2024 ancient DNA rejects a 1600s population collapse.

6. **Ahu construction timeline**: DiNapoli et al. 2020 found steady construction tempo through contact, not the "largest structures AD 1400-1600 then cessation" implied.

## Bibliographic Profile

The 2001 paper's bibliography reveals a significant shift in Stevenson's citation network:

**Added** (not in 1984/1986): Earle 1997 (chiefdom political economy), Ladefoged 1995/Graves & Ladefoged 1995/Ladefoged & Graves 2000 (evolutionary ecology, Hawaiian agriculture), Dunnell 1999 (waste in evolutionary archaeology), Kirch 2000, Hommon 2001, Hunt & Lipo 2001. These constitute the evolutionary ecological and chiefdom-theoretical framework.

**Dropped** (in 1984/1986 but not 2001): Goldman 1970, McCall 1979, Metraux 1940, Routledge 1919, Fortes, Firth, Kirchhoff, Hayden & Cannon, Stevenson 1986. The entire post-raid ethnographic foundation and British social anthropology framework are absent.

**Retained**: Stevenson 1984 (self-citation for data), Stevenson & Cristino 1986 (survey), McCoy 1976, Martinsson-Wallin 1994, Skjolsvold 1994. The empirical archaeological sources survive.

**New self-citations**: Stevenson 1997, Stevenson et al. 1999, Stevenson & Haoa 1998, Stevenson in press (2002). The settlement model and chiefdom district framework now derive from Stevenson's own evolving publication chain.

The net effect: the paper's theoretical framework comes from evolutionary ecology (Ladefoged, Earle) and the empirical claims from Stevenson's own surveys, while the ethnographic sources on which the chiefdom districts actually depend are invisible.

## Data Presented

- Six-type classification of agricultural features (mulched soils, veneer surfaces, stacked boulders, pu, manavai, planting circles)
- Three-zone settlement/agricultural model for Heki'i District (coastal, lowland inland, lowland interior)
- Three-zone model for Akahanga/Vaihu District (coastal, lowland inland, upland interior)
- Chronological framework: coastal settlement AD 800, upland expansion AD 1250, intensive agriculture AD 1400, abandonment AD 1700
- Rainfall data: 1972-1997 Mataveri records, CV 0.17-0.26
- Orographic rainfall model (Honorato et al. 1991): double rainfall above 200m
- Map of ten chiefdom districts (Figure 2, after Stevenson 2002)

## Post-Slave-Raid Dependency

The chiefdom district boundaries mapped in Figure 2 derive entirely from post-slave-raid sources (Routledge 1919, Metraux 1940) via McCoy 1976 and Stevenson 2002. None of these sources are cited in the 2001 paper. The political organization claims ("chiefdom districts," "chiefdom collapse," "chiefly houses," "elite managers") inherit their content from this ethnographic reconstruction but present themselves as emerging from archaeological survey data and evolutionary ecological theory. The hidden dependency is the paper's most significant methodological problem: readers cannot evaluate the evidentiary chain because its foundational links are concealed. [POST-RAID]
