# Summary: Golson 1965

**Full citation**: Golson, J. 1965. "Thor Heyerdahl and the Prehistory of Easter Island." *Oceania* 36(1):38-83.

**Type**: Critical review article of the Norwegian Archaeological Expedition reports (Heyerdahl & Ferdon 1961). Detailed reanalysis of ahu excavation data, radiocarbon chronology, and cultural affiliation.

## Overview

A meticulous 46-page archaeological critique of the conclusions drawn from the Norwegian Archaeological Expedition to Easter Island (1955-56), focusing on ahu architecture, radiocarbon chronology, and cultural origins. Golson argues that the three-period archaeological sequence (Early, Middle, Late) reflects continuous Polynesian cultural development, not the arrival of different populations as Heyerdahl proposed. He challenges the chronological framework, suggesting later dates for all three periods, and demonstrates that the available evidence is insufficient for the confident conclusions the expedition drew.

## Key Arguments

### Polynesian Cultural Continuity
Golson's central thesis: "Easter Island culture history, read in terms of the form and fortunes of its temples, is Polynesian throughout" (p. 80). Ahu show continuity of ceremonial site, ground plan, masonry skills, and burial practice across all three periods. Artifacts are "securely within the Polynesian tradition" (p. 81). Architectural changes can be explained by internal development; no external population or South American influence is required. Golson proposes reversing the Vinapu sequence (Vinapu 2 before Vinapu 1), which places the ahu type with closest Polynesian marae parallels earliest in the sequence.

### Chronological Critique
Golson challenges the expedition's dating at multiple points. The Poike Ditch early date (AD 386 ± 100) is questioned because identical slingstones appear in both the mound and ditch fill, dated ~1300 years apart. The Rano Raraku quarry date (AD 1206 ± 250) was "adjudged by the laboratory inadequate for accurate ageing." All period boundaries may be later than proposed: Early-Middle boundary perhaps AD 1250 (not 1100), Late Period perhaps beginning c. AD 1750 (not 1680). These later dates have been partially vindicated by subsequent research (Hunt & Lipo 2006, DiNapoli et al. 2020, Robinson & Stevenson 2017).

### Late Period and Statue Toppling
The "Late Period" is characterized by statue toppling, semi-pyramidal ahu construction, and mataa proliferation. Golson notes that Roggeveen (1722) and González (1770) mention no overturned statues; this is first recorded by Cook in 1774. This observation, combined with other evidence, supports a later onset of the "statue-overthrowing time" than the expedition's c. 1680 date.

### Orongo House Dating
Golson challenges Ferdon's claim that Orongo-style stone houses with corbelled roofs are ancient, arguing "the claim for the antiquity of the Orongo house is invalid on the evidence used" (p. 81). If the stone houses are late, they may have evolved from the boat-shaped timber houses (hare paenga). This is consistent with Robinson & Stevenson 2017's finding of peak Orongo activity at c. AD 1795.

### Methodological Critique
Golson warns against "the practice of using traditions to make good the deficiencies of archaeological evidence in the strictly archaeological sphere" (p. 74). He notes that the traditional event (Poike battle) used to date the Late Period is "external to the events at the ahu in terms of which the Late Period is defined" (p. 75). This methodological concern anticipates the current paper's argument about the circularity of using ethnohistoric accounts to interpret archaeological evidence.

## Political Organization

Golson does not discuss political organization. The words "chiefdom," "chief," "hierarchy," "Service," "Sahlins," and "Goldman" do not appear. The only uses of "political" refer to Late Period "political conditions" and "political unrest" (both p. 74, following Mulloy). The article is purely archaeological/methodological in focus.

## Source Dependencies

Based entirely on the Norwegian Archaeological Expedition reports (Heyerdahl & Ferdon 1961). No ethnographic or ethnohistoric sources used for interpretation of political organization. Additional archaeological comparisons drawn from Duff 1964, Emory 1933, Green 1963, and Skinner 1942.

## Significance for Chiefdom Attribution

1. **Pre-chiefdom baseline**: Published three years after Service 1962 but contains no chiefdom terminology. The chiefdom framework had not yet been applied to Easter Island.

2. **Polynesian continuity argument**: Golson's demonstration that Easter Island culture is "Polynesian throughout" became the archaeological foundation for subsequent chiefdom attributions. If Easter Island is continuously Polynesian, and Polynesian societies are chiefdoms (Sahlins 1963), then Easter Island must be a chiefdom. The logical gap is that material cultural continuity does not entail political organizational continuity.

3. **Chronological challenges**: Golson's skepticism about the chronological framework has been partially vindicated. Later dates for all periods compress the timeline and challenge the narrative of long-term chiefdom development followed by collapse.

4. **Tradition-archaeology circularity**: Golson's critique of using traditions to date archaeological sequences anticipates the broader circularity argument: using ethnohistoric accounts (themselves derived from post-raid informants) to interpret archaeological evidence, then citing the archaeological evidence as "confirming" the ethnohistoric framework.

5. **Orongo dating**: Golson's challenge to the antiquity of Orongo stone houses, if correct, means the birdman ceremony formalization is late, potentially contact-era (Robinson & Stevenson 2017), rather than a centuries-old institution.

## Limitations

- Focused on ahu architecture and chronology; no coverage of settlement patterns, domestic structures, or social organization
- Does not address political organization at all; limited relevance to the chiefdom question except as chronological and methodological context
- Some arguments rest on reversing the Vinapu sequence, which remains debated
- Proposes later dates based on the "mere manipulation of a few radiocarbon dates of doubtful value" (p. 77), as Golson himself acknowledges
- No comparison with Polynesian political ethnography; purely archaeological focus
