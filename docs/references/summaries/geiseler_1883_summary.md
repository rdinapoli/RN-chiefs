# Source Summary: Geiseler 1883

## Overview

Kapitänleutnant Geiseler of the German Imperial Navy visited Rapa Nui aboard SMS Hyäne for four days, September 19-23, 1882. His report, *Die Oster-Insel: Eine Stätte prähistorischer Kultur in der Südsee*, was published in 1883 in Berlin by order of the Kaiserliche Admiralität. The report combines brief archaeological survey (platforms, quarry, Orongo stone houses), ethnographic information collected through the intermediary Alexander P. Salmon, and a collection of artifacts sent to Berlin. This summary is based on the German original; the English translation by Ayres and Ayres (1995) is not yet in the repository.

The population at the time of the visit was 150 (67 men, 39 women, 44 children), plus approximately 20 Tahitians employed by the Brander firm. This represents a partial recovery from a nadir of approximately 111 in 1877, but still only about 5% of the pre-raid population of roughly 3,000.

## Key Findings for Political Authority

**Complete collapse of political organization (direct observation, 1882).** Geiseler records the total absence of functioning political authority. "A king does not exist, since the last one died in the Peruvian slavery" (p. 5). "Since that time every man is his own master, who recognizes no authority over himself and seeks his own justice" (p. 22-23). "A political life has not existed since the deportation of the last king, as every man is his own master, and a national consciousness seems to have been entirely lost" (p. 42). Geiseler describes a "complete dissolution of all political community" (p. 23) and notes that the entire organization now rests solely on the family, with each family head as "the highest person" (p. 42).

**"Old chief" as an empty title (direct observation, 1882).** Those identified as "old chiefs" command no respect or advantage: "These so-titled people appear to be nothing less than 'chiefs'; almost childish, they show for example nothing of the refined deportment of the old Samoan chiefs; they are not at all respected on account of their age" (p. 22-23). Chiefs have no power to punish theft; "there remains for the one robbed only to seek justice for himself" (p. 28-29). Chiefs use their authority-staffs (Hoheitszeichen) as clubs (p. 5-6), a detail that captures the degradation of formerly symbolic objects to utilitarian ones.

**Former regime described as "almost despotic" (reported tradition, via Salmon).** "In earlier times one distinguished an almost despotic regime of the kings toward the common people. After them the chiefs had the most prestige and formed the advisors in all cases" (p. 42). This description of pre-contact kingship as "almost despotic" (faft befpotifdjes Regiment) is notably stronger than what any contact-era European account records as direct observation.

**Succession to the brother, not the son (reported tradition, via Salmon).** "At death, after the burial of the old king, the dignity passed without further ado and without special ceremonies to the heir, the brother" (p. 42). This contradicts both Thomson's account (which describes primogeniture) and Roussel's king list (which implies father-to-son succession in its genealogical structure). The discrepancy across three post-raid sources about something as fundamental as the rule of succession is significant.

**No priests; kings formerly served as priests (reported tradition, via Salmon).** "They have no priests and are said never to have had them. In earlier times, when the kings still had considerable power and prestige, these served as priests, who led and directed the religious festivities" (p. 33). This echoes the religious vacuum documented by Eyraud (1864) and Roussel (1866-1873).

**Rongorongo known only to kings and chiefs (reported tradition, via Salmon and "old chief").** "This script-language was known only to the former kings and the chiefs; the common people understood it neither to write nor to read" (p. 24). The tablets were used to transmit "short notes on important matters" to chiefs of other villages and to record genealogies. The script was restricted to the elite, but by 1882 no one could read it.

**Land ownership individual and familial (direct observation, 1882).** "On the entire island every piece of land has its special name and its owner. They hold fast very much to this land ownership and inherit it from family to family" (p. 42). "Every family father has the right to declare war or feud and combat, as soon as he, his family, or kindred is insulted and receives no satisfaction" (p. 42). Land ownership is hereditary, fiercely maintained, and tied to family rather than to any chiefly hierarchy.

**Rank-differentiated social practices (reported tradition).** Marriage was formerly arranged by chiefs and restricted by rank; "mesalliances" occurred only when rank-appropriate matches were impossible due to the shortage of women (p. 29-30). Punishment for adultery differed by rank: chiefs' wives were killed; commoners received beatings (p. 30). Only chiefs and kings were buried in special stone tombs; common people were placed under open platforms (p. 11). These details describe a stratified society but come entirely through reported tradition, not direct observation of a functioning system.

**Specialist idol-makers as a status class (reported tradition and observation).** A "special class" of idol-makers formerly existed, "who practiced this craft professionally and stood in high esteem, similar to the boat-builders on Tonga and Fiji" (p. 13-14). The consciousness of descent from such families persisted to 1882; Geiseler was shown a man whose great-grandfather had been an idol-maker. By 1882, no special occupational classes were distinguished, as the Rapanui people "without any political cohesion, have disintegrated within themselves" (p. 19).

**Tangata manu as physical competition, not political office.** Geiseler's account of the egg-fetching from Motu Nui (p. 17-18) describes a dangerous physical competition involving climbing, swimming, and combat between swimmers. Unlike Thomson's and Roussel's accounts, Geiseler does not describe the birdman as assuming political authority. The emphasis is on Make-Make worship and the eggs as a seasonal food source, not on political succession.

## Context and Limitations

**[POST-RAID]** Geiseler visited 20 years after the slave raids. The population of 150 had been reduced from approximately 3,000, a decline of 95%. The last king, Maurata, and his brother Kaimokoi had died in Peruvian slavery. All political institutions had collapsed.

**Four-day visit.** The visit lasted only from September 19-23, 1882. This is shorter than Thomson's twelve days and far shorter than Roussel's seven years of residence. The brevity severely limits the depth of ethnographic inquiry.

**All ethnographic information intermediated through Salmon.** Alexander P. Salmon, a half-blood Tahitian related to the Tahitian royal family, was the sole translator and cultural broker for the visit. Salmon had been resident on the island since the early 1870s (employed by the Brander firm) and spoke Rapanui fluently (p. 24). Geiseler identifies him on arrival (p. 6) and credits him with arranging performances, conducting inquiries with native informants, and providing interpretive commentary. There is no indication that Geiseler communicated directly with any Rapanui person without Salmon's mediation.

**Salmon was also Thomson's translator.** Salmon served as the primary translator and cultural intermediary for Thomson's 1886 visit, four years after Geiseler's. This means that the convergences between Geiseler's and Thomson's accounts (the "almost despotic" regime, rongorongo restricted to elites, specialist idol-makers, origin traditions) may reflect Salmon's interpretive framework and narrative preferences rather than independent traditions from different informants. Where Geiseler and Thomson agree, the agreement may demonstrate nothing more than the consistency of Salmon's account of Rapanui culture across two visits.

**Salmon's own cultural lens.** Salmon was related to the Tahitian royal family and "thoroughly familiar with the customs and habits of the Polynesians" (p. 43-44). His description of former Rapanui kingship may have been inflected by Tahitian models of hereditary authority. His preferred origin theory placed Rapanui settlement from the Tuamotus (p. 43-44), situating the island within a Polynesian framework. When Geiseler reports traditions of "almost despotic" kingship, it is impossible to determine whether this reflects what Rapanui informants actually said, what Salmon understood them to say, or what Salmon himself believed about how Polynesian societies should have been organized.

**Competing origin traditions.** The extraction records at least three competing origin traditions: from Rapa/Oparo (an island Salmon misidentifies as being in the Tuamotus rather than the Australs), from the Galapagos, and from the west (p. 43). The existence of multiple contradictory traditions within a population of 150 people, reported through a single intermediary with his own preferred theory, illustrates the fragility of oral tradition in this context.

**Chronological position: between Pinart (1877) and Thomson (1886).** Pinart, who visited in 1877, reported that the islanders "retained no memory at all" of certain traditions. Geiseler, visiting five years later, records detailed traditions of "despotic" kingship, succession rules, and social classes, all through Salmon. Thomson, visiting four years after Geiseler, records an even more elaborate tradition including a 57-king list, again through Salmon. The progressive elaboration of tradition across these three visits, all mediated by the same individual, raises the possibility that Salmon was constructing or systematizing tradition over time rather than passively transmitting stable oral history.

**Discrepancy on succession rules.** Geiseler reports brother-to-brother succession; Thomson reports primogeniture; Roussel's king list implies patrilineal succession. Three post-raid sources, recorded within 17 years of each other from the same small population, disagree on the most basic structural feature of the kingship they describe. This disagreement is not reconcilable by assuming regional variation or informant error; it indicates that no reliable knowledge of pre-contact succession practices survived the demographic collapse.

## Relevance to the Paper

Geiseler's report is significant for the chiefdom argument in four respects:

1. **Direct observation of total political collapse.** Geiseler provides the clearest firsthand description of the complete absence of political authority on Rapa Nui. His observations that "every man is his own master," that chiefs carry no power or respect, and that political life has ceased entirely document the condition of the society from which all subsequent "traditional" information about kingship and hierarchy was drawn. The political system whose former existence is being described by informants had, by 1882, left no functioning trace.

2. **The Salmon problem.** Geiseler's report is the earliest source to document Salmon's role as cultural intermediary in detail. Because Salmon also served Thomson four years later, the Geiseler report establishes that convergences between the two accounts cannot be treated as independent corroboration. Any claim that rests on Geiseler-Thomson agreement must account for the shared intermediary. This is directly relevant to the paper's argument about the circularity of the evidentiary chain: if the "tradition" reported in both sources reflects one man's framework rather than stable oral history, then the apparent consistency of the ethnographic record is an artifact of the recording process.

3. **Progressive elaboration of tradition.** The sequence from Pinart 1877 (no memory retained) through Geiseler 1882 ("almost despotic" regime described) to Thomson 1886 (57-king list and elaborate governmental structure) shows traditions becoming more detailed over time, not less. This pattern is the opposite of what would be expected if stable traditions were being gradually lost. It is consistent with traditions being actively constructed or systematized, possibly by Salmon, during this period.

4. **The succession rule discrepancy.** The brother-succession reported by Geiseler contradicts both Thomson (primogeniture) and the genealogical structure of Roussel's king list. This disagreement about the most fundamental feature of kingship, the rule of succession, undermines the use of any post-raid source as reliable evidence for pre-contact political organization.
