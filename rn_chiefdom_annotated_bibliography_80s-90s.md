# Annotated Bibliography: Rapa Nui Political Organization, Chiefdoms, and Sociopolitical Structure (1984–2001)

Sources from the Stevenson OHD database and RN-RAG corpus, organized by relevance tier. Covers the period when the chiefdom model was most actively applied to Rapa Nui and the sociopolitical interpretive framework was established.

---

## I. Primary Works: Political Organization as Central Topic

**Stevenson, Christopher M. 1984.** "Corporate Descent Group Structure in Easter Island Prehistory." Ph.D. dissertation, Department of Anthropology, Pennsylvania State University. 377 pp.

Uses corporate descent group models (Hayden and Cannon 1982) to reconstruct sociopolitical organization from settlement pattern data on the southern coast. Argues for long-term equilibrium of segmentary political groups (ca. AD 1000–1680), proliferation of lineage ahu during population growth, inter-kin group competition driving moai gigantism, the hypothesized emergence of chiefs from resource scarcity (AD 1500–1722), and political segmentation at ca. AD 1680 when the system "reverts to a set of more autonomous and segmented political groups." The foundational archaeological model for Rapa Nui political structure. Note: very poor OCR in corpus limits text search, but extracted claims capture key arguments.

**Stevenson, Christopher M. 1986.** "The Socio-Political Structure of the Southern Coastal Area of Easter Island: AD 1300–1864." In P. V. Kirch (ed.), *Island Societies: Archaeological Approaches to Evolution and Transformation*, pp. 69–77. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.

Published synthesis of the dissertation's sociopolitical model. Maps lineage territories along the southern coast, discusses ariki mau, matatoa warrior chiefs, tribute systems, moiety divisions (Hotu Iti), kinship organization, elite residence patterns, and the shift from hereditary to achieved authority. The most-cited short treatment of Rapa Nui prehistoric political structure.

**Van Tilburg, Jo Anne. 1986.** "Power and Symbol: The Stylistic Analysis of Easter Island Monolithic Sculpture." Ph.D. dissertation, Institute of Archaeology, University of California, Los Angeles. 777 pp.

Interprets moai stylistic variation as reflecting political power dynamics; statue size and elaboration index chiefly competition and corporate group prestige. Note: very poor OCR in corpus.

**Van Tilburg, Jo Anne. 1994.** *Easter Island: Archaeology, Ecology, and Culture*. Washington, D.C.: Smithsonian Institution Press / London: British Museum Press. 191 pp.

The most comprehensive single-volume treatment of Rapa Nui sociopolitical organization from this period. Discusses ariki mau and sau vs. haka'iki; dual political/cosmological opposition; land control by chiefs (not descent groups) as a step toward stratification; competitive ahu/moai construction as inter-clan rivalry; lineage (ure) organization under senior males; professional craft guilds (maori); and the birdman cult at Orongo as a pan-island political ritual. Distribution of monolithic statue sites is mapped relative to Routledge's (1919) kin group divisions.

**Cristino, Claudio P., et al. 1999.** "Ahu Tongariki, Easter Island: Chronological and Sociopolitical Significance." *Rapa Nui Journal* 13(3): 67–69.

Argues Tongariki's construction sequence, from separate lineage ahu merged into one massive platform with 15 moai, reflects growing sociopolitical complexity and consolidation within one of the island's two major population segments. Documents "profound socio-political and religious changes" in late prehistory possibly triggered by environmental degradation.

**Beardsley, Felicia R. 1990.** "Spatial Analysis of Platform Ahu on Easter Island." Ph.D. dissertation, University of Oregon, Eugene. 406 pp.

Spatial analysis of ahu distribution proposing archaeological correlates for mata (clan) territories and territorial boundary zones. One of the few works attempting to ground ethnohistoric accounts of sociopolitical divisions in quantitative archaeological evidence. Notes that prior discussions relied on analogies from other Polynesian islands. Note: essentially no extracted text in corpus (210 chars, all tables).

**McCall, Grant. 1994.** *Rapanui: Tradition and Survival on Easter Island*. 2nd ed. Honolulu: University of Hawai'i Press. [1st ed. 1981, Allen and Unwin.]

Ethnographic and historical synthesis treating cultural specialists, oral historical knowledge, social memory, and Rapanui social continuity. Discusses the depth of local historical knowledge and the role of cultural specialists in maintaining social identity. A Spanish translation was published in 1999 by the Easter Island Foundation.

## II. Major Works with Substantial Political Organization Discussion

**Martinsson-Wallin, Helene. 1994.** *Ahu: The Ceremonial Stone Structures of Easter Island. Analyses of Variation and Interpretation of Meanings*. Aula Qvarternaria 4, Societas Archaeologica Upsaliensis (Aun 19). Uppsala.

Dissertation analyzing ahu variation across the island. Interprets ahu size, location, and elaboration in terms of chiefdom-level political power, lineage territories (Tupahotu, Miru, Hotu Iti, Ko Tuu), corporate group organization, social stratification, inter-group competition, and redistributive systems. Describes Englert's eight principal mata and four minor groups. Includes extensive territorial distribution data by clan and discussion of specialized occupational groups (maori). One of the most data-rich treatments of political territorial organization.

**Fischer, Steven Roger. 1997.** *Rongorongo: The Easter Island Script*. Oxford Studies in Anthropological Linguistics 14. Oxford: Oxford University Press. 296 pp.

While primarily about the undeciphered script, contains the most extensive textual discussion of sociopolitical context in the corpus: ariki mau (22+ mentions), descent groups (21+), clans (17+), tribute (15+), paramount chiefs, the Tu'u vs. 'Otu 'Iti factional wars, birdman cult (tangata manu), and rongorongo as an elite franchise linked to political consolidation following the wars between the island's two major descent groups.

**Van Tilburg, Jo Anne, et al. 1998.** "Megaliths and Mariners: Experimental Archaeology on Easter Island (Rapa Nui)." In C. M. Stevenson, G. Lee, and F. J. Morin (eds.), *Easter Island in Pacific Context: South Seas Symposium*, pp. 187–211. Los Osos: Easter Island Foundation.

Postulates a sociopolitical and economic model of the "average Rapa Nui chiefdom," estimating labor organization for moai transport within ramage/lineage structures. Treats chiefdom as the operative political unit and models its productive capacity and labor mobilization. Used photogrammetric data from Ahu Akivi statue 01/53 for computer-simulated transport configurations.

**Van Tilburg, Jo Anne, and Georgia Lee. 1987a.** "Rock Art of Rapa Nui." *Occasional Papers of the Easter Island Committee* 1. Los Angeles: Institute of Archaeology, UCLA. 17 pp.

Interprets petroglyphs within a "Polynesian socio-political framework of differential access to important resources." Discusses rock art as evidence of chiefly status (the vaka vaero royal canoe), birdman cult imagery, ramage organization, and political ideology.

**Van Tilburg, Jo Anne, and Georgia Lee. 1987b.** "Symbolic Stratigraphy: Rock Art and the Monolithic Statues of Easter Island." *World Archaeology* 19(2): 133–149.

Examines the symbolic relationship between petroglyphs and moai as reflecting shifts in political ideology.

**Stevenson, Christopher M., Joan Wozniak, and Sonia Haoa. 1999.** "Prehistoric Agricultural Production on Easter Island (Rapa Nui)." *Antiquity* 73: 801–812.

Links agricultural intensification (lithic mulch gardens) to feeding expanding populations and supporting "social competition amongst the élite components of chiefdoms" (Kirch 1994; Spriggs 1985, 1986). Discusses how productive capacity influenced "the impetus and level of social stratification in ranked society."

**Skjølsvold, Arne. 1994.** *Archaeological Investigations at Anakena, Easter Island*. The Kon-Tiki Museum Occasional Papers 3. Oslo. 216 pp.

Major excavation report for Anakena, legendary landing place of Hotu Matu'a and seat of the paramount chief. Discusses political structure, chiefly authority at the paramount settlement, tangata manu cult, clan divisions (Tupahotu, Miru), and cites Stevenson 1986 extensively.

**Charola, A. Elena (ed.). 1994.** *Easter Island: The Heritage and its Conservation*. New York: World Monuments Fund / ICCROM. 74 pp.

Multi-author overview with substantial sections on the ariki mau institution, ariki paka (royal family), descent groups, paramount chiefs, the birdman cult (14 mentions), tangata manu, clan organization, and Miru lineage. Includes a glossary of political/social terms.

**Charola, A. Elena (ed.). 1990.** *Easter Island: The Care of its Ancient Monuments and their Environment*. ICCROM / Easter Island Foundation. 370 pp.

Multi-author conservation volume with historical overview chapters containing substantial discussion of sociopolitical development, matatoa warriors, tangata manu transition, tribute systems (39 mentions), and ariki paka authority.

**Vargas Casanova, Patricia (ed.). 1998 [published 1999].** *Easter Island and East Polynesian Prehistory*. Proceedings of the II International Congress on Easter Island and Eastern Polynesian Archaeology. Santiago: Instituto de Estudios Isla de Pascua, Facultad de Arquitectura y Urbanismo, Universidad de Chile. ISBN: 956-19-0287-7. xvii + 228 pp.

Edited volume with 17 chapters by authors from Chile, the USA, French Polynesia, New Zealand, Italy, Norway, and France, covering colonization chronology, settlement, linguistics, physical anthropology, and the statues. Provides a broad framework for situating Rapa Nui within East Polynesian prehistory.

**Vargas Casanova, Patricia. 1998.** "Rapa Nui Settlement Patterns: Types, Function and Spatial Distribution of Household Structural Components." In P. Vargas Casanova and C. Cristino (eds.), *Easter Island and East Polynesian Prehistory*, pp. 111–130. Santiago: Instituto de Estudios Isla de Pascua, Universidad de Chile.

Describes house structures (hare), stone garden enclosures (manavai), and domestic architecture across the island. Relevant because residential architecture and garden enclosures relate to household-level social organization and land management.

## III. Secondary Works with Significant Political Organization Content

**Ramirez Aliaga, José Miguel. 1989.** "The Anthropological Background of Easter Island: An Outline." *Rapa Nui Journal* 3(2). 10 pp.

One of the few 1980s sources explicitly treating Rapanui social roles and their legitimation. Discusses the paramount chief (ariki mau), possessors of ritual knowledge (maori), warriors (matato'a), cosmological justification for social hierarchy, chiefdom structure, territorial limits of descent groups, centralized control of religious/economic activity, and the political impetus for the birdman cult transition.

**Stevenson, Christopher M., et al. 2001.** "Productive Strategies in an Uncertain Environment: Prehistoric Agriculture on Easter Island." *Rapa Nui Journal* 15(1): 17–22.

Includes a map of "Rapa Nui chiefdom districts circa AD 1500" and discusses elite competition, chiefly tribute, descent group territories, and polity structure in the context of agricultural production.

**Kirch, Patrick V. 2000.** *On the Road of the Winds: An Archaeological History of the Pacific Islands Before European Contact*. Chapter 7. Berkeley: University of California Press.

Synthesis chapter on East Polynesian chiefdoms with Rapa Nui as a key case. Discusses chiefly authority, political centralization, chiefdom evolution, descent groups, prestige-good exchange, paramountcy, warfare, and polity structure. Kirch applies the chiefdom model to Rapa Nui by analogy from the broader Polynesian comparative framework, without conducting archaeology on the island.

**Fischer, Steven Roger. 1994.** "Preliminary Evidence for Cosmogonic Texts in Rapanui's Rongorongo Inscriptions." *Journal of the Polynesian Society* 103: 303–321.

Analysis of the Hotu Matu'a foundation narrative with substantial discussion of descent group structure, chiefdom organization, clan and birdman cult references.

**Mulloy, Emily Ross. 1993.** "The Long and Short of It: Some Thoughts on the Meaning of the Names Hanau Eepe and Hanau Momoko in Rapanui Tradition." In S. R. Fischer (ed.), *Easter Island Studies: Contributions to the History of Rapanui in Memory of William T. Mulloy*, Oxbow Monograph 32. Oxford: Oxbow Books. 2 pp.

Re-examines the meaning of the two legendary population groups (hanau eepe / hanau momoko), traditionally mistranslated as "long ears" / "short ears." Relevant because this distinction has been central to discussions of social divisions and possible dual political organization.

**Mulloy, William. 1991 [posthumous reprint].** "Contemplate the Navel of the World." *Americas* 26(4). 16 pp.

Republication of Mulloy's overview of Easter Island cultural development. Discusses chiefdom models, kinship organization, matatoa warrior chiefs, prestige competition, and the ca. AD 1680 political transformation ("decisive conflict").

**Martinsson-Wallin, Helene. 1996.** "Variation and Meaning of Easter Island Ahu." In C. M. Stevenson and W. S. Ayres (eds.), *Easter Island in Pacific Context*. Los Osos: Easter Island Foundation.

Interprets ahu variation in terms of sociopolitical meaning, including ranked society, elite centers, lineage territories, and stratification. Discusses alternative interpretations beyond simple competitive models.

**Van Tilburg, Jo Anne. 1996.** "Mechanics, Logistics and Economics of Transporting Easter Island (Rapa Nui) Statues." *Rapa Nui Journal* 10(4): 110–115.

Moai transport logistics framed within chiefdom-level labor organization. The tangata manu cult interpreted as "an ideological adaptation to food resource availability." Discusses sociopolitical and environmental variables.

**Van Tilburg, Jo Anne. 1995.** "Moving the Moai: Transporting the Megaliths of Easter Island: How Did They Do It?" *Archaeology* 48(1). 11 pp.

Popular treatment discussing birdman (tangata manu) cult at Orongo in the context of declining food resources. Relevant because the birdman competition is widely interpreted as a political transition from hereditary to competitive leadership.

**Owsley, Douglas W., et al. 1994.** "Biological Effects of European Contact on Easter Island." In C. S. Larsen and G. R. Milner (eds.), *In the Wake of Contact: Biological Responses to Conquest*, pp. 161–178. New York: Wiley-Liss.

Bioarchaeological study documenting evidence of warfare, tribal conflict, cranial trauma, and social disruption in skeletal populations before and after European contact. The warfare evidence is important for understanding political instability.

**Stefan, Vincent H. 1999.** "Craniometric Variation and Homogeneity in Prehistoric/Protohistoric Rapa Nui (Easter Island) Regional Populations." *American Journal of Physical Anthropology* 110: 407–419.

Uses craniometric data to test whether burial populations in different territorial districts (Miru, Tupahotu, etc.) represent biologically distinct tribal/lineage groups. One of the few works testing the archaeological model of territorial political units using independent skeletal evidence.

**Dalton, Thomas R., and R. Morris Coats. 2000.** "Could Institutional Reform Have Saved Easter Island?" *Journal of Evolutionary Economics* 10: 489–505.

Economic/institutional analysis applying common-pool resource theory to Rapa Nui. Discusses social stratification, warfare, tribal governance, and whether institutional reform could have prevented societal collapse.

**Hunt, Terry L., et al. 2001.** "Cultural Elaboration and Environmental Uncertainty in Polynesia." In C. M. Stevenson, G. Lee, and F. J. Morin (eds.), *Pacific 2000: Proceedings of the Fifth International Conference on Easter Island and the Pacific*, pp. 103–115. Los Osos: Easter Island Foundation.

Uses Rapa Nui as a case study for cultural elaboration theory, discussing how chiefdom-level competition drove monument construction under environmental uncertainty.

**McCall, Grant. 1993.** "Little Ice Age: Some Speculations for Rapanui." *Rapa Nui Journal* 7(4): 65–70.

Speculates on climate-driven political disruption, connecting Little Ice Age effects to warfare, kinship-based polity fragmentation, and political instability.

**Love, Charles. 1990.** "The Interpretation of Site 5-72, an Easter Island Ahu." *Rapa Nui Journal* 4(1). 16 pp.

Ahu excavation report discussing chiefly functions of ceremonial platforms, lineage associations, birdman cult connections, tangata manu, and warfare evidence.

**Bahn, Paul G. 1993.** "The History of Human Settlement on Rapanui." In S. R. Fischer (ed.), *Easter Island Studies: Contributions to the History of Rapanui in Memory of William T. Mulloy*, Oxbow Monograph 32, pp. 85–88. Oxford: Oxbow Books.

Brief overview touching on sociopolitical complexity, political structure changes, and sociopolitical dimensions of settlement.

**Delsing, Riet. 1998.** "Cultural Politics and Globalization on Rapa Nui." [Conference paper or proceedings; exact venue unconfirmed.]

Discusses reassertion of Polynesian heritage and cultural difference by Rapanui people, rivalry between colonial powers, clan identity, and modern sociopolitical identity in relation to pre-contact organization. Note: a 1996 version may exist as a conference presentation preceding this publication, but is unverified.

**Van Tilburg, Jo Anne, and Ted Ralston. 1999.** "Engineers of Easter Island." *Archaeology* 52(6). 8 pp.

Analysis of moai form diversity (cross-section, type categories). Relevant because standardization and diversification of moai forms imply organized production, craft specialization, and possibly competitive signaling among lineages.

## IV. Works Addressing the Birdman Cult as Political Institution

**Charola, A. Elena. 1997.** "Death of a Moai." *Rapa Nui Journal* 11(2). 62 pp.

Substantial discussion of birdman cult, tangata manu institution, Hotu Iti faction, Hanau Eepe/Hanau Momoko division, and warfare.

**Orliac, Michel. 1992.** "HMS Topaze on Easter Island, Hoa Hakananai'a and Five Other Museum Sculptures in Archaeological Context." *Journal of the Polynesian Society* 101(1). 5 pp.

Discusses birdman cult sculptures (including Hoa Hakananai'a) as expressions of elite political authority, with references to the ariki mau and Hanau Eepe/Momoko factions.

**Lee, Georgia. 1993.** "The Petroglyphs of Tongariki." *Rapa Nui Journal* 7(3). 5 pp.

Documents petroglyphs at Ahu Tongariki with discussion of birdman cult imagery, clan associations (Tupahotu), and political significance.

**Lee, Georgia. 1989.** "Petroglyph Motif Distribution in East Polynesia." *Asian Perspectives* 26(1). 5 pp.

Comparative analysis of petroglyph motifs with discussion of clan-based distribution of rock art and Miru lineage associations.

**Koll, Robert R. 1991.** "Petroglyphs inside Orongo's Houses, Easter Island." *Rapa Nui Journal* 5(2). 2 pp.

Documents petroglyphs inside Orongo ceremonial village (seat of the birdman cult) with discussion of elite imagery.

## V. Contextual and Supporting Works

**Clark, Malcolm. 1990.** "Rapa Nui and the Population Explosion." *Rapa Nui Journal* 4(2). 2 pp.

Touches on chiefdom organization, chiefly authority, and tribal structure in the context of population growth.

**Mulloy, Emily Ross. 1991.** "The Destruction of Tongariki." *Rapa Nui Journal* 5(3). 2 pp.

Brief account of the 1960 tsunami with discussion of Hotu Iti moiety territory, warfare between factions, and tribal associations.

**Santa Coloma, María Eugenia. 1995/1997/1998.** "Seasonality of Marriages on Easter Island" (1995, 1997) and "Endogamy in Easter Island" (1998).

Studies of endogamy, marriage registers, and population dynamics. Marriage patterns reflect and reinforce social boundaries.

**Bierbach, Annette, et al. 1996.** "Religion and Language of Easter Island: An Ethnolinguistic Analysis of Religious Key Words of Rapa Nui in their Austronesian Context." 24 pp.

Ethnolinguistic analysis including descent group vocabulary (Tupahotu, Miru), tribal categories, and lineage terminology.

**Stevenson, Christopher M. 1993.** "Easter Island Excavations: Summary Report 1993." *Rapa Nui Journal* 7(4). 3 pp.

Field report interpreting Site 10-244 as a "chiefly residence" with rebuilding and reuse through the mid-1600s.

**Ayres, William S. 1985.** "Easter Island Subsistence." [In G. W. Gill and W. S. Ayres (eds.)] 25 pp.

Discussion of subsistence strategies. Not directly about political organization but relevant as background on the economic base that chiefdom models assume was under centralized control.

**Ramirez, José Miguel. 1994.** "Rapa Nui or Rapanui: Political and Academic Perspectives." *Rapa Nui Journal* 8(2). 2 pp.

Addresses the politics of naming rather than prehistoric political organization per se.

**Green, Roger C. 1998.** "Rapanui Origins Prior to European Contact, the View from Eastern Polynesia." In P. Vargas Casanova (ed.), *Easter Island and East Polynesian Prehistory*, pp. 87–110. Santiago: Instituto de Estudios Isla de Pascua, Universidad de Chile.

**Green, Roger C. 2000.** "Origins for the Rapanui of Easter Island Before European Contact: Solutions from Holistic Anthropology to an Issue No Longer Much of a Mystery." *Rapa Nui Journal* 14: 71–76.

Primarily about settlement chronology and origins, not political organization, but settlement models bear on founding population structure.

**Gill, George W. 1993.** "Introduction to the Proceedings of the Third International Conference on Easter Island Research." 2 pp.

Conference introduction. Contextual only.

## VI. Relevant Comparative Works (Hawaiian and Polynesian Political Models Applied to Rapa Nui)

These works are included because they supplied the comparative Polynesian chiefdom framework that was imported into Rapa Nui interpretations. Understanding the source of the analogy is essential to the paper's argument about circularity.

**Graves, Michael W., and Thegn N. Ladefoged. 1995.** "The Evolutionary Significance of Ceremonial Architecture in Polynesia." In P. A. Teltser (ed.), *Evolutionary Archaeology: Methodological Issues*, pp. 149–174. Tucson: University of Arizona Press.

General Polynesian comparative treatment including Rapa Nui, analyzing ceremonial architecture within an evolutionary framework. Relevant as a methodological bridge between Hawaiian models and Rapa Nui interpretation.

**Ladefoged, Thegn N., and Michael W. Graves (eds.). 2002.** *Pacific Landscapes: Archaeological Approaches*. Los Osos: Easter Island Foundation.

Edited volume containing Stevenson's "Territorial Divisions on Easter Island in the Sixteenth Century: Evidence from the Distribution of Ceremonial Architecture" (pp. 213–229), which applies territorial models derived from broader Polynesian frameworks to Rapa Nui ahu distributions.

## Notes on Dropped or Unverified Entries

**Van Tilburg 1989 (RAG-only retrieval).** The RAG retrieved a passage about "a Rapa Nui chief at Ahu Akivi and 'the society of which he was a part,'" attributed to a 1989 Van Tilburg article. No formally published 1989 Van Tilburg article matching this description appears in her complete bibliography (EISP website) or in Graves' CV. The passage likely derives from her 1994 book or from unpublished EISP field reports. The 1989 date may reflect the field season rather than a publication year, or a metadata error in the RAG pipeline. Dropped pending identification of source PDF.

**Ladefoged and Graves 1997 (RAG-only retrieval).** Described as a spatial/territorial analysis related to settlement patterns. No 1997 Ladefoged and Graves publication on Easter Island or territorial analysis appears in either author's complete CV. The closest candidates are their 1995 chapter on ceremonial architecture in Polynesia (listed above) or the 2002 edited volume. Likely a date error in the RAG. Dropped.

**Delsing 1996.** Possible conference version of the 1998 "Cultural Politics and Globalization on Rapa Nui." Unverified; the 1998 entry is retained as the confirmed publication.
