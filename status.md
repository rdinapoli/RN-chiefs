## Current Phase

**Phase 2: Chiefdom Concept Genealogy** (complete, 2026-03-16)

Contact-era and post-slave-raid ethnographic sources fully processed (Eras 0-1, 21 sources). Batch 1 (in-repo PDFs) complete. Chiefdom concept genealogy chain fully processed.

**Chiefdom concept genealogy complete**: Sahlins 1954 -> Sahlins 1955 -> Sahlins 1957 -> Sahlins & Service 1960 -> Service 1962 -> Sahlins 1963 -> Goldman 1970 -> Kirch 1984. All links in the chain have been processed. Key findings per source:

- **Sahlins 1954** (133 claims): Easter Island placed in Group IIa based entirely on post-slave-raid sources; redistribution evidence admitted as lacking (p. 101), filled by Polynesian analogy; ramage classification acknowledged as ecologically anomalous.
- **Sahlins 1955** (62 claims): Environmental channeling hypothesis; moai as redirected communal labor. Stone carver passage describes lineage-level commissioning, not centralized command. Redistribution asserted flatly, stripping dissertation qualifications.
- **Sahlins 1957** (claims file): Published journal version of dissertation Part Two only; most concise statement of framework, most stripped of Easter Island caveats.
- **Sahlins & Service 1960** (31 claims): Theoretical manifesto defining general vs. specific evolution. "Chiefdom" appears once (p. 36), not elaborated. Easter Island 6 times as adaptive specialization. The intellectual bridge to Service 1962.
- **Service 1962** (42 claims): Formalizes band/tribe/chiefdom/state. Easter Island NEVER MENTIONED. Chiefdom = "redistributional societies with a permanent central agency of coordination" (p. 144). Easter Island fails every criterion.
- **Sahlins 1963** (40 claims): Big-man/chief binary places all Polynesia on "chief" side by geographic definition. Easter Island's matatoa system fits "big-man" better.
- **Goldman 1970** (141 claims): Classified Easter Island as "Open" society; "had never evolved a central authority" (p. 94). Redistribution lay with commoner heads, not ariki mau. No central authority undertook monument construction.
- **Kirch 1984** (121 claims): Ahu "do not reflect some kind of highly centralized, island-wide political authority" (p. 270); quarry = "independently working crews" (pp. 270-271). Chiefdom maintained by APS inheritance assumption, not archaeological demonstration.

**Stevenson 1984-to-1986 transition documented** (2026-03-16): Stevenson 1984 dissertation (95 claims) and Stevenson 1986 chapter (90 claims) both processed. The 1986 chapter in Kirch's *Island Societies* volume does NOT use "chiefdom"; Service and Kirch are not cited. But the theoretical infrastructure (Fortes, Firth, Kirchhoff, Hayden & Cannon) that made corporate group analysis a principled alternative is stripped away, the explicit rejection of unilinear evolution is dropped, and "reverted to non-ranked structure" becomes "corporate solidarity weakened." Editorial context does the reframing. Documents Step 3 of the five-step circularity: independent archaeological analysis absorbed into chiefdom framework through editorial recontextualization rather than authorial adoption.

**Ayres 1973 processed** (2026-03-16): Tulane PhD dissertation, 402 pp, first systematic ahu study in cultural context. 65 claims extracted. Pre-chiefdom-model baseline: "chiefdom" does not appear; Service 1962 not cited; Goldman's "Open" framework adopted throughout. Ahu attributed to nonunilinear descent groups controlling land (p. 329), not chiefly redistribution. Ariki mau "was not a political ruler but more of a spiritual one" (p. 25). Craft guild for statue carving (p. 338). No redistribution discussion. Fellow Mulloy student with Stevenson 1984; both independently avoid chiefdom terminology.

- **Roussel's Mangarevan projection problem** (PRE-WRITING REQUIREMENT): Roussel 1869 is the strongest chiefdom-supporting early post-raid source (king list, four social classes, first-fruits ceremony), but Roussel came to Easter Island directly from Mangareva, where he had been a Picpus missionary. Mangareva was a more stratified society (Goldman classifies it as "Stratified," the highest tier; Sahlins places it intermediate). Roussel likely projected Mangarevan political categories (akariki, hereditary rank, tribute systems) onto what he observed on post-raid Easter Island. Before writing the paper, we need to establish: (1) what Mangarevan political organization actually looked like at contact and during Roussel's tenure; (2) which specific Roussel claims about Easter Island map onto Mangarevan categories rather than anything independently attested on Rapa Nui; (3) whether Van Bakel 1989 and Kirch & Conte 2004 (which qualifies rigid stratification, finding "open society" features) affect the comparison. A bibliography of Mangarevan primary and analytical sources is at `docs/working/mangareva_bibliography.md`. Key sources to acquire and process: Beechey 1831 (pre-missionary baseline), Buck 1938 (standard reference), Goldman 1970 Ch. on Mangareva (already in repo), Van Bakel 1989 (critical source analysis), Kirch & Conte 2004 (archaeological qualification). Note that Kirch & Conte 2004 finding "open society" features in Mangareva is itself interesting: if even Mangareva was less stratified than the standard portrait, then Roussel's projection of that standard portrait onto Easter Island is doubly problematic.
